> **30-Second Overview:** The 2022 Russo-Ukrainian War changed Taiwan's defense logic. In 2024, military service was restored from four months to one year. In 2025, the Han Kuang 41 exercise lasted the longest ten days and nine nights in history, mobilizing 20,000 reservists. In November of the same year, the Lai Ching-te government proposed a NT$1.25 trillion special budget to procure 200,000 drones. However, on the same list, most of the money was still spent on expensive traditional equipment like tanks, fighter jets, and submarines. Former Chief of the General Staff Li Hsi-ming smiled wryly at this: "If tanks can be asymmetric weapons, what is not an asymmetric weapon?" Taiwan is walking two defense paths simultaneously, and these two paths contradict each other.

In late October 2025, at the National Review Ground in Hukou, a row of M1A2T "Abrams" tanks stood on the concrete ground. Lai Ching-te stepped down from the rostrum to preside over the commissioning ceremony, announcing that the "strongest tank on the surface" had officially been incorporated into the Republic of China Armed Forces' combat sequence. The first batch of 38 vehicles arrived in Taiwan in December 2024, the second batch of 42 arrived in July 2025, and the final batch of 28 completed delivery in the first quarter of 2026, totaling 108 vehicles, deployed at the Hukou-based 584th Armored Brigade and the 269th Mechanized Brigade[^1].

At the same time, former Chief of the General Staff Li Hsi-ming was signing books at a bookstore. His book, _Taiwan's Winning Chance_, argues that Taiwan should not buy expensive large-scale weapons but should invest in numerous cheap, mobile, and concealable small systems. When asked how he viewed the Ministry of National Defense's use of "beachhead decisive battle" as the reason for purchasing M1A2T tanks, he smiled wryly and said, "I can only smile wryly at this. If tanks can be asymmetric weapons, what is not an asymmetric weapon?"[^2]

This sentence is the most honest key to understanding Taiwan's defense in 2026. The government speaks of the Porcupine Strategy while signing contracts for the most expensive US weapons; it claims defense autonomy while 90% of the budget goes to US suppliers; it extends military service while admitting that 200,000 drones are the future main combat power. These contradictions are not oversights; they represent an island pulling back and forth between two survival logics. It wants to become a porcupine, but its body still remembers it was originally a leopard.

## Why a Porcupine

The term "Porcupine Strategy" was coined by William Murray, a professor at the US Naval War College, in a 2008 paper. His argument was simple: Taiwan cannot win against the People's Liberation Army (PLA) in conventional military power. Any warship costing billions or any expensive fighter jet may survive for only hours after war breaks out. The only reasonable approach is to buy large quantities of cheap land-based mobile missiles, naval mines, and air defense systems, turning Taiwan into a "not worth eating" porcupine[^3].

This concept was not immediately accepted in Taiwan. It was former Chief of the General Staff Li Hsi-ming who truly wrote it into military strategy. In 2017, while in office, Li completed an internal document known by the military as the "Overall Defense Concept" (ODC). The core question was only one: If the PLA really attacks, how does Taiwan make itself "not tasty"? Li's answer was naval mines, mobile missile vehicles, shoulder-fired missiles, and large numbers of drones: cheap, numerous, concealable, and hit-and-run[^4].

His logic was the same as Murray's: Taiwan does not need to win; it only needs to make the cost of invasion so high that the other side dares not gamble.

However, the ODC was coldly handled after Li retired in 2019. The Ministry of National Defense bought more fighter jets, more tanks, and continued to promote the locally built submarine project. When reporters asked Li for his comments, he said President Tsai Ing-wen was "the president who respects the military the most I have ever seen," but he added, "Whether she understands the ODC? I am not sure, because different people might make different interpretations."[^5]

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> The counter-intuitive nature of the ODC lies in the fact that it challenges the entire military's self-identity. For professional soldiers, "fighter jets, warships, tanks" are the pillars of service culture; buying drones is equivalent to admitting that the Army, Navy, and Air Force all become "secondary" roles. The reason the ODC was coldly handled after Li's retirement was partly due to strategic divergence and partly due to service survival instincts.

## From Four Months to One Year

Taiwan's military service system is the thermometer of this island's security anxiety.

In the 1950s, conscription was three years. During the peak of the Cold War, Taiwan maintained 600,000 active-duty troops, with males enlisting upon reaching adulthood. In the 1990s, following the end of the Cold War, the term began to shorten. In the 2000s, "conscript-mobilization combined" was promoted, and in 2013, conscription was shortened to four months. Military commentators jokingly called this a "summer camp"[^6].

The turning point was the Russo-Ukrainian War in February 2022. A country once thought to surrender within three days held out using drones, portable Stinger missiles, and total mobilization, and drove the Russian army out of Kyiv within half a year. The resonance this war caused in Taiwanese society was indescribable. Suddenly, everyone was asking a question they had previously dared not ask: What if it were us?

On December 27, 2022, Tsai Ing-wen stood at a press conference to announce the restoration of conscription to one year, effective from January 2024. She said this was a "difficult but necessary decision." The monthly salary for conscripts increased from approximately NT$6,500 to NT$26,307[^7].

The training content for the new one-year service period was also updated: 8 weeks of basic induction, 18 weeks of garrison training, 7 weeks of specialized training, 13 weeks of base training, and 6 weeks of joint exercises, with rifle live-fire shooting not less than 800 rounds. Compared to the old four-month system's stereotype of "standing guard and sweeping floors," this was a backbone reconstruction.

> **⚠️ Controversial Viewpoint**
> Poll support for restoring one-year military service exceeds 70%, but the response of the young generation facing enlistment is complex. VOA interviewed several eligible young men; some admitted to "combat fear," some questioned whether one year of training could truly possess combat capability, and some directly stated, "I do not want to pay with my life for the mistakes of political figures." Supporters believe this is the minimum national defense obligation, while critics argue that one year is still not enough. One-year military service is both a consensus and a compromise; no one is truly satisfied, but most accept this is the furthest they can currently go[^8].

## The 147 Days of Hai-Shun

Taiwan has long relied on US military sales. F-16 fighter jets, Patriot missiles, M1A2 tanks, Harpoon missiles—almost all heavy equipment comes from Washington. This ties Taiwan's defense to US political will: if the White House changes hands, the speed of military sales may change.

"Defense Autonomy" has therefore become a long-term goal, with the locally built submarine project as the flagship plan. On September 28, 2023, the first self-built submarine, _Hai-Shun_ (Hull No. 711), was launched at the CSBC Kaohsiung Shipyard. The ship is 70 meters long with a displacement of approximately 2,500 tons, adopting an X-tail rudder design. Tsai Ing-wen said on that day: "History will remember this day." The plan subsequently called for the construction of seven more ships of the same class[^9].

But history may not remember the 2023 launch ceremony; it may remember the delays of the following two years. The delivery schedule originally set for 2024 was delayed. On June 17, 2025, _Hai-Shun_ officially entered sea trials, first conducting floating tests, then shallow-water diving, and finally deep-water diving. By November 28, 2025, _Hai-Shun_ ended a 147-day mooring period without going to sea and went to sea again for floating verification, with several key steps remaining before completing diving tests[^10].

When asked about the schedule of _Hai-Shun_, Defense Minister Kuo Li-hsiung gave an answer rarely spoken by high-ranking military officials: "Other countries usually take more than 7 years to build new submarines, with some examples taking 16 years. Our initial setting of 5 years was overly optimistic, lacking an understanding of reality, especially without considering national conditions."[^11]

Kuo Li-hsiung did not set a delivery schedule. He only said: "Only when testing is complete and safe will the subsequent delivery procedures enter."

> **✦** "History will remember this day." This was Tsai Ing-wen's original words at the _Hai-Shun_ launch ceremony on September 28, 2023. Two years later, _Hai-Shun_ has not yet completed its diving tests. History will remember this day, but perhaps not in the way the person who said those words on that day intended.

## Between the "Strongest Tank" and the "Cheapest Drone"

In November 2025, the Lai Ching-te government proposed the _Special Regulations on Strengthening National Defense Resilience and Acquiring Asymmetric Combat Power_, planning to allocate a special national defense budget of NT$1.25 trillion (approximately US$40 billion) over 8 years. If passed, Taiwan's defense spending would rise from the current 2.5% of GDP to 3.3%. Of this, approximately NT$950 billion (76%) would flow to US arms dealers for the purchase of traditional weapon platforms[^12].

The other half of the picture of the same budget: The plan calls for the procurement of over 200,000 various types of drones and more than a thousand unmanned surface vessels, covering multi-rotor reconnaissance drones, fixed-wing attack drones, loitering munitions, etc. Approximately NT$300 billion will be manufactured locally in Taiwan, creating a so-called "non-Red supply chain," an defense industrial system excluding Chinese components[^13].

Putting these two halves together reveals the contradiction in Li Hsi-ming's wry smile: The Porcupine Strategy is accepted in name, but the budget still favors traditional large-scale weapons.

At the same time, the National Science and Council's (NSC) domestic drone program features three names, representing Taiwan's attempt to localize the "Ukrainian experience":

- **Jian-Xiang (Sword Eagle)**: Anti-radiation loitering munition, mass-produced starting in 2023, producing 104 units for the Air Force within six years. It has radar electronic parameter analysis capabilities, able to detect enemy air defense radar signals and then conduct high-speed "suicide attacks," similar to the Israeli Harpy concept[^14].
- **Rui-Yuan Type II**: Medium-to-long range reconnaissance drone, wingspan 12 meters, control distance 300 kilometers, maximum range 2,000 kilometers, with a derived "armed attack type" under development.
- **Teng-Yun Type II**: MALE (Medium Altitude Long Endurance) large drone, using the same engine as the US military's MQ-9B, staying airborne for over 20 hours, not yet mass-produced.

All three are not cheap, nor are they considered "large quantities." The true 200,000 drones will likely be smaller, cheaper, possibly even using 3D-printed fuselages for tactical drones. On the Ukrainian battlefield, an FPV drone costing tens of thousands of yuan can destroy a tank worth hundreds of millions; this calculation has changed the way all modern armies compute.

However, the fate of the NT$1.25 trillion budget is not a strategic issue, but a political one. Since December 2, 2025, the opposition-dominated Legislative Yuan has blocked the special budget bill at least eight times. The opposition questions the budget compilation method and oversight mechanisms, while the ruling party accuses blocking the budget as blocking national defense. US Congressional delegation members visiting Taiwan publicly called on the Legislative Yuan to pass the budget, and the Ambassador to the US spoke directly in support[^15].

This Legislative Yuan offensive and defense is actually the third layer of contradiction in Taiwan's defense: Whether to spend this money, and how to spend it, is itself the most sensitive issue regarding cross-strait relations.

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> National defense budgets are a mirror, reflecting not military power but political will. There are many countries that have money to buy weapons; there are few countries willing to bear political costs to buy weapons. Taiwan is both of these countries, both wealthy and divided. The true bet of the NT$1.25 trillion is not what is procured, but whether it can "pass politically" itself.

## Black Bear Academy: Chen Shui-bian's Open Letter

The deepest transformation in Taiwan's defense is not in the weapon list, but in the conversations in living rooms and coffee shops.

"Black Bear Academy" is a civilian organization founded in 2021 by Shen Po-yang, a professor of criminology at National Taipei University, and Ho Cheng-hui, a senior civil defense researcher. It focuses on "Total Defense" education. Its basic camp has four classes: modern military science popularization to dispel military rumors, information identification and cognitive warfare to dispel fake news, wartime first aid hemostasis and patient transport, and evacuation preparation planning[^16].

In 2022, semiconductor entrepreneur Tsao Hsing-cheng announced a donation of NT$600 million to fund Black Bear Academy. On January 7, 2025, former President Chen Shui-bian publicly disclosed on the internet that Tsao Hsing-cheng had fulfilled this commitment, revealing that the goal set between the two was to "train 3 million Black Bear Warriors within 3 years," accounting for about one-third of Taiwan's 9 million households, hoping that at least one person in each household would possess basic defense knowledge[^17].

Black Bear Academy's classes were full from the start. A female student said in a _Vision_ magazine interview: "I do not want to fight a war; I do not want to be unable to protect my children when war comes." This sentence later became Black Bear Academy's own promotional copy[^18].

> **✦** "I do not want to fight a war; I do not want to be unable to protect my children when war comes." This is the most honest sentence about Taiwan's defense consciousness in 2026. It is not patriotism; it is the most primal family instinct.

The Total Defense Mobilization Preparation Act simultaneously established the reserve mobilization system. After 2022, reserve training was expanded from 5 days to 14 days, significantly increasing training intensity. Official estimates suggest that over 2 million reservists can be mobilized; this number, combined with 180,000 active-duty troops, means that theoretically, the armed manpower mobilizable during wartime approaches one-tenth of Taiwan's labor force.

## Han Kuang 41: Ten Days and Nine Nights of Deep Defense

At dawn on July 9, 2025, the Han Kuang 41 exercise unfolded simultaneously across Taiwan. Lasting 10 days and 9 nights, it was the longest in the history of the Han Kuang exercises since their inception in 1984, mobilizing 20,000 reservists, the largest number ever[^19].

The biggest difference between Han Kuang 41 and the past is the mood of the script. Past Han Kuang exercises tended to display military might: tank parades, fighter jets skimming the sky, naval ship formations. The core assumption of Han Kuang 41 is cruel: **The ROC Armed Forces will only have partial air superiority during wartime and must fight a protracted war.**

The exercise is divided into six stages:

1. **Days 1-3**: China's gray zone harassment, Taiwan enters "normal crisis handling." This is a lesson from the Russo-Ukrainian War for Taiwan; war does not start with tanks crossing the border, but with fishing boat harassment, communication jamming, and a flood of fake news.
2. **Day 4**: War preparation deployment, moving troops and setting up positions.
3. **Days 5-10**: Formal outbreak of war, divided into four sub-stages: "Anti-landing, Coastal Combat, Deep Defense, Protracted War."

The last three sub-stages—Coastal Combat, Deep Defense, Protracted War—are the true new things in Han Kuang 41. It explicitly admits: The active-duty army cannot hold all the deep territory; reservists must go to the second line, using conscripts and reserve forces to drag down the invading enemy, allowing volunteer-formed main combat forces to find gaps to counterattack.

This is the shadow of the ODC. Li Hsi-ming's idea in those years was to turn Taiwan into a "net"; if the front line cannot be held, retreat to the deep territory, making the enemy pay a price wherever they retreat. Han Kuang 41 is the first time this concept has been systematically adopted in an exercise script[^20].

## 160 Kilometers and a Bet

The narrowest point of the Taiwan Strait is approximately 130 kilometers, and the widest is approximately 400 kilometers. This body of water is known in military academia as a natural barrier of "Anti-Access/Area Denial" (A2/AD). For the PLA to cross the Taiwan Strait, it needs not just ships and aircraft, but continuous weeks of air and sea superiority and amphibious landing capability, which is something only two or three existing armies in the world can do[^21].

Taiwan's defense strategy is built on a cold calculation: **No need to win, only need to hold out until international intervention intervenes.** A porcupine does not need to defeat the hunter; it only needs to make the hunter feel "eating this is not worth it." M1A2T tanks, F-16V fighters, Hai-Shun submarines, their mission is not to defeat the PLA; their mission is to drag out time. 200,000 drones, 20,000 reservists, 3 million Black Bear Warriors, are doing the same thing.

But the premise of this calculation is: The international community will intervene.

The F-16V Block 70 was originally scheduled for delivery in 2024, but due to US supply chain and software issues, it was delayed until 2027[^22]. _Hai-Shun_ has been delayed by more than two years from launch to expected delivery. The NT$1.25 trillion special budget has been blocked eight times in the Legislative Yuan. M1A2T tanks arrived in Taiwan as scheduled, but it is the type of weapon Li Hsi-ming sees as "how can a tank be an asymmetric weapon." Every schedule, every budget, every type of equipment reminds us of the same thing: Taiwan's defense is not a plan for Taiwan alone.
