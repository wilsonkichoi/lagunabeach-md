On the late night of October 25, 1971, in the United Nations General Assembly hall in New York. Republic of China Foreign Minister Zhou Shu-kai finished reading his statement, turned, and walked down the green marble podium. The entire delegation filed out in succession. From entry to exit, less than twenty minutes[^1]. Half an hour later, Resolution 2758 passed 76 to 35, "Chiang Kai-shek's representatives" were expelled, and the People's Republic of China formally took over the China seat. Taiwan went from being a founding member of the United Nations (on August 24, 1945, Chiang Chung-cheng signed the instrument of ratification of the _Charter of the United Nations_ in Chongqing[^2]) to an outsider in the international order overnight.

> **30-second summary**: On the surface, this was the Republic of China's tragic, defiant exit under the banner of "no coexistence between Han and traitor." In reality, Chiang Kai-shek had already accepted a "Two Chinas" compromise in early 1971, and the U.S. State Department even praised Taiwan for demonstrating "extraordinary flexibility"[^3]. The real turning point was Henry Kissinger's secret visit to Beijing in July of that year — while officials in Taipei and Washington were still in the dark, he had already shown his hand to Zhou Enlai: the United States would not genuinely fight to keep Taiwan in the United Nations. Half a century later, the U.K., the EU, Australia, and the Netherlands have all stated: the text of Resolution 2758 does not contain the word "Taiwan."

## From Founding Member to Seat Defense

The Republic of China was a founding member of the United Nations and one of the five permanent members of the Security Council. On June 26, 1945, in San Francisco, T.V. Soong signed the _Charter of the United Nations_ on behalf of the Republic of China as the first signatory; on August 24 of the same year, Chiang Chung-cheng signed the instrument of ratification in Chongqing[^2].

The problem emerged after 1949: the People's Republic of China was established in Beijing, while the Republic of China government retreated to Taiwan, yet both claimed to be the sole legitimate representative of "China." From the 1950s onward, every UN General Assembly session featured a row over "China's representation." In 1961, the United States introduced the "Important Question" resolution, designating any change to China's representation as an "important question" requiring a two-thirds majority to pass, thereby blocking Beijing by procedural means[^3].

Former Foreign Minister George Yeh had a famous metaphor: "The 'One China' policy is like painting the floor of a room — once you've finished painting, you retreat to outside the room door."[^4] This prophecy came true in 1970. At the 25th General Assembly that year, the Albanian-sponsored "expel us, admit the bandits" resolution achieved a simple majority for the first time (51 to 49). The Republic of China held onto its seat only because the "Important Question" resolution's two-thirds threshold stood in the way[^3].

The red light was already on.

## Chiang Kai-shek's Wavering: Two Chinas in the Diaries

Scholars long attributed the UN withdrawal to Chiang Kai-shek's stubborn adherence to "no coexistence between Han and traitor." But after the National Palace Museum opened the _Chiang Chung-cheng Diaries_ and diplomatic archives in 2010, the historical picture was rewritten.

Scholar Wang Hao, who studied these archives, pointed out that on December 16, 1970, at a National Security Council meeting, Chiang Kai-shek explicitly instructed the Ministry of Foreign Affairs to study all UN regulations, "making the utmost effort to safeguard our legitimate status in the United Nations, but also preparing for the worst-case scenario of withdrawal"[^3]. At a diplomatic meeting on December 31, he told the ambassador corps even more plainly: he did not oppose studying a "dual representation" plan, but the Republic of China must retain its permanent seat on the Security Council[^3].

In other words, "no coexistence between Han and traitor" was a strategy for maintaining legal legitimacy domestically and negotiating with the United States; privately, Chiang Kai-shek had already psychologically accepted "Two Chinas." But his red line was the Security Council seat — it symbolized the Republic of China's legal status as a "great power."

On May 26, 1971, Vice President Yen Chia-kan chaired the National Security Council on Chiang's behalf. Secretary-General of the National Security Council Huang Shao-ku and Foreign Minister Zhou Shu-kai proposed a startling shift: if a diplomatic partner wished to establish relations with Beijing, diplomatic ties could be maintained as long as three preconditions were met (not recognizing the CCP as the sole legitimate government of China, not involving the territory of Taiwan of the Republic of China, and continuing to recognize our government as the government of the Republic of China, not a "Taiwan government")[^3]. This was in substance an acceptance of a "Two Chinas" arrangement. Chiang Ching-kuo responded on the spot that "further thought was needed."

Chiang's diary also reveals his inner torment. He wrote of "the ancient teaching of being a pillar in the midstream and fighting as a lone army — it is a glorious isolation," then revised it days later to "a weak nation's diplomacy cannot but have an inner firmness and outer flexibility; one should have a resolute decision in one's heart, but outwardly, until the time for action arrives, one should adapt to circumstances and respond opportunistically"[^3]. On May 27, he was diagnosed with an enlarged heart, fell seriously ill in mid-June, and did not recover until the end of July — and this window of incapacity coincided precisely with the timing of Kissinger's secret visit to Beijing.

## Dual Representation: The Road Not Taken

In early 1971, the Nixon administration formally proposed the "Dual Representation" plan — allowing the People's Republic of China to obtain the permanent Security Council seat while preserving the Republic of China's membership in the General Assembly[^5]. On April 23, Nixon sent special envoy Robert Murphy to meet Chiang Kai-shek. To secure Chiang's agreement, Murphy unilaterally promised that "the Republic of China would retain its Security Council seat" — a commitment that clearly exceeded White House instructions and authorization[^3].

Chiang Kai-shek finally relented. But he warned Murphy: if the Security Council seat were stripped away, he would "have no choice but to prefer broken jade to an intact tile."

The problem was that Murphy had no authority to make this promise. On May 27, Nixon spoke his true feelings to Kissinger and Secretary of State William Rogers: "I really wish we could stick to principle and fail, and tell them to get lost." Kissinger suggested something even more insidious: another way to "be defeated" was to delay and stall, then feign taking a "Two Chinas" position and fail — "that would also show we tried everything"[^3].

This delay strategy ultimately succeeded, "deceiving Rogers and U.S. Ambassador to the UN George H.W. Bush (later President), as well as Taiwan's diplomats, and even many later historians"[^3]. The elder Bush was a genuine champion of the dual representation plan, fighting desperately for votes in the General Assembly hall until the very last moment — unaware that he was being used as a pawn[^6].

## Kissinger's July: Stabbed in the Back

On the afternoon of July 1, 1971, on the eve of his secret visit to Beijing, Kissinger spoke with Republic of China Ambassador to the United States Shen Chien-hung. Kissinger declared earnestly: "President Nixon's friendship toward your president and your government runs deep... his determination to do everything possible to safeguard our seat in the General Assembly is beyond doubt. President Nixon will also never consider any measure that would cost us our Security Council seat. Please rest assured on this point."[^3]

Ten days later, the words Kissinger spoke to Zhou Enlai in Beijing meant the exact opposite.

From July 9 to 11, Kissinger feigned a stomach ailment in Pakistan and flew secretly to Beijing to meet Zhou Enlai. According to later declassified transcripts of the conversation[^7], Kissinger proactively revealed the bottom line of the American "complex dual representation" plan to Zhou Enlai: Beijing could join the United Nations by a simple majority and obtain the Security Council seat, but expelling the Republic of China would require a two-thirds majority. He promised Zhou Enlai: "As long as China joins the United Nations, then obtaining two-thirds of the votes to expel Taiwan will only be a matter of time; the key is that both the U.S. and China are fully aware of this arrangement, reach an understanding, and wait patiently."[^3]

Zhou Enlai was unmoved: "We have already waited 21 years." He warned: "Taiwan will also oppose it, and opposition will come from all directions." Kissinger raised his hands in surrender, acknowledging this "is a very good way to end this problem"[^3].

On July 15, Nixon publicly announced Kissinger's visit to China. Small and medium-sized countries that had been sitting on the fence shifted their positions en masse. From October 20 to 26, while the General Assembly was debating China's representation, where was Kissinger? In Beijing again — this time on an official visit, paving the way for Nixon's trip to China the following year[^8]. Taipei diplomats described it: "We were fighting bloodily on the front lines, while our ally was having tea in the enemy's capital."

Former Vice Foreign Minister Chien Fu stated bluntly in an interview years later: "The Americans would rather sacrifice the Republic of China to court the Chinese Communist Party."[^9]

## Those 17 Minutes: Zhou Shu-kai Steps Down from the Podium

The proceedings on October 25 were highly dramatic.

In the afternoon, Saudi Arabian representative Jamil Baroody proposed a compromise: let Beijing obtain the Security Council seat, while Taiwan remain in the General Assembly under the name "Republic of Taiwan," pending a cross-strait resolution of their differences[^7]. Had this proposal passed, Taiwan's fate might have been completely rewritten. But the Republic of China delegation insisted on "One China" and did not actively pursue it; the proposal was shelved.

At 9:47 PM, the "Important Question" resolution was voted down — 55 to 59. At this moment, the battle was already lost.

Zhou Shu-kai immediately raised his hand to speak. He walked to the podium and read a prepared statement: "The Republic of China has decided to withdraw from the United Nations, an organization in which it participated in founding." He then led Liu Kui (then Permanent Representative to the UN) and other delegation members in walking out[^1][^10].

At 10:25 PM, Resolution 2758 was put to a vote: 76 in favor, 35 opposed, 17 abstentions, 3 absent. The resolution text was only four paragraphs long. The key sentence read: "Decides to restore all its rights to the People's Republic of China... and to expel forthwith the representatives of Chiang Kai-shek from the place which they unlawfully occupy at the United Nations and in all the organizations related to it."[^11]

It is worth noting: the resolution text does not contain the word "Taiwan," nor does it define the territorial scope of "China"[^11]. This became the spark for five decades of international law disputes to come.

## Chiang Kai-shek's "Message to Compatriots" and the Shock at Home

On the morning of October 26, Chiang Kai-shek issued the _Message to All Compatriots on the Republic of China's Withdrawal from the United Nations_: "Our nation, upholding the position of 'no coexistence between Han and traitor' and in defense of the dignity of the Charter... could not but announce with anguish our withdrawal from the United Nations organization that we participated in founding through arduous effort."[^12] The eight characters at the end — "self-respect and self-reliance, remain composed in the face of change" — were printed on textbooks and carved over school gates for the next thirty years.

But the shock at home was far more brutal than the official language. NTU Law School alumni Hung San-hsiung and Chen Ling-yu recalled: "When the news reached Taiwan, the dream of 'recovering the mainland and rescuing our compatriots' was shattered in an instant, not to mention that 'no coexistence between Han and traitor' became nothing but empty words."[^13] This defeat directly ignited the NTU student movement and the "Defend the Diaoyu Islands Movement" from 1971 to 1973. Students began to challenge the party-state mythology, and many future leaders of the dangwai (outside-the-party) and democracy movements had their political awakening in this storm.

Yao Chia-wen, publishing _The 1971 Myth: The United Nations_ in 2021, stated bluntly that Chiang Kai-shek "would rather be expelled than represent Taiwan" — had the Baroody plan been accepted and Taiwan remained under the name "Taiwan," the island's subsequent international situation would have been entirely different[^14]. But Wang Hao's research indicates: the problem was actually that Nixon and Kissinger had already decided to sacrifice Taiwan; "no amount of flexibility on Chiang's part could have saved the situation" — this is the story of two national leaders joining forces to betray an ally[^3].

## Echoes 50 Years Later: What Did Resolution 2758 Actually Say?

The consequences of withdrawing from the UN were domino-like. From 1971 to 1979, the number of countries with which the Republic of China had diplomatic relations plummeted from 68 to just 22; Japan severed ties in 1972, the United States in 1979[^15]. Taiwan was excluded from the WHO, ICAO, and Interpol, and its passport was frequently misidentified.

But since the 2020s, the international community has begun to reexamine Resolution 2758.

From 2024 onward, the United States, the United Kingdom, the European Parliament, Australia, the Netherlands, and other countries have passed resolutions or issued statements explicitly stating that Resolution 2758 only addressed "China's representation," did not address "Taiwan's political status," and did not authorize China to represent Taiwan[^16]. On April 23, 2025, the United States publicly criticized China for "misinterpreting" Resolution 2758 in the UN Security Council for the first time; on May 5, the U.S. House of Representatives passed the _Taiwan International Solidarity Act_ by unanimous consent, formally codifying this position in domestic law[^17].

Returning to those 17 minutes in 1971 — the footsteps of Zhou Shu-kai walking down the podium locked Taiwan into its international predicament for half a century. But the text of the resolution itself never completely closed the door. The gray space left in the cracks is precisely the opening that Taiwan's diplomatic workers are striving to widen today.
