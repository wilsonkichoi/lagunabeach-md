# Taiwan Elections and Party Politics

> **30-second overview:** In the 1950s, the "democracy showcase" elections let people vote for legislators but not for president. By 1996, 14.31 million voters cast the first direct presidential election in any Chinese-speaking society.[^9] In less than half a century, Taiwan went through three party alternations (2000, 2008, 2016), transforming elections from a political ornament of the martial law era into a quadrennial civic ritual. From the flames of the Zhongli Incident to the three-way standoff of 2024, this island's democracy was not designed — it was fought for, one ballot at a time.

On November 19, 1977, at polling station 213 in Zhongli, Taoyuan County, a poll worker was observed smudging a ballot with an ink-stained thumb. Word spread, and over ten thousand citizens surrounded the Zhongli Police Station. By nightfall, flames had consumed the building. Two people died: Jiang Wenguo, a National Central University student, was shot in the head; 19-year-old Zhang Zhiping fell in the street.[^1]

That fire did not just burn down a building. It sent a signal: Taiwan's voters would no longer tolerate stolen ballots. In that election, Tangwai candidate Xu Xinliang won a landslide victory over the KMT's Ou Xianyu with 230,000 votes.[^2] The Zhongli Incident became the first blaze of Taiwan's democracy movement. Ballots could be stolen, but the popular will could not.

## The Evolution of a Ballot

### The Democracy Showcase (1950s)

In the 1950s, the Nationalist government needed to prove to the United States that it was "Free China." Local elections served as that "showcase" — letting America see the form of democracy while the KMT kept tight control over the content. The 1950 Taiwan Provincial Assembly election was the first popularly elected body after the war, but the rules were clear: you could elect legislators but not the provincial governor; you could criticize policy but not question the leadership.[^3]

Even under strict control, elections still opened cracks. In 1951, Wu San-lien was elected mayor of Taipei as an independent, with 65.6% of the vote — becoming the first popularly elected mayor of a capital city under the Republic of China.[^4] The KMT quietly backed him behind the scenes, but the mere fact of a "non-KMT" mayor taking office at Taipei City Hall showed other indigenous politicians what was possible.

### War on the Ballot (1970s)

In the 1970s, Taiwan was hit by successive shocks — expulsion from the United Nations (1971) and the severance of diplomatic relations with the United States (1979) — and the KMT's legitimacy began to waver. The middle class grew, and Tangwai voices began to surface in elections.

In 1975, 67-year-old Yilan politician Guo Yuxin ran for the Legislative Yuan. When the results were announced, nearly 100,000 invalid ballots had appeared in Yilan County alone — several times the normal proportion. Later, during a road construction project, a bag of discarded valid ballots was unearthed, every one of them marked for Guo Yuxin. The lawyers who fought the election lawsuit for him were named Yao Chia-wen and Lin Yi-hsiung — four years later, both would become defendants in the Kaohsiung Incident.[^5]

Guo Yuxin's votes were stolen. But that lawsuit taught an entire generation of Tangwai activists: the law could be a weapon.

### From Underground to the Grand Hotel (1980s)

The Kaohsiung Incident (1979) dealt a heavy blow to the Tangwai movement, but it also raised the next generation. The defense attorneys for the arrested leaders — Chen Shui-bian, Hsieh Chang-ting, and Su Tseng-chang — became the new faces of the democracy movement. Family members of the arrested — Lu Hsiu-lien, Chen Chu, and Zhou Qingyu — entered politics, becoming the female force of the Kaohsiung generation.

On September 28, 1986, 132 Tangwai figures gathered in the Dunmu Room on the second floor of Taipei's Grand Hotel and announced the founding of the Democratic Progressive Party. This violated the then-existing "Period of National Mobilization for Suppression of the Communist Rebellion Civic Organizations Act" — forming a party was a crime. When the news reached Chiang Ching-kuo, his aides recommended a crackdown. Chiang said: "The times are changing, the tide is changing, and the environment is changing."[^6]

Ten days later, Chiang told Washington Post publisher Katharine Graham that Taiwan would lift martial law and open up politically. The DPP's founding was not suppressed — the KMT chose tolerance.

Two months later, in the legislative elections, the DPP won 22.2% of the vote and 12 seats. A party only sixty days old had already found its footing.[^7]

## Electing Their Own President

### Full Re-election of the Legislature (1991–1992)

In 1991, the "ten-thousand-year legislature" that had persisted for forty-three years was finally fully re-elected. The old legislators and National Assembly delegates elected on the mainland in 1997, who had never faced re-election, stepped down. The 1992 full re-election of the Legislative Yuan marked the first time the Taiwanese people truly elected their own parliament.[^8]

### 1996: Missiles and Ballots

On March 23, 1996, 14.31 million voters walked into polling stations and cast the first direct presidential election in Taiwan — and in any Chinese-speaking society.

The PRC had fired missiles into the waters off Taiwan before the election, intending to intimidate voters. The result was the opposite: turnout surged to 76%. Lee Teng-hui won 5.81 million votes (54%), Peng Ming-min 2.27 million (21.1%), Lin Yang-kang 1.60 million (14.9%), and Chen Lü-an 1.07 million (10%).[^9] The missiles did not scare anyone away.

### 2000: Three-Way Race and the First Alternation

The 2000 presidential election was the most dramatic night in Taiwan's political history. The KMT split: former Taiwan Province Governor James Soong ran as an independent, splitting the pan-Blue vote with the KMT's Lien Chan.

The results: Chen Shui-bian 4.97 million votes (39.3%), James Soong 4.66 million (36.8%), Lien Chan 2.92 million (23.1%). The DPP won the presidency with less than 40% of the vote.[^10]

The key was not who won, but what the losers did. The KMT lost fifty-five years of power — no tanks rolled, no coup was staged. Lien Chan and James Soong conceded. Power transferred peacefully. Taiwan proved, for the first time, that its democracy was not just theoretical.

### 2004: Two Bullets

On March 19, 2004, the day before the election, Chen Shui-bian and Lu Hsiu-lien were shot while campaigning in Tainan. One bullet grazed Chen's abdomen; the other struck the knee guard on Lu's leg. Both were hospitalized and discharged the same day.[^11]

The next day, Chen defeated Lien Chan by fewer than 30,000 votes. Pan-Blue supporters gathered in front of the Presidential Office to protest, alleging the shooting was staged. The prime suspect, Chen Yi-hsiung, was found drowned in Tainan's Anping Harbor ten days later. The truth remains disputed to this day.

This was the moment Taiwan's democracy came closest to breaking. The election result was questioned by nearly half the electorate; street protests lasted for weeks. But in the end, legal procedures did their job: recounts, lawsuits, court rulings. The system held.

## The Fracturing Party Landscape

Taiwan's party history is not as simple as "two-party system." It is a story of constant splitting, regrouping, death, and rebirth.

Internal rifts within the KMT erupted in the 1990s. In 1993, the New Party was founded by the New KMT Alliance, who opposed Lee Teng-hui's "localization" direction. In 2000, James Soong established the People First Party after his election loss. In 2001, after Lee Teng-hui was expelled from the KMT, he backed the founding of the Taiwan Solidarity Union.[^12]

The DPP side also had cracks. In 2015, the energy of the Sunflower Movement was channeled into the New Power Party, which advocated a more radical Taiwan-centered line than the DPP. In 2019, Taipei Mayor Ko Wen-je launched the Taiwan People's Party, attempting a "beyond Blue-Green" third way.[^13]

By 2024, Taiwan's party landscape had roughly settled into three major forces: the DPP (Green), the KMT (Blue), and the TPP (White). But history tells us this map could fracture again at any time.

## How Taiwanese People Campaign

### Rally Culture: Democracy's Carnival

The most distinctive spectacle of Taiwanese elections is the campaign rally. Candidates rent public squares, erect stages, have celebrity warm-up acts, and crowds wave flags chanting "Dong suan! Dong suan!" (Taiwanese Hokkien for "elected," a homophone of "凍蒜").

Rallies are more than political mobilization — they are community festivals. Vendors line the streets, children ride on their fathers' shoulders, and elderly people carry stools to save seats. Elections are one of the few occasions where Taiwanese people can simultaneously fulfill political participation, social needs, and entertainment consumption.

### Returning Home to Vote: An Annual Migration

Taiwan still has no absentee voting system. Voters must cast their ballots in person at their registered household address — no mail-in ballots, no early voting.[^14]

This means that every election day triggers a massive "return home to vote" migration. Kaohsiung natives working in Taipei, Yunlin natives employed in Hsinchu — all scramble for train tickets to go home and vote. The high-speed rail adds extra runs; bus companies deploy additional vehicles. Taiwan's HSR stations on election day are more crowded than during Lunar New Year.

Opponents of absentee voting worry that the more than one million Taiwanese working in China could be subject to Beijing's influence if they could vote remotely. Supporters argue that denying people the right to vote where they live is a de facto voting barrier. This dispute remains unresolved.

### Election Night

Taiwan's vote count is a live national broadcast. After polling stations close at 4 p.m., election workers open the ballot boxes on the spot, counting each ballot one by one, calling out the names. Television cameras are set up at every counting station for live broadcast.

No country's vote count is more transparent than Taiwan's. You can stand outside a polling station and watch every ballot being displayed, called out, and tallied. From the closing of the polls to the approximate final result, it usually takes only three to four hours.

## Recent Key Moments

### 2018: The Han Wave and the Referendum Tsunami

The 2018 local elections were a political earthquake. KMT candidate Han Kuo-yu, a political outsider, parachuted into Kaohsiun and defeated the DPP's Chen Chi-mai with 890,000 votes, ending two decades of Green rule in the city.[^15] Across Taiwan, the KMT won 15 of 22 county and city mayor seats; the DPP was left with only six. Tsai Ing-wen resigned as party chair.

The simultaneous citizen-initiated referendums were even more stunning. Ten referendum questions covered energy, food safety, same-sex marriage, and gender-equity education — voters faced ten referendum ballots at once, and polling stations saw massive queues. Among them, the referendum defining marriage as between a man and a woman passed, creating a direct clash between public opinion and law on Taiwan's path to becoming Asia's first country to legalize same-sex marriage.[^16]

### 2020: 8.17 Million Votes

Two years later, the 2020 presidential election saw a complete reversal. Hong Kong's Anti-Extradition Law Movement concretized Taiwanese society's fear of "one country, two systems." Tsai Ing-wen staged a comeback from her 2018 defeat, winning **8,170,186 votes** — the highest vote total for any candidate in Taiwan's electoral history. Han Kuo-yu received 5.52 million votes; turnout was 74.9%.[^17]

The number 817 later became a political symbol — embraced by supporters as a symbol of democratic will, cited by opponents as evidence of polarized politics.

### 2024: The Three-Way Race Returns

The 2024 presidential election reprised the three-way standoff of 2000. Lai Ching-te (DPP) won with 5.58 million votes (40%), Hou Yu-ih (KMT) 4.67 million (33.5%), and Ko Wen-je (TPP) 3.69 million (26.5%).[^18]

This was the first time in Taiwan's direct-election history that a candidate with less than 50% of the vote was elected, and the first time a party won the presidency for three consecutive terms. At the same time, the DPP lost its Legislative Yuan majority — with the executive and legislative branches controlled by different parties, Taiwan entered a new era of "divided government."

## A Democracy Still Evolving

In 2005, the National Assembly voted to abolish itself — this body, elected in Nanjing in 1947, completed its final task in Taiwan and passed into history. The same constitutional amendment cut the Legislative Yuan from 225 seats to 113, extended the term from three years to four, and changed the electoral system to a single-district, two-vote system. Future constitutional amendments must pass a citizen referendum, with a threshold so high it is nearly impossible to cross.[^19]

Taiwan's electoral system continues to evolve. A 2022 citizen-initiated referendum proposed lowering the voting age from 20 to 18, but because this was a constitutional amendment, it required approval from more than 9.61 million people — in the end, only 5.64 million voted in favor, and it failed to pass. Money politics, disinformation, and voting system reform are all unfinished business.

From the flames of the 1977 Zhongli Incident to 14 million people quietly lining up to vote in 2024 — Taiwan spent less than half a century turning elections from a right that required fire to protect into a habit that runs on its own, without anyone needing to remind anyone.

Not every election is perfect. But every time, the losers leave, and the winners take office. On this planet, that is not as self-evident as it looks.
