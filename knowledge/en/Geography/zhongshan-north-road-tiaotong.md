# Zhongshan North Road Tiaotong: The Road to the Shrine Paved by the Japanese, Eventually Reclaimed by Japanese Companies

> **30‑second overview:** On April 17, 1923, 19‑year‑old Japanese Crown Prince Hirohito rode a carriage from Taipei Station along a 15‑metre‑wide road whose destination was the Taiwan Shrine in Yuanshan[^1]. The Japanese named it “Chihshi Street”. Construction to widen it began on March 30, 1937[^2]; on March 28, 1941 at 11 a.m. a completion ceremony was held at the shrine. The road became a 40‑metre, five‑lane boulevard with a central median of camphor trees, sidewalks lined with maple trees, mercury street lamps, and underground wiring; the project cost more than 1.62 million yen[^2]. On October 25, 1945 at 10 a.m., Andō Toshikichi surrendered to Chen Yi at the Taipei Public Hall (now Zhongshan Hall)[^3]; Chihshi Street was renamed “Zhongshan North Road” in honor of the founding father Sun Yat‑sen. In 1951, following the Korean War, the U.S. Military Advisory Group (MAAG) arrived[^4]; the third segment of Zhongshan North Road became a “U.S. concession”, and the Qingguang market turned into a hub for P.X. (Post Exchange) imported goods[^5]. On September 29, 1972, the day the Republic of China severed diplomatic ties with Japan, Japanese firms did not leave; after the U.S. withdrawal on April 28, 1979, the former USTDC headquarters became the Taipei Fine Arts Museum[^6]. In the 1980s Japanese companies moved in en masse, and more than 400 Japanese‑style izakayas operated simultaneously in the Tiaotong alleys[^7]. Tonight at nine p.m., stepping into the three‑lane Tiaotong, the izakaya curtains rise, spilling out laughter in a mix of Japanese and Taiwanese. A 5‑kilometre road, three foreign empires layered one after another, the road the Japanese built to the shrine was finally reclaimed by Japanese trading houses. This article argues that the Zhongshan North Road lived by Taipei residents is not a “Sun Yat‑sen Memorial Road” but a material landscape left by an island that experienced three successive foreign imperial administrations.

## Nine p.m. in the Three‑Lane Tiaotong

If you ask a 50‑year‑old Taipei resident “the most charming moment on Zhongshan North Road”, they will not mention the red‑tiled roof of the Grand Hotel Yuanshan. They will say the moment they walk into the three‑lane Tiaotong at nine p.m.

From Exit 4 of Zhongshan MRT Station, walk north 50 metres to the right‑hand side of Lane 53 of Section 1, and you enter the three‑lane Tiaotong area. The alley is 4 metres wide, flanked by three‑ to five‑storey old buildings; the ground‑floor signs display Katakana and Chinese characters side by side: “Torichū” (鳥忠), “Jorī Pasta” (ジョリーパスタ), “Ryū Izakaya” (龍居酒屋), “Yaki‑tori Miyachika” (焼鳥みやちか). Before 5:30 p.m. the place is as quiet as an office alley, but after 7 p.m. the signs light up one by one; by nine p.m. the second izakaya’s curtain lifts, releasing a blend of Japanese and Taiwanese laughter.

Seven‑Lane Tiaotong is the most famous of the Tiaotong culture – Section 1, Lane 121 of Zhongshan North Road plus Lane 119 of Linsen North Road[^8]. Netflix’s 2021 series _The Romance of the Bright Lights_ was filmed in this lane; the “Hikari” hotel entrance appears in the Sugar Bar bar on Seven‑Lane Tiaotong[^9]. Real‑life filming also took place in Lane 133 of Linsen North Road for the first‑meeting scene between Ruby Lin and Yang You‑ning.

The Tiaotong culture, however, extends farther – “one‑lane” is the municipal avenue, “two‑lane” is Section 1, Lane 33 of Zhongshan North Road, “three‑ to nine‑lane” run from Lane 33 to Lane 135 of Section 1 and from Lane 67 to Lane 145 of Linsen North Road, arranged neatly; “ten‑lane” is Lane 159 of Linsen North Road[^8]. The ten‑lane alleys run parallel northward, modeled after Kyoto’s grid‑like “machikiri” design: east‑west streets are called “tō” (通) and north‑south streets are called “suji” (筋)[^10].

This grid was not a natural bar street. In 1922, when the Japanese incorporated this area into the upscale residential district “Taisho Town”, they built exclusive residences for Japanese officials and hybrid‑style Western houses; it was the most secure and respectable residential area during the Japanese colonial period[^10]. A century later, the same grid houses different occupants, but the alleys remain – the material skeleton of Taisho Town is the physical basis of the Tiaotong bar scene.

> **📝 Curator’s note:** General introductions often describe Tiaotong as “the bars opened by the Japanese when they came to Taiwan”, which omits a crucial chronological nuance. In 1922, when the Japanese planned the upscale residential district, there were no bars. After Japan’s 1945 withdrawal, the Nationalist government repurposed the Japanese‑style dormitories into civil‑servant housing – still a residential zone. In 1950, the Korean War broke out; in 1951 the U.S. military arrived, and **the U.S. opened bars in these alleys** (American‑style bars for American soldiers, not Japanese izakayas). The 1972 Sino‑Japanese diplomatic break, the 1979 U.S. withdrawal, and the 1980s Japanese economic bubble brought Japanese firms back – the “Japanese‑style bars” were actually the **second** Japanese incursion, this time by corporate employees rather than colonial officials. Same grid, same orientation, but a 35‑year interlude of U.S. presence separates the two phases. Today’s nine‑p.m. curtain lift and bilingual jokes are the result of a three‑stage relay: Japanese colonial upscale housing → post‑war U.S. military bars → 1980s Japanese corporate izakayas.

## A Road Renamed Three Times

To understand Zhongshan North Road, first know its names.

During the Qing era, this land lay outside the Taipei city walls. The Taipei Prefecture walls, completed in 1884, enclosed 1.4 km²; east of the East Gate (Jingfu Gate) lay rice fields, cemeteries, and scattered settlements. Today’s southern end of Section 1, near the Executive Yuan, was then called “Kawahashi‑chō” (樺山町), a name adopted in the 1922 Japanese town‑name reform to honor the first Japanese Governor of Taiwan, Kawahashi Sakki[^11].

From 1898 onward, the Japanese began reshaping the land north of Kawahashi‑chō, constructing a north‑south road leading to Yuanshan. What lay in Yuanshan? In October 1901 the Taiwan Grand Shrine was completed at the foot of Jiantan in Yuanshan[^12]. The shrine’s principal deity was Prince Kitashirakawa Naruhisa, who led the Imperial Guard’s expedition to Taiwan in 1895 and died of illness in Tainan that October[^12]. The Japanese enshrined this “imperial prince who died for the conquest of Taiwan” as Taiwan’s “guardian deity”; the shrine held the rank of “Kanpeisha” (官幣大社), the highest‑rank shrine in colonial Taiwan[^12].

The 5‑kilometre road from Taipei Station to the Taiwan Shrine had a special Japanese name – **Chihshi Street**. “Chihshi” (敕使) refers to “imperial envoys” in Japanese. On April 16, 1923, 19‑year‑old Crown Prince Hirohito (later Emperor Shōwa) rode a carriage from Taipei Station along Chihshi Street to pay respects at the Taiwan Shrine[^1]. Accompanying him were Prince Kitashirakawa Naruhisa, Prince Fushimi Hiroyasu, and other imperial family members[^1].

Hirohito’s 1923 visit directly shaped the later form of Chihshi Street. Starting in 1936, the Japanese began planning a widening because 1940 marked the imperial “2600th year” (Kōki 2600) celebration; the Taiwan Shrine also needed expansion, so the pilgrimage road had to be upgraded[^2]. Construction began on March 30, 1937; on March 28, 1941 at 11 a.m. a completion ceremony was held at the shrine – the road widened from 15 metres to 40 metres, became a five‑lane boulevard, with a central fast lane and two slower side lanes all paved with asphalt, trees planted along the entire length, a camphor‑tree median island, maple trees on the inner sidewalks, mercury street lamps, and underground wiring. The cost was over 1.62 million yen – a substantial budget even as wartime material shortages intensified, indicating the road’s importance in the imperial narrative[^2].

> **📝 Curator’s note:** The March 28, 1941 completion ceremony was the last large‑scale public‑construction rite of the Japanese colonial administration. Later that year, the Pearl Harbor attack launched the Pacific War, and the Taiwan Governor‑General’s construction budget was quickly swallowed by the war effort. Chihshi Street was finished at the very last moment – 40 metres wide, lined with camphor and maple trees, with underground wiring – and then the Japanese left. Walking today along Sections 1‑3 of Zhongshan North Road, the median trees have been replaced a few times, but the 40‑metre width set in 1941 has never changed. **A road was expanded halfway, the empire collapsed, yet the road itself remained**; some of the 1941‑planted trees survived until the 1970s before being replaced. The Japanese ceremonial space is a material legacy of the city, whether you like it or not.

On October 25, 1945 at 10 a.m., three months after World War II ended, Andō Toshikichi, holding both the titles of commander of the Japanese 10th Area Army and Governor of Taiwan, surrendered to ROC representative Chen Yi at the Taipei Public Hall (now Zhongshan Hall)[^3]. The surrender ceremony marked the end of the Governor‑General’s authority that very day.

Chihshi Street ended as well. After the Nationalist government took over, the road was renamed “Zhongshan North Road” in honor of Sun Yat‑sen[^11], and the Meiji Bridge became “Zhongshan Bridge”[^13]. The imperial pilgrimage road became a Sun Yat‑sen memorial road – the name changed, but the road remained the same.

## The 1947 Machine‑Gun Sweep

Less than 16 months after the renaming, a pivotal event occurred on the road that altered post‑war Taiwan’s destiny.

On the evening of February 27, 1947, Taiwan Provincial Monopoly Bureau agents seized cigarette dealer Lin Jiang‑mai in front of the Tianma Tea House in Dadaocheng; during the arrest they wounded Lin with a gunstock, provoking public outrage – agent Fu Xuetong, in the chaos, mistakenly shot and killed passerby Chen Wen‑xi[^14]. The next day, February 28, a crowd gathered.

Around 1 p.m., about 400‑500 people set out from Taipei Station, marching south along Chongqing South Road toward the Taiwan Provincial Governor‑General Office (now the Executive Yuan). The route was: Chengde Road → Nanjing West Road → Dihua Street → Minsheng West Road → Yanping North Road → Beimen → Zhongxiao West Road → Chongqing South Road → Zhongshan South Road → Governor‑General Office[^15]. When the procession reached the Zhongshan Road intersection, before reaching the plaza, guards raised rifles and halted the march; subsequently, guards on the second floor opened fire with a machine gun, killing at least two and wounding several others[^15].

The machine‑gun sweep occurred at the Zhongshan Road intersection – today the crossing of Zhongshan South Road and Zhongxiao West Road, only one street north of Section 1 of Zhongshan North Road. That afternoon, Taipei City Council President Huang Chao‑qin convened an emergency meeting at Zhongshan Hall (the former Public Hall). On March 1, 1947, the “Investigation Committee on the Tobacco‑Related Bloodshed” was established at Zhongshan Hall, later renamed the “February 28 Incident Committee”[^16].

> **📝 Curator’s note:** The mainstream 228 narrative focuses on the Tianma Tea House (the first shot) and the Governor‑General Office (the machine‑gun site). Zhongshan Hall’s role is often omitted. The Public Hall hosted the Japanese surrender ceremony on October 25, 1945, and on February 28, 1947 it hosted a decisive meeting that shaped Taiwan’s post‑war trajectory for the next 38 years. **From peace to war, from surrender to suppression, this building witnessed two extreme historical moments within a year and four months.** Today, Zhongshan Hall’s plaza is as quiet as a tourist spot, but the ground beneath it is the physical site of two post‑war watershed events. The starting point of Zhongshan North Road – the Zhongshan South‑North intersection in front of the Executive Yuan – is only 800 metres from Zhongshan Hall and 1.5 km from the Tianma Tea House. The small triangular area formed by these three points is the physical epicenter of Taiwan’s post‑war tragedy.

## The 1951 Wave of U.S. Troops

On December 7, 1949 the Nationalist government announced the relocation to Taiwan. Chiang Kai‑shek arrived on the night of December 10 at 8:30 p.m.[^17]. From that year onward, Zhongshan North Road became one of the Republic of China’s most important axial roads – the northern end linked the military stronghold of Yuanshan, the southern end connected the Executive Yuan, the Presidential Office, and the five ministries.

But the next transformation of Zhongshan North Road came from war.

The Korean War broke out on June 25, 1950. U.S. President Truman immediately dispatched the Seventh Fleet to the Taiwan Strait and resumed military aid to the ROC. In 1951, the **U.S. Military Assistance Advisory Group (MAAG)** formally entered Taiwan[^18].

Where was the MAAG headquarters? In the third segment of Zhongshan North Road, near the Yuanshan section[^18]. The specific site is today the **Zhongshan Fine Arts Park** (the few‑hectare land between Minzu East Road and Zhongshan North Road)[^18]. After the Sino‑American Mutual Defense Treaty took effect on April 26, 1955, the **U.S. Taiwan Defense Command (USTDC)** was established, and from November 1, 1955 it was located in Yuanshan, with its headquarters now the **Taipei Fine Arts Museum**[^6].

At its peak, MAAG had 2,347 enlisted personnel[^18]. Throughout the 1950s‑70s, the third segment of Zhongshan North Road concentrated the USTDC, MAAG, the U.S. Embassy (from 1953 housed in a Victorian‑style building at No. 18, Section 2, Zhongshan North Road) and the American military families’ Tianmu housing complex (built from 1953, around Lane 181, Section 7, Zhongshan North Road)[^19][^20].

From Section 1 up to Section 3 (a five‑kilometre stretch), the U.S. diplomatic, military, and residential bases stood side by side. Only six years after the name change from Chihshi Street to Zhongshan North Road, the area became a “U.S. concession”.

**Qingguang Market** emerged in this context. Established in 1951 near the intersection of Zhongshan North Road and Shuangcheng Street, it was originally to be called “Zhen‑guang Market”, but because the Taiwanese pronunciation of “Zhen‑guang” sounded like “absurd”, it was renamed “Qingguang” meaning “bright and clever”[^21]. The market’s growth hinged on the U.S. P.X. (Post Exchange) – imported goods (jewelry, clothing, gifts, department‑store items) flowing from the P.X. were sold at Qingguang stalls[^21]. In the 1950s‑60s, Qingguang was the fastest way for Taipei residents to experience “American” material life.

During the 1959–1960 “August‑7 Flood”, the USTDC mobilized three U.S. Navy helicopters for rescue; the U.S. later dispatched the aircraft carrier _USS Sikorsky Bay_ carrying 21 Sikorsky H‑34 helicopters to Taiwan under Operation Hunger[^22]. Post‑disaster reconstruction poured massive U.S. aid into Taiwan.

On August 26, 1963, the **Taipei U.S. Military Club** was established in Section 3; from 1965 to 1972, during the Vietnam War, it hosted hundreds of thousands of U.S. troops on R&R (Rest and Recuperation) trips[^23]. At the height of the war, several thousand American soldiers flew weekly from Vietnam to Taipei, spending money in the Qingguang commercial zone. American Bars, imported‑goods shops, and hostess bars formed a consumption ecosystem rooted in the U.S. military presence, not in the later 1980s Tiaotong culture.

> **📝 Curator’s note:** Popular accounts attribute the Tiaotong “bar culture” to the Japanese colonial “geisha‑accompanied drinking” tradition, reversing the chronology. **The 1920s Taisho Town was an upscale residential area with no bars**. After Japan’s 1945 withdrawal, the Nationalist government turned the former Japanese dormitories into civil‑servant housing – still no bars. **Bars only appeared after the 1951 U.S. arrival**, initially in the American Bar style – cowboys, jazz, martinis, dancing hostesses. After the 1972 diplomatic break and the 1979 U.S. withdrawal, **the 1980s Japanese firms took over the same premises**, converting American Bars into Japanese‑style izakayas, swapping dancing for “mamasan” hostessing – but the material streetscape inherited the U.S. era, not the Japanese colonial period. The “Japanese‑style” of Tiaotong is a post‑1980s repatriation, not a 1920s relic.

## 1972: The Day Japan Did Not Leave

On September 29, 1972, at the East Hall of the Great Hall of the People in Beijing, Japanese Prime Minister Kakuei Tanaka and Foreign Minister Masayoshi Ohira signed the **Joint Communiqué of China and Japan**, announcing diplomatic relations between the People’s Republic of China and Japan[^24].

The same day, the Republic of China announced, under the principle “the Chinese and the Japanese cannot coexist”, the severance of diplomatic ties with Japan[^24]. The Japanese Embassy in Taipei was quickly re‑designated as the “Taipei Office of the Exchange Association” (a non‑official body).

Nevertheless, Japanese businessmen did not depart.

The 1970s were the high‑growth “Shin‑bō” and “Iwai” boom period for Japan; corporations from Tokyo and Osaka established overseas subsidiaries en masse, and Taipei became a key destination for Japanese expatriates[^7]. The 1972 diplomatic break did not force Japanese firms out; instead, the political vacuum after the break created a need for dense non‑official commercial footholds. Where did Japanese firms settle? Naturally, they returned to the grid squares of the former Taisho Town – the eastern side of Zhongshan North Road and both sides of Linsen North Road, i.e., the Tiaotong alleys.

Viewed as a relay between the U.S. and Japanese presence: 1972 Sino‑Japanese break → 1979 U.S. withdrawal → 1980s Japanese corporate entry. This eight‑year transition completely rewrote the consumption pattern on the same street. On April 26, 1979, **the USTDC held its final flag‑lowering ceremony at its Yuanshan headquarters**[^6]. On April 28, 1979, the last U.S. defense commander, Rear Admiral James B. Linder, left Taiwan[^6]. By May 3, 1979 the final U.S. serviceman departed[^18].

Qingguang Market felt the impact immediately. “After the 1979 withdrawal of the U.S. Military Advisory Group and the defense command, the once‑vibrant Qingguang Market gradually declined, losing its former bustle”[^21]. Yet the Tiaotong alleys did not decline – the same year the U.S. left, Japanese firms had already begun taking over.

During Japan’s 1980s economic bubble, Japanese corporate presence in Taipei exploded. Along Linsen North Road, Japanese bars, izakayas, restaurants, and karaoke venues proliferated; at its peak, **over 400 establishments operated simultaneously**[^7]. In March 1989, **the first Cashbox KTV opened on Linsen North Road**, expanding to five branches by year‑end[^25]. The late‑1980s to early‑1990s Linsen North Road became one of the birthplaces of Taiwan’s KTV industry.

The term **Tiaotong** – Japanese “jō” (條, alley) + “dōri” (通, street) – was also solidified during this period. Before the 1980s, Taipei residents referred to the area as “Linsen North Road” or “the alleys of Section 1 of Zhongshan North Road”; after the 1980s, “Seven‑Lane Tiaotong”, “Eight‑Lane Tiaotong”, etc., became common.

> **📝 Curator’s note:** On the day the 1972 Joint Communiqué was signed, Japan’s embassy withdrew, but most Japanese corporate staff stayed. Why? Because the 1970s Japanese economy was export‑driven – semiconductors, automobiles, electronics needed overseas markets, and Taiwan was the geographically and culturally closest option. **After diplomatic severance, both sides abandoned formal diplomatic equality but continued extensive economic exchange.** The same logic applied to the 1979 Sino‑American break – the U.S. troops left, but the 1979 Taiwan Relations Act allowed the U.S. to maintain robust unofficial relations via the American Institute in Taiwan, which persists today. **Taipei served as a material laboratory for the “diplomatic break but continued interaction” model of the 1970s‑80s.** The two USTDC sites in Section 3 became the Fine Arts Museum and Fine Arts Park – military land downgraded to cultural use. Yet Japanese firms kept operating in the Tiaotong alleys, a change invisible at the street‑level.

## 1996: Renaming the Southern End

Before 1996, the road in front of the Presidential Office was called “Jieshou Road” – renamed in 1946 to celebrate Chiang Kai‑shek’s 60th birthday[^26]. The same road continued north as Zhongshan North Road through Sections 1‑3, reaching Yuanshan. This 6‑kilometre north‑south axis is the most concentrated material landscape of political power in Taiwan.

On March 21, 1996, newly elected Taipei mayor Chen Shui‑bian renamed “Jieshou Road” to **Keelung‑Kadran Avenue**[^26]. The rationale was clear – Jieshou Road commemorated an individual leader, while Keelung‑Kadran Avenue honors the indigenous peoples of the Taipei basin[^26].

This renaming creates a symmetrical structure in Zhongshan North Road’s history. In 1923 Crown Prince Hirohito rode from Taipei Station along Chihshi Street – the imperial ceremonial road. In 1996 Chen Shui‑bian named the southern end after the island’s original inhabitants, a reverse gesture against the colonial past. **73 years after the Japanese emperor’s pilgrimage, the opposite end of the same road was dedicated to the pre‑Japanese indigenous peoples.**

1996 also marked a turning point for the two ends of Zhongshan North Road. The former U.S. Embassy building, slated for demolition in 1995, was designated a municipal historic site on February 20, 1997[^19]. On November 10, 2002, after restoration, it reopened as **Guangdian Taipei** (Taipei Home), operated by the Taiwan Film Institute under director Hou Hsiao‑hsien, with hardware sponsorship from the TSMC Cultural and Education Foundation[^27]. The Victorian‑style building, which had lain idle for 18 years after the 1979 Sino‑American break, became a hub for Taiwanese film culture.

On December 20, 2002, **Zhongshan Bridge (the former Meiji Bridge) was dismantled**, cut into 435 pieces and stored at the former site of the Re‑Spring Swimming Pool[^13]. Completed in 1933, the reinforced‑concrete arch bridge had been listed among the “Eight Views of Taiwan”[^13]; after 69 years it was removed during Mayor Ma Ying‑jeou’s tenure, officially for “obstructing the Keelung River flood‑control channel”. The physical link from Chihshi Street to Zhongshan North Road that survived the war was finally severed.

## Three Places, Three Imperial Traces

Having walked through the story of Zhongshan North Road’s Tiaotong culture, locals will not take you to the Grand Hotel Yuanshan for photos, nor to Din Tai Fung for dumplings. Locals will guide you to three sites, each corresponding to a different imperial imprint.

**Guangdian Taipei** (No. 18, Section 2). Built in 1926 as a Southern‑style Victorian white mansion, it became the U.S. Consulate in 1949, the U.S. Embassy in 1953, lay idle after the 1979 diplomatic break, was listed as a municipal historic site in 1997, and reopened as a cinema in 2002[^19][^27]. Today Guangdian Taipei screens four to six art films daily; the ground floor houses a wool‑and‑flower coffee bar. Sitting on the second‑floor balcony, you can watch the constant traffic of Section 2 while standing on the very floor where U.S. ambassadors ate breakfast from 1953 to 1979. **From a U.S. political outpost to a Taiwanese film‑culture venue**, the conversion is more concrete than any textual description.

**Grand Hotel Yuanshan** (No. 1, Section 4, Yuanshan). From 1901 to 1944 the site housed the Taiwan Shrine (later upgraded to Taiwan Grand Shrine); in 1944 a Japanese aircraft (legend says a friendly‑fire incident) destroyed it[^28]. On May 10, 1952, Chiang Sung‑mei‑ling led the “Taiwan Friendship Association” to take over the former shrine grounds and rebuild it as “Taiwan Grand Hotel”, later renamed “Grand Hotel Yuanshan”[^28]. By 1963 the hotel’s foundation was complete; in 1973 architect Yang Zhuocheng completed the 14‑storey Chinese‑palace‑style red‑tile building – from then on, the Grand Hotel became one of Taipei’s most iconic landmarks. **From the highest‑rank Shinto shrine of the Japanese empire to the Republic of China’s welcoming gateway**, the same plot of land shifted from ritual to hospitality, yet the “top‑tier” political symbolism never vanished.
