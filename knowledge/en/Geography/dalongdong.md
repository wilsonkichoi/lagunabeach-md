# Dalongdong: The Incense of Baoan Temple, the Bell of the Confucius Temple, and the Blue Sky with a White Sun at Yuanshan, Three Eras of Taipei Faith

> **30-second overview:** Walk out of MRT Yuanshan Station and within 1.5 kilometers you find three spaces of faith: Baoan Temple, first built in rudimentary form by Tong'an migrants in 1742; the Taipei Confucius Temple, rebuilt through fundraising by local gentry in 1925; and the Grand Hotel Taipei, designed by Yang Cho-cheng in 1973. Along the same axis, three different eras of “orthodoxy” stand side by side. The history beneath your feet is not merely 300 years old: 5,300 years ago, Neolithic people at the Yuanshan shell midden already lived here. In the seventh year of the Jiaqing reign of the Qing dynasty, Wang Yuanji and five other Tong'an households opened 44 tiled shops; the “long tong” in “xinglong Tong'an” became today’s Datong District. In the ninth year of the Xianfeng reign, Chen Wei-ying passed the provincial examination, and local residents, “regarding scholars as dragons,” changed Dalongtong into Dalongdong. In 1853, Tong'an migrants defeated in the Ding-Xia feud first took refuge at Baoan Temple as a defensive center, then moved to Dadaocheng. In 1907, the Japanese demolished the original Taipei Prefectural School Confucius Temple to build a Japanese-language school; the Chen Yueji family and Koo Hsien-jung donated more than 3,000 ping of land so that Confucius would have a home again. In 1944, a Japanese passenger plane crashed while landing at Songshan Airport and destroyed Taiwan Jingū; after the war, the shrine was removed, and Soong Mei-ling’s Taiwan Goodwill Association took over the original site in 1952. In 1995, Liao Wu-chih refused government subsidies and spent NT$260 million of temple funds to restore Baoan Temple, which in 2003 received Taiwan’s first UNESCO Asia-Pacific Heritage Award. What this article wants to say is this: the uncles and aunties practicing tai chi on Dalong Street are standing on Taipei’s most compressed slice of time.

## Seven in the Morning, Tai Chi in the Plaza Before Baoan Temple

Exit MRT Yuanshan Station from Exit 2, cross Kulun Street, turn left, and in five minutes you reach the plaza in front of Baoan Temple.

At seven in the morning, this plaza belongs to uncles and aunties. A dozen people form a circle to practice tai chi, while elementary school students from Dalong Elementary pass by with backpacks. The children do not look at the temple. Their eyes stop at the wall by the school gate, a wall built in 1933, with arcades and terrazzo stairs[^1]. But their school began in 1896. That year, the Japanese established the “Third Affiliated School of the Japanese Language School” inside Baoan Temple; school and temple shared a courtyard until 1933, when the school moved into the neighboring campus[^1].

In other words, the schoolchildren walking past the temple today, backpacks on their shoulders, are walking the same road as the schoolchildren who studied the Japanese fifty-sound syllabary behind Baoan Temple 130 years ago.

Four people are worshiping at the incense burner on the east side of the temple plaza. One elderly grandmother holds three incense sticks in one hand and steadies herself with a cane in the other, waiting for her turn. She is worshiping Baosheng Dadi. This deity was a man from Tong'an in the Northern Song dynasty, originally named Wu Ben (pronounced “Tao”), born in 979 and deceased in 1036; during his lifetime he was skilled in medicine and saved lives[^2]. When Tong'an migrants crossed the sea to Taiwan, they brought this belief with them. In 1742, the seventh year of the Qianlong reign of the Qing dynasty, people from Tong'an in Quanzhou brought a branch spirit of Baosheng Dadi from Baijiao Ciji Temple in Longhai, Zhangzhou, Fujian, and built a simple wooden temple at Daronpon[^3]. That was the embryo of a “temple,” and Baosheng Dadi had arrived.

The main hall would take another 88 years to take shape. Only in 1830, the tenth year of the Daoguang reign, was the temple formally completed[^3].

On the wall beside the temple are more than two hundred years of cut-and-paste ceramic decoration. Brick-red and emerald-green glass fragments form the Eight Immortals, dragons and phoenixes, and flowers; painted dougong brackets sit below. Before 1995, these ceramic pieces were peeling away, the paintings had faded, and termites had entered the beams and columns[^4]. In 1985, the government designated Baoan Temple a second-grade historic site, but after designation, no one restored it.

> **📝 Curator’s note:** The historical axis of Dalongdong is especially clear because three spaces of faith stand within 1.5 kilometers of one another: Baoan Temple to the south (1742-1830), worshiping Baosheng Dadi, the medical deity of Tong'an migrants; Taipei Confucius Temple 300 meters farther north (1925-1939), worshiping Confucius, the shared figure of Han Chinese literati; and, another kilometer north across the Keelung River, the Grand Hotel Taipei (1952-1973), with the red tiles and yellow eaves of the Nationalist government’s reception site for foreign guests. Three eras, three “orthodoxies,” on a single axis. Other historic districts in Taipei, such as Bangka, Dadaocheng, and Ximending, also have layers, but rarely are three layers still in their original places in this way. Bangka Longshan Temple, founded in 1738, is a single Qing-period focal point; Dadaocheng, from 1885, is a major commercial center under Japanese rule; Ximending, from 1908, is a Japanese-era entertainment district. Only along the Dalongdong-Yuanshan axis do three layers remain before your eyes: Qing rule, what the Japanese period demolished, and what the postwar party-state built.

## Not Just 300 Years: Digging Five Thousand Years Down

Walk north from Baoan Temple, pass Jiuquan Street and Kulun Street, and you will see Taipei Expo Park. On the eastern half of the park is an inconspicuous patch of grass. Beneath it lies the Yuanshan Archaeological Site.

The site was discovered on March 7, 1897, by the Japanese scholars Ino Kanori and Miyamura Eiichi[^5]. From 1953 to 1954, Shih Chang-ju, a professor in National Taiwan University’s Department of Archaeology, led faculty and students in a systematic excavation on the northwestern foothills of Yuanshan[^5]. The excavated material broadly divides into two layers: the lower layer is Corded Ware culture, dating to 6,500-4,500 years before present; above it is Yuanshan culture, dating to 4,500-2,500 years before present[^5][^6].

The most prominent feature of Yuanshan culture is its shell midden. The soil layers contain large quantities of discarded edible shells, animal bones, fish bones, pottery, stone tools, and bone and antler implements[^6]. The main shellfish species are large clams, river snails, Stenomelania snails, oysters, top shells, and Nassariidae snails. The first three are freshwater shellfish; the latter three are saltwater shellfish[^6].

What does this mean? It means that Neolithic Yuanshan people could gather both freshwater and saltwater shellfish. At the time, the Taipei Basin was still a brackish lake, the ancient Taipei Lake, and Yuanshan was a small island in it. They cultivated rice, kept domestic animals, hunted, fished, and lived in a settlement with “a rigorous social organization and a symbolic belief system of agricultural ritual”[^6].

On April 25, 1988, the Ministry of the Interior announced Yuanshan as a national first-grade historic site[^6]. It was Taiwan’s earliest designated prehistoric site, and also “the beginning of archaeology in Taiwan”[^7]. On May 1, 2006, its status was changed to a national archaeological site[^6].

> **💡 Did you know:** The “shells” in the Yuanshan shell midden were not merely food waste. After Neolithic Taipei residents ate the shellfish meat, they used the shells as building material for paving floors, building walls, and burial equipment; some bone and antler implements, such as fishhooks, needles, and spearheads, were also ground from animal bones. In other words, they did not waste material. The grass where we walk dogs today in Taipei Expo Park has, 50 centimeters below the surface, the kitchen waste and tools left by these people: a human settlement from 5,000 years ago.

Continue north from the Yuanshan Archaeological Site and you will see Jiantan Mountain. In legends from the 1660s, Koxinga’s army passed through the area and encountered a monster that stirred up wind and waves; Koxinga threw a treasured sword into the water to subdue it[^8]. The 1732 _Taiwan Zhilüe_, compiled in the tenth year of the Yongzheng reign, records another version: on the bank of Jiantan stood a tall bishop wood tree, and “it is said that the Dutch inserted a sword into the tree; the bark grew over it, enclosing the sword within, and thus the place was named”[^9]. The difference between the two versions is this: Koxinga’s sword was in the water; the Dutch sword was in the tree. Neither version has been confirmed by scholars, but the name “Jiantan,” literally “Sword Pond,” has remained part of Taipei’s landscape into 2026.

## From Daronpon to Dalongdong: A Place Name in Four Layers

Before the three characters “Dalongdong” appeared, this land had other names.

The earliest records are Dutch-period documents from 1645 to 1655. At the time, the Ketagalan people, specifically a Basay-speaking group, had a settlement called “Daronpon,” also written Paronpon and Parongpom[^10]. After Han Chinese settlers arrived, they transliterated the sound into Hokkien as “Dalongbeng,” also written “Balangbeng”[^10][^11].

In 1709, the 48th year of the Kangxi reign of the Qing dynasty, five Quanzhou investors, Chen Fengchun, Lai Yonghe, Chen Tianzhang, Chen Xianbo, and Dai Tianshu, jointly applied to Zhuluo County to open cultivation in Dajiala[^12]. The “Chen-Lai-Zhang” reclamation enterprise received an official cultivation permit[^12]. Its permitted territory extended from today’s southern Wanhua to Dalongdong, and from Guandu to Xinzhuang, covering more than half of the Taipei Basin[^12]. Han Chinese settlers gradually entered Daronpon[^11].

In 1742, the seventh year of the Qianlong reign, Tong'an migrants built Baoan Temple in rudimentary form at Daronpon[^3].

In 1802, the seventh year of the Jiaqing reign, six households from Tong'an in Quanzhou, including **Wang Yuanji, Wang Zhiji, Chen Lanji, Chen Shengji, Gao Mingde, and Zheng Xiyuan**, opened 44 tiled shops here[^11]. Each shop was one zhang and eight chi wide and nineteen zhang deep, arranged into a street in the “one-dragon with drainage alley” layout[^13]. The street was commonly called the “Forty-four Kan,” with _kan_ meaning “shopfront” in Taiwanese. These six households decided to rename the place “Dalongtong,” a near-homophone of “xinglong Tong'an,” or “flourishing Tong'an”[^11][^14].

“Dalongtong” was used for more than 50 years. By the Daoguang and Tongzhi reigns of the Qing dynasty, from 1820 to 1870, more local residents were taking the imperial examinations. Because locals “regarded scholars as dragons,” and because the nearby Yuanshan hill rose abruptly from the plain “like a dragon,” forming a “dragon lair,” “Dalongtong” was changed to “Dalongdong”[^11][^15].

Four layers of place names: **Daronpon (Basay) → Dalongbeng (Hokkien transliteration) → Dalongtong (after Tong'an migrants settled) → Dalongdong (after the rise of local scholars)**.

The last name has remained in use to this day. In 1990, when Taipei City redrew its administrative districts, this area was placed in Datong District; across the Keelung River it belongs to Zhongshan District. The district name “Datong” comes directly from “xinglong Tong'an.”

## 1853: The First Stop for Defeated Tong'an Migrants Was Not Dadaocheng

In 1853, the third year of the Xianfeng reign of the Qing dynasty, the Ding-Xia feud broke out in Bangka. The “Ding,” or upper, guilds, composed mainly of people from the three Quanzhou counties of Jinjiang, Nan'an, and Hui'an, fought a major armed conflict against the “Xia,” or lower, guilds, also called the Xiamen guilds, composed mainly of Tong'an migrants from Quanzhou[^16]. The Sanyi people used Qingshui Zushi Temple as a passageway and set fire to Bajiazhuang, where Tong'an migrants lived. The Tong'an migrants were defeated and retreated.

The standard historical narrative says: “The defeated Tong'an migrants were led by Lin Youzao and fled to Dadaocheng, where they opened a new commercial port”[^16].

But one stop is missing.

The Wikipedia entry on “Dalongdong” records: “The defeated Xia-guild Tong'an migrants **retreated to Dalongdong, using Baoan Temple as their defensive center, and later migrated to Dadaocheng**”[^11]. In other words, after the 1853 defeat, the first stop for Tong'an migrants leaving Bangka was Dalongdong; Dadaocheng came later. They first hid in Baoan Temple, built in rudimentary form in 1742 by Tong'an migrants who had come to Taiwan earlier, used it as a defensive center, and then moved south from Dalongdong, across Datong District, to Zhong Street in Dadaocheng[^11][^16].

This route is entirely reasonable geographically: walking north from Bangka, crossing the upper Tamsui River, and skirting Taipei Prefecture City brings you to Dalongdong. Their fellow townspeople were there, and Baoan Temple’s Baosheng Dadi was the deity of Tong'an migrants. The temple had already existed for 111 years before the Ding-Xia feud. For the Tong'an migrants retreating in 1853, Baoan Temple played a role beyond that of an ordinary temple: it was a concrete refuge for people from the same native place.

The Tong'an migrants later carried the statue of Xia-Hai City God southward and settled on Zhong Street in Dadaocheng[^16]. On March 18, 1859, the ninth year of Xianfeng, Taipei Xia-Hai City God Temple was completed in Dadaocheng[^16]. From Bajiazhuang in Bangka to Dalongdong Baoan Temple, and then to Dihua Street in Dadaocheng, this road traces the six-year trajectory of Tong'an migrants after 1853.

> **📝 Curator’s note:** The same group left traces in three Taipei settlements: Bajiazhuang in Bangka, their base before 1853; Dalongdong Baoan Temple, the first stop after defeat; and Dadaocheng, their final foothold. The three places meant different things to Tong'an migrants: Bangka was the “lost home,” Dalongdong the “refuge of fellow townspeople,” and Dadaocheng the “commercial stronghold where they began again.” Walking through these three places today, one can see the different bodily postures left by the same group at different stages: Bangka Qingshan Temple says “we were here all along”; Baoan Temple says “we have always been here”; Xia-Hai City God Temple says “we fled from there to here.”

## A Xiucai Every Five Steps, a Juren Every Ten: Chen Wei-ying and His Teacher’s Mansion

Walk 500 meters east from Baoan Temple, pass Hami Street, and reach Section 4 of Yanping North Road. There you will see a gray-white Minnan-style old house: the Chen Yueji Ancestral Residence, commonly called the “Teacher’s Mansion”[^17].

This old house was first built in 1807, the twelfth year of Jiaqing[^17][^18], and renovated in the early Xianfeng period. It consists of two large compounds, one four-entry and one three-entry, placed side by side: the ancestral hall and the public residence hall[^17]. It is the largest surviving Minnan-style old residence in Taipei City[^18]. The family that built it came from Zhangzhou, Fujian, later moved to Tong'an, and in 1770, the 35th year of Qianlong, Chen Xunyan settled in Dalongdong[^17].

The Chen family had a son named **Chen Wei-ying** (1811-1869)[^19].

In 1859, the ninth year of Xianfeng, Chen Wei-ying passed the provincial examination and became a juren[^19]. He was Dalongdong’s most famous juren, but what truly made the locality proud was something else: he turned the juren’s home into an academy. Chen Wei-ying once served as education instructor in Min County and as head of Bangka’s Xuehai Academy and Yilan’s Yangshan Academy[^19]. Local people honored him as “Teacher” and called his home the “Teacher’s Mansion”[^17]. Later generations praised him as the “Literary Patriarch of Northern Taiwan”[^20].

The Chen family produced three juren in succession[^18]. Chen Wei-ying built three pairs of stone flagpoles in the front courtyard, with coiling dragons above and climbing lions below. This “coiling dragon above, climbing lion below” form symbolized “advancing still further after reaching the top of a hundred-foot pole”[^18]. As of 2026, only one of the three pairs of stone flagpoles remains intact; it is Taiwan’s only surviving “coiling dragon above, climbing lion below” stone pole[^18].

That Dalongdong became “the most scholarly place in all Taipei” was Chen Wei-ying’s achievement. In 1853, the third year of Xianfeng, he founded Shuren Academy inside Baoan Temple and taught there himself[^21]. Many students of the academy passed the examinations, and the local literary culture flourished, earning the praise **“a xiucai every five steps, a juren every ten”**[^11][^15]: walk five steps and you meet a county-level licentiate; walk ten steps and you meet a provincial graduate.

In 1862, the first year of Tongzhi, the Dai Chaochun incident broke out. Chen Wei-ying spent his own money to organize militia forces to help the authorities and was therefore awarded the right to wear a peacock-feather plume[^19]. After the incident was pacified, he built a retreat on the left bank of Jiantan, today’s Yuanshan, called “Taigu Nest.” This retreat stood near the hilltop where the Grand Hotel Taipei would later be located[^22][^23]. At Taigu Nest, Chen Wei-ying wrote poems and couplets, later collected by others as _Records of Stolen Leisure_ and _Collected Couplets from Taigu Nest_[^19][^20].

On October 9, 1869, the eighth year of Tongzhi, Chen Wei-ying died of illness[^19]. But the name “Teacher’s Mansion” did not end on the streets of Dalongdong.

Shuren Academy later moved out of Baoan Temple in 1928, because “local residents felt that an academy should not be attached to a temple”[^21]. It was independently rebuilt as Wenchang Shrine, completed in 1932[^24], and remains today in an alley on Hami Street.

In 1896, 27 years after Chen Wei-ying’s death, the Japanese established the Third Affiliated School of the Japanese Language School inside Baoan Temple, the predecessor of Dalong Elementary School[^1]. One temple housed two schools in succession: after Shuren Academy in 1853 came the Japanese-language school in 1896. In the same courtyard, the earlier students wore long gowns and worshiped Wenchang; the later students wore caps and studied the Japanese syllabary.

## The 1917 Duichangzuo: Two Carpenters Compete, Their Taunts Carved into Wood

In 1917, the sixth year of the Taishō era, Baoan Temple was renovated.

The temple invited two of the most famous carpenters of the time: **Chen Ying-bin**, a master of the Zhangzhou school, and **Guo Ta**, a woodcarver from Guoxing Street in Banqiao. They agreed to divide the central axis between them: on the temple facade, the dragon side and tiger side would be assigned separately, with Chen Ying-bin taking the dragon side and Guo Ta the tiger side. They also agreed on a shared theme: “The Eight Immortals Make Havoc in the Eastern Sea”[^25].

This practice, in which two master craftsmen worked simultaneously while tacitly competing with each other, is called **duichangzuo**, or “matched-site work,” in Taiwan’s traditional architecture[^25][^26]. After completion, the details left by the craftsmen became clues for later generations to identify “who made which side.”

Chen Ying-bin won through the delicacy of his carving, and the temple authorities judged him the winner. Guo Ta was unconvinced. On the woodcarving of the tiger side, he carved several taunting phrases:

> “**A fake lion defeats a real lion**” (carved on a dougong bracket)  
> “**Made by Guo Ta, carpenter of Guoxing Street**” (signed on the circular-frame carving on the tiger side)  
> “**True craftsmanship needs no alteration**” (signed)  
> “**Good workmanship needs no patching or splicing**” (on the circular-frame carving)[^25][^26]

The meaning is roughly: real skill requires no post-production, and a good craftsperson does not need to splice pieces together. These were remarks left after he was judged the loser. They can still be seen at Baoan Temple today and serve as physical evidence for identifying the outcome of the duichangzuo competition[^25].

The murals along the corridor outside the main hall were added later. In 1973, the temple hired the Tainan painting master Pan Li-shui (1914-1995) to paint seven large murals[^27]. Their themes include “Han Xin Endures Humiliation by Crawling Between the Legs,” “The Eight Hammers Battle Lu Wenlong at Zhuxian Town,” “Zhong Kui Welcomes His Sister Back to Her Natal Home,” “The Eight Immortals Make Havoc in the Eastern Sea,” “Hua Mulan Joins the Army in Her Father’s Place,” “Three Battles with Lü Bu at Hulao Pass,” and “The Worthy Mother Xu”[^27]. A 1917 woodcarving contest plus 1973 painted narratives: in one temple, Baoan Temple layers two eras of craft.

The octagonal coiling-dragon stone columns before the hall date to the Jiaqing reign, 1796-1820, and are the earliest surviving stone carvings in the temple[^3]. The column bodies coil upward from the base, with eight-sided relief carvings of cloud patterns, dragon claws, and dragon whiskers. Looking down from the column tops, one can read the design by which the stone carvers arranged motifs according to the eight trigrams.

The 36 guardian-general statues on the two sides of the temple were commissioned in 1829, the ninth year of Daoguang, when the temple invited the Quanzhou master Xu Yan to Taiwan. The carvings took five years to complete[^28]. In other words, in 1829, Tong'an migrants spent five years and invited a Quanzhou master across the sea so that divine generals could guard the temple.

## 1907: When the Japanese Demolished the Confucius Temple, Confucius Had Nowhere to Live

In 1879, the fifth year of Guangxu, Chen Xingju, the first prefect of Taipei Prefecture, used “remaining materials and leftover funds” to build a joint civil and martial temple inside the south gate of Taipei Prefecture City, near today’s Taipei First Girls High School and National Taipei University of Education[^29]. The Confucian temple stood on the left, the martial temple on the right. In 1884, the tenth year of Guangxu, the original Taipei Prefectural School Confucius Temple was completed[^29].

But this Confucius Temple lived only 23 years.

In 1907, the Japanese colonial authorities demolished the original Confucius Temple and established the Taiwan Governor-General’s Japanese Language School on the site, later Taipei Normal School and now National Taipei University of Education[^30][^31]. In 1925, across from that site, they also built Taihoku Prefectural First Girls’ High School, today’s Taipei First Girls High School[^30].

Confucius had lost his home.

In 1925, Taipei’s local gentry gathered in Yongle-cho, Dadaocheng, to discuss what should be done about Confucius. Should the temple be rebuilt? Where should it be built?[^30][^31]

The poet **Chen Peigen** of the Chen Yueji family, a descendant of Chen Wei-ying, stepped forward and said: “If it is rebuilt in Dalongdong, I am willing to donate private land”[^31]. Chen Peigen donated more than 2,000 ping[^30].

Beyond Chen Peigen, there was another important land donor: **Koo Hsien-jung**, of the Koo family of Lukang and father of Koo Chen-fu. Koo Hsien-jung purchased additional land and donated more than 1,000 ping[^30][^31]. Chen Peigen plus Koo Hsien-jung, more than 3,000 ping in all, became the site of today’s Taipei Confucius Temple.

Construction began in 1927[^31]. The work on Dacheng Hall, the core building of the Confucius Temple, was undertaken by **Wang Jinmu** of the Xidi school[^31]. The Xidi school was a carpentry lineage from Hui'an, Quanzhou, and one of the most important traditions of large-scale temple carpentry in Taiwan at the time. Dacheng Hall was completed in 1929[^31]. Other parts of the Confucius Temple, including Lingxing Gate, Li Gate, Yi Road, and the Wanren Palace Wall, were built slowly. Only in 1939, the fourteenth year of Shōwa, was the whole complex completed[^31].

In other words: 1907, the original Confucius Temple demolished; 1925, rebuilding discussed; 1927, construction begun; 1929, Dacheng Hall completed; 1939, the whole complex completed. After a gap of 32 years, Confucius finally had a home again in Dalongdong.

After the war, in 1971, the Taipei City Government established the **Taipei Confucius Temple Governing Board**[^31]. The “Improvements to the Ritual Music for Sacrifices to Confucius” plan, formulated in September 1970, stipulated: “The spring sacrifice shall be a private ritual; the autumn sacrifice, the ceremony marking Confucius’s birthday, shall be government-sponsored”[^31].

Today, every year on September 28, Teachers’ Day and Confucius’s birthday, the Taipei Confucius Temple holds the Shidian ceremony at the mao hour before dawn[^32]. The eight-row yi dance is performed by sixth-grade students from Dalong Elementary School[^33]. There are 8 rows and 8 columns, 64 students in all, including both boys and girls, wearing yellow robes with dark-green belts and black boots[^33]. The entire ritual follows ancient forms: no firecrackers, no theater, no burning of paper money[^32].

From Chen Xingju building Taipei’s first Confucius Temple in 1879, to its demolition by the Japanese in 1907, to Chen Peigen and Koo Hsien-jung donating land for reconstruction in 1925, to full completion in 1939, to municipal takeover in 1971, and to sixth-graders from Dalong Elementary dancing the eight-row yi dance in 2026: one temple took 147 years to traverse the axis from empire to Japanese rule to the postwar period to the contemporary city.

## The Afternoon When a Plane Crashed into the Shrine

Walk 700 meters north from Taipei Confucius Temple, cross the Keelung River, and you reach Yuanshan.

In 1900, the 33rd year of Meiji, the Japanese began building Taiwan Shrine on this small hill[^34]. It was completed in October 1901, the 34th year of Meiji[^34]. The main deity enshrined there was Prince Kitashirakawa Yoshihisa, who died in Taiwan[^34]. In 1895, when the Japanese army took control of Taiwan, Prince Yoshihisa commanded the Guards Division and died of illness in Tainan[^34]. To commemorate him, the Japanese built Taiwan Shrine at the rank of kanpei-taisha, an imperial shrine of the highest class[^34]. It was the only shrine of that rank in Taiwan[^34].

Sections 1, 2, and 3 of Zhongshan North Road in front of the shrine formed the “Chokushi Road,” an official road built by the Japanese colonial authorities beginning in 1898 for visits to the shrine[^35]. It was completed as a tree-lined boulevard in 1929[^35].

In 1943-1944, Taiwan Shrine was expanded, Amaterasu Ōmikami was added for worship, and the shrine was elevated to the status of Jingū[^34].

On the afternoon of October 25, 1944, the nineteenth year of Shōwa, **a Japanese passenger plane crashed into Taiwan Jingū while landing at Songshan Airport**[^34]. It was the final stage of World War II, and the Japanese government no longer had the capacity to repair it. The shrine burned down in this way[^34][^36].

After the war, the Nationalist government took over Taiwan in 1945. Lacking luxurious state guest facilities, it removed the shrine remains and converted the site into the Taiwan Grand Hotel[^37]. On May 10, 1952, the **Taiwan Goodwill Association Foundation**, led by Soong Mei-ling, took over operations and renamed it the Grand Hotel Taipei[^37]. Its first general manager was Kung Ling-wei, also known as Miss Kung the Second, Soong Mei-ling’s niece[^37].

The first foreign head of state received there was King Bhumibol of Thailand, together with Queen Sirikit[^38].

On October 10, 1973, Double Ten National Day, the 14-story Chinese palace-style building designed by architect **Yang Cho-cheng** was completed[^37]. It had 14 floors, golden glazed tiles, red corridor columns, and covered the entire western hilltop of Jiantan Mountain. Seen from Zhongshan North Road, the whole building resembles a miniature version of the Hall of Supreme Harmony in the Forbidden City[^38].

Yang Cho-cheng’s major works, in addition to the Grand Hotel Taipei, include **Chiang Kai-shek Memorial Hall, the National Concert Hall, the National Theater, and the Cihu Mausoleum**[^39]. These four, together with the Grand Hotel Taipei, form the five-piece set of Chinese palace-style architecture that Yang left in Taipei, all completed in the 1970s and 1980s under the patronage of Chiang Kai-shek and Soong Mei-ling[^39].

> **⚠️ Contested view:** The Grand Hotel Taipei stands on the original site of Taiwan Jingū, destroyed by an airplane crash in 1944. On the same hilltop, the Japanese first built a kanpei-taisha to commemorate a prince who died during the conquest of Taiwan; after the war it became the red-tiled, yellow-eaved “Chinese palace-style” venue where the Nationalist government received foreign guests. Two architectural languages, two eras of ritual power, layered on the same land. On the morning of June 27, 1995, a fire broke out on the roof of the Grand Hotel’s 12th floor, caused by careless welding during glazed-tile work[^40]. It burned for four hours. Fortunately, there were no casualties, and the building’s structure was not damaged[^40]. A shrine that had only just been destroyed by an airplane in 1944; a new building completed in 1973; another fire in 1995. The same land, two fires, in 1944 and 1995, each tied to the rituals of political power.

## 1995: Liao Wu-chih Refuses Government Subsidies to Restore the Temple, Producing Taiwan’s First UNESCO Asia-Pacific Heritage Award Site

From the Grand Hotel Taipei, turn back south, descend the hill, cross the Keelung River and Jiuquan Street, and you return to Baoan Temple.

In 1994, **Liao Wu-chih** became vice chair of Baoan Temple[^41]. He had worshiped at Baoan Temple for decades, but this time he was going to do what an “alternative temple steward” would do[^4]: in 1995, he presided over the comprehensive restoration of Baoan Temple[^41].

Liao Wu-chih decided to **give up government historic-site subsidies**.

“To avoid being constrained by the government’s outdated systems and to achieve the best results, Baoan Temple decided to forgo government subsidies, raise funds on its own, and conduct and supervise the restoration itself”[^41]. This became Taiwan’s first case of privately funded, privately led restoration of a historic site[^41].

The “outdated government systems” referred to the bidding rules for historic-site projects at the time: they had to follow public-works standards, choose the lowest bidder, and use ordinary construction schedules[^4]. But the restoration of a traditional temple is not ordinary construction. Cut-and-paste ceramic work requires masters who understand that craft; painted decoration requires painters who understand temple painting; large timber framing requires descendants of the Xidi or Zhangzhou schools[^4]. If public-works standards were followed, the contractor might not understand temple craft, and the repaired temple might become a “wrongly repaired” temple[^4].

Liao Wu-chih’s choice was this: he would rather spend seven years restoring the temple slowly, using NT$260 million of temple funds[^41], than fail to restore every piece of cut-and-paste work, every dougong bracket, and every painting correctly.

Seven years. NT$260 million. In early 2002, the Sanchuan Hall, eastern guard room, western guard room, rear hall, and main hall were completed in succession[^3]. On June 30, 2003, the temple held an “settling the dragons and thanking the earth” ceremony[^3], a traditional ritual celebrating the completion of restoration.

That same year, 2003, UNESCO awarded Baoan Temple an Honourable Mention in the Asia-Pacific Awards for Cultural Heritage Conservation[^4][^41]. It was Taiwan’s first UNESCO Asia-Pacific Heritage Award[^4]. The jury statement described it as “A model for community-based restoration”[^4].

Liao Wu-chih later became chair of Baoan Temple[^41]. Today, every year on the fifteenth day of the third lunar month, the birthday of Baosheng Dadi, the temple holds the Baosheng Cultural Festival[^42]. In 2021, the “Baoan Temple Baosheng Dadi Birthday Celebration” was registered as a cultural asset in the folk-custom category[^3]. Baosheng Dadi’s procession is “one of Taipei’s three major temple festivals”[^3]. During the festival, rituals include processions, fire-walking, and the burning of fire lions in the temple plaza[^42].

> **📝 Curator’s note:** Liao Wu-chih’s choice had two meanings. The first was an **insistence on craft quality**: he would rather refuse government subsidies than fail to find the right masters for the right parts of the restoration. The second was the **possibility of civic autonomy**: Baoan Temple’s NT$260 million restoration budget came entirely from devotees’ donations, proving that Taiwan’s civil society has the ability to independently support historic preservation. UNESCO’s 2003 jury phrase, “a model for community-based restoration,” marked the first time an international organization recognized that the ecosystem formed by Taiwan’s temple culture, devotee networks, and craft transmission could sustain the continuity of a two-hundred-year-old temple. This model was later referenced by other historic sites in Taiwan, but few temples could replicate it. What made Liao Wu-chih distinctive was that his determination found in Dalongdong a community with the depth to sustain it.

## The 1972 Wanda Plan: The Forty-four Kan Cut in Half

On June 10, 1972, the 61st year of the Republic of China calendar, Taipei City was upgraded to a special municipality[^43]. The city government began implementing the **Wanda Plan**, with a budget of NT$300 million over two years, aiming to improve the old districts from Bangka to Dalongdong[^43].

In 1973-1974, the city government widened Hami Street and Jiuquan Street, extended Chengde Road, and widened and straightened Lanzhou Street into today’s Dalong Street[^43].

The Forty-four Kan stood on Hami Street[^43]. Old aerial photographs from 1973 show that when the Wanda Plan widened Hami Street, the first entry of the shop-houses on the southern side of the Forty-four Kan was partially demolished[^43]. In other words, the 44 tiled shops opened in 1802 by Wang Yuanji and the other five households, Dalongdong’s earliest street, were cut in half by the city government.

Which half was cut? The first entry of the southern shop-houses. The tiled shops’ one-dragon layout consisted of “shopfront → drainage alley → rear building.” The southern shopfronts were demolished, leaving only the rear half and part of the drainage alley[^43].

A 170-year-old street was left less than half intact.

Walking on Hami Street today, what can be seen as the “old-street appearance of the Forty-four Kan” is in fact a mixture: some walls are from the old tiled shops of 1802; some storefronts were rebuilt after the 1973 widening; some parts were renovated in the 1980s and 1990s. The 2025 project “Spatial Reconstruction of the Dalongdong Forty-four Kan Street,” conducted by the Research Center for Humanities and Social Sciences Center for Geographic Information Science at Academia Sinica, seeks to reconstruct the historical layers of this street using GIS[^44].

Walk from Baoan Temple toward Hami Street and you pass Dalong Night Market[^45]. The night market centers on the intersection of Dalong Street and Hami Street, with food stalls as its main feature: Guo Ji Chunky Pork Thick Soup, A-Ren Fried Rice, Black Tea House, which opened in 1981 and still sells a small black tea for NT$15 and a 1,000 c.c. largest size for only NT$25, Dingwang Soup Dumplings, and truffle fried rice at Yu no Zhan[^45]. These shops have averaged 30 to 40 years in business. They are the stomach of an old community.

After the night market, walk toward the Confucius Temple and you pass the alley of Shuren Academy Wenchang Shrine, moved out of Baoan Temple in 1928 and completed in 1932[^21][^24]. After Dalong Elementary School, founded in 1896, with its campus completed in 1933[^1], comes Taipei Confucius Temple.

This 1.5-kilometer axis, Baoan Temple → Shuren Academy Wenchang Shrine → Dalong Elementary School → Taipei Confucius Temple → Yuanshan Archaeological Site → Yuanshan Park → Grand Hotel Taipei, is Taipei’s most compressed slice of time:

- 5,300-year-old Neolithic Yuanshan shell midden
- Seventeenth-century Ketagalan Daronpon community
- 1742 Tong'an migrants build Baoan Temple in rudimentary form
- 1802 Forty-four Kan tiled shops
- 1807 Teacher’s Mansion
- 1830 Baoan Temple formally completed
- 1859 Chen Wei-ying passes the provincial examination
- 1879 First Taipei Confucius Temple, inside the city
- 1897 Yuanshan Park and the Yuanshan Archaeological Site discovered
- 1907 Japanese demolish the original Confucius Temple
- 1925-1939 New Dalongdong Confucius Temple
- 1944 Jingū destroyed by airplane crash
- 1952 Grand Hotel Taipei renamed
- 1972 Wanda Plan cuts into the Forty-four Kan
- 1973 Yang Cho-cheng’s 14-story palace-style building completed
- 1988 Yuanshan Archaeological Site listed as a first-grade historic site
- 1995-2003 Liao Wu-chih self-funds temple restoration and receives UNESCO award

Walking the full axis takes 30 minutes.

## The Seven A.M. Tai Chi Is Still Going

Return to the plaza from the beginning.

At seven in the morning, the tai chi group in front of Baoan Temple is still practicing. A dozen uncles and aunties move as slowly as if time has been slowed down. Where they stand, beneath the ground, are the temple foundations built by Tong'an migrants in rudimentary form in 1742. Five meters farther down is the reclamation territory of the Chen-Lai-Zhang enterprise from 1709. Twenty meters farther down is the shell-midden layer left by Yuanshan people 5,300 years ago.

They do not think about this. They simply come here every morning at seven to practice tai chi. Afterward they go beside the temple to eat a bowl of pork thick soup or drink a cup of black tea from Black Tea House, the shop opened in 1981 that still sells a small cup for NT$15[^45], just like yesterday, just like the day before, just like before Liao Wu-chih decided in 1995 to restore the temple at his own expense, just like before Shuren Academy moved out of Baoan Temple in 1928, just like before Chen Wei-ying passed the provincial examination in 1859.

To the north, 1.5 kilometers away, the red tiles of the Grand Hotel Taipei reflect the morning light. Farther north across the Keelung River, the ridgeline of Jiantan Mountain slopes south. In between lies Taipei Expo Park, with the unexcavated parts of the Yuanshan shell midden still buried beneath it.

Seven hundred meters to the south, Dacheng Hall’s yellow tiles at Taipei Confucius Temple rest quietly in the shade. In a few months, before dawn on September 28 at the mao hour, 64 sixth-graders from Dalong Elementary School will stand in this courtyard and perform the eight-row yi dance, their movements as slow as those of Yuanshan people gathering shellfish 5,300 years ago.

At the incense burner on the east side of the temple plaza, three sticks of incense are burning. The smoke does not rise straight; the morning wind blows it crooked, drifting north. To the north is Jiantan Mountain: the hill where the Jingū burned down in 1944, where Yang Cho-cheng built a palace-style hotel in 1973, where Chen Wei-ying built Taigu Nest and wrote poetry in 1862, and where Yuanshan people lived 5,300 years ago.

The smoke drifts over and disperses on the hilltop.

Three eras of Taipei faith stand side by side along a 1.5-kilometer axis. They are neither sequential nor overwritten; they simply stand together. Beneath one’s feet lies Taipei’s most compressed slice of time.
