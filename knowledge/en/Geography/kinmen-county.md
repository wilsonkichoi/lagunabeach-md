# Kinmen County: The 56 Hours in 1949 That Determined Kinmen's Fate for 75 Years

> **30-second overview:** Kinmen is about 1.8 kilometers from Jiaoyu, Xiamen, at its nearest point, and 358 kilometers from Taipei. The Cold War placed a county somewhere its geography would not have put it. In 1387, Zhou Dexing built the Kinmen garrison city, taking the place name from the eight characters “as impregnable as walls of metal and a moat of boiling water, a mighty guard at the sea gate.” At 2 a.m. on October 25, 1949, three Communist regiments, 9,086 men, landed on the beaches of Guningtou; Nationalist forces repelled them after 56 hours of fierce fighting. Had those 56 hours not held, there would be no such term today as “the two sides of the strait.” In the 44 days beginning at 5:30 p.m. on August 23, 1958, 474,910 artillery shells fell on an island of 151 square kilometers, and three deputy commanders were killed at the Taiwu Mountain command post on the same afternoon. The subsequent policy of shelling on odd-numbered days and not shelling on even-numbered days continued for another 21 years, ending only on January 1, 1979, when the United States and China established diplomatic relations. From 1956 to 1992, Kinmen spent 36 years under warzone administration: curfew was at 10 p.m. (9 p.m. in Matsu), entry and exit required permits, radios had to be registered, and flotation devices were banned at the shore. Today, 145,000 people are registered in Kinmen, and at 4 a.m. more speedboats leave Shuitou Pier for Wutong, Xiamen, than planes fly to Taipei Songshan. Yeh Hua-cheng’s great-grandson has waited 67 years for an apology.

## Looking at Xiamen from Kinmen Means Looking Across a 1.8-Kilometer Strait

If you stand at the northernmost point of Dadan Island in Kinmen and look north, the high-rises of downtown Xiamen appear to float directly on the horizon.

The closest point between Kinmen and mainland China is Jiaoyu in Tong'an District, Xiamen, at a straight-line distance of about **1.8 kilometers**[^1]. From that distance, look back the other way: Kinmen is **358 kilometers** from Taipei, nearly 200 times farther than its distance from Xiamen. If you take a Mini Three Links speedboat from Kinmen's Shuitou Pier, you reach Xiamen's Wutong Pier in **45 minutes**. If you fly to Songshan, the flight plus airport transfers, check-in, and baggage consumes half a day[^2].

Kinmen County's geographical fact is this: **it is this close to the mainland, yet it is not a mainland county; it is this far from Taipei, yet it is a county governed by Taipei**.

The county's full administrative name is “**Kinmen County, Fujian Province, Republic of China**.” It does not belong to Taiwan Province. Like Lienchiang County (Matsu), it belongs to Fujian Province. That province was made nominal in 1996, had its functions streamlined in 1998, and was formally de-tasked in 2019, but on ID cards and in household registration systems, Kinmen residents remain people of “Kinmen County, Fujian Province” to this day[^3].

Kinmen's main island covers 134 square kilometers. Together with Lieyu (Little Kinmen), Dadan, Erdan, Wuqiu, and other islands, the county covers **151.6 square kilometers**. It is divided into six townships and towns: Jincheng, the county seat; Jinhu, Jinsha, and Jinning on the main island; Lieyu Township on Little Kinmen; and Wuqiu Township, an administrative anomaly we will return to later[^4]. Geologically, granite predominates. Taiwu Mountain is the main island's spine. It is only 253 meters above sea level, but this low mountain endured the artillery storm of those 44 days in 1958.

To understand Kinmen, one must first set aside the intuition that “geography determines belonging.” **Kinmen is a county of Taiwan, but Kinmen has never been Taiwan's geography**.

It was once a symbol of “Juguang Day” military political education and is now one of Kinmen County's most representative landmarks. ## At 2 a.m., the 56 Hours on the Beaches of Guningtou

At 2 a.m. on October 25, 1949, Communist troops began coming ashore.

That summer, the overall outcome of the Chinese Civil War had already been settled. Nationalist forces were losing position after position on the mainland, and their main forces were withdrawing to Taiwan. In May that year, Kinmen had been taken over by Nationalist troops under Tang Enbo's command. In October, the Communist victory in the Battle of Xiamen had just demonstrated their capacity for a sea crossing, and morale was high. The 28th Army was ordered to cross the water and attack Kinmen.

That night, three Communist regiments, the 244th Regiment of the 82nd Division, 28th Army; the 251st Regiment of the 84th Division; and the 253rd Regiment of the 85th Division, departed from Xiamen and landed on the north coast of Guningtou Village and around Lincuo. The first landing wave totaled about **9,086 men**[^5]. ⚠️ This figure comes from Nationalist military histories and mainstream sources on both sides of the strait. Because sea conditions and the Nationalist blockade prevented subsequent waves from landing, some sources estimate that if troops still on the water are included, the total committed force may have been higher.

The battle's key moment came at daybreak. After landing, Communist troops advanced toward Jincheng Township in three routes, and the Nationalist position became extremely precarious amid nighttime close combat. **M5A1 tanks** changed the situation. At the time, Nationalist forces had only three U.S.-supplied M5A1 light tanks in Kinmen, numbered 64, 65, and 66. Tank 66 had broken down on the Guningtou beachhead before the battle; paradoxically, it became the beachhead's earliest firepower anchor[^6]. The Communist troops had crossed as infantry and had no anti-tank weapons. On the sandy beachhead, those three tanks became the protagonists of the popular memory of “tanks saving Kinmen.”

By October 26, Nationalist forces had completed an encirclement, compressing the Communist troops against Guningtou's north coast. In the early hours of October 27, surviving Communist troops attempted to withdraw by sea and were annihilated by Nationalist forces on the shallows. From 2 a.m. to the morning of the 27th, the battle lasted a full **56 hours**[^7]. Nationalist military histories record more than 15,000 enemy killed or captured in total[^8]. ⚠️ If only the first landing wave of 9,086 men is counted, a killed-and-captured total exceeding the landing force creates a logical contradiction. Some explanations treat the figure as including troops destroyed on the water, with casualties and prisoners counted across categories. This article presents the Nationalist military-history framework while noting this unresolved arithmetic.

> **📝 Curator's Note:** At the 2024 commemoration of the 75th anniversary of the Battle of Guningtou, Kinmen County Magistrate Chen Fu-hai's official phrase, “remember history, cherish peace, defend sovereignty,” could easily sound like routine bureaucratic language[^9]. But the battle's true historical position does not lie in the rhetoric. By the summer and autumn of 1949, Communist forces had already “liberated” most of mainland China. If Kinmen had fallen, the stepping-stone from Xiamen into the Taiwan Strait would have opened, and the defense of Taiwan proper would have become far more difficult. Only after the Korean War broke out in 1950, when the U.S. Seventh Fleet was assigned to defend the Taiwan Strait, was the Taiwan Strait defense line locked into place by international circumstances. But Guningtou in 1949 had already pressed the pattern of divided rule onto geography. **Those 56 hours were not merely the duration of a victory in one battle. They were the starting point of 75 years of divided rule across the strait**. If the Communist landing had succeeded, there would be no still-functioning government called the “Republic of China” today, no specialized term called “cross-strait relations,” and no .md file like the one you are reading now.

## At 5:30 p.m., Three Deputy Commanders Died on Taiwu Mountain

At 5:30 p.m. on August 23, 1958, artillery shells fell without warning.

It was one of the most tense summers of the Cold War. U.S.-China relations were locked in a trial of strength. Mao Zedong decided to “shell Kinmen” in order to test the American response and announce to the world Beijing's sovereignty claim over the Taiwan Strait. Communist forces had no intention of landing on Kinmen. Instead, they intended to blast the issue onto the international negotiating table with artillery shells.

In the first two hours of shelling, **tens of thousands of shells** fell on Kinmen. The Nationalist Kinmen Defense Command was located at the Taiwu Mountain command post. Three deputy commanders, **Zhao Jiaxiang**, **Zhang Jie**, and **Ji Xingwen**, were killed one after another near the Taiwu Mountain command post on the same afternoon[^10]. Ji Xingwen was the division commander who fired the first shot in 1937 at the Marco Polo Bridge Incident, opening full-scale war between China and Japan. Twenty-one years later, he was killed in Kinmen by a Chinese Communist shell. Chiang Ching-kuo was also inspecting Kinmen that day and survived by fortune.

Over the next 44 days, the total number of shells fired by Communist forces, according to Ministry of National Defense military histories, was **474,910**[^11]. Kinmen's total area is about 151.6 square kilometers, meaning that on average nearly **3,132 shells** fell on each square kilometer.

> **✦** “During the August 23 Artillery Battle, Communist forces fired a total of 474,910 shells at Kinmen.”[^11]

On September 7, a U.S. Navy-escorted supply fleet arrived in the waters off Kinmen, helping to break the Communist naval blockade. On September 11, U.S. Secretary of State John Foster Dulles publicly stated that “aggression against the offshore islands would be regarded as aggression against Taiwan itself.” On October 5, Communist forces announced a unilateral ceasefire. But the war did not truly end. On October 25, the ninth anniversary of the Battle of Guningtou, Mao Zedong issued the “Second Message to Compatriots in Taiwan,” announcing that there would be “**shelling on odd-numbered days and ceasefire on even-numbered days**.” This became known as “odd-day shelling, even-day no shelling”[^12].

This rule lasted for **21 years**. From October 25, 1958, until the **formal establishment of U.S.-China diplomatic relations on January 1, 1979**, shelling did not formally end until the United States required its cessation as a precondition for diplomatic recognition. For those 21 years, Kinmen residents' daily life meant this: **on 15 odd-numbered days each month, they had to take shelter from shelling; only on even-numbered days could normal life resume**. In the later period, most shells were propaganda shells filled with leaflets rather than lethal munitions. But for residents, **you did not know whether the next shell carried leaflets or explosives, nor whether some day would return to 5:30 p.m. on August 23**.

## For Those 36 Years, Kinmen's Curfew Was 10 p.m.

Warzone administration began in July 1956 and ended on **November 7, 1992**, lasting a full **36 years**[^13].

The specialized term “warzone administration” deserves to be unpacked. It was a civil-military integrated governance system implemented by the Republic of China government in Kinmen and Matsu: the county magistrate was concurrently held by a military officer, local administration was placed under the defense command, and parts of civilian judicial procedure were replaced by military law. The Temporary Provisions Effective During the Period of Communist Rebellion were abolished in 1991, but warzone administration in Kinmen and Matsu had a separate legal basis and had to be abolished separately. Martial law on Taiwan proper ended in 1987. Kinmen and Matsu did not exit the martial-law system until **November 7, 1992**, **more than five years later than Taiwan proper**[^13].

During those 36 years, what kinds of rules constrained residents' daily lives?

Curfew was at **10 p.m.**[^14]. In Matsu it was 9 p.m.; that one-hour difference reflected how the military sensitivity of the two places had been calculated into different numbers. Entry and exit were controlled: Kinmen residents needed an “entry-exit permit” to go to Taiwan proper, while residents of Taiwan proper needed a family guarantee or military approval to enter Kinmen. No flotation devices were allowed at the shore. Swim rings, rubber boats, basketballs, and tires were all on the prohibited list, lest residents use them to swim to the other side. Radios had to be registered, and certain frequencies were banned to prevent listening to Communist propaganda broadcasts. During some periods, buildings could not exceed two stories. For a time, military scrip rather than New Taiwan dollars circulated in Kinmen.

Local democratic mechanisms were frozen. County magistrates and township heads were appointed by the military, not elected. Kinmen did not have its first elected county magistrate until 1993, 43 years after Taiwan proper held its first county and city magistrate elections in 1950.

> **✦** “In Kinmen, five years after martial law was lifted, we were still under military control. Do you understand what that felt like? Martial law was lifted on Taiwan proper in July, and we had to wait until November 1992.”[^14]

At its peak during military rule, the size of the Nationalist garrison in Kinmen is estimated to have reached **50,000 to 100,000 troops**, while the resident population at the time was about 50,000 to 70,000. **There were more soldiers than civilians**[^15]. On an island of 151 square kilometers lived a force approaching one-third the size of today's entire Taiwan Army. Underground tunnel networks spread across the island: Taiwu Mountain, Zhaishan, Jiugong, Mashan, Qionglin. Beneath each place name were military works hewn from concrete and granite. The Zhaishan Tunnel, through which tourists paddle kayaks today, was excavated between 1961 and 1965. Its main function was to serve as a resupply base for submarines and small landing craft, and it could accommodate 42 small boats[^16]. The Jiugong Tunnel on Lieyu (Little Kinmen) is even larger, 780 meters long, with 30 to 50 meters of granite overhead, enough to withstand a direct hit from an ordinary bomb.

**The war did not end. It was hidden inside the granite**.

## Yeh Hua-cheng's Great-Grandson Waited 67 Years for an Apology

Kinmen Kaoliang Liquor is the symbol of Kinmen most familiar to people in Taiwan. But the origin story of this bottle of liquor has two versions: the official version and the Yeh family version.

The official version is that **Kinmen Distillery was founded by defense commander Hu Lien** in the early 1950s, using locally grown sorghum in Kinmen as raw material to produce liquor, thereby increasing military funding and stabilizing public morale[^17]. This version entered the county gazetteer, the official history of Kinmen Distillery, and every tourism guide.

The Yeh family version is another matter. **Yeh Hua-cheng** was a local Kinmen distiller. Around the time Nationalist forces withdrew to Taiwan in 1949, he founded “Jincheng Distillery” in Kinmen City, later renamed “Jiulongjiang Distillery,” and developed kaoliang liquor brewing techniques. In 1952, Hu Lien used military authority to **forcibly expropriate** the distillery and renamed it “Kinmen Distillery,” later Kinmen Kaoliang Liquor Inc. Yeh Hua-cheng remained as a technical adviser, but he lost ownership and recognition as founder[^18].

> **✦** “The formula and brewing techniques for kaoliang liquor were created by my great-grandfather Yeh Hua-cheng. In 1952, they were forcibly expropriated, and to this day Kinmen Distillery has never formally acknowledged this history. We are not asking for compensation. We are asking only for history to be treated justly.” — Yeh Hua-cheng's great-grandson **Yeh Wei-jen**[^18]

Around 2019, Yeh Hua-cheng's great-grandson Yeh Wei-jen began publicly speaking out about this history. What he wanted was formal recognition: the three characters “Yeh Hua-cheng” written into the distillery's official history, not monetary compensation. From 1952 to the year he began speaking publicly, exactly **67 years** had passed.

Today, Kinmen Distillery is a public enterprise owned by the Kinmen County Government. Its annual revenue exceeds NT$10 billion, and it is one of the county government's main sources of fiscal income. Every bottle of Kinmen Kaoliang Liquor sold creates more budgetary space for Kinmen County's health insurance subsidies, elderly allowances, and student subsidies. **The Yeh family's forcibly expropriated private distillery became the cornerstone of Kinmen County's public finances**.

> **📝 Curator's Note:** The common Kinmen narrative describes kaoliang liquor as a model of “successful transformation of a warzone economy”: Hu Lien is the hero, and the distillery is an achievement of a Taiwan brand. This narrative is not factually wrong, but **it reverses cause and effect**. Yeh Hua-cheng's forced expropriation was not “a minor episode in a success story.” It was the **structural starting point** of that success story. In Kinmen in 1952, residents had no channels of appeal at all. During military rule, civilian property was taken over by the military. Even if the Yeh family wanted to protest, whom could they turn to? By the time warzone administration was abolished in 1992, Kinmen Distillery had already become an established Kinmen brand. After democratization in the 2000s, Yeh's descendants began speaking publicly, but by then, asking the distillery to rewrite its official history meant touching the local government's tax base. **How violence under military rule is obscured by economic success** takes its most concrete material form in Kinmen in the bottle of Kinmen Kaoliang you may have ordered at a re-chao, a Taiwanese stir-fry restaurant.

## Four Characters from 1387: Impregnable as Metal and Boiling Water, Mighty Guard at the Sea Gate

If Guningtou is only the beginning of Kinmen's modern history, Kinmen's older history must return to the 20th year of the Hongwu reign in the Ming dynasty.

In 1387, the 20th year of the Ming Hongwu reign, the Marquis of Jiangxia, **Zhou Dexing**, was ordered to build coastal-defense garrison cities along the Fujian coast. He built a garrison city on this island, taking its name from the phrase “**as impregnable as walls of metal and a moat of boiling water, a mighty guard at the sea gate**,” shortened to “**Kinmen**,” literally “Golden Gate”[^19]. Before that, the island was called “Wuzhou” or “Xianzhou” in Tang and Five Dynasties sources. Only after the Ming garrison city was built did the name “Kinmen” become fixed.

In the Tang dynasty, this place was pastureland for horses; legend holds that Chen Yuan grazed horses here. In 959, the sixth year of the Xiande reign of Later Zhou during the Five Dynasties, Jinjiang County was established under Quanzhou, and Kinmen fell under its jurisdiction. During the Song and Yuan periods, Quanzhou's overseas trade flourished, and Kinmen was a transshipment hub. The Yuan dynasty established salt fields here. After the Ming garrison city was built, migrants from Quanzhou in Fujian, especially Tong'an County, settled here in large numbers. This is the historical origin of Kinmen residents speaking Southern Min and having an accent close to Xiamen's.

In the late Ming and early Qing, Kinmen encountered a man named **Koxinga, Zheng Chenggong**. In 1646, the third year of the Qing Shunzhi reign, the 23-year-old Zheng Chenggong raised forces in Kinmen and Xiamen against the Qing under the banner of “opposing the Qing and restoring the Ming”[^20]. Near Mingde Lake on Nanci Mountain in Kinmen, tombs of Ming loyalists remain. The archway in front of the Koxinga Temple in Jincheng Township is among the most common representative images of Kinmen in CC BY-licensed image repositories.

After the Zheng regime surrendered to the Qing, Kinmen entered two relatively peaceful centuries. Large numbers of emigrants went to Nanyang, or Southeast Asia, including the Philippines, Malaysia, and Indonesia, to make a living, then brought wealth home to build Western-style houses, forming the distinctive “fanzai lou,” or overseas Chinese mansions, of the late Qing and early Republican period. **Shanhou Village** is the best-preserved representative example. Located in Jinsha Township and built in the late Qing and early Republican period, around the 1900s to 1920s, it consists of **16 to 18** traditional Southern Min houses, with counts varying slightly by source. They were built by the Shanhou Wang family after returning from residence in Japan. The architectural vocabulary combines Southern Min tradition, Japanese details, and Western decorative elements[^21]. It was listed among the first group of historic sites in 1979 and opened to the public after restoration in 1998.

![Shanhou Village, also known as Shanhou Folk Culture Village, in Jinsha Township. Built by the Shanhou Wang family after returning from residence in Japan, it is one of the best-preserved clusters of traditional Southern Min houses. Photo by Shoestring, 2009.](/article-images/geography/kinmen-03.webp)

**Jincheng Model Street** is evidence from another timeline. Completed in 1924, this old street of connected two-story arcade shophouses has facades that combine Southern Min, Southeast Asian, and Western styles. Its name came from the Kinmen county magistrate at the time, who praised the street as a “model of construction.” The greatest difference between Model Street and old streets of the same period on Taiwan proper under Japanese rule is this: **it was built by returning overseas Chinese, not by Japanese people**. Kinmen was never directly ruled by Japan, except for the brief Japanese occupation of Kinmen and Xiamen during the Second Sino-Japanese War from 1937 to 1945. The time embodied on this street therefore belongs to the language system of “overseas Chinese time,” a separate axis from “Japanese colonial time.”

![Jincheng Model Street, an old street of connected two-story arcade shophouses completed in 1924. Its facades combine Southern Min, Southeast Asian, and Western styles. It was built by returning overseas Chinese, not by Japanese people. Photo by Shoestring, 2009.](/article-images/geography/kinmen-04.webp)

As for the distinctive stone lion sculptures at village entrances, the **Wind Lion Gods**, there are more than **70** across Kinmen, with different counts ranging from 70 to 90[^22]. Kinmen Island is flat, and the winter northeast monsoon is strong. Traditional Southern Min folk belief holds that “lions bite the wind” and can suppress harmful wind forces, so stone lions were placed at village entrances as guardians. Okinawa's “shisa” serve a similar function, and scholars have suggested they may be connected to the migration of Southern Min fishers to the Ryukyus during the Tang and Song periods. But this interpretation remains debated and should not be stated as settled fact.

![A Kinmen Wind Lion God. Villages place stone lions at their entrances to suppress harmful wind forces. They are recorded from the Ming and Qing periods and became especially prevalent in the Qing. There are more than 70 across the island, made of sandstone or granite and appearing in varied forms. Photo by P95521708, 2018.](/article-images/geography/kinmen-05.webp)

## Wuqiu Speaks a Third Language

If five of Kinmen's six townships and towns can be reached by residents of Kinmen Island and Lieyu by car or scooter, the sixth is an administrative anomaly.

**Wuqiu Township** belongs to Kinmen County, but geographically it is **more than 170 kilometers from Kinmen's main island**, in the waters north of Penghu and south of Matsu[^23]. To conduct county business, Wuqiu residents must first take a boat to Taichung Harbor or Keelung Harbor on Taiwan proper, then transfer onward to Kinmen. That is a more roundabout route than going directly to Matsu. Stranger still is the language Wuqiu residents speak. It is different from Kinmen's Southern Min and from Matsu's Fuzhou speech, an Eastern Min language. They speak **Puxian**, a Min subdialect that developed in Putian and Xianyou, today part of Putian City in Fujian, and is not mutually intelligible with either Southern Min or Eastern Min[^23].

This linguistic fact is the farthest extension of the boundary called “Kinmen County”: one county with three languages, Kinmen main island Southern Min, Lieyu Southern Min, cognate but with a slightly different accent, and Wuqiu Puxian.

This is also the starting point for distinguishing Kinmen from Matsu, or Lienchiang County. The two offshore-island counties both implemented warzone administration in 1956, both abolished it in 1992, and both opened Mini Three Links routes in 2001. But their fates share only an institutional frame; their flesh and bone grew separately.

Kinmen residents speak **Southern Min with a Tong'an accent**, close to the Southern Min of southern Taiwan proper and to the Xiamen accent; communication across the water is not a problem[^24]. Matsu residents speak **Fuzhou speech, an Eastern Min language**, “largely similar with minor differences” to Fuzhou speech in the city of Fuzhou, but completely unintelligible with the Southern Min of Taiwan proper. The linguistic difference reflects migration origins: Kinmen migrants mainly came from Tong'an in Quanzhou, Fujian, while Matsu migrants mainly came from Changle and Luoyuan in Fuzhou.

The structure of distance is also different. Kinmen is 1.8 kilometers from Xiamen at its nearest point, directly facing the Xiamen commercial sphere. Matsu is about 50 kilometers from Mawei in Fuzhou and is not in Fuzhou's immediate suburbs. As a result, the everyday density with which Kinmen residents go to Xiamen to do business, buy daily necessities, and seek medical treatment is far higher than the density of Matsu residents' ties to Fuzhou.

Their battle histories are still more asymmetrical. Kinmen has Guningtou (1949), the August 23 Artillery Battle (1958), and odd-day shelling, even-day no shelling (1958-1979): **two decisive battles plus 21 years of shelling**. Matsu had the Battle of Dongquan (1954), the Battle of Yijiangshan (1955, not located in Kinmen), and scattered shelling, but never a land battle on the scale of Guningtou.

Population scale differs by an order of magnitude. Kinmen has 145,000 registered residents; Matsu has only 13,000. **There are more than ten times as many Kinmen residents as Matsu residents**. Kinmen has National Quemoy University, founded in 1997 as the first university in Kinmen's history[^25]. Matsu has no university.

> **📝 Curator's Note:** The common framework of “offshore-island counties” bundles Kinmen and Matsu into one concept: “Cold War front line,” “battlefield tourism,” “cross-strait confrontation.” This compression is convenient in media headlines, but **it writes two counties of entirely different flesh and bone as if they had the same résumé**. Kinmen is Southern Min, with common roots in Kinmen and Xiamen, close to the Xiamen commercial sphere, marked by large-scale land battles and a military-rule garrison on the scale of 100,000 people. Matsu is Eastern Min and Fuzhou-speaking, farther from Fuzhou, a chain of fishing-village islands suspended at sea, with a military-rule garrison of about 50,000. The two counties are like fraternal twins of the same fate: **the same system, different flesh and bone**. If you say “Kinmen-Matsu troop withdrawal,” “Kinmen-Matsu lifting of martial law,” or “Kinmen-Matsu Mini Three Links,” remember that behind this plural are two independent counties, two independent languages, and two independent memories of war.

## At 4 a.m., the Boats from Shuitou Pier to Xiamen

Return to the strait with which we began.

On **January 2, 2001**, the first “trial direct sailing” departed from Kinmen's Shuitou Pier for Xiamen's Wutong Pier. This was the earliest pilot opening of the “Three Links” across the strait, covering postal, commercial, and transportation links, and it came **seven years before the 2008 full Three Links**[^26]. From that day to 2024, Mini Three Links passenger traffic accumulated to about **24 million trips**. ⚠️ Statistics vary slightly by source; 24 million is an approximate figure. The voyage takes **45 minutes**, and a one-way ticket costs about NT$600 to NT$900, depending on cabin class.

The Mini Three Links were suspended in February 2020 because of COVID-19, partially resumed in February 2023, limited to Taiwan citizens, and gradually restored to two-way travel beginning in 2024[^26]. For Kinmen residents, the Mini Three Links are daily provisioning, not a political symbol. Many of Kinmen's fresh foods and daily goods enter through Xiamen channels. Xiamen's large department stores and medical facilities have real appeal to Kinmen residents. Many Kinmen residents cross the border to work in Xiamen, and Taiwanese businesspeople and managers in Xiamen also use the Mini Three Links to move between the two sides.

This is Kinmen's most concrete contemporary contradiction.

**At 4 a.m., there are more speedboats from Shuitou Pier to Xiamen than planes to Songshan**. Xiamen is 45 minutes away. In two hours you can go to Xiamen, finish lunch, and come back, including transit time. But flying to Taipei takes at least more than an hour in the air plus airport transfers, adding up to half a day. **The everyday density determined by geography and the sense of belonging determined by politics pull against each other every day on this 151-square-kilometer island**.

Kinmen County's registered population in 2024 was about **145,000**, but its **actual resident population is lower**. Many Kinmen residents work on Taiwan proper while maintaining household registration in Kinmen to receive county government subsidies and share allocations, since Kinmen Distillery distributes annual shares to registered residents[^27]. “Having household registration in Kinmen” and “living in Kinmen” are two different things, and this gap is calculated daily in county budgeting, elections, and health insurance subsidies.

On October 25, 2024, the 75th anniversary commemoration of the Battle of Guningtou was held in Kinmen. When County Magistrate Chen Fu-hai read the phrase “cherish peace, defend sovereignty,” the site contained the beachhead where Communist troops once landed, the Zhaishan Tunnel where tourists now line up to kayak, Xiamen speedboats leaving Shuitou Pier every hour, residents registered in Kinmen but working in Taipei, and Yeh Hua-cheng's great-grandson, who had not been invited.

When you take a trip to Kinmen, do not look only at the Zhaishan Tunnel.

Go to the Guningtou Battle Museum and study the timeline panels for those 56 hours in 1949. The restored M5A1 tank is parked right there on the beachhead. Go to the August 23 Artillery Battle Museum and see how the figure of 474,910 shells becomes 3,132 shells per square kilometer over 44 days. Walk down Model Street and see the Western-style shophouses built by returning overseas Chinese in 1924, remembering that the time on this street is not Japanese colonial time. Visit Shanhou Village and see how late-Qing and early-Republican Southern Min houses used granite to stack 16 connected courtyard compounds. Buy a bottle of kaoliang liquor at Kinmen Distillery. The bottle bears the four characters “Kinmen Distillery,” but in your mind you know there are still three characters, “Yeh Hua-cheng,” not printed on it.

Then arrive at Shuitou Pier before 4 a.m.

Look at the people waiting to board: Kinmen residents going to Xiamen for dental care, Taiwanese businesspeople heading to factories in Xiamen, tourists curious to try the Mini Three Links. When the first boat leaves the pier toward Jiaoyu, Xiamen, the distance across the sea is only **1.8 kilometers**. But beneath those 1.8 kilometers of water lie **the beachhead at 2 a.m. on October 25, 1949; Taiwu Mountain at 5:30 p.m. on August 23, 1958; the sound of shells on every odd-numbered day during the 21 years from 1958 to 1979 when odd days meant shelling and even days did not; every 10 p.m. curfew during the 36 years of warzone administration from 1956 to 1992; and the distillery forcibly expropriated from Yeh Hua-cheng in 1952**. Seventy-five years of fate are layered into this 45-minute voyage.

**What those 56 hours determined was not only Kinmen. It was Taiwan**.

## Further Reading

- [Lienchiang County](/geography/連江縣) — Sibling article in the 22 Counties and Cities series, another offshore-island county of Fujian Province. Like Kinmen, it had warzone administration from 1956, abolition in 1992, and Mini Three Links from 2001, but it speaks Eastern Min Fuzhou speech, is 50 kilometers from Fuzhou, and never had a land battle on the scale of Guningtou. It shares Kinmen's system but not its flesh and bone.
- [Keelung City](/geography/基隆市) — Pilot article in the 22 Counties and Cities series. Northern Taiwan's only deep-water port versus Kinmen's southern coastal-defense fortress: two timelines of Taiwan's ports.
- [Penghu County](/geography/澎湖縣) — The third county in the offshore-island group of the 22 Counties and Cities series. Like Kinmen it is an offshore island, but it has a Japanese colonial history, allowing comparison with Kinmen's lack of direct Japanese colonial rule.
- [Koxinga](/people/鄭成功) — He raised forces in Kinmen in 1646 against the Qing and in support of Ming restoration. The decision this man made from Kinmen later changed Taiwan.
- [Chiang Kai-shek](/people/蔣中正) — The decision-maker who inscribed “Never Forget Juguang” on Taiwu Mountain in 1958 and ordered Kinmen to be defended at all costs in 1949.
- [Martial Law Period](/history/戒嚴時期) — Martial law ended on Taiwan proper in 1987, while Kinmen and Matsu did not abolish warzone administration until 1992. Read this article alongside it to see two versions of martial law.
- [Taiwan Strait Crises and the Development of Cross-Strait Relations](/history/台海危機與兩岸關係發展) — The August 23 Artillery Battle is one of the most concrete physical pieces of evidence of hot war within the Cold War. The “5:30 p.m.” section of this article extends that history at the county level.
- [Taiwan's Defense and Military Modernization](/society/台灣國防與軍事現代化) — Compare Kinmen's evolution from a garrison of 50,000 to 100,000 under military rule to fewer than 10,000 troops today with the broader trajectory of ROC military modernization.

- [Legends of Mazu and Baosheng Dadi](/culture/媽祖與大道公的傳說) — Kinmen's Southern Min religious beliefs share origins with those on Taiwan proper and differ from Matsu's legends of the sacred cave of the Heavenly Empress.
