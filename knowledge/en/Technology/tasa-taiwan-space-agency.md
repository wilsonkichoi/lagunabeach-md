In 2016, the music video for Mayday’s “Stubborn” featured a middle‑aged man building a rocket in a barren field. He was treated as a day‑dreaming lunatic, and eventually strapped a homemade rocket to his back and flew into the sky. Most Taiwanese first learned about Wu Tsung-hsin not through any government agency but through that video—the “Rocket Uncle”[^1].

Five years later, the man who had been treated as a joke took over the nation’s space program.

> **30‑second overview:** When the National Space Center was founded in 1991 it didn’t even have an official name; it was called the “Preparatory Office of the National Space Program Office.” In 2023 it finally obtained legal corporate status and changed its English name from NSPO to TASA—deliberately inserting **Taiwan**[^2]. Today it is far more than that ad‑hoc unit: the Hsinchu control center monitors the heartbeat of eight orbiting satellites around the clock, Formosat‑7 daily delivers about four thousand occultation data points to global weather models, and after the 0403 Hualien earthquake it re‑tasked Formosat‑5 six minutes after the quake and captured images within three hours, identifying more than a thousand new landslides[^51][^53]. It is now assembling the Formosat‑8 constellation, building the all‑weather Formosat‑9 radar satellite, and has approved a moon mission that could launch as early as 2028[^61]. For thirty years it publicly claimed to be doing weather and science; only in 2025, after Formosat‑8’s launch, did it admit in a _The Reporter_ feature and in the president’s public remarks that it had always been watching the military moves across the Strait[^4].

## A “Preparatory Office” Begins

On 3 October 1991, the Executive Yuan approved the first phase of the Long‑Term Space Technology Development Plan and simultaneously established the “Preparatory Office of the National Space Program Office”[^5].

Notice the three characters “Preparatory Office.” It was not a bureau, not a department, not even a formal center—just a temporary arrangement, a body waiting to see if it could survive. It received a budget and an English acronym NSPO, but it still lacked a permanent Chinese name.

That was a time when Taiwan itself was still arguing over what to call itself—a few years after the lifting of martial law, before the president had ever been directly elected, the island decided to start sending things into space. The original concept was simple: buy a satellite, have an American contractor build it, then learn by sending Taiwanese engineers to the factory. The first‑phase plan was drawn that way: three satellites, a step‑by‑step progression from purchase to learning[^5].

The name “Preparatory Office” was unintentionally honest. It described a country that was not yet ready, yet still put its intentions out there.

In the following decade‑plus, the agency kept changing its name, and the changes were telling. In June 2003 it merged into the newly created National Applied Research Laboratories and became the “National Space Program Office,” English: National Space **Program** Office. Two years later, in April 2005, it was renamed the “National Space Center,” English: National Space **Organization**[^6]. It seemed only a matter of swapping “Program” for “Organization,” but the acronym NSPO remained unchanged—​the same letters now stood for two different English titles. Even the name was still being tried, tried, and feared to be discovered as a trial.

> **📝 Curator’s note**  
> Why did the agency keep changing its name? Because it was always under someone else’s umbrella. It started as a project of the National Science Council, later became a center of the National Applied Research Laboratories, and never had its own legal personality, so its name was never its own. A thing without “personhood” only carries the label its superiors stick on it—labels that are applied, torn off, and reapplied. This mirrors Taiwan’s own situation: an entity whose name on the international stage is decided by others. It would not regain the ability to name itself until many years later.

During those years of name‑testing, the agency relied on a handful of satellites to gradually build its legitimacy.

## Thirty Years of Saying It Is Watching the Weather

On 27 January 1999, Formosat‑1 lifted off from Cape Canaveral. The bus was built by the U.S. company TRW; Taiwan sent more than twenty engineers to the U.S. plant to learn, and only contributed a few components[^7]. It was Taiwan’s “first satellite,” but more like a learner’s ticket—it taught the agency how to talk to something up in the sky.

The next two satellites set the tone for the agency’s public messaging for the next thirty years. Formosat‑2, launched in 2004, was Taiwan’s first remote‑sensing satellite, built by France’s EADS Astrium, passing over Taiwan once a day with two‑metre black‑and‑white imagery[^8]. It is best remembered for disaster relief: the South‑Asia tsunami, the Sichuan earthquake, and Typhoon Morakot, supporting more than three hundred imaging missions[^8].

Formosat‑3, launched in 2006, was even more straightforward: a six‑satellite micro‑satellite constellation cooperating with the U.S. COSMIC program, using GPS occultation to measure the atmosphere[^9]. It was called “the most precise thermometer in space,” pushing global weather‑forecast accuracy forward[^9].

![Formosat‑3 / COSMIC micro‑satellite payload structure—six micro‑satellites forming a constellation, using GPS occultation to measure the atmosphere, dubbed “the most precise thermometer in space,” improving global weather‑forecast precision](/article-images/technology/formosat3-cosmic-constellation.jpg)

> **💡 Did you know?**  
> The Formosat series runs from 1 to 8, skipping numbers 4 and 6. Formosat‑4 was cancelled due to procurement problems and its mission merged into Formosat‑5; Formosat‑6 was planned for a 2009 self‑launch but later outsourced and cancelled[^10]. Thus the Formosat family has two gaps—two roads that were never taken.

Disaster relief, weather reporting, atmospheric measurement—these have been the agency’s public face for three decades. It claims to do science, which is true, but it is also a deliberately chosen narrative. In a context where it cannot loudly proclaim that it is preparing for a potential adversary, “we are watching the weather” is a safe line. The real focus would only be disclosed much later.

## The Fall of Formosat‑5

On 25 August 2017, Formosat‑5 rode SpaceX’s Falcon 9 into orbit. This satellite was different: Taiwan’s first so‑called “independently developed” high‑resolution remote‑sensing satellite, costing NT$5.659 billion, six years in development, and the first time five key components—payload computer, power control, flight software, etc.—were built domestically[^11].

![Formosat‑5 launch on 24 August 2017, Falcon 9 from Vandenberg Air Force Base—Taiwan’s domestically built satellite riding a borrowed rocket](/article-images/technology/formosat5-falcon9-launch-2017.jpg)

> **✦** “As soon as you see a rocket launch that Taiwan built yourself, the nation’s confidence soars; you instantly become a 100 % Taiwanese.” — Wu Tsung-hsin[^12]

Formosat‑5’s first obstacle was not technical but political. It originally intended to use a standard‑grade CCD sensor, but high‑end remote‑sensing CCDs are subject to export controls, and Taiwan could not obtain them[^54]. The team therefore switched to a consumer‑grade CMOS sensor, redesigning it to space‑grade specifications and producing the world’s first high‑resolution remote‑sensing satellite using a CMOS sensor—thanks to more than fifty industry‑academia‑research teams working for five years[^54]. The word “independent” finally acquired concrete meaning: when it came to critical parts, others simply would not sell them.

Then it stumbled.

The images returned after launch were blurry, with concentric light rings around urban structures. The nation held its breath: would this NT$5.6 billion “Taiwan pride” become expensive space junk[^13]?

The rescue came from the satellite‑image team. Liu Hsiao‑ching, who led the team at the time, later recalled that Formosat‑5’s collimator had not been properly calibrated, failing to provide true parallel light; “a one‑millimetre deviation can cause massive defocus”[^14]. The National Applied Research Laboratories proposed three solutions, ultimately using software to iteratively de‑blur the images, achieving commercial‑grade quality by the end of November[^14]. A year and four months later, the Control Yuan investigated and traced the problem to a collimator purchased in 2011 for US$8 million, concluding that its calibration management was flawed[^15].

That stumble meant more to the agency than any success. “Independence” became a phrase that, at 561 km altitude, could be ruined by a single ground instrument that was not properly calibrated. Taiwan fell for the first time, then got back up on its own.

## The Rocket Uncle From the Barren Field Takes the Train

The year Liu Hsiao‑ching rescued Formosat‑5, Wu Tsung-hsin was still building his private rockets in a barren field.

Wu Tsung-hsin was born in 1964 in a rural area of Tainan; his parents were uneducated[^16]. He attended Tainan First High School, then National Taiwan University’s Department of Mechanical Engineering, earned a Ph.D. in aerospace at the University of Michigan, worked as a deputy researcher at the Space Center in 1995, and later taught at National Chiao‑tung University[^16]. In 2012 he founded the ARRC (Advanced Rocket Research Center), crowdfunding rockets: a university professor and a group of students testing their own rockets on Taiwan’s coast. In 2015 he stood on the TEDxTaipei stage, delivering his entire talk in Taiwanese, concluding with: “We will never give up; if we want to leave Earth, that’s the right thing to do!”[^17]

> **⚠️ Controversial view**  
> The Rocket Uncle story is not all romance. In 2016 Wu Tsung‑shin co‑founded the private company Jin‑Sheng Space Technology with former NASA scientist Chen Yen‑sheng[^18]. On 13 February 2020, Jin‑Sheng’s “Flying Mouse‑1” ignited in Daren Township, Taitung, but failed to lift off[^18]. It later emerged that the launch site had been applied for under the name “shrimp farm,” actually built on indigenous reservation land, violating regional planning law; Taitung County fined the company three times for a total of NT$860,000, and former chairman Lee Chin‑yi was sentenced to 50 days of detention (commutable to a fine)[^19]. Wu left Jin‑Sheng in 2018 over ideological differences and declared that ARRC was unrelated to Jin‑Sheng[^18]. Nevertheless, this shadow is part of his personal arc—Taiwan’s space dream has stepped on environmental‑justice and indigenous‑land‑rights issues.

On 1 August 2021, the man once treated as a joke took over as director of the National Space Center[^20]. He described the transition as moving from “barking at a train” as an academic critic to “driving the train” himself[^21].

> **📝 Curator’s note**  
> Wu Tsung‑shin is almost a microcosm of the agency. A man from a poor farming background who dreamed of leaving Earth, once dismissed as a day‑dreamer, now carries the nation’s rocket dream. He is often asked why Taiwan does not build an entire satellite system itself; his retort is: “We have the capability, why should we sharpen other people’s knives?” On a personal level that is ambition; on an institutional level it encapsulates the meaning of “independence.” The private rocket in the barren field and the national satellite in orbit are, in fact, the same pursuit.

## Putting **Taiwan** Back Into the Name

Between Wu’s arrival and his departure, the agency finally began to acquire the two things it lacked most: legal personality and its own name.

On 31 May 2021, the Legislative Yuan passed the Space Development Act in its third reading[^22]. This was Taiwan’s first dedicated space law, six chapters, twenty‑two articles; Article 11 stipulates that launch vehicles must be launched from “national launch sites” within Taiwan’s territory[^23]. This means that, for the first time, Taiwan legally recognized the need for its own launch site.

Even more crucially, the following year. On 19 April 2022, the Legislative Yuan passed the National Space Center Establishment Act, which took effect on 1 January 2023[^24]. Article 2 states plainly: “The Center shall be an administrative corporation; its supervising authority shall be the National Science and Technology Council”[^25].

The phrase “administrative corporation” gave the agency, after thirty‑two years, its first legal “personhood.” Previously it was a center under the National Applied Research Laboratories with no authority to sign contracts or procure independently. After the reform it could sign contracts, procure on its own, and its staff salaries were detached from the civil‑service system; Wu said this sped up hiring by nearly tenfold[^26].

Along with the reform, the English name changed from NSPO to TASA, i.e., **Taiwan Space Agency**. The official rationale was to “insert Taiwan into the name to improve international recognizability”[^27].

> **✦** A nation that could not even print its own name on a sports field finally put **Taiwan** on its own space agency.

![Opening ceremony on 1 January 2023, the National Space Center’s sign reads “TASA National Space Center / Taiwan Space Agency” – after thirty‑two years the agency finally gained legal personhood and inserted Taiwan into its English name](/article-images/technology/tasa-opening-ceremony-2023.jpg)

This moment is the quietest yet most pivotal in the story. Swapping an acronym does not change the technology—satellites remain the same, engineers remain the same. But an institution that for three decades had its name decided by others finally inserted the two characters **Taiwan** into its own name. Premier Su Tseng‑chang said in the draft announcement that the goal was to “project Taiwan’s national power into space”[^28]—political rhetoric, perhaps, but at this moment of renaming it referred to a concrete action.

## What the Agency Does Today

To understand today’s TASA, walk into the control center in Hsinchu Science Park’s “Exhibition Road” building. On the screens, the heartbeats of eight satellites pulse in unison[^51].

Each satellite’s over‑flight time over Taiwan is fixed; the control center must upload commands and download data within the few minutes it passes overhead. Of the eight, the oldest is the over‑aged Formosat‑5 (launched 2017); the middle six are Formosat‑7, a COSMIC‑2 constellation launched in 2019 that delivers about four thousand occultation data points daily to global weather‑forecast models[^51][^55]; the 2023 “Wind‑Hunter” (Triton) sniffing sea‑surface winds; and the newest, the first Formosat‑8 satellite launched at the end of November 2025, still in trial operation, slated for full service in July 2026[^52]. A 1991 preparatory office now commands a 24‑hour, always‑on satellite fleet.

At the other end of the facility, the next batch of satellites is being assembled. Formosat‑8 will be an eight‑satellite constellation, to be launched in batches, all in place by 2031[^52]. When the first “Zeppelin” satellite transmits its first images in early 2026—covering Hsinchu, Tainan Anping, Kaohsiung’s Xingda Port, and even Tokyo’s National Stadium and Barcelona’s airport—the team will already be testing the second satellite[^52]. Further ahead, the Formosat‑9 radar satellites are still in the parts‑procurement stage, two units scheduled for launch in 2028 and 2030 respectively[^57].

> **💡 Did you know?**  
> The Formosat‑8 generation carries sixteen domestically produced key components for on‑orbit verification, all of which passed[^52]. A component’s suitability for space cannot be proven by ground testing alone; it must survive the vacuum and radiation of orbit before it can be listed in a specification sheet and sold. Formosat‑8 thus provides a “this part has been to space” certificate for more than twenty Taiwanese manufacturers[^52].

The images captured by the satellites are turned into services. TASA runs an image‑service platform that government agencies can request for disaster‑area mapping, agricultural crop monitoring, or coastal‑line planning[^51]. Historical imagery from Formosat‑2 and Formosat‑5 has also been uploaded to the government open‑data portal[^51].

![Turquoise shallow waters and deep blue trench off the Bahamas, a sand island in the middle. This is one of the scenes collected by Formosat’s image‑service platform, showing the world from Taiwan’s own eyes (National Space Center TASA Formosat image selection)](/article-images/technology/formosat-bahamas-shoals-tasa.jpg)

_Formosat imagery of the Bahamas. Thirty years after the nameless preparatory office, today’s “eyes” can see the other side of the world with crystal clarity._

That is only the space‑side. On the ground, TASA is grading the land in Jiupeng, Pingtung, to build its own launch site; it is also constructing a NT$3.388 billion rocket‑integration base in Shalun, Tainan, slated for operation in 2030[^56]. Wu describes TASA’s current mission in a long sentence: “Deploy three satellite constellations—communication, optical remote sensing, and synthetic‑aperture radar—to build Taiwan’s autonomous three‑dimensional communication and Earth‑observation network”[^58]. In plain language, Taiwan wants its own communications network, its own clear‑vision eyes, and its own all‑weather radar—each layer independent of foreign providers.

## What the Satellites Actually Do for Taiwan

On the morning of 3 April 2024, a magnitude‑7.4 earthquake struck offshore Hualien—the strongest in Taiwan in twenty‑five years. Six minutes after the quake, TASA engineers remotely re‑tasked Formosat‑5; three hours later the satellite passed over the epicenter and captured emergency imagery[^53].

That is the most concrete illustration of a satellite’s value. Formosat‑5’s single‑pass swath is only twenty‑four kilometres; to cover the entire Hualien mountain region it must fly multiple passes and stitch the images together[^53]. The Rural Water Conservation Agency used Formosat‑5 and other satellite images to assess post‑quake landslides, counting 1,391 new collapse sites covering 943.76 hectares, of which 444 fell within debris‑flow‑prone zones and 62 directly threatened railways[^53]. A team from National Central University calculated that the Shoulin area alone suffered about 20.6 square kilometres of landslides[^53]. These numbers are not academic papers; they are the basis for emergency evacuations, road closures, and disaster‑resource allocation.

![False‑colour image of Formosat‑5 (red = vegetation) showing a large landslide beside an industrial road in Taitung’s Chih‑ke Mountain after the 2022 earthquake. The same workflow—post‑quake rapid re‑imaging and pre‑/post‑disaster comparison—was used for the 0403 Hualien quake](/article-images/technology/formosat5-taitung-landslide-2022.jpg)

_Formosat‑5’s interpretation of the Chih‑ke Mountain landslide after the 2022 Taitung earthquake. The same workflow identified over a thousand new collapse sites after the 0403 Hualien quake._

> **✦** “(National‑security‑target imaging) is usually the first priority for all domestically built satellites; even if a buyer spends a lot of money, they may not get a slot.” — _The Reporter_[^59]

During the earthquake, TASA also activated its “Sentinel Asia” mechanism, pulling satellite imagery from Japan’s JAXA, Thailand’s GISTDA, and India’s ISRO[^53]. In other words, Taiwan now has its own satellites, but in a disaster it can also tap into an international mutual‑aid network. A country without its own satellites must wait for others to spare time and bandwidth—often those slots are reserved for each nation’s own security missions, and money alone does not guarantee access[^59]. True independence means no queue.

In September 2025, a dam‑break lake formed upstream of the Matai‑Anxi River in Hualien. Using the same workflow, multiple satellites—including Formosat‑5— confirmed roughly five hundred hectares of collapse and lake area; local governments installed water‑level gauges and calculated evacuation timing based on the data[^60]. Image analyst Zhang Lixue explained that a satellite can only capture about twenty‑four kilometres per pass, so several satellites and multiple passes must be stacked for analysis[^60].

Beyond the sky, Taiwan’s vulnerability also lies under the sea. In February 2023, two undersea cables linking the Matsu Islands were severed within six days by Chinese vessels—first a fishing boat cut the Taiwan‑Matsu 2 cable, then a cargo ship snagged and broke Taiwan‑Matsu 3[^62]. Thirteen thousand people on Matsu were offline for fifty days, relying on microwave backup; a Line message could take fifteen to twenty minutes to transmit[^62]. Ninety‑nine percent of Taiwan’s external internet traffic travels over fourteen undersea cables, and the Taiwan‑Matsu cables break on average five to ten times a year—twenty‑five to fifty times the global average[^62].

That is why Taiwan is developing its own low‑Earth‑orbit communications satellites. But it must be honest: satellites cannot replace undersea cables. A single communications satellite’s capacity is about one‑hundred‑thousand times less than an undersea cable[^62]. Satellite backup matters only for those fifty days when the cables are cut, ensuring at least one lifeline can still say “we are still here,” not to replace the cable network.

> **📝 Curator’s note**  
> Formosat‑3’s “most precise thermometer in space” label was not Taiwanese self‑praise. In 2020 its successor COSMIC‑2’s occultation data were incorporated into the European Centre for Medium‑Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF) and the U.S. National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) global forecast systems[^55]. ECMWF assessed that the data improved tropical forecasts at the 100 hPa level by about five percent; NOAA called the collaboration “the biggest technology cooperation between the United States and Taiwanese authorities”[^55]. A small agency not recognized by most countries relies not on diplomatic recognition but on the fact that it can actually capture and compute data that the world’s weather models need. This is Taiwan’s back‑door route to embed itself in the international system through technology.

## The Cool Experiments

If you only look at the Formosat series, you might think TASA only builds large satellites. Its truly interesting work hides in the tiny, seemingly impossible experiments that solve niche problems.

On 9 December 2024, a tiny satellite called “Wang‑Lai” (ONGLAISAT) was ejected from the International Space Station’s airlock and entered orbit[^63]. It is the size of a briefcase, a Taiwan‑Japan collaboration with the University of Tokyo[^63]. It carried the world’s first space‑validated CMOS TDI sensor, developed by the Taiwan Semiconductor Research Center of the National Applied Research Laboratories[^63]. Despite its size, it achieved 2.5–3 metre resolution, with a 100 % success rate over fifty imaging passes, ranking it among the top CubeSats worldwide[^63]. It proves that Taiwan can not only build big satellites but also pack micro‑remote‑sensing capability into a briefcase.

On the rocket side, there is a world‑first. Wu’s old team at ARRC, together with National Yang‑Ming University, launched a hybrid rocket called HTTP‑3A in July 2022[^64]. It achieved a feat previously only possible with liquid rockets—thrust vector control, i.e., the ability to steer the rocket in flight—making it the world’s first hybrid rocket with this capability[^64].

> **⚠️ Controversial view**  
> The HTTP‑3A flight, to be honest, was not perfect. It was intended to reach 10 km altitude but only reached about three kilometres due to higher‑than‑expected atmospheric drag[^64]. Nevertheless, project lead Wei Shih‑hsin explained that the first thirty seconds of guidance and control operated exactly as planned, “equivalent to a 99 % success.” A rocket built by seven‑thousand people and funded by NT$25 million of crowd‑sourced money proved the hardest technology—directional control. The altitude fell short, but the steering worked.

Formosat‑8 also hides a green experiment many readers miss. Traditional satellite attitude‑control thrusters use hydrazine, a highly toxic propellant that requires full protective gear on the ground[^65]. Formosat‑8 is the first to use a domestically developed hydrogen‑peroxide (high‑concentration H₂O₂) propellant—essentially concentrated hydrogen peroxide[^65]. National Cheng‑Kung University’s Zhao Yi‑qin pioneered the technology and calculated that thrust from hydrogen peroxide is only about ten percent less than hydrazine’s[^65]. That ten‑percent loss buys a propellant that does not poison operators nor pollute the environment—crucial for an agency that is still building launch sites and will handle propellants more often.

The smartest trick is on the “Wind‑Hunter” (Triton) satellite. It measures sea‑surface wind without transmitting any signal to the ocean. Its method: use existing GPS satellite signals as “illuminators.” Those signals bounce off the sea surface; the rougher the sea and the stronger the wind, the more chaotic the reflected signal becomes[^66]. Triton simply receives the reflected signals and infers wind speed from the signal’s turbulence—no active transmission, no contact with the sea.

![Clean‑room testing of the Triton satellite—Taiwan’s first domestically built meteorological satellite, originally the thirteenth Formosat‑7 unit, later spun off, with an 80 %+ domestic production rate, using GNSS‑R reflected signals to measure sea‑surface wind](/article-images/technology/triton-inspection-nspo-2022.jpg)

## Borrowed Skies

With a name and legal personality, the agency began plugging the hardest missing piece—its infrastructure had long been built on borrowed foundations.

On 9 October 2023, the Wind‑Hunter satellite launched from the Kourou launch site in South America aboard a European VEGA rocket[^29]. It was originally the thirteenth Formosat‑7 satellite, later spun off; its domestic production rate rose to 82–83 %, with Hsiang‑Hsiang responsible for system integration[^29]. To download its data, Taiwan borrowed a ground station in Alaska operated by the U.S. NOAA[^29].

This illustrates the agency’s situation: it must borrow SpaceX or European rockets to reach orbit, and borrow U.S. ground stations to download data. The most fundamental limitation lies in a place most people do not think of.

Taiwan is not a United Nations member. This means it cannot directly apply to the International Telecommunication Union (ITU) for radio‑frequency coordination and orbital slots—prerequisites for launching satellites. Consequently, both the launch itself and the acquisition of orbital resources must go through other countries[^30].

> **📝 Curator’s note**  
> We tend to think of “space independence” as a matter of pride—whether Taiwan has the guts to do it itself. For this agency, independence is less a choice than a survival strategy forced upon it. U.S. ITAR export controls make “buying a whole system” extremely difficult; non‑UN membership makes “borrowing a launch” the only option. Thus Taiwan learned to build its own satellites partly because, when you need to do something sensitive, no one will gladly lend you the means. Taiwan’s space autonomy grew out of a situation where “no one will lend you” became the driver.

## Where It Is Heading

Land, buildings, people, and now money are in place. In 2024 a new office opened in Hsinchu Science Park; on 26 March 2025 the National Science Council selected Jiupeng Village, Manzhou, Pingtung, as the permanent national launch site; on 20 October 2025 the Executive Yuan approved expanding the third‑phase long‑term space‑technology development plan from NT$25.1 billion to NT$71 billion, extending the timeline to 2031[^31][^32][^34].

> **💡 Did you know?**  
> Taiwan has several launch sites that are easily confused. The Jiupeng (Jiupeng) base in Pingtung is a military facility of the National Applied Research Laboratories, operational since 1975 for missile tests[^33]. The Xuhai launch site in Mudan, Pingtung, was inaugurated by the National Science Council in 2022 for scientific sounding rockets; Wu’s ARRC launched rockets there[^33]. Jiupeng was only selected as the national launch site in 2025[^32]. The “Flying Mouse‑1” launch in 2020 took place in Nantan, Taitung—different from the three sites above[^33].

Nevertheless, the agency still lacks its most crucial component: its own orbital launch vehicle. It can build satellites, but must hitch them to foreign rockets. This is the gap to be filled over the next decade. TASA’s planned orbital launch vehicle is a two‑stage liquid‑propellant design, aiming to deliver a 200‑kilogram payload to low‑Earth orbit, targeted for 2034; in between there will be two sub‑orbital test flights (STV) slated for 2029 and 2031, first reaching the 100‑kilometre Kármán line, then progressing toward true orbital capability[^35][^61]. Sources differ on exact dates, so a conservative estimate is “the 2030s” for completion.

The communications‑satellite layer is also being expanded. Taiwan’s low‑Earth‑orbit communications constellation, colloquially called the “Taiwan‑version Starlink,” follows a 2 + 4 model—the government builds the first two satellites to set specifications, then assists the private sector in building the remaining four[^37]. The background is the impressive performance of SpaceX’s Starlink in supporting communications during the Ukraine war[^37]. The first “1A” satellite was originally scheduled for 2025 launch, delayed to 2027 because the communications payload did not meet specifications[^38].

> **⚠️ Controversial view**  
> Not everyone thinks the pace is fast enough. Think tank GTI points out that Taiwan’s planned six‑satellite low‑orbit communications constellation by 2029 falls far short of the roughly 120 satellites needed for a resilient national communications network—“a huge gap from six.” An agency that finally has a bureau, a legal personality, but still no rocket and an undersized constellation—this is another honest side of the story.

Above all, in 2025 a previously unthinkable goal quietly emerged: the moon.

![Formosat‑9’s active phased‑array SAR payload—SAR satellites are immune to day/night and weather, providing radar imagery that complements Formosat‑8’s optical remote sensing](/article-images/technology/formosat9-sar-tasa.png)

The moon mission has already been budgeted, approved, and is no longer just talk. In 2025 TASA launched Taiwan’s first lunar‑exploration mission, with the earliest launch slated for 2028, having already tendered a NT$3.35 billion contract for a lunar lander integration service[^61]. The payloads are two Taiwan‑made scientific instruments: a lunar vector magnetometer and a “Formosa Moon Ultraviolet Telescope”[^61]. Wu once told the NASA Administrator that “Taiwan wants to be a part of the lunar adventure” (NASA has not formally confirmed Taiwan’s participation in the Artemis program)[^61].

As for even farther ambitions, Wu often mentions a vision: “Maybe in the future every Taiwanese youth’s coming‑of‑age ceremony could include a visit to a rocket launch and the national space museum”[^61]. A spaceport and a space museum are still concepts, not yet approved. The moon, however, is already on the road.
