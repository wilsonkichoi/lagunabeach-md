> **30-second overview:** In September 2011, six young people from National Taiwan University's College of Electrical Engineering and Computer Science founded Rayark Games on Jinshan North Road in Taipei, with NT$30 million in capital and a 16-person team. In January 2012, Cytus launched on iOS and reached No. 1 on the charts in 14 countries within a month. The subsequent Deemo entered Japan's Oricon chart, Implosion won 2015 iOS Game of the Year, and VOEZ became a Nintendo Switch launch title. Fourteen years later, Rayark has released more than ten games and surpassed 130 million downloads worldwide, but the keyword surrounding the company has shifted from "genius" to "what a pity." After struggling on for eight years, Sdorica formally entered maintenance mode in February 2026; the Implosion animated film, crowdfunded on Kickstarter in 2015, remained undelivered in 2026; and after the 2020 ICE Morse code incident, Rayark complied with the China market and modified the game. Aesthetics made Rayark visible to the world, and also made it stumble in every non-music arena.

## What an Arcade Machine Taught Them

![The official Rayark Inc. logo, the brand mark used since 2012, a white letter R on a red background](/article-images/technology/rayark-logo-2012.png)
_The official Rayark Inc. logo. Source: [English Wikipedia File:Logo of Rayark Inc.png](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Logo_of_Rayark_Inc.png). Fair use editorial commentary._

Rayark's story begins with a failure.

In 2008, Yu Ming-Yang was still in the first year of his master's program at National Taiwan University's Graduate Institute of Networking and Multimedia. He and two friends founded "Hypaa Studio" at school and developed a large-scale arcade touchscreen music game, THEIA[^kopu]. That same year, South Korea's PENTAVISION released DJ Max Technika on arcade platforms. Its gameplay was close to THEIA, but it completely outclassed THEIA in music quality, interface design, and overall polish[^pentavision-wiki].

"We thought that if the gameplay was interesting, it would sell, but the market response taught us that visuals and music are very important," Yu later recalled[^kopu].

That sentence became the underlying logic of every Rayark product that followed: gameplay was only the foundation; aesthetics were the moat.

Three years later, in September 2011, the team founded Rayark Games on Jinshan North Road in Taipei, with NT$30 million in capital and a 16-person team, shifting its focus entirely from arcade machines to mobile platforms[^rayark-wiki]. The six co-founders were Chung Chih-Yuan, Yu Ming-Yang, Chang Shih-Chun, Hsieh Chang-Yen, Yang Shan-Yong, and Li Yong-Ting. Five came from National Taiwan University's Department of Computer Science and Information Engineering (Yang Shan-Yong, class B91; Yu Ming-Yang and Hsieh Chang-Yen, class B92), and one came from NTU's Graduate Institute of Communication Engineering (Chung Chih-Yuan)[^csiecomm-2013][^rayark-wiki].

> **💡 Did you know**
> Yu Ming-Yang was the only one of the six whose undergraduate department was not in the College of Electrical Engineering and Computer Science. He studied in NTU's Department of Forestry as part of class B92. "I originally wanted to study computer science, but because I did not do well on the exam, I went to study forestry," he said in a 2012 interview[^csiecomm-2013]. From forestry to NTU's Graduate Institute of Networking and Multimedia, and then to founding a mobile music game company at age 26, this cross-disciplinary path became a source of Rayark's aesthetic sensibility and, unexpectedly, made him the company's public-facing representative.

Chung Chih-Yuan served as CTO. Several years later, he led the team in a full migration from PaaS to a Kubernetes container architecture. Rayark operated nearly fifty K8s clusters on Google Cloud, and "three people could maintain a game with a million players"[^ithome-rayark]. Hsieh Chang-Yen later became the producer of Sdorica[^sdorica-wiki]. Li Yong-Ting was a game director and one of Rayark's early company spokespeople[^bnext-rayark].

The six founders' shared background in NTU's College of Electrical Engineering and Computer Science later became the engineering chassis beneath Rayark's aesthetic formula: they could fine-tune the judgment precision of a music game, build cross-platform engines, and keep servers stable on the day Nintendo Switch launched. But in the fields of long-term RPG operations, animation production, and political public relations, that chassis would run into completely different problems.

## Cytus and Deemo: The Two Pillars of a Music Empire

On January 12, 2012, Cytus launched on iOS[^cytus-wiki].

![Cytus II gameplay screenshot showing the dynamic scan-line note-tapping judgment system, the signature mechanic of the Cytus series](/article-images/technology/cytus-ii-gameplay.png)
_The Cytus II version of the Cytus series' signature "dynamic scan-line" gameplay. Source: [English Wikipedia File:Cytus II gameplay.png](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cytus_II_gameplay.png). Fair use editorial commentary._

The gameplay was simple and intuitive: tap notes as a dynamic scan line passes over them. But what truly made it stand out was the quality of its art and music. Its science-fiction visual design and high-caliber original electronic music gave Cytus a distinct identity in a mobile music game market crowded with similar products. Within a month of launch, it reached No. 1 on the App Store charts in 14 countries, held the top spot on Japan's music game chart for 29 consecutive days, and reached one million paid downloads in April 2015[^cytus-wiki].

On November 13, 2013, Deemo launched[^deemo-wiki]. If Cytus was science-fiction electronica, Deemo was classical piano. Players played piano in a black-and-white world with the feel of a fairy tale. The music shifted from electronic to classical and lyrical, and the visual style moved from cool-toned science fiction to warm hand-drawn art.

![Cover of DEEMO: Last Recital for PS Vita, showing the signature image of a young girl playing piano in a black-and-white fairy-tale room](/article-images/technology/deemo-cover.jpg)
_Cover of DEEMO: Last Recital for PS Vita; its black-and-white fairy-tale style is the signature art of the Deemo series. Source: [English Wikipedia File:DeemoLastRecitalVita.jpg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:DeemoLastRecitalVita.jpg). Fair use editorial commentary._

Deemo became one of Rayark's biggest box-office works. By October 2014, it had accumulated 7 million downloads; when the animated film was announced in 2021, it had reached 28 million downloads[^deemo-wiki]. Its soundtrack sold strongly in Japan, and the game received App Store Taiwan's 2013 Best of Independent Developer award and Google Play Taiwan's 2014 Best Game Music award. The Nintendo Switch version received a Metacritic score of 88[^deemo-wiki]. Its sequel, DEEMO II, launched on January 13, 2022, surpassed one million global pre-registrations, broke one million downloads in its first week, and served as Rayark's tenth-anniversary title[^deemo2-gnn].

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> Cytus and Deemo established Rayark's brand formula: music game × high-quality art × narrative packaging. This formula gave Rayark a unique position in the global mobile game market, but it also boxed in every attempt that came later. Looking back fourteen years on, all of Rayark's most successful works have been music games, and all of its least successful attempts were efforts to apply this formula to non-music fields.

## The Year Switch Launched, They Were Doing Five Things at Once

Between 2015 and 2018, Rayark advanced five product lines at the same time.

Implosion launched on iOS and Android on April 8, 2015, and arrived on Nintendo Switch on July 6, 2017[^implosion-wiki]. It was a 3D action game whose visual quality was close to console level among mobile games of its time. Sold as a paid App Store download with no in-app purchases, it earned a Metacritic score of 93, entered the App Store Top Grossing chart at No. 7 within a week of launch, and received 91.9% five-star reviews[^implosion-wiki]. It won 2015 iOS Game of the Year Asia, the 2015 Bahamut ACG Creation Award, and the 2014 Unity Awards Golden Cube[^implosion-wiki].

In June 2016, VOEZ launched on mobile. The following year, on March 3, 2017, VOEZ became one of the Nintendo Switch launch titles, and Rayark was one of the few non-Nintendo first-party music game developers on the Switch launch lineup[^voez-4gamers]. By early 2017, global downloads had exceeded 10 million[^voez-4gamers]. Nintendo Life gave it 8/10 and called it the "Switch's secret weapon"[^voez-nintendo-life].

![Cover of VOEZ for Nintendo Switch, featuring the signature watercolor-style mirrored composition of two figures](/article-images/technology/voez-switch-cover.jpg)
_Cover of VOEZ for Nintendo Switch, published by Flyhigh Works; a Switch launch title on March 3, 2017. Source: [English Wikipedia File:SwitchVOEZ.jpg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:SwitchVOEZ.jpg). Fair use editorial commentary._

On January 18, 2018, Cytus II launched, a sequel to the original Cytus six years later[^cytus2-gematsu]. Its new system pushed music game players' judgment skills to the limit, and it later accumulated more than 18 million downloads worldwide. In May 2026, Rayark announced that Cytus II would be released for Nintendo Switch 2 and Switch in 2027, with more than 500 tracks included[^cytus2-gematsu][^cytus2-qooapp].

Implosion proved that Rayark's technical capacity was sufficient to cross genres. But the business model of an action game was completely different from that of a music game. Music games maintained revenue through continuous song-pack updates; an action game was a one-time purchase.

Then came Sdorica on April 19, 2018[^sdorica-wiki].

## Leaving the Comfort Zone: Sdorica's Eight Years

![Sdorica game logo, the aurora-subtitled version with character art on an aurora theme](/article-images/technology/sdorica-logo.png)
_The Sdorica game logo, aurora subtitle version. Source: [English Wikipedia File:Sdorica logo.png](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Sdorica_logo.png). Fair use editorial commentary._

Sdorica was Rayark's boldest attempt.

Planning began in 2013, development started in 2014, and the game was originally scheduled to launch in 2016. It was delayed to 2017, then delayed again before finally entering open beta on April 19, 2018. After a five-year development cycle, it had more than 1.5 million pre-registrations, surpassed 2.5 million downloads on April 30, and passed 5 million downloads on May 11[^sdorica-wiki]. After launch, daily active users exceeded 1 million.

This was a mobile RPG about heroes and soul-infusion in the world of Sdorica, with Hsieh Chang-Yen as producer[^sdorica-wiki][^moegirl-sdorica]. Rayark took the art budget of a music game and used it for RPG character illustrations, applying its narrative abilities to a character progression system. But the team soon discovered that a music game's playlist and an RPG's content update cadence required completely different muscles.

Problems erupted one after another after launch. Servers underestimated player numbers; gacha events did not open on schedule; a Dragon Boat Festival version produced Chinese mojibake; and afterglow powder was not distributed, among other issues[^sdorica-udn]. Between June and July 2018, Rayark's operational decisions triggered strong player backlash, including unannounced character stat adjustments and controversy over high-priced bundles. Within four months, the game slid from its peak and became a turning point in Rayark's reputation[^sdorica-bahamut-policy].

> **💡 Did you know**
> Rayark almost never missed on music games and action games, but stumbled on an RPG that required long-term live operations. The core capability of a music game or premium game is "making a good product." The core capability of RPG operations is "continuously serving a group of people." These are completely different muscles.

Eight years later, on February 11, 2026, Rayark formally announced that Sdorica would enter maintenance mode[^sdorica-gachago][^sdorica-bahamut-policy]:

> New main-story chapters, special events, limited-time bundles, and other content would all stop. The final two characters, Valdrir DR, would be sent to all players through the in-game mailbox, while Chiyuki DR would be obtainable through a rerun of the Spring Water Intoxication event. Special Infuse events would continue to rerun until July 2026, and after the final rerun, no new reruns would be opened. The servers would continue operating for the foreseeable future.

"The active development lifecycle of Sdorica has ended." This was the moment when Rayark itself declared the end of its RPG battlefield[^sdorica-gachago]. For core players who had stayed for years, the announcement was crueler than a sudden server shutdown: the servers would stay open, old characters could still be built, but new story would never come again. Rayark itself was also clear about the cost of this decision. Eight years of story, eight years of character design, and an eight-year accumulation of worldbuilding entered a frozen state, leaving players to decide how long they wanted to remain there.

For Rayark in early 2026, Sdorica's maintenance mode was necessary loss-cutting. For eight years, Sdorica had continuously consumed Rayark's R&D resources while also blurring the clarity of its brand positioning. A company founded on "making the most beautiful music games" was tied to an RPG it could no longer sustain, and every additional year of delayed loss-cutting made the company's overall narrative harder to explain. The side effect of the maintenance mode announcement may be that the Cytus and DEEMO series become the clear core of the Rayark brand again.

## Morse Code 1344 7609 2575

In March 2020, Rayark music director ICE, whose real name is Wilson Lam, a Hong Kong musician who joined Rayark in 2014, released the album Consciousness in a personal capacity. It included the song "Telegraph : 1344 7609 2575," uploaded to his personal SoundCloud and YouTube[^silentblue-wilson-lam][^itechpost-cytus2]. The "1344 7609 2575" in the title was Chinese Morse code.

On July 17, 2020, Chinese internet users dug up the song and found that the Morse code decoded to: **Hongkongers, add oil; liberate Hong Kong; revolution of our times**[^udn-ice][^newtalk-ice].

The next day, July 18, Ice announced in a personal statement that he was leaving Rayark. The original text, translated in full:

> Regarding the controversy caused by the piece of music I released in March 2020, which has led to much discussion among internet users: because this was my private activity, did not fall within the scope of work, and has nothing to do with Rayark Games, I have therefore decided to resign of my own accord and leave Rayark Games effective immediately[^newtalk-ice].

On the same day, Chinese publisher Longyuan Network and Rayark Games issued a joint announcement stating that Cytus II had been removed from sale in China pending rectification[^gnn-ice]. Taiwanese players expressed dissatisfaction on Rayark's fan page and questioned whether the company was engaging in ideological censorship. As of July 20, Rayark had still not issued a formal explanation on its official Taiwan platforms[^techorange-ice]. In May 2021, Longyuan and Rayark officially announced that after rectification, six tracks created by ICE would be removed, compensation would be provided to players who had paid for them, and the Chinese server of Cytus II would return to sale[^4gamers-cytus2-ice-removal].

Search results for ICE's creative trajectory after his personal departure stop at July 2020 (unverified beyond).

This was Rayark's version of a "political landmine" incident. A year earlier, in 2019, Red Candle Games (/technology/赤燭遊戲)' Devotion had been completely removed from sale in China because of a talisman incident, and Red Candle chose to directly exit the China market while preserving its Taiwanese stance. Rayark's handling of the 2020 ICE incident went in the opposite direction: the person involved left, the game was modified in compliance, and the China market was preserved.

> **⚠️ Contested Viewpoint**
> This was a starkly different choice by two benchmark Taiwanese indie teams facing the same pressure from the China market. English-language outlet PocketGamer.biz reported the story under the headline "China pulls Cytus II from the App Store for secret pro-democracy Morse code message"[^pocketgamer-cytus2], while discussions in the Chinese-language world focused on whether "Rayark knelt" and whether cutting ties was correct. One event, two readings.

## Implosion: Zero Day's Eleven Years

![Promotional poster for Implosion: Never Lose Hope, showing a mecha action scene with App Store and Google Play availability information](/article-images/technology/implosion-cover.png)
_Promotional poster for Implosion: Never Lose Hope, a 3D mecha action game that won 2015 iOS Game of the Year Asia. Source: [English Wikipedia File:Implosion- Never Lose Hope - cover.png](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Implosion-_Never_Lose_Hope_-_cover.png). Fair use editorial commentary._

Some promises look worse than silence.

On December 5, 2015, Rayark launched a Kickstarter campaign for the animated film Implosion: Zero Day, a planned 90-minute animation with a funding goal of US$400,000[^inside-zero-day]. Within 48 hours, it had reached 18% of its goal; within 10 days, it had passed NT$5 million[^implosion-zero-day-wiki]. It was originally scheduled for completion in August 2018.

It did not deliver in 2018.

On January 24, 2019, an announcement acknowledging a third delay admitted that "the IZD animated film could not be completed in 2018 as expected," and offered a refund option: backers could choose to receive refunds while still receiving the rewards corresponding to their tiers[^disp-bbs-implosion]. Refunds were tallied in February 2019, and the first batch of merchandise was sent in March, including hoodies, T-shirts, the collector's edition film script, the game soundtrack, comic chapters, and film wallpapers. The complete merchandise would be sent only after the film was finished.

From 2020 to 2026, there were five years of silence. Rayark's official Implosion page and the English Wikipedia entry for Implosion still maintained that the animation was "in progress," with no release date[^implosion-wiki]. Global Kickstarter backers had paid for eleven years of waiting and, by 2026, still had not seen the animation.

"They do not even dare to talk about the film's progress; they cannot provide even half a schedule." One backer wrote this under the 2019 delay announcement[^disp-bbs-implosion].

This is the only Rayark project that truly constitutes an "undelivered" promise. More serious than the operational cracks in Sdorica is the company's long-term silence toward global Kickstarter backers. But the issue received far less media attention in the Chinese-language world than the ICE incident, Sdorica's maintenance mode, or the AI art controversy. Perhaps the Chinese-language indie game industry is more tolerant of this kind of delay, or perhaps the consequences of the delay were ultimately dispersed across all the backers who had contributed to that 2015 Kickstarter campaign.

## Those Who Remain

Rayark Games Inc. as of May 2026: about 270 employees, NT$64 million in capital, headquarters at No. 47 Dongxing Road, Xinyi District, Taipei City. Since 2012, it has released more than ten games and accumulated more than 130 million downloads worldwide[^rayark-104]. It has a Rayark Japan branch in Tokyo and is not publicly listed.

The timeline of the past three years:

- In May 2023, Rayark faced an AI art controversy. Online rumors claimed "full AI creation + layoffs of artists." On May 27, Rayark clarified on Twitter that it had "not used AI creation to produce large quantities of images needed for games, and certainly had not dismissed its art team because of it"[^rayark-twitter-ai][^gnn-rayark-ai]
- On November 30, 2023, Rayark Café closed
- On October 14, 2024, Rayark Concept's online store ceased operations[^rayark-concept-fb]
- On February 11, 2026, Sdorica entered maintenance mode
- On May 22, 2026, Rayark announced that Cytus II would be released for Nintendo Switch 2 and Switch in 2027[^cytus2-gematsu]

Compared with music game companies from the same era, PENTAVISION, the company that had crushed Hypaa Studio in 2008, took an entirely different path. It was liquidated and dissolved in 2012 due to financial difficulties, then fully absorbed by its parent company Neowiz[^pentavision-wiki]. In 2017, Neowiz revived the series with DJMax Respect. In December 2024, DJMax Respect V surpassed 6 million downloads, and in 2025, Neowiz posted record-high revenue[^digitaltoday-neowiz].

PENTAVISION survived by being folded into a larger company and then revived. Rayark chose the opposite path: remain independent, expand across categories, and absorb the cost of every mistake on its own.

Rayark Games' situation is the most typical dilemma after success for a Taiwanese indie game company: the first product breaks out through passion and talent; subsequent products require organizational capacity and operational discipline. The market ceiling of music games meant Rayark had to expand into other categories, but every expansion diluted its original brand recognition. Cytus players and Sdorica players were completely different groups. Serving both under the same brand name may have left both groups feeling that "Rayark changed."

Yet Rayark remains one of the few Taiwanese game brands that can be named in the global market. Cytus II's 18 million downloads, the Deemo series' 28 million downloads, Implosion winning iOS Game of the Year, VOEZ as a Switch launch title, and the DEEMO animated film produced by Production I.G and Signal.MD with a theme song by Yuki Kajiura: in Taiwan's 2026 mobile game market, no second company can replicate these achievements[^deemo-movie-rayark].

In 2008, Yu Ming-Yang learned from an arcade machine that would not sell that "aesthetics are very important." Eighteen years later, that sentence remains Rayark's greatest asset and perhaps its greatest shackle. When you define yourself as "the people who make the most beautiful games," the market's tolerance for error falls to zero.

✦ Aesthetics made Rayark visible to the world, and also made it stumble in every non-music arena.

Sdorica's eight-year operational cracks came from the fundamental conflict between the formula of "art × music × story" and the nature of a long-term RPG service. Every event update required new high-quality character illustrations, and every narrative expansion required original music to match. This was possible at the scale of a 16-person team with NT$30 million in capital; within the cadence of a gacha game with hundreds of thousands of daily active users, it became a systemic time sink. The quality standard Rayark built through music games, "polish until readers can recognize it at first glance," collided with the content velocity of a mobile RPG, where players on average consume a major update within three days. The two were fundamentally in conflict.

The core problem behind the eleven-year non-delivery of the Implosion animation was that the standard of "making the most beautiful animation" met the execution limits of an independent company. Animation production at the level of Production I.G requires an organized production pipeline and a stable long-term writing team. When Rayark promised on Kickstarter to make a 90-minute animation, it was using the muscles of game development to make a promise in the animation industry. Rayark had the ability to meet the technical threshold for the Implosion animation; the difficulty lay in making it, then sustaining it all the way to release. An independent company of this scale could not carry that burden.

The ICE incident showed the inevitable result of extending the logic of "brand purity" to employees. Rayark's entire brand was built on a narrative of aesthetic leadership, political neutrality, and broad access to transnational markets. When an employee released a pro-Hong Kong song outside of work, the internal consistency of that brand narrative was challenged. Rayark's choice was to let the employee resign voluntarily, comply with the China market by modifying the game, and preserve its China publishing channels. This was the internally optimal solution within that brand logic, and also the control case against another indie path, the one taken by Red Candle.

What will the next Rayark look like? Perhaps there will not be one; perhaps it will require different muscles. When aesthetics are everything, every non-aesthetic choice becomes a crack, including politics, operations, and time. But Rayark's fourteen-year trajectory also proves one thing: Taiwanese indie game teams are capable of securing a rare brand position in the global music game market, something in Asia only Japan's BEMANI system and South Korea's DJ Max series had previously achieved. Rayark's story is not over. The day Cytus II arrives on Nintendo Switch 2 in 2027 will be the next moment for testing whether this aesthetic empire can continue to define made-by-Taiwan in the AI era.
