> **30-Second Overview:** On April 28, 2026, Taiwan's total stock market capitalization surged to US$4.47 trillion, overtaking Canada to become the world's 6th largest stock market; it had already surpassed the UK earlier that month. The driving force behind this was TSMC—with a market cap of US$1.8 trillion, its weight in the TAIEX approached 45%. Thirty-six years ago, the 10,000-point crash that collectively wiped out 4.6 million accounts, and the Hung Yuan investment scheme that swallowed the retirement savings of 160,000 people with monthly interest rates of 4%, have not yet dried from the island's financial memory. From the dozen-odd listed companies in the year the Taiwan Stock Exchange was founded, to 13.93 million accounts sharing the world's 6th largest capital market today, Taiwan took sixty-four years. But this 6th-place ranking carries a structural paradox: remove TSMC, and the remaining 1,000-plus stocks together would barely rank around 15th globally.

## April 28, 2026, the Bloomberg Terminal in New York

It was April 28, 2026, midnight Taipei time. The Bloomberg terminal flashed a headline: "Taiwan Overtakes Canada as World's Sixth-Largest Stock Market"[^bloomberg].

The numbers were clinical: Taiwan's total listed company market cap at US$4.47 trillion, Canada at US$4.44 trillion—the Taiwan Stock Exchange edged out this G7 nation by less than 1%[^biggo].

Hours later, a taxi driver on Xinyi Road in Taipei switched the radio to a financial station. The host was reading off another record broken just that month: in April alone, the TAIEX had gained 7,200 points, a 22.7% surge[^biggo2], and on the morning of April 27, it had touched 40,194.92 points intraday—the first time in history it broke through the 40,000 mark[^taipeitimes].

The driver didn't look up. He only said: "TSMC might push to 2,500 this time."

He had no idea what Bloomberg had flashed, and didn't need to—for him, this island's stock market had always been distilled into the price of a single stock.

And his instinct was, in fact, the same conclusion reached by analysts around the world holding Bloomberg terminals.

## Firecrackers, the Stock Exchange, and 160,000 People's Retirement Savings

To understand the red line of April 2026, you have to go back to the black line of February 1990.

On February 12, 1990, the Taiwan Stock Exchange Weighted Index hit 12,682 points—an all-time high. Someone set off firecrackers outside the exchange[^泡沫]. At the time, there were roughly 4.6 million active trading accounts across the island; nearly every household had someone buying stocks, and retail investors accounted for 90% of trading volume[^技術新報]. Taxi drivers discussed hot picks, aunts at the night market placed orders by phone at brokerage windows, and civil servants secretly watched the ticker from their offices.

Eight months later, the index fell to 2,485 points. An 80% collapse[^udn1990].

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> The phrase "Taiwan's money rises to one's ankles" (台灣錢淹腳目) was a product of this era. Too many dollars, too few places to put them—the stock market and real estate became two reservoirs with no floodgates.

Compounding the disaster was the Hung Yuan Investment Corporation. This underground investment company had been absorbing private capital since 1981 at a monthly interest rate of 4% (a 4% monthly return). At its peak, it had amassed approximately NT$94 billion. On January 9, 1990, Hung Yuan announced it would stop disbursing funds. Over 160,000 creditors lost everything overnight—most were military personnel, civil servants, and teachers who had stashed their retirement savings and life savings into the scheme[^鴻源].

> **⚠️ Controversial View**
> Hung Yuan's ringleader, Shen Chang-sheng, was sentenced to seven years in prison plus a NT$3 million fine, and was released on parole in 1994[^鴻源wiki]. "Seven years in prison for 160,000 people's retirement savings" remains the most painful scar in Taiwan's financial regulatory history. When Shen died suddenly in Xiamen in 2022, the public still questioned where the money for his lavish post-prison lifestyle had come from[^沈長聲].

The aftershocks of this double blow lasted an entire decade. Throughout the 1990s, "stocks" was a dirty word in many Taiwanese households. Parents warned their children: don't touch stocks, that's gambling.

## Foreign Capital, ETFs, and TSMC's Gravitational Field

The new-century Taiwan stock market was reshaped by three things.

The first was foreign capital. In the early 1990s, foreign ownership was under 5%. In the 2000s, as the government relaxed restrictions and introduced the QFII system, it gradually rose. Institutional investors replaced retail traders as the dominant force, and the trading logic shifted from "listening to hot tips" to "reading financial statements"[^技術新報].

The second was ETFs. In June 2003, Yuanta Financial Holdings launched 0050 (Yuanta Taiwan 50), Taiwan's first ETF. It did one simple thing: it allowed people who didn't understand individual stocks to participate in the collective growth of Taiwan's fifty largest companies. Twenty years later, 0050's beneficiary count exceeded 2.74 million, and together with the later high-dividend ETF cohort, ETFs became the default household wealth management tool in Taiwan[^ETF].

The third—bigger than the first two combined—was TSMC.

Any discussion of the 2026 Taiwan stock market must address [Taiwan Enterprise: TSMC](/en/economy/tsmc-taiwan-semiconductor/). The Taiwan stock market is like a solar system, and TSMC is the sun. **As of March 10, 2026, TSMC's weight in the TAIEX reached 45.0041%**[^TWSE權重]. With its share price around NT$2,265, every NT$1 move in this single stock moves the broader index by approximately 8 points[^TWSE權重].

Describing this proportion as "a significant constituent" would be a serious understatement. It has become the market itself.

## The New Year's Red Envelope of January 5, 2026

January 5, 2026—the second trading day of the Year of the Horse. The TAIEX gapped up at the open, touched 30,339.32 intraday, and closed at 30,105.04, officially entering the 30,000-point era[^三萬點]. That day, TSMC rose 5.36%, touching NT$1,695 intraday, and this single stock contributed roughly 680 points to the index. In just two days, Taiwan's total market cap grew by NT$3.7 trillion, translating to an average paper gain of NT$268,000 per investor[^三萬點].

Behind this 30,000-point milestone lay a compressed timeline: after the 10,000-point bubble of 1990, it took the Taiwan stock market nearly thirty years to reclaim 10,000 points; from 10,000 to 20,000 took seven years; from 20,000 to 30,000, only two.

> **✦** "That 10,000-point level thirty years ago was a bubble piled up by 4.6 million accounts rushing in and out. This 30,000-point level thirty-six years later is a ceiling propped up by 13.93 million accounts sitting on ETFs, plus a single TSMC. The index is numbers in both cases, but the capital structure is completely different."

By April 2026, that momentum had not yet ended.

## April's 22.7% Single-Month Surge

April was a颠覆性 month for the Taiwan stock market.

Around April 15, Taiwan's total stock market cap surged to US$4.14 trillion, overtaking the UK (US$3.99 trillion) to become the world's 7th largest stock market[^超英]. During the same period, South Korea's KOSPI also surpassed the UK at US$4.04 trillion, rising to 8th—within a single month, two East Asian semiconductor economies had simultaneously overtaken a G7 veteran market[^韓國].

On April 24, the Financial Supervisory Commission officially finalized the "TSMC Clause": the cap on a fund's holding of a single stock was relaxed from 10% to 25%. The next day, TSMC's share price gapped up to a new all-time high[^台積電條款].

At 9:25 a.m. on April 27, the TAIEX jumped to 40,194.92 points—the first time in history it touched 40,000. Although the index gave back some gains by the close, settling at 39,616.63, trading volume surged to NT$1.18 trillion[^taipeitimes]. Adam Lin, an analyst at摩尔投顧 (Moore Investment Advisory), told the _Taipei Times_: "TSMC was the major driver of the Taiex's gains today." He added that he was not worried about the index's ability to hold—"I expect TSMC will continue to move higher on its sound fundamentals." (Quoted from [Taipei Times 2026-04-28](https://www.taipeitimes.com/News/biz/archives/2026/04/28/2003856343)).

The day after the 40,000-point milestone (April 28), Taiwan's total stock market cap rose to US$4.47 trillion, overtaking Canada (US$4.44 trillion) to claim 6th place globally[^bloomberg]. For the entire month of April, the TAIEX accumulated a gain of 22.7%, rising 7,200 points—one of the most ferocious single-month rallies in the Taiwan stock market's history[^biggo2].

There is a detail in this list worth pausing over: the top five are traditional heavyweight financial economies; Taiwan ranks 6th, followed by two G7 members. An island three hundred times smaller than Canada and with 40% fewer people than the UK has outpaced them on the scale of capital markets.

J.P. Morgan Asset Management wrote bluntly in its 2026 Taiwan stock market outlook: "The AI arms race is driving capital expenditure, fueling Taiwan's export and semiconductor growth," "The Taiwan stock market has risen over 90% in nearly 3 years," "The P/E ratio has risen from 12.4x to 17.7x," and "Tech stocks account for 70%–75% of Taiwan's market cap"[^JPM].

Then it wrote a very measured sentence: "Industry concentration and valuation expansion may add volatility, making active stock-picking ability a critical factor."

The meaning of this sentence only becomes clear in the next section.

## The 44% Watermelon Effect

On April 28, 2026, Taiwan's stock market cap was US$4.47 trillion; TSMC's market cap was US$1.8 trillion, with a TAIEX weight approaching 45%[^bloomberg][^TSMC].

To translate this ratio into a more vivid picture: among over 1,000 listed companies, the top one consumes 45% on its own; the remaining 1,000-plus split the remaining 55% among themselves.

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> One scholar described it to the media in April 2026: "TSMC is like a giant watermelon that has sucked all the nutrients away from the other watermelons." (Paraphrased from [旺得富, 2026-04-25](https://wantrich.chinatimes.com/news/20260425900045-420101)) This metaphor has a formal name in economics: "Dutch Disease"—the over-dominance of a single industry siphoning talent, capital, and land from the entire economy, eventually squeezing even traditional industries and services to the margins[^荷蘭病]. The more dazzling Taiwan's AI dividend becomes, the more locked-in this structure grows.

This 45% figure sits alongside several other numbers.

In January 2026, Goldman Sachs released its "2026 Investment Outlook: 10 Key Investment Trends" report, led by Zhang Bokai, Head of Greater China Technology Research, identifying 25 buy-rated stocks in the Taiwan market: TSMC (2330), ASE Technology Holding (3711), Foxconn (2317), Wistron (3231), Wiwynn (6669), Auras Technology (3017), Chaun-Choung Technology (3324), Delta Electronics (2308), Taiwan Union Optronics (2383), Largan Precision (3008), King Slide (2059), Chenbro (8210), MiTAC (3706), SZS (3376), Taiwan Union Opto-Electronic (6274), Kinpo Electronics (2368), Nan Ya PCB (8046), and others[^高盛].

The investment themes across the entire list are remarkably consistent: semiconductors, AI servers, optical transceiver modules, thermal modules, ODMs, PCs, smartphones, PCBs, L4 autonomous vehicles, and low-earth-orbit satellites[^高盛]. Not a single financial stock, not a single consumer stock, not a single traditional industry leader.

In other words, when Goldman Sachs strategists looked at Taiwan's US$4.47 trillion market cap and asked "where to place the next bet," their answer was: double down on the same horse.

The performance of active ETFs points in the same direction. As of April 29, the Nomura Hongyun Fund had a year-to-date return of 89.58%, Yuanta DuoDuo 77.56%, Uni-President Heima 75.30%, and Franklin Templeton First Rich 75.09%—all more than doubling the TAIEX's 36.45% return[^主動]. The common thread across these funds: heavy allocations to AI semiconductors, thermal solutions, power supplies, and high-speed transmission.

Throughout April, foreign investors' single-month net buying of Taiwan stocks at one point exceeded US$8.9 billion; on April 21, single-day net buying reached NT$60.153 billion, the 7th largest on record[^外資]. Money flowed in from around the world, and through the single stock of TSMC, it was amplified into the red line of the broader index.

The moment of highest efficiency is also the moment of highest fragility.

## 13.93 Million Accounts and a Single ETF

At the end of February 2026, the cumulative number of Taiwan stock market accounts reached 13.93 million, an increase of approximately 80,000 from January, hitting a new all-time high for the consecutive period[^開戶]. On an island with a total population of 23.4 million, nearly 60% of people have a securities account.

What deserves even closer attention is the structure: investors under 30 account for roughly 60% of new accounts, and the 20–30 age group exceeds 1.7 million. Day trading, fractional shares, regular installment investing, and ETF subscriptions are the everyday tools of this generation—they barely know the word "ticker tape."

From "stocks are gambling" in the 1990s to "not investing is the risk" in the 2020s, Taiwanese society's attitude toward capital markets flipped over thirty years.

| 1990                                                   | 2026                                             |
| ------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------ |
| 4.6 million active accounts                            | 13.93 million cumulative accounts                |
| Retail investors accounted for 90% of trading volume   | Institutions + ETFs continue to grow             |
| Brokerage phone orders, ticker tapes                   | Swiping phones, regular installment investing    |
| Hung Yuan's monthly 4% interest rate absorbing capital | 0050 with annualized 5%–8% returns               |
| One company's collapse destroying 160,000 people       | A single stock determining the market's position |

The left side of this table holds traumatic memories; the right side holds the AI dividend. Thirty-six years separate them—but the line between them was not a straight climb upward.

## The Same Index, Two Perspectives

On February 9, 1962, the Taiwan Stock Exchange opened for business, with only a dozen-odd listed companies on its debut[^TWSE]. Sixty-four years later, on April 28, 2026, the TAIEX hovered within reach of 40,000 points, and total market cap rose to 6th largest in the world.

Written as a press release, these sixty-four years look glorious: from the industrial era of cement, paper, agriculture, forestry, and mining, to the knowledge economy era of wafer fabrication, AI thermal management, and optical communications. Taiwan's capital market spent a generation growing from a village grocery store into the world's 6th largest stock exchange.

But this 6th-place ranking in 2026 carries a structural paradox.

Of Taiwan's US$4.47 trillion market cap, TSMC alone consumes nearly 45%. If TSMC were removed from the TAIEX, the remaining 1,000-plus stocks, totaling roughly US$2.67 trillion, would likely fall to around 10th globally, in the same league as Saudi Arabia. In the world of capital markets, the relationship between the identity of "6th largest in the world" and "an island" is thinner than one might imagine.

> **✦** "On April 28, 2026, the Bloomberg terminal in New York flashed 'Taiwan Overtakes Canada.' That same night, a Taipei taxi driver waited by the radio for the next financial segment. He didn't care which G7 country Taiwan had surpassed—he cared about whether TSMC would close above 2,300 the next day. Bloomberg's headline wrote of 'an island's victory'; the driver cared about 'a single stock's level.' The same event, two perspectives—and the latter may be closer to the truth than the former."

The 1990 10,000-point crash destroyed a generation's trust in the stock market. The 2026 bull run that charged past 40,000 points and overtook Canada rebuilt that same trust—only this time, the object of trust shifted from "the hot tips I picked myself" to "that one TSMC the whole world is buying."

This summit, shared by 13.93 million accounts, has a foundation that is 45% a single rock. The rock is hard—but there is only one of it.

What will Taiwan's capital market learn in the next thirty years? Ask your neighbor who bought the dip three times in 1990—he'll probably tell you: the most dangerous moment in the market is precisely when everyone thinks it can't go down.
