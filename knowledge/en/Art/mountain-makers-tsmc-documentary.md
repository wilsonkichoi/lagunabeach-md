# Mountain Makers: A Century's Gamble — A Taiwanese Semiconductor Epic That Had to Sand One Word Off Its Title Before It Could Be Spoken

> **30-second overview:** Released on June 13, 2025, _A Chip Odyssey_ uses five years, more than 80 interviewees, and a production budget in the tens of millions of NT dollars to tell the story of Taiwan's semiconductor industry, from the 1974 breakfast meeting at Xiao Xinxin Soy Milk Shop to C. C. Wei walking into the White House in 2025[^1]. But its original title was _Silicon Shield_ -- later changed on the advice that Taiwanese semiconductor companies with factories in China did not want to be imagined by the other side as a shield[^2]. This epic, which declares the irreplaceability of Taiwan's semiconductor industry, had to sand down even the sharpest word in its title first; "silicon shield" therefore retreated into a single line of narration, and the title became _A Chip Odyssey_.

## The Masked Man in the Last Row of a Xinyi District Cinema

On the evening of June 26, 2025, at a cinema in Taipei's Xinyi District, an elderly couple wearing masks bought their own tickets, entered the theater, and chose seats in the last row. No one noticed them until the lights came up at the end of the film[^3].

Ninety-four-year-old Morris Chang slowly walked down the aisle, raising both masked hands high to wave at the audience[^4]. Scattered applause began, then became applause from the whole theater; he stood in front of the first row and acknowledged everyone[^4]. Hsiao Chu-chen was there. She later described herself as "nervous and excited, and of course a little disappointed too"[^5]. The disappointment came from two things: the founder of TSMC did not have a single head-on interview shot in the film; he chose to come before this epic about the company he had founded as an audience member, not as an interview subject.

> **Curator's Note**
>
> The most moving moments of a text often happen outside the text. There is no Morris Chang interview on the screen in _A Chip Odyssey_ -- but the film's real climax is the founder himself standing up from the last row of the cinema when the end lights come on. The text overflows the screen; the absent protagonist stitches himself back in as an "audience member."

Hsiao Chu-chen once wrote on Facebook about why there was no Morris Chang interview: "after a year or two of effort, he was busy writing his book," and "TSMC's restrictions on filming were, of course, layers of protection"[^6]. "Busy writing his book" refers to the second volume of the autobiography he spent seven years writing: covering 1964 to 2018, nearly 200,000 Chinese characters, published by CommonWealth Publishing on November 29, 2024[^7], exactly 26 years after the first volume in 1998. The second volume had already been published by the end of 2024, but _A Chip Odyssey_ was not released until the following June. In other words, he really was writing throughout the filming period; but by the time he was finally free to watch the film, it had already been edited, and the multi-camera pickup profile shots had already been placed in it. Hsiao used the technique of "multi-camera pickup shooting" to work around the absence of a head-on interview: at public events, she repeatedly set up cameras to capture Chang from the side, cutting footage of his appearances into the film so that he exists in it as someone "caught on camera" rather than "interviewed"[^4].

## The Word That Was Removed

"Silicon Shield" was originally the film's title.

Hsiao Chu-chen first submitted the sponsorship proposal under the title _Silicon Shield_. After receiving the plan, the TSMC Education and Culture Foundation advised avoiding the term[^8] -- the reason being that no Taiwanese semiconductor company with factories in China would want to be imagined by the other side as a "shield"[^2]. Hsiao accepted. The title became _A Chip Odyssey_ in English and _Mountain Makers_ in Chinese; "silicon shield" retreated into a line of narration and a background frame within the film.

> **Contested View**
>
> There was more than one voice within the same foundation opposing "silicon shield." Board member Chen Chien-pang put it more sharply in public: "I suggest that Taiwanese people stop using the term silicon shield," and "if people think economic stability can guarantee national security, just look at Ukraine and you will know that this statement does not hold"[^9]. Chen was speaking about whether Taiwanese people should continue using the term at all; it had nothing to do with the film title. He used the metaphor of "branching incense from a temple" to describe TSMC building fabs in the United States. The name change therefore cannot be read so simply as "being silenced"; closer to the truth is that TSMC's side actively leaned toward removing the implication of a "shield." The act of weighing the term is itself where the contradiction lies.

The common reading treats this as self-censorship, but that reading comes too easily. So easily that it reverses cause and effect: censorship is usually "you are not allowed to say it," while this case was "think clearly first about what will happen if you say it." The former is suppression; the latter is political judgment -- when a country boasts of its strongest strategic card, it first weighs how the other side will hear it. The term "silicon shield" did not disappear from the film: it remains in narration and positioning within the film, and FPRI's Philadelphia screening and TECO circulars still introduced the film with "Silicon Shield"[^10]. Only the title layer changed -- that is, the first thing viewers see on ticketing websites, posters, and subtitles.

The three Chinese characters for "mountain makers" therefore took on a new function: they shifted the focus from "who are we defending against" to "what are we doing." It was a different way of saying the same thing, with lower political costs. The act of weighing words is itself a miniature of Taiwan's condition: you do not need to be banned; you learn on your own to think once before every act of speech.

## Pulling the Camera Away From TSMC

If removing "silicon shield" was subtraction, "mountain makers" was addition.

Hsiao Chu-chen interviewed more than 80 industry elders over five years[^11], deliberately pulling the camera away from TSMC. The film's "first male lead" on screen is former ITRI president Shih Chin-tay -- one of the RCA seed team, later director of ITRI's demonstration fab, which produced Taiwan's first commercial IC chip, CIC0001[^12]. Hsiao described this structural choice herself: "You created history; I will preserve history"[^13].

> **-** "The film's core narrative unfolds by following the steps of former ITRI president Shih Chin-tay. As a key member of the RCA project and later a leader at ITRI, his own experience is a microcosm of Taiwan's semiconductor industry."[^14]

Alongside him are MediaTek's Tsai Ming-kai, UMC's Robert Tsao, TSMC's F. C. Tseng, Macronix's Miin Wu, Lin Ben-chien, Chiang Shang-yi, Simon Sze, RCA's Yang Ting-yuan -- a long chain of names across different companies, generations, and segments of the industry[^15]. There are also unnamed first-generation women operators: "I was terrified of dropping and breaking a wafer, because even several months' salary might not be enough to pay for it," and "I bought my first car by selling eight shares of UMC stock"[^16]. They are seen before the camera, but they are not given names.

Shih Chin-tay himself says in the film, "I was very moved. I kept thinking, don't cry"[^17]. At the Hsinchu premiere in May, F. C. Tseng said something different: "In the past, as long as you worked hard, you would definitely succeed. What worries me is how we can continue progressing in the future while balancing a stable environment"[^18]. The tone of the two lines is not quite the same: one looks back; the other warns. Hsiao places herself between the two -- "I did not want to make a film about 'success.' Instead, I hoped everyone would see adversity, and see the courage born of adversity, the courage to face hardship"[^19].

"Mountain makers" is therefore deliberately treated as plural. It is a whole layer of decentralized tribute.

But the media spotlight is unruly. Hsiao Bi-khim spoke after seeing the film in June; Tsai Ing-wen entered the cinema in August after the box office passed NT$25 million[^20]; Morris Chang himself paid for a ticket on June 26[^3] -- these images quickly contracted the focus back to Morris Chang alone. The title tells you there are many mountains and many mountain makers, yet the gravity of hero worship outside the frame keeps pulling the mountains in the same direction. The film itself is aware of this pull.

## Not Taking One Person's Ten Million

CNEX co-founder Ben Tsiang and Chen Tien-shun, son of Yungtay Engineering founder and executive director of MOXA -- these are the film's two producers[^21]. Chen set a financial discipline for the film: "One person's NT$10 million is not as good as ten people's NT$1 million; the latter brings greater influence and resonance"[^22].

The film cost tens of millions of NT dollars to shoot, and licensing archival footage -- including clips from the Hollywood film _Fatal Attraction_ -- was expensive[^23]. But Hsiao Chu-chen, Chen Tien-shun, and Ben Tsiang reached a consensus: to preserve creative autonomy, production funding would come from multiple donations and sponsorships rather than from a single corporation[^23]. The editor was Chen Po-wen, working with the director for the fourth time; the score was by Lim Giong, the musician who left a sonic coordinate for Taiwanese cinema through the electronic scores of _Millennium Mambo_ and _Goodbye, Dragon Inn_[^24].

> **Did You Know**
>
> The film did not originate from any official commission. In 2019, Ben Tsiang and Hsiao Chu-chen attended the memorial service for Hu Ding-hua, the overall director of the 1976 RCA technology transfer project, and confirmed the subject on the way back[^25]. Hu did not live to see the film begin production; when it was released five years later, one of the top-five box-office records in Taiwan documentary history bore the mark of the decision planted at that memorial service.

But the discipline of independent creation has its own boundaries. In a June speech, Foreign Minister Lin Chia-lung publicly pledged that he was "willing to assist in producing multilingual versions so that overseas offices can promote it"[^26]. The overseas tour has largely followed the axis of Taiwan's overseas offices (TECO) and TSMC fab sites: the FPRI screening in Philadelphia was sponsored by TECO NY and offered an English-dubbed version[^10]; the UCLA screening was co-organized by TECO LA, and the event description directly said it would provide "a deeper understanding of the stories and spirit behind Taiwan's silicon shield and sacred mountain protecting the nation"[^27]; at the May 21 screening at the Atlanta-Fulton Public Library, TECO director-general Lin Chu-en, Fulton County Chairman Robert Pitts, and Georgia State Representative Karen Lupton appeared together for the post-screening discussion[^28].

Independent funding, diplomatic channels for the tour -- no one has directly named this tension as criticism, but the tension is there. A documentary that deliberately refused funding from a single corporation ultimately found screening channels through the network of overseas offices; a funding structure dispersed for creative autonomy ultimately still caught the hand naturally extended by the state apparatus. These two facts do not contradict each other, but both are true at once. Hsiao responded this way to another kind of pressure: some people questioned whether she had taken money to make a propaganda film; others labeled the film as soon as political figures appeared in it[^29]. Her answer was: "But I can only return to history and take responsibility for my own viewpoint. As long as there are people who gave and contributed in history, we will not cover them up or keep them from appearing for any reason"[^29].

## From Nanyang Street to Arizona

The film's chronology begins in February 1974.

At No. 40 Nanyang Street in Taipei, a seven-person breakfast meeting took place at Xiao Xinxin Soy Milk Shop[^30] -- Vice Premier Fei Hua, Minister of Economic Affairs Sun Yun-suan, Minister of Transportation and Communications Kao Yu-shu, ITRI president Wang Chao-chen, Telecommunications Research Institute director Fang Hsien-chi and deputy director Kang Pao-huang, and RCA research director Pan Wen-yuan[^31]. Hsiao Chu-chen spent more than seven months looking for this location: "Honestly, once I saw people, I was not afraid anymore"; "(I searched for) more than seven months, and it was condensed into these six or seven minutes in the film"; "Of course I felt stubborn and angry. I thought Taiwan was ridiculous. It has only been 50 years -- we were not even looking for a 200-year-old ruin!"[^32]

Pan Wen-yuan then secluded himself for two weeks in Room 508 of the Grand Hotel and wrote the "ten-thousand-character memorandum" that promoted the semiconductor industry[^33]. Hsiao rented the same room to film that two-week "historical site"[^33]. By using the words "historical site," she was speaking of an archaeological attitude toward time -- for her, this country's contemporary history is like a trace that has been stepped thin, and one must squat down and look closely while the people are still here.

> **-** Fifty years of industrial history is short, and Hsiao Chu-chen's steps in visiting those who lived it are shorter than history. But she knows that the seven months behind those few minutes of footage are both the film's thinnest place and its thickest place.

In 1976, ITRI and RCA of the United States signed an integrated-circuit technology-transfer licensing agreement[^34]. The first 19 people sent to RCA for training were led by Yang Ting-yuan, who served both as overall team leader and New Jersey regional leader; six months later, the ITRI demonstration fab they operated reached a yield rate of 70 percent, far higher than the 50 percent at RCA, the parent plant for the technology transfer[^34]. UMC was established in 1980; Morris Chang returned to Taiwan in 1985 to serve as ITRI president and proposed the VLSI project; in 1987, TSMC was founded with joint investment from the Executive Yuan Development Fund, Philips of the Netherlands, and private investors, pioneering the pure-play foundry model[^35]. In the 1990s, Lin Ben-chien advanced immersion lithography. On March 3, 2025, C. C. Wei walked into the White House and announced at least another US$100 billion in investment in the United States to build five fabs[^1].

This 50-year timeline is not narrated as PowerPoint bullet points. Hsiao lets it pass through concrete scenes: the breakfast meeting at Xiao Xinxin Soy Milk Shop, the ten-thousand-character memorandum in Room 508 at the Grand Hotel, the RCA seed team at the New Jersey plant, Hsinchu Science Park, and aerial shots of the Fab 21 construction site in Arizona. Every stop has a physical location one can see and step into.

## Epic, Hymn, and Born of Adversity

An English review used a very precise adjective: _unapologetically lionizes_[^36].

In its November 2025 review, Cinema Escapist positioned _A Chip Odyssey_ as a visual companion to Chris Miller's _Chip War_; it used the phrases _unapologetically lionizes_ and _all of the individuals featured...are framed as self-sacrificing_ to describe the film's heroizing treatment of Taiwan's semiconductor pioneers and its framing of self-sacrifice[^36]. The review also suggested that non-Taiwanese viewers should read _Chip War_ or listen to Acquired Podcast before watching the film, as that order might make more sense[^36].

The critique is sharp, but there is one piece it does not see.

"Unapologetically lionizes" captures the hymn-like rhythm on the film's surface, but it passes over another axis that Hsiao Chu-chen herself set in advance: "I did not want to make a film about 'success'"[^19]. F. C. Tseng's line -- "In the past, as long as you worked hard, you would definitely succeed. What worries me is how we can continue progressing in the future while balancing a stable environment"[^18] -- the veterans in the film chased for 50 years by Sun Yun-suan's phrase "only success is permitted, failure is not," and the retired women operators afraid that a broken wafer would cost them their salaries[^16]: these are the underside of the hymn, the footprints beneath it. Hsiao describes the film as "not covering things up"[^29], meaning that good and bad both remain in the frame: the political pressure she encountered, the fact that the semiconductor plan back then did not undergo deliberation by public opinion, and the film's retention of concerns about Dutch disease[^37].

Academia has another, sharper track of critique. A 2025 paper, "Taiwan's 'Silicon Shield': Techno-authoritarian territories," uses _techno-authoritarianism_ to describe this system -- _law, transparency and democracy are compromised for national interest_ -- and organizes the costs under the silicon shield into three main axes: environmental degradation, food insecurity, and toxic industrial risks[^38]. The paper does not mention _A Chip Odyssey_; it puts the silicon shield itself on the operating table. Reading this paper alongside the film makes clear the negative space that the screen does not address: labor costs, water use, electricity use, land, and Indigenous peoples.

## From Hsinchu to Dresden

The international tour rolled on through 2026.

On March 17, 2026, there was a free screening at the Taiwan Cultural Center in Tokyo[^39]. On April 9, at the First Members' Office Building of Japan's House of Representatives, Keiji Furuya, chair of the Japan-ROC Diet Members' Consultative Council; Tsai Ming-yaw, deputy representative of Taiwan's office in Japan; legislators Ho Hsin-chun and Kuo Kuo-wen; and more than 50 Japanese legislators and representatives sat in a parliamentary conference hall and watched the film[^40]. Hsiao Chu-chen said in her remarks: "Taiwan-Japan relations are friendly, and Japan is also a very important partner. To be able to screen the film in such an important venue as Japan's parliament and introduce Taiwan's technological development journey through a documentary is highly meaningful"; "this was her first time walking into Japan's parliament, and she felt both happy and excited"; and the work had been screened in "about 15 countries"[^40].

> **Did You Know**
>
> National borders are also one of the film's silent contexts. In the same year it was screened at the First Members' Office Building of Japan's House of Representatives, _A Chip Odyssey_ also entered a municipal library attended by a Georgia state legislator, TU Dresden with remarks by Taiwan's representative to Germany Shieh Jhy-wey, and George Washington University with Taiwan's ambassador to the United States Alexander Yui in attendance -- these venues happen to be the reverse image of Taiwan's map of diplomatic recognition. In the gaps of formal diplomatic relations, the densest tour has grown instead.

On May 20, the Foreign Policy Research Institute (FPRI) in Philadelphia held a screening sponsored by TECO NY, with an English-dubbed version and Shihoko Goto as moderator[^10]. On May 21, the film screened at the central branch of the Atlanta-Fulton Public Library for about 300 people[^28]. On May 23, it screened at George Washington University in Washington, D.C., with Taiwan's ambassador to the United States Alexander Yui in attendance and more than 150 participants[^41]. On May 31, at the Northwest Film Forum at the University of Washington, producer Ben Tsiang took part in a Q&A, hosted by UW Taiwan Studies[^42].

June 6, Dresden, Germany.

This screening had a different meaning. Dresden is the site of ESMC, TSMC's European joint-venture fab with Bosch, Infineon, and NXP. The fab had just topped out in January 2026 and is expected to begin mass production at the end of 2027[^43]. In the venue sat 230 leaders from industry, government, and academia, along with young students, including TU Dresden president Ursula Staudinger, ESMC president Christian Koitzsch, Taiwan's representative to Germany Shieh Jhy-wey, and Silicon Saxony managing director Frank Boesenberg[^44]. Koitzsch said during the post-screening discussion: "Taiwanese society demonstrates a high degree of inclusion and pragmatism, and after establishing a direction, it can swiftly put it into action." "This kind of capacity for action is precisely what Germany, currently constrained by bureaucracy, most needs to learn"[^44].

> **Curator's Note**
>
> A film whose title had to sand away the words "silicon shield" being screened at the site of the ESMC fab -- this is almost a visual metaphor for this country's geopolitical contradiction. Taiwan branches its semiconductor ecosystem into Saxony; the deity sent outward must return to the original temple to join the festival. And the original temple has to be careful even when saying it is a temple.

By then, Hsiao Chu-chen had already taken the film through 15 countries. She had not preset how far this tour would go -- "It screened in Tokyo on March 17, and in May it continued to Dresden," she said. She also offered a more intimate description: "After finishing this film, I also feel that I am a mountain maker. Even if I am a small grain of sand, I am still part of the mountain-making movement"[^45].

## Two Documentaries, Two Channels

On June 13, 2025, Taiwanese cinemas released two documentaries at the same time. One was _A Chip Odyssey_, about semiconductors. The other was _Invisible Nation_, about democracy -- made over seven years by American director Vanessa Hope[^46].

![The Taiwan poster for Invisible Nation, whose main visual shows the snow-covered main peak of Yushan, with Tsai Ing-wen and several Taiwanese women political figures arrayed below the mountain](/article-images/art/invisible-nation-poster.webp)
_The Taiwan poster for *Invisible Nation*, released on the same day as *A Chip Odyssey*. One film uses semiconductors to explain why Taiwan is irreplaceable; the other uses democracy to explain why Taiwan deserves to be seen -- the same country, two narrative axes walking side by side into the cinema. Fair use editorial commentary on *Invisible Nation* (2023). Source: [Swallow Wings Films / invisiblenation.net](https://www.invisiblenation.net/)._

Both ultimately landed around NT$37 million at the box office: _Invisible Nation_ at about NT$37.7 million and _A Chip Odyssey_ at about NT$36 million, both entering the top five in Taiwan documentary box-office history[^47]. Media called them the "two documentary heroes"[^46], but in fact they are two audio channels for the same condition: one explains, through technology, "why Taiwan is irreplaceable"; the other explains, through democracy, "why Taiwan deserves to be seen."

But both must speak through some form of mediation. _A Chip Odyssey_ was made by a Taiwanese director and had to sand one word off its title; _Invisible Nation_ was made by an American director and had to use an outsider's lens to reach the international stage[^46]. The same country has to change its tone when boasting of itself and borrow an outsider's mouth when introducing itself.

After watching the film in June, Hsiao Bi-khim said: "More importantly, this film expounds a kind of belief, a force that supports us in enduring through adversity"[^48]. "Taiwan's survival likewise allows only success, not failure. I believe that in the future, as long as everyone in their own professional field upholds the spirit of mountain making, does not fear difficulty, and continues forward, Taiwan will surely be able to ride the winds and waves of a rapidly changing international situation and stand firm"[^48]. In August, when the box office passed NT$25 million, Tsai Ing-wen brought former vice president Chen Chien-jen and his wife to the theater: "I am very grateful for the making of this film. The director and producers helped us present the things that have happened over these years and the challenges we have faced in a clearly structured way. I want to say thank you to you!"[^49]

Both speeches are dignified, and both fold the film into a national narrative frame. But the position the film itself chooses is actually much humbler than the speeches -- Hsiao Chu-chen once said: "After finishing this film, I also feel that I am a mountain maker. Even if I am a small grain of sand, I am still part of the mountain-making movement"[^45].

## The Mountain Maker Is Not Really Present

The story returns to the evening of June 26, 2025, in a cinema in Xinyi District.

Ninety-four-year-old Morris Chang and his wife sat in the last row and watched the entire film. When the end lights came on, he slowly walked down the aisle wearing a mask, raised both hands high, waved to the audience, and acknowledged everyone[^4]. Hsiao Chu-chen sat in the theater -- across five years, she had never managed to conduct a head-on interview with this core figure among the "mountain makers"; in the end, this core figure came before the film himself as an "audience member."

> **-** What the epic did not capture, reality supplied.

The film's tension therefore circles back to its starting point: the person who was not interviewed, the word removed from the title, and the money deliberately dispersed -- these are the film's real spine. For a Taiwanese semiconductor epic, it is easy to say, "we built a mountain." What it truly says, the harder thing to put into words, is that while everyone is looking at this mountain, this country's mountain makers have learned how to leave history for themselves somewhere between what can be said and what cannot.

The box-office numbers that burned for a year, the overseas screenings one after another, the speeches by the vice president and former president, the praise from ESMC's president -- all of these will dissipate. But the figure who stood up from the last row that evening will remain. He did not walk onto the screen; he walked out from the audience.

From the moment this country learned, when boasting of itself, to first weigh how the other side would hear it, every mountain maker had already become an audience member -- watching how the mountain they built is spoken of by others, heard by others, and entered by others with a ticket in hand.
