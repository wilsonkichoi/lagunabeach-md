# Sunset Rollercoaster: Fourteen Years from a Photo Booth Background to Coachella, Singing Taipei Sunsets in English

> **30-second overview:** Sunset Rollercoaster is an independent band formed in Taipei in 2009. They did something few in Taiwan's indie scene had done: they sang subtropical Taipei entirely in English. After releasing their debut album _Bossa Nova_ in 2011, the band disbanded. In 2015, vocalist Kuo Kuo regrouped the band after being exempted from military service because of 1,300-degree myopia and finishing his stint as guitarist in Deserts Chang's Algae band. "My Jinji," from the 2016 EP _JinJi Kikko_, surpassed 100 million streams on Spotify[^1]; in 2018 they became the first Taiwanese band to appear on Audiotree Live in the United States; in 2023 they became the first Taiwanese band in more than two decades to be invited onto a major stage at the Coachella Valley Music and Arts Festival[^2]; in 2024, _AAA_, their collaboration with Korean band Hyukoh, received nine nominations at the 36th Golden Melody Awards and took home three awards. A casually selected Photo Booth background became, fourteen years later, the beam of light through which Taiwanese music was most widely heard by the world.

In Taipei in 2010, a 19-year-old student sat in front of a MacBook. He had just opened a MySpace band page and needed a profile pic. Not knowing what to photograph, he opened the Mac's built-in Photo Booth. Among the presets was a background image: a roller-coaster silhouette at sunset. He picked that one.

"That's how the 'Rollercoaster' came about," Kuo Kuo (Tseng Kuo-hung) said in an interview eight years later.[^3]

That casually chosen background later became everything about the band: its name, the cover of its first mini EP, the temperament of its entire musical universe. It had never been calculated to represent anything. But fourteen years later, it came to represent something no one could have predicted then. The path by which Taiwanese music reached the world did not come from the K-pop industrial system, nor from government cultural diplomacy. The whole thing began with a 19-year-old guy randomly choosing a picture in Photo Booth on his MacBook.

## That Photo Booth Preset

Sunset Rollercoaster did not appear out of nowhere.

Before forming Sunset Rollercoaster, Kuo Kuo had already been involved in several bands. The earliest was Acid Lips, a post-punk band from his high-school years. Later came F.L.A.T CLUB[^4], Boyz & Girl, and, around 2010, Come on! BayBay!, which was recording an album at the same time. Come on! BayBay!'s drummer then was "Birdman" Huang Shih-wei, and among its members was someone named Chen Hung-li, who would later return to Sunset Rollercoaster as bassist.[^5]

In 2009, Kuo Kuo formed Sunset Rollercoaster with Kevin Lee (Lee Po-hao, then a medical student at Taipei Medical University) and drummer Lo Tsun-lung. Their first show was on January 29 at Underworld on Shida Road.[^6] That grubby little bar, later demolished amid urban renewal, was a temple-front gathering place of Taipei's indie scene in the 2000s.

The band name came afterward. Kuo Kuo's version in an interview:

> "Myspace needed a profile pic and I didn't know what to shoot, so I used a preset in Apple computer photo booth called Sunset Rollercoaster... That's how the 'Rollercoaster' came about."[^3]

The anti-industrial nature of this origin story later defined the band's temperament for fourteen years. No brand consultant, no naming meeting, no SEO consideration. A default Photo Booth image later became the words on the backdrop of a Coachella desert stage.

> 📝 **Curator's note:** The common narrative of "indie going international" tends to attribute every turning point to a band making the correct strategic decision. Sunset Rollercoaster's story runs in the opposite direction: all the key moments were uncalculated choices in the present. The Photo Booth image was just an idle pick one afternoon in a dorm room; the choice to sing entirely in English was, in Kuo Kuo's own words, because it "felt less incongruous"; Coachella came because the organizers sent the invitation themselves. The Taiwanese band most heard by the world is the one that least resembles something meticulously designed.

## Abbey Road Mastering, Then Nothing

On September 22, 2011, Sunset Rollercoaster self-released their first album, _Bossa Nova_.[^7]

The album title came from bossa nova, but the music itself was closer to a mixture of lo-fi surf rock and post-punk. It was recorded at Rooftop Audio in Taipei, then sent to Abbey Road Studios in London for mastering by Steve Rooke himself.[^8] A first album made by a group of university students through rough-and-ready methods had its final step completed in the same studio where The Beatles recorded _Abbey Road_.

Then the band broke up.

Fans thought this was simply a small Taipei band that never took off: one album, then disappearance. At the end of 2012, Kuo Kuo joined Anpu's Algae band as guitarist, replacing the original member Sente, and toured with them for four years.[^9] Drummer Lo Tsun-lung went off to form a dark industrial side project called Fossil with Kuo Kuo. The band's official website went without updates for four years.

"We wanted to try something else," Kuo Kuo later said in an interview with Bandcamp Daily.[^10]

This four-year silence is the essential contrast behind the later "Coachella myth." Sunset Rollercoaster's story is not an inspirational template of "indie from zero to overnight fame"; it is the story of "a group of people doing completely different things for four years, then returning and somehow growing into their real form."

## The 1,300-Degree Myopia Notice

One afternoon in 2015, Kuo Kuo received a conscription notice.

He had originally been about to enter military service. But one month before enlistment, the Ministry of National Defense sent an exemption notice. The reason was his 1,300-degree myopia. A fate that would have had him shave his head and put on a uniform within a month was flipped by an optometry record.

That year Kuo Kuo was 24. He had just finished touring with Algae. Sunset Rollercoaster had been disbanded for four years, and he had no full-time job. With the stage experience accumulated over four years performing alongside Anpu, plus a circle of old friends from his alma mater's theater scene who could play synthesizer, saxophone, and clarinet, Kuo Kuo suddenly realized he could pull all these lines in the same direction.

"He decided to really treat the band as something serious," he later recalled.[^11]

On July 1, 2015, Sunset Rollercoaster held their first comeback show at The Wall in Taipei.[^12] Of the original trio (Kuo Kuo, Kevin, Tsun-lung), two remained. Kevin did not return, and neither did Birdman. The new members were old Come on! BayBay! bandmate Chen Hung-li (bass and synthesizer), Wang Shao-hsuan (keyboard), and Huang Hao-ting (saxophone). The five-piece setup brought in horns, synthesizers, and a funky soft-rock texture no one in Taiwan's indie scene had really played before.

That year, they began making the three songs that would later become _Jinji Kikko_.

> 💡 **Did you know:** During Kuo Kuo's four years as guitarist in Algae, he performed onstage with Anpu (Deserts Chang) more than 100 times. Since Sunset Rollercoaster regrouped, Anpu and Kuo Kuo have not formally collaborated on any song. Both are among Taiwan's most iconic indie creators, and they were once colleagues in the same band, but after stepping out of it, they went in completely different directions.

## Jinji Is Not Kumquat

On March 1, 2016, the _JinJi Kikko_ EP went online.[^13] Three songs: "Burgundy Red," "My Jinji," and "New Drug."

The first to break out was "My Jinji." In 2017, Spotify's algorithm began placing the song in Discover Weekly and in Indonesian and Korean Indie/City Pop playlists. Within a year, the song had more monthly listeners in Indonesia than in Taiwan.[^14] NME later reported that Jakarta, Indonesia, was the city with the largest number of monthly Spotify listeners for Sunset Rollercoaster worldwide.[^15]

The word "Jinji" led many people to assume it meant "jinju," or kumquat. The band has never explained it. But according to Kuo Kuo's interviews, the sound is actually close to "Dindi" in the Portuguese-language bossa nova classic "Dindi," meaning "dear" or "baby."[^16] The three songs have a strange resonance with Antonio Carlos Jobim's 1960s Brazilian jazz, while also growing out of 2010s Taipei.

The first major overseas performance after the EP went online was SummerStage Taiwanese Waves in New York's Central Park on July 16, 2016.[^17] Sunset Rollercoaster performed on the same bill as Anpu and Won Fu. It was the first time Taiwanese bands appeared at Central Park's SummerStage as an organized lineup, with three acts on the same bill.

Then messages from international listeners began arriving.

"I think Taipei has a kind of flatter feeling. Everyone is very similar... The whole city provides a feeling that is somewhat comfortable, but where you can never break through the predicament," Kuo Kuo once told The Reporter's Chiang Yi-ting.[^18] Yet this flat city, sung by him entirely in English, allowed listeners around the world to hear a Taipei they had assumed was flat but was in fact deeply complex.

> 📝 **Curator's note:** Why choose English? Kuo Kuo's answer is more counterintuitive than people tend to imagine. "When you're a band that sings English songs, the audience who already listens to Western music finds it least incongruous to absorb. The third thing is, if it's English, your customer base is the whole world."[^19] The sentence sounds more like a producer's cool calculation of audience scale than a declaration of ambition to make "international" music. Most of Taiwan's indie scene chooses Taiwanese, Mandarin, Hakka, or Indigenous languages; behind each language is an answer to "Who am I?" Sunset Rollercoaster's answer is: I want to be heard by the whole world, and English is the greatest common denominator. The honesty of that calculation cannot be filed in the same drawer as the English-language turn of the K-pop industrial system.

## The Five Audiotree Songs

September 26, 2018, Chicago.

Sunset Rollercoaster entered the Audiotree Live recording room and recorded a full five-song session: "Greedy," "Summum Bonum," "New Drug," "My Jinji," and "I Know You Know I Love You." It went online on October 18.[^20] Audiotree is one of the most important in-studio session platforms in the American independent music scene, inviting only a selected number of bands each year to record full sessions. Sunset Rollercoaster became the first Taiwanese band to be invited.

Audiotree's recording format is a trial. A small room, very little post-production, live takes uploaded directly: every pitch slip, pause, and rhythmic wobble can be seen. The aesthetic position of this format is: show me you can do this in the room, not the layered version from the studio. What Sunset Rollercoaster delivered that day was forty minutes of synthesizer, saxophone, bass, and drums all breathing alive in the same space. The live version of "Summum Bonum" has a saxophone solo looser than the studio version; the synthesizer tone of "New Drug" carries the recording room's own fine reverb. Within a year after the session went online, it accumulated millions of views, becoming the first major gateway through which Sunset Rollercoaster's international listenership grew at scale.

That March, they released their second full-length album, _Cassa Nova_.[^21] Nine songs, turning completely away from the lo-fi surf rock of _Bossa Nova_ toward City Pop led by disco, funk, and synthesizers. The album's whole temperament feels like a subtropical afternoon in the 1970s Japan of Tatsuro Yamashita and Taeko Onuki, but the synthesizer tones carry a late-2010s vaporwave coating.

Across 2018, they played more than 100 shows, from Tokyo, Seoul, Shanghai, Hong Kong, and Jakarta to Los Angeles, New York, and Mexico City.[^22]

One of them was on a stage converted from a shopping-mall parking lot in Jakarta, Indonesia. The audience packed in so tightly that the stage shook, and the performance ended early. The show was later recorded in The Reporter's long-form article as the moment when subtropical City Pop first truly detonated among subtropical audiences in the subtropics.[^23]

That year, BIOS Monthly gave them a phrase: "crossing the seas to drink and compete."[^24] In the interview, Kuo Kuo was very clear about his situation: the Mandarin-language market was too small to sustain an all-English band, so they had to go abroad to find audiences; but the cost of touring abroad was high enough to deter most independent bands. Sunset Rollercoaster grew its own path between these two pressures: connecting as directly as possible with overseas promoters, avoiding Taiwanese management-company intermediaries, keeping stage setups as simple as possible, and packing only essential synthesizers. The phrase "crossing the seas" was later repeatedly quoted in the Asian indie scene, describing how a band turned "having no industrial backing" into a functioning business model. Geographic movement was only the surface; the real core was a redefinition of commercial rhythm.

## The Sonora Stage in the Desert

On July 27, 2019, they appeared on the Red Marquee stage at Japan's Fuji Rock Festival.[^25] It is one of Asia's largest rock festivals, and Red Marquee is its second-largest stage.

On October 30, 2020, they released their third album, _Soft Storm_.[^26] During a 2019 tour in Los Angeles, Kuo Kuo unexpectedly met an idol he had admired since adolescence: Ned Doheny, the American 1970s session musician who had written songs for the Eagles and Linda Ronstadt. Kuo Kuo invited Ned on the spot to fly to Taipei and collaborate. Ned really came, and wrote "Overlove" with Sunset Rollercoaster. Then COVID-19 broke out, and follow-up recording plans in Los Angeles were interrupted.[^27]

After _Soft Storm_ went online, NME ranked it fourth on its list of the best Asian albums of 2020. Daniel Peters wrote:

> "It's a left-turn by a band fully confident in their strengths, looking inward to focus on craft and instinct."[^28]
>
> (A left turn by a band already fully confident in its strengths, turning its gaze inward to focus on craft and instinct.)

On August 21, 2021, they won Best Band at the 32nd Golden Melody Awards for _Soft Storm_. The jury's comment: "It breaks the aesthetic barriers of Mandarin-language music, is not only technically mature, but has also influenced many young bands. It has internationality and a sense of the future."[^29]

Two years later, on January 11, 2023, Coachella announced its full 2023 lineup. Sunset Rollercoaster's name appeared on the Sonora stage. The headline of Blow StreetVoice's report that day read:

> "For the first time in more than two decades since the Coachella festival began, a Taiwanese band has been invited onto a major stage."[^30]

On April 15, 2023, at 7:55 p.m. Pacific Daylight Time, at the Empire Polo Club in Indio, California, Sunset Rollercoaster took the Sonora stage.[^31] The nine-song setlist opened with Shostakovich's Waltz No. 2 (an homage to _Eyes Wide Shut_), followed by "Vanilla Villa," "Burgundy Red," "My Jinji," "Summum Bonum," "Jellyfish," "I'm a Fish," and "Candlelight" as the closer.

"Hello, we are Sunset Rollercoaster from Taiwan. We're very happy to be with everyone tonight," Kuo Kuo said in his opening remarks.[^32]

The Sonora stage is one of Coachella's smaller indoor stages, with a capacity of about 5,000, but that day the crowd spilled beyond the tent. Most European and American fans belonged to the generation that had discovered "My Jinji" through Spotify Discover Weekly, and for the first time they had the chance to see that the people who sang the song were Asian, and specifically Taiwanese, not Japanese or Korean. A few audience members held up black T-shirts printed with SUNSET ROLLERCOASTER. Several Taiwanese students had driven across state lines to see the performance. Taipei's guidebooks, California's desert, subtropical City Pop: three lines that should never have intersected layered into one afternoon inside the Sonora tent.

A week later, on April 22, Coachella's second weekend, they took the Sonora stage again, this time with ten songs.[^33]

"I feel that right now there isn't any band in Taiwan as worshipful of foreign things as we are, as eager to gain recognition from the world. So I thought I would just try and see how far it could go," Kuo Kuo said of his ambition in an interview with VERSE magazine.[^34]

> ⚠️ **Contested view:** The claim that Sunset Rollercoaster was "the first Taiwanese band at Coachella in 33 years" spread in 2023 after some Chinese-language media copied Taiwan News, but it is logically impossible: Coachella only began in 1999, so by 2023 it had existed for only 24 years. Blow StreetVoice's phrasing, "the first invited in more than two decades," is the accurate description. This distortion, enlarged as "astonishing numbers" circulate, is a typical pattern that emerged after a Taiwanese band became visible at scale in the English-speaking world for the first time: the sense of glory overtook fact-checking.

## Becoming One's Own Light

In 2017, the Korean band Hyukoh toured Taipei for the first time. Backstage, vocalist Oh Hyuk was recommended by The Black Skirts to go see Sunset Rollercoaster perform. Oh Hyuk was starstruck after watching them, and Kuo Kuo recognized him as well. The two frontmen added each other on Instagram, and Kuo Kuo took Oh Hyuk around Taipei.[^35]

Seven years later, from 2023 to 2024, Kuo Kuo and Oh Hyuk met every month, rotating among Gapyeong, Seoul, and Jeju Island to write songs. Over one year they wrote eight songs. There were no management-company arranged meetings, no memorandum of collaboration, no preset single-release timetable. The two frontmen worked like 1970s singers: staying for a week in each other's city studios, setting out instruments, drinking, talking about dreams from the night before, and then writing a song. Kuo Kuo later described that year in an interview with Interview Magazine: "We're both very lazy, lazy enough that it was just right for doing this, because being too diligent would have ruined it." This anti-industrial rhythm of collaboration is almost a violation of the laws of physics within the industrial systems of K-pop and Mandarin pop.

On July 10, 2024, _AAA_ was released as a collaborative album by Sunset Rollercoaster x Hyukoh.[^36] Eight songs: "Kite War," "Y," "Antenna," "Glue," "Young Man," "Do Nothing," "Aaaannnnteeeeennnaaaaaa," and "2F 年輕人." The album followed none of the existing formulas for "cross-national collaboration" (no Taiwan-Korea duet, no bilingual splicing). Instead, two Asian indie bands built a bridge that had not previously existed between City Pop and alt-rock.

A line Chien Miao-ju wrote in 2018 in UDN Opinion now describes _AAA_ even more precisely: "It ruminates on a large volume of British and American rock influences... It is retro-romantic and also avant-garde and new. The gentle, fluid vocal style remains very Asian."[^37]

On June 28, 2025, the 36th Golden Melody Awards ceremony was held at Taipei Arena. _AAA_ received nine nominations, including Song of the Year, Best Band, Best Album Producer, Best Vocal Recording Album, and Best Music Video. It ultimately took home three awards: Best Music Video, Best Vocal Recording Album, and Best Album Design.[^38] That a cross-national band project was affirmed across the central axes of Taiwan's most authoritative Mandarin-language music awards system, including Best Album and Best Producer, marked the first time since the Golden Melody Awards began in 1990 that a collaborative album by a "half-Korean band" had reached this level of nomination.

After Sunset Rollercoaster's Coachella performance, a VERSE magazine reporter asked Kuo Kuo a question: did he feel that he now counted as "Taiwan's glory"?

> "I don't want to take on the title of 'Taiwan's glory.' As long as you do your own work well and become your own light, that is the best thing."[^39]

On August 8, 2025, they again released their fifth full-length album, _QUIT QUIETLY 悄悄消消_, without warning.[^40] Eleven songs, accumulated over the five years since _Soft Storm_, with cross-genre collaborators including Oh Hyuk, Leah Dou, Bulareyaung Dance Company, and Yuma Abe, vocalist of Never Young Beach.

> ✦ No one in that small bar on Shida Road in Taipei in 2010 would have predicted that inside that Mac's Photo Booth presets, an image of a roller coaster and a sunset silhouette would, fourteen years later, become the words on the backdrop of a Coachella desert stage. Nor would anyone have predicted, on the afternoon in 2015 when Kuo Kuo received his military-service exemption notice, that a guy whose 1,300-degree myopia nearly prevented him from becoming a soldier would end up singing Taipei's humid afternoons entirely in English, placing the way an island was most heard by the world into two syllables: JinJi.
>
> Singing Taipei's sunset entirely in English, they made Taiwan more audible to the world.
>
> That light traveled from a casually chosen Photo Booth background to the stage in the desert.
