# Hsu Cho-yun: Writing the Long River of Chinese History with Two Fingers, Wang Leehom's Great-Uncle's Ninety-Five Years

> **30-second overview:** Hsu Cho-yun, the historian born in Xiamen in 1930, who followed the Nationalist government to Taiwan in 1949 and went to Pittsburgh in 1970, lived his life in a wheelchair because of a congenital limb disability. Yet with the only two fingers that could still exert force, he typed nearly forty books. He was elected to Academia Sinica's 13th class of academicians in 1980 and won the sixth Tang Prize in Sinology in 2024, the first laureate from Taiwan in the strict sense; he donated the full NT$50 million prize to establish the Hsu-Sun Scholarship[^1]. His eldest sister Hsu Liu-fen was Wang Leehom's grandmother, and his second younger sister Hsu Wan-ching was Li Chien-fu's mother: one family of a Wuxi naval major general linked, all at once, to classrooms in Sinology at the University of Chicago, the office of the chair of National Taiwan University's Department of History, the lyrics and melody of "Descendants of the Dragon," and the kinship term "maternal great-granduncle," searched more than two thousand times but never clicked[^2]. On August 3, 2025, he died in Pittsburgh at the venerable age of 95; when CNA interviewed him at 94, he said: "At the moment I draw my last breath, I will still be learning."[^3]

In June 2024, a CNA reporter flew to Pittsburgh to interview Hsu Cho-yun, who had just learned that he had received the sixth Tang Prize in Sinology. At 94, he sat in a wheelchair, both hands bent inward, his eyesight so deteriorated that he could no longer turn pages himself, his mobility almost entirely dependent on help from his wife, Sun Man-li[^4].

But every morning, he still sat down at the computer and used the only two fingers he had left that could exert force to type, continuing to read and write. The book he was writing that day was called _Jingwei Huaxia_ (The Warp and Weft of Huaxia): a history of ancient China of more than 300,000 Chinese characters, written over three years and revised through eight drafts, ultimately typed entirely with those two fingers[^5].

When the reporter asked how he felt about winning the prize, he did not talk about the prize. He said: "At 94, I want to turn in my paper to society. I hope society says to me: you have passed. But I cannot say to myself that I have passed. At the moment I draw my last breath, I will still be learning."[^3]

One year later, on August 3, 2025 (the early morning of August 4, Taiwan time), Hsu Cho-yun died in his sleep in Pittsburgh, at the venerable age of 95. His final Weibo post, published on July 24, recalled the War of Resistance in 1938, the eve of the Battle of Taierzhuang, and the moment when Sichuan troops passed through Shashi; the next day, his grandnephew-in-law Wang Leehom posted on social media to mourn his "seventh granduncle"[^6].

📝 **The trigger for rewriting this article was Google Search Console data, not the news of his death**: in the English-speaking world, there were more than two thousand searches per week for "Hsu Cho-yun Wang Leehom," all with 0 clicks. Readers wanted to know the family relationship between Wang Leehom and this historian, but Taiwan.md previously lacked that bridge. The scope filled in here expands from family genealogy to a Wuxi family of a naval major general, the lyrics and melody of "Descendants of the Dragon," the University of Chicago's social-science approach to history, and the long river in _China: A New Cultural History_ that "places China back within the world," reopening an entryway for a new generation of readers.

## The Twins of Gulangyu, Xiamen, and Wartime Flight through Shashi, Hubei, and Chongqing

On September 3, 1930, Hsu Cho-yun and his twin younger brother Hsu Yi-yun were born on Gulangyu Island in Xiamen, Fujian[^7]. Their ancestral home was Wuxi, Jiangsu. Their father, Hsu Feng-tsao, was a major general in the Republic of China Navy and had participated in the Xinhai Revolution; their mother, Chang Shun-ying, came from an official family in Wuxi, was educated, and beyond supporting her husband and raising children was also the spiritual center holding the extended family together[^8].

Of the twins, one was born physically intact, the younger brother Hsu Yi-yun; the other, the elder brother Hsu Cho-yun, had congenital deformities of all four limbs, with inward-turning hands and feet, and needed crutches just to walk with difficulty. Such a contrast between twins is extremely rare in medicine[^9]. For a family in 1930s China, this meant that through all the relocations, war, and exile of the next several decades, Hsu Cho-yun would be the child carried in arms, carried on backs, and cared for.

In 1935, his father was transferred, and the family moved from Xiamen to Shashi in Hubei. Two years later, the Marco Polo Bridge Incident broke out, and the 7-year-old Hsu Cho-yun collided with one of the most turbulent eras in Chinese history. The family thereafter wandered as refugees through Shashi, Hubei, Sichuan, and Chongqing; this displaced childhood became one of the base tones of his lifelong historical writing[^10].

📝 **His final Weibo post before death stopped at the moment just before the Battle of Taierzhuang**: "I watched these young soldiers pass one by one before me. At that moment, I changed from a child into an adult. Dimly, I understood that these very young soldiers passing through Shashi were going from Xinyang straight to Taierzhuang, and in the end not one of them would make it home. Not a single one would return." He added below: "A child becomes an adult not by age, but by state of mind."[^11] The post was published on July 24, 2025. Ten days later, he died in Pittsburgh. He wrote ancient Chinese history, Han agriculture, and Western Zhou feudalism all his life, but the beginning and end of his own life were both connected to that riverside road in Hubei in 1938.

In the midst of war, limited mobility instead became Hsu Cho-yun's time for reading. He later called himself "a fortunate person": "When I studied, I met good teachers; when I married, I met a good wife; even my congenital disability brought me benefits: more time to stay at the desk."[^12]

In 1949, the Hsu family came to Taiwan with the retreating Nationalist government, and the 19-year-old Hsu Cho-yun entered the Department of History at National Taiwan University. That generation of intellectuals who carried learning across the sea from mainland China to Taiwan transplanted academic traditions originally built in Beiping and Shanghai to Taipei, where they took root again. It was in this environment that Hsu Cho-yun established a foundation in Sinology capable of conversing with Western scholarship[^13].

## Under Herrlee Creel at the University of Chicago: Bringing the Social Sciences into Chinese History

In 1953, Hsu graduated from the NTU Department of History; in 1956, he received a master's degree from NTU's Graduate Institute of Liberal Arts. That same year, he went to the University of Chicago for further study, lived at the Chicago Theological Seminary, and studied under the Sinologist Herrlee G. Creel[^14].

At that time, American Sinology was undergoing a methodological transformation. Traditional "Sinology" had centered on textual interpretation, philology, and evidentiary scholarship; "China Studies" began to introduce the tools of the social sciences, including sociology, anthropology, economics, and political science, treating China as an object that modern academic disciplines could analyze. When Hsu received his PhD in 1962, he stood precisely at this turning point[^15].

His dissertation was later revised into _Ancient China in Transition: An Analysis of Social Mobility, 722-222 B.C._, published by Stanford University Press in 1965. The book used quantitative methods to analyze social mobility during the Spring and Autumn and Warring States periods: which aristocratic families disappeared from the political stage, which rising classes moved upward through military merit or learning, and how the social structure loosened on the eve of Qin-Han unification. After reading it, the Harvard Sinologist John K. Fairbank called it "a little classic"[^16].

📝 **This book was also a methodological seed for Taiwan's historical profession**. Hsu was 32 when he returned to Taiwan in 1962. He first became an assistant research fellow at the Institute of History and Philology, Academia Sinica (part-time from 1956, promoted to associate research fellow in 1962), and in 1964 he also became professor and chair of NTU's Department of History (1964-1970)[^17]. In those six years, he did something that would later profoundly change historical scholarship in Taiwan: **he introduced the methods, schools, and questions of the social sciences into history classrooms in Taiwan**. As department chair, he organized workshops and international conferences, invited methodological debates from American historiography into NTU, and trained a cohort of students who would later occupy important positions across the historical professions of Taiwan, China, Hong Kong, and beyond[^18].

The three representative works he later completed in succession, _Han Agriculture: The Formation of the Early Chinese Peasant Economy_ (English edition, University of Washington Press, 1980)[^19], _Qiu Gu Bian_ (Essays in the Search for Antiquity, Linking, 1982)[^20], and _The History of Western Zhou_ (Linking, 1984)[^21], can be read as three self-applications of this methodology:

| Work                          | Period Treated       | Methodological Instruments                                                                                                    |
| ----------------------------- | -------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| _Han Agriculture_             | Han dynasty          | Quantitative reconstruction of peasant life and the mutual reinforcement of market-economy networks and intensive cultivation |
| _Qiu Gu Bian_                 | Antiquity to Qin-Han | The evolution of the civil-service system connecting central/local and political/social binaries                              |
| _The History of Western Zhou_ | Western Zhou         | The origins of "Huaxia" consciousness constructed by lineage groups                                                           |

He himself used "the three primary colors of Chinese culture" to describe the problems these three books addressed: three slices of a single set of fundamental questions that one historian pursued continuously over thirty years, rather than three unrelated studies[^22].

## Half a Century in Pittsburgh: A Big-History Method That Rejected Sinocentrism

In 1970, at 40, Hsu Cho-yun immigrated to the United States with his wife Sun Man-li and their eight-month-old only son, Leo Hsu, joining the Department of History at the University of Pittsburgh[^23].

He spent 28 years in Pittsburgh (1970-1998), moving from visiting professor (1970-72) to tenured professor (1972-82), university professor (1982-98), and, after retiring in 1998, university professor emeritus until his death. The University of Pittsburgh retained his title for a total of 55 years[^24]. At the same time, he never left Taiwan's academic world: from 1981 to 1997 he served as a corresponding research fellow at Academia Sinica's Institute of History and Philology, and after 1989 he was successively appointed distinguished research fellow until 2006. Academia Sinica in Taipei, the Pittsburgh campus, and lecture itineraries across Taiwan, China, Hong Kong, and the broader Sinophone world: these three coordinates formed the map of the second half of his academic life[^25].

During the Pittsburgh period, he carried out two structural tasks. First, he continued deepening the methodology of bringing the social sciences into Chinese history. Second, he applied this method at a larger scale: not looking only at one dynasty, and not looking only at China itself, but comparing China within the context of world civilizational history[^26].

📝 **The phrase "big history" was later most widely associated in Chinese with Ray Huang, but the big history Hsu Cho-yun practiced was another kind**. Huang focused on why China could not break out of traditional modes of governance, as in _1587, a Year of No Significance_ and _China: A Macro History_. Hsu focused on how Chinese civilization itself was shaped through contact with other civilizations, including the steppe, India, Islam, and the West, while also shaping others. His core conviction was a rejection of "Sinocentrism": to understand China, one must place China back in the context of world history, rather than treating it as a self-sufficient, isolated, closed system evolving along its own internal logic[^28].

In 1989, he participated in founding the Chiang Ching-kuo Foundation for International Scholarly Exchange. The foundation originated in 1986, when he, Yu Ying-shih, K. C. Chang, and other ethnic Chinese professors teaching in North America collectively wrote to President Chiang Ching-kuo to advocate its creation. At the time, they were concerned about the decline of overseas Sinological research and hoped Taiwan would fund an international scholarly grant institution to promote Sinology[^29]. After the foundation was established on January 12, 1989, Hsu Cho-yun served as chair of its North American Committee, a position he held until shortly before his death: 36 years in all[^30].

Among the students he trained during the Pittsburgh period were Wang Fan-sen, later director and vice president of Academia Sinica's Institute of History and Philology, and Tu Cheng-sheng, who served as minister of education and director of the National Palace Museum[^31]. Together with long-term collaborators and contemporaries such as Hsing I-tien and Chen Jo-shui, "Hsu Cho-yun's student circle" was an influential network in the historical profession across Taiwan, China, and Hong Kong from the 1980s through the 2010s.

## Family Genealogy: From Hsu Feng-tsao to Wang Leehom, Three Generations of Sinophone Cultural Elites

Hsu Cho-yun was the seventh of nine siblings. This is why Wang Leehom called him "seventh granduncle" in his post[^32].

When the family context is laid out, it becomes an overlooked map of postwar Taiwanese culture:

| Sibling Order         | Name            | Main Identity                                                                                                                                                                                                                                           | Connection to the Next Generation                                                                                                                                   |
| --------------------- | --------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Eldest sister         | Hsu Liu-fen     | Graduate of Tsinghua University's Department of Economics, chair of the Department of Accounting and Statistics at Taipei College of Commerce, author of _Principles of Accounting_ and _English-Chinese/Chinese-English Dictionary of Accounting_[^33] | → Married Wang Hsin-ming → son Wang Ta-chung, a neurosurgeon → grandson **Wang Leehom**[^34]                                                                        |
| Second younger sister | Hsu Wan-ching   | Graduate of National Southwestern Associated University                                                                                                                                                                                                 | → Married Li Mo, former vice minister of education and vice minister of economic affairs → son **Li Chien-fu**, original singer of "Descendants of the Dragon"[^35] |
| Seventh, elder twin   | **Hsu Cho-yun** | Academia Sinica academician and historian                                                                                                                                                                                                               | The subject of this article                                                                                                                                         |
| Seventh, younger twin | Hsu Yi-yun      | Chemical-engineering scholar and former chair of the Executive Yuan's Atomic Energy Council[^36]                                                                                                                                                        | —                                                                                                                                                                   |
| Others                | —               | —                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                       | —                                                                                                                                                                   |

In other words, Hsu Cho-yun held two roles within this family: he was Wang Leehom's "seventh granduncle" and Li Chien-fu's uncle. Wang Leehom and Li Chien-fu are cousins: Hsu Liu-fen was Wang Leehom's grandmother, Hsu Wan-ching was Li Chien-fu's mother, and Hsu Liu-fen and Hsu Wan-ching were biological sisters.

The Hsu family was an official family from Wuxi. The father, Hsu Feng-tsao, was a major general in the Republic of China Navy; the mother, Chang Shun-ying, came from a scholarly household. In the next generation in Taiwan, the family spread into accounting, vice-ministerial offices in the Ministry of Education, academia, the popular-music industry, and nuclear-energy policy. In the third generation, it connected to the lyrics and melody of "Descendants of the Dragon" in 1980, written and composed by Hou Dejian and first sung by Li Chien-fu, the son of Hsu Cho-yun's younger sister Hsu Wan-ching, and to Wang Leehom, a representative figure of Sinophone popular music in the 2000s: two branches of the same Wuxi family[^37].

📝 **The reason this family line deserves serious treatment is not gossip**. It precisely shows how "second-generation mainlander" intellectual families that came to Taiwan with the Nationalist government in 1949 distributed their cultural capital within three generations into several of postwar Taiwan's most important fields: academia (Hsu Cho-yun), education policy (Li Mo), the accounting profession (Hsu Liu-fen), nuclear technology (Hsu Yi-yun), and popular music (Wang Leehom and Li Chien-fu). Roots in China, growth in Taiwan, academic training in the United States, circulation through the Sinophone world: one family, five outlets.

On August 5, 2025, Wang Leehom mourned this "seventh granduncle" on social media, writing about the childhood of his father, Wang Ta-chung, a neurosurgeon and graduate of NTU's medical school[^38]:

"One of my father's warmest childhood memories was the bedtime stories his seventh uncle read to him. His seventh uncle, Hsu Cho-yun, was frail and sickly from childhood and congenitally disabled, but his inner world was extremely rich, and his wisdom was unmatched."

"Although he was the seventh of nine siblings, and his body was impaired, what he left to the world was a towering silhouette."

"His figure may have been thin and small, but his spirit was as high as a mountain. Everything he left behind will never be forgotten. Mr. Hsu Cho-yun, may you rest in peace. Thank you, seventh granduncle. May God bless you with rest."

The detail about bedtime stories was later amplified by many media outlets: every night, Hsu Cho-yun would make up a serialized story for this nephew, Wang Ta-chung; many were improvised and contained lessons about life[^39]. An uncle who could not run or jump with both feet held his young nephew with stories. Decades later, that nephew's son spoke to global Sinophone audiences through popular music. From Hsu Feng-tsao's generation of naval major generals to Wang Leehom's generation of Golden Melody singers, with a man who wrote Chinese history with two fingers in between, this line itself is a miniature history of the twentieth-century Chinese diaspora.

## _China: A New Cultural History_: Writing China as a Long River for an Age of Ordinary Readers

In 2006, _Wangu Jianghe: Zhongguo Lishi Wenhua de Zhuanzhe yu Kaizhan_ (_China: A New Cultural History: The Turning Points and Development of Chinese History and Culture_) was published by Hansheng in Taipei[^40].

This 270,000-character book was a Chinese history that Hsu Cho-yun wrote for "an age of ordinary people." In his preface, he said: "Today's readers are different from those of the past. In this age of ordinary people, broadly speaking, anyone with at least a high-school education may be interested in history. This book is a history written for this generation of Chinese people."[^41]

📝 **To "popularize" scholarship is a higher demand, not a lowering of the threshold**. To write for high-school graduates while still making university professors nod is a rare ability in any language. The writing strategy of _China: A New Cultural History_ is to discard a linear chronicle of "dynastic succession" and instead use "the diffusion and contraction of culture" as the main axis: Chinese civilization is like a great river, growing from trickles to a powerful torrent, absorbing tributaries along the way, while also changing course, overflowing, and drying up.

His points of entry were the questions ordinary readers truly ask: what did common people in the medieval period eat? What were the differences between Yangtze culture and Yellow River culture? Why did Taiwan appear in Chinese history only in the modern period? What did Confucius's "ren" really mean? Why did China not produce Western-style industrialization? Each of these questions could be a popular essay in its own right, but Hsu Cho-yun wove them together in one book[^42].

After publication, _China: A New Cultural History_ circulated widely in Taiwan, Hong Kong, mainland China, and overseas Chinese communities, accumulating sales in the millions over two decades; simplified-character editions were introduced successively by SDX Joint Publishing and Guangxi Normal University Press in mainland China[^43]. The book is a popular work, but the academy accepted it as well. A history book that professors can read and high-school graduates can read: this is a rare writing achievement in the twenty-first-century Chinese-speaking world.

He next used the same "civilizational big history" framework to write three sister works in succession:

**_Wo Zhe yu Ta Zhe: Zhongguo Lishi shang de Neiwai Fenji_ (_Self and Other: The Internal and External Boundaries in Chinese History_, 2009, China Times Publishing / Chinese University Press)**[^44]: this book directly handles the historical question of "how China defined itself and how it defined others." Hsu proposed six systems for analyzing the formation and collapse of "self/other" relations: interactions between China and other nations or peoples; interactions between China proper and peripheral peoples; interactions between central regimes and local society; interactions between upper and lower strata of society; market-economy networks; and cultural interactions between mainstream "orthodoxy" and "heterodox" challengers. He wrote with great restraint: "For the 'inside/outside' and 'self/other' relations of complex systems, the most important point lies not in distinctions among ethnic groups, but in cultural recognition."[^45] Against the backdrop of intense political identity tensions across Taiwan, China, and Hong Kong in 2009, this was a writing posture that deliberately pulled the scale back to history.

**_Huaxia Lunshu: Yige Fuza Gongtongti de Bianhua_ (_A Discourse on Huaxia: The Transformation of a Complex Community_, Guangxi Normal University Press, 2015)**[^46]: this book took another step forward. He argued that China should not be defined from a "fixed frame," meaning what China looked like in a given dynasty or moment, but should be understood from "process," meaning how China changed. Concretely, he broke this down into the dynamic interweaving of four variables: political power, economy, society, and cultural concepts.

**_Jingwei Huaxia_ (_The Warp and Weft of Huaxia_, CITIC, 2023; expanded traditional-character edition from Hong Kong Joint Publishing, 2024)**[^47]: this was the last major work that he, at 93, completed with two fingers through eight drafts. When Xu Zhiyuan visited him in Pittsburgh in 2019 and again in 2023, the book was still being written; in the final traditional-character expanded edition of 2024, he added two new long essays, about 20,000 Chinese characters in total, on "the Great Flood" and "interactions between the northern steppe and the Central Plains"[^48].

📝 **Taken together, these four books are one historian's complete reexamination of the concept of "China"**: from the river metaphor of _China: A New Cultural History_, to the internal and external boundaries of _Self and Other_, to the processual definition in _A Discourse on Huaxia_, and finally to the enlarged pattern from continent to ocean in _The Warp and Weft of Huaxia_. In his later years, Hsu Cho-yun explicitly wrote "Chinese history" as "a history of China's relationship with the world." This was also the comment made by the Tang Prize jury in 2024: "Professor Hsu's historiography is able to see the large picture. His specialized studies of ancient China uncover essential questions of long-term history; his interpretations of general history emphasize cultural inclusion and exchange, seeking China's place in the world."[^49]

## A Guided Reading List: Forty Books, Five Dimensions

Hsu Cho-yun wrote nearly forty books in his lifetime. Their subjects include ancient history, Han society, Western Zhou feudalism, overseas Sinology, questions of Chinese identity, and, in his later years, observations on contemporary America and notes on self-settlement. For readers encountering him for the first time, there is no need to begin with the densest academic books. The following suggestions are organized into five dimensions by reading order:

**1. Entry Point: Chinese Big History for Ordinary Readers**

- **_China: A New Cultural History: The Turning Points and Development of Chinese History and Culture_ (Hansheng, 2006)**[^40]: his most widely circulated work. From the long-river perspective of civilizational diffusion and contraction, it surveys a Chinese cultural history without dynastic boundaries in 270,000 Chinese characters.

**2. Advanced: The Internal and External Boundaries of Civilization**

- **_Self and Other: The Internal and External Boundaries in Chinese History_ (China Times Publishing, 2009)**[^44]: addresses questions of Chinese identity through a six-system framework.
- **_A Discourse on Huaxia: The Transformation of a Complex Community_ (Guangxi Normal University Press, 2015)**[^46]: defines "China" processually, through the interweaving of four variables: political power, economy, society, and culture.
- **_The Warp and Weft of Huaxia_ (expanded traditional-character edition, Hong Kong Joint Publishing, 2024)**[^47]: revised through eight drafts, expanding the pattern "from continent to ocean."

**3. Academic Representative Works: Bringing Social Science into Chinese History**

- **_The History of Western Zhou_ (Linking, 1984; second edition 1986; revised third editions 1990/1993; expanded Beijing SDX edition 1994; expanded new edition 2020)**[^21]: a study of the origins of "Huaxia" consciousness constructed through lineage groups.
- **_Han Agriculture_ (English edition, University of Washington Press, 1980; later translated into Chinese as _Han Agriculture: The Formation of the Early Chinese Peasant Economy_)**[^19]: a quantitative reconstruction of Han peasant life and market-economy networks.
- **_Ancient China in Transition_ (English edition, Stanford University Press, 1965)**[^16]: revised from his dissertation, a quantitative analysis of social mobility during the Spring and Autumn and Warring States periods, praised by Fairbank as "a little classic."
- **_Qiu Gu Bian_ (_Essays in the Search for Antiquity_, Linking, 1982)**[^20]: studies the evolution of central/local civil-service systems from antiquity to Qin-Han.

**4. Autobiography and Late Thought**

- **_Xinlu Licheng_ (_The Journey of My Mind_, Wenxing, 1964; Zhuanji Wenxue, 1979; Xiamen University Press, 2015)**[^50]: an autobiography written at 34, tracing his intellectual journey from Xiamen to Taipei to Chicago.
- **_Meiguo Liushi Nian Cangsang: Yige Huaren de Jianwen_ (_Sixty Years of Change in America: A Chinese Person's Observations_, Linking, 2019)**[^51]: his observations from half a century in the United States and his diagnosis of American society.
- **_Wang Li Zou, Andun Ziji_ (_Go Inward, Settle Yourself_, Beijing Daily Press, 2022, coauthored with Feng Junwen)**[^52]: notes of thought from age 89, a book of self-settlement for an impatient age.

**5. Popular Works and Interviews**

- **The five-volume _Hsu Cho-yun Talks History_ series**[^53]: popular versions of his big-history approach.
- **_Hsu Cho-yun's Ten-Day Talks: The Pattern of Today's World and the Future of Humanity_ (Hong Kong Joint Publishing)**[^54]: a late-life set of conversations organizing his thoughts on the world order.

📝 **If you can read only one book: start with _China: A New Cultural History_**. To understand his historical method, the preface to the expanded new edition of _The History of Western Zhou_ is more powerful than the book itself. To understand his overall view of China, _Self and Other_ is the most condensed. To understand him as a person, read _The Journey of My Mind_ together with _Go Inward, Settle Yourself_.

## The 2024 Tang Prize: Donating the Full NT$50 Million to Establish the Hsu-Sun Scholarship

On June 20, 2024, the Tang Prize Foundation announced the laureate of the sixth Tang Prize in Sinology: Hsu Cho-yun. The prize carried NT$50 million, including NT$10 million in research funding[^55].

He was, in the strict sense, the first Tang Prize laureate in Sinology from Taiwan. The first five laureates, Yu Ying-shih of Harvard, Stephen Owen of Harvard, Hazama Naoki of Kyoto, Wang Gungwu of Singapore, and Erik Zürcher of Leiden, all completed their secondary education and foundational university training overseas. Hsu Cho-yun, by contrast, was a scholar who "truly grew up in Taiwan": he came to Taiwan with the Nationalist government in 1949, graduated from NTU's Department of History in 1953, and received a master's degree from NTU's Graduate Institute of Liberal Arts in 1956[^56].

⚠️ **The Tang Prize jury's citation directly identified the core of his historical work**: "Professor Hsu's historiography is able to see the large picture. His specialized studies of ancient China uncover essential questions of long-term history; his interpretations of general history emphasize cultural inclusion and exchange, seeking China's place in the world. His works reveal concern for the nation, the people, and humanity in the world; he is a historian of both broad learning and commitment to improving society."[^49]

But what was more widely reported was his next action after winning: he donated the entire NT$50 million prize to establish the Hsu-Sun Scholarship, supporting young scholars around the world pursuing doctoral research in Sinology[^1]. The scholarship was named jointly for Hsu Cho-yun and his wife, Sun Man-li, recognizing Sun's 55 years of support throughout his academic career.

📝 **The significance of this action was not just generosity**. For Hsu Cho-yun, who participated in founding the Chiang Ching-kuo Foundation in 1989 and long chaired grant support for North American Sinology, "the cultivation of the next generation of Sinologists" was one of the main lines of his life's work[^29]. Donating the full Tang Prize transformed "what our generation has achieved" directly into "whether the next generation will have the opportunity to continue." For a 94-year-old historian whose eyesight had deteriorated and whose mobility depended entirely on others, this was the most concrete act of transmission he could make.

In late September 2024, Tang Prize Week was held in Taipei. Hsu Cho-yun could not attend in person because of health reasons and delivered his laureate lecture, "Finding a Path in a Confused World," by video. That same month, Xu Zhiyuan's _Thirteen Invitations_ organized his two visits to Pittsburgh beginning in 2019 into Season 8, Episode 1. The topic Hsu discussed most in front of the camera was his unease about the present world: he saw the tearing of American society, China's confusion, and Taiwan's anxiety. He used "I cannot help thinking of the late Qing" to describe the current international order[^57].

## A Cross-Strait Academic Bridge: Maintaining Distance Beyond "Sinocentrism"

One position most repeatedly cited from Hsu Cho-yun's later years was his criticism of "Sinocentrism"[^58].

He argued: "I hope no longer to hear the rhetoric of 'China is amazing.'"[^59] This position made him seem "not Chinese enough" in the eyes of Chinese nationalists, while in the eyes of some Taiwan-centered historians he was still "too Chinese." But this was precisely the position he wanted: not choosing sides within any nationalist narrative, but using the longer temporal scale of "China's relationship with the world" to view present cross-strait political anxiety[^60].

⚠️ **In September 2024, one remark he made in a CNA interview was widely quoted**: "Trump's 'protection money' talk is like gangsters in the concessions."[^61] This was said around the days of his sixth Tang Prize laureate lecture. He compared the current American posture toward allies to the posture of foreign powers toward China in the foreign concessions of the late Qing. The analogy itself was an application of the "big history" method: anxieties about the current international order have historical comparison points at the scale of the long river.

Toward Taiwan, Hsu Cho-yun always maintained a special feeling. In his 2024 CNA interview, he told the Taiwanese reporter[^3]:

"Taiwan makes me long for it, and makes me feel regret. I always hope that one day the waters over the Strait will be calm, brothers on the two sides will shake hands, and both sides will no longer be hostile, no longer divided from one another."

"I have no regrets. I have fulfilled the responsibility I owed Taiwan."

Before his death, he had already purchased a burial plot in Wuxi and had his gravestone carved in advance, hoping to be buried with his parents after death. "Fallen leaves return to their roots" was his choice of final belonging[^62]. On the gravestone he quoted Lu You's poem "Showing My Son": "only grieved that I did not see the nine provinces united." Commentators on both sides of the Strait read different meanings into this line: some read it as "regret over Chinese unification," while others read it as a deeper sorrow that no political narrative could contain the dispersal of his life.

📝 **This position was extremely difficult**: he studied at university in Taiwan, earned his PhD in the United States, spent half a century in Pittsburgh, had millions of readers in China through _China: A New Cultural History_, and held the identity of an Academia Sinica academician in Taiwan. Every identity had expectations of him; no identity could fully contain him. His final choice was "I have fulfilled the responsibility I owed Taiwan." That responsibility was defined by him, crossing levels of political stance and national identity, and settled on using the long scale of a historian to offer Taiwan, China, Hong Kong, and the broader Sinophone world a shared temporal measure for understanding one another.

One sentence Hsu Cho-yun repeated throughout his life was: "History tells us that any civilization, at the moment when it is strongest, is also brewing its crisis. Conversely, at its weakest moment, it often has the seeds of revival buried within it."[^63] He wrote Chinese history all his life, but what he truly cared about was the gaze between the upstream and downstream of the long river itself. Dynastic strength or decline was only the surface. What a 95-year-old historian typed out with two fingers was a dose of temporal calm for an impatient age.

## Death at 95, and the Long River Left for Future Readers

On August 3, 2025, Hsu Cho-yun died in his sleep in Pittsburgh, United States, at the venerable age of 95. His wife, Sun Man-li, was by his side[^64].

Academia Sinica issued a press release evaluating this member of its 13th class of academicians: "Academician Hsu Cho-yun mastered learning from both China and the West and was a globally renowned leader in historical scholarship. He specialized in ancient Chinese history, social history, and cultural history; his writings were abundant and deeply original. His vision was broad, using the perspective of world history to explore ancient Chinese history without being limited by China."[^25] Mainland China's Taiwan Affairs Office also made the rare public statement that his passing was "deeply regrettable." This event of simultaneous mourning on both sides of the Strait was itself the final annotation to Hsu Cho-yun's position[^65].

On August 5, Wang Leehom mourned his "seventh granduncle" on social media. That same day, Li Chien-fu also posted in memory of this "uncle": the original singer of "Descendants of the Dragon" and the songwriter behind "Ai Yinwei Zai Xinzhong," descendants of the same Wuxi Hsu household, saying goodbye across three generations to the same boy on crutches[^66].

In early August, the University of Pittsburgh's Department of History released a short In Memoriam: "Renowned Historian Passes Away at 95." The university where Hsu Cho-yun had spent 55 years sent him off with a four-word title[^67].

Yeh Kuang-shih, a former minister of transportation and a collaborator at the Chiang Ching-kuo Foundation, wrote a passage recalling the last time he met Hsu Cho-yun: "Hsu Cho-yun was not only a great scholar; his conduct and way of handling affairs were also extraordinary. He was an important driving force behind the founding of the Chiang Ching-kuo Foundation."[^68]

Hsu Cho-yun left nearly forty books. The earliest, _The Journey of My Mind_, was published in 1964, when the author was 34; the last, the expanded edition of _The Warp and Weft of Huaxia_, appeared in 2024, when the author was 94. Across 60 years, he used a pen, and later two fingers, to pursue the same long river of Chinese history.

✦ On the riverside road in Shashi, Hubei, in 1938, Sichuan troops passed before him on their way to Taierzhuang.
On the deck at Keelung Harbor in 1949, the flag of the Nationalist government fluttered in the wind.

"At the moment I draw my last breath, I will still be learning." He said this in an interview at 94. One year later, it came true.

What he left behind is a long river that keeps flowing, with no final answer.
