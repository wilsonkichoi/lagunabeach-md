# Hsu Cho-yun: Writing the Long River of Chinese History with Two Fingers, Wang Leehom’s Granduncle Across Ninety-Five Years

> **30-second overview:** Born in Xiamen in 1930, crossing to Taiwan with the Nationalist government in 1949, and arriving in Pittsburgh in 1970, the historian Hsu Cho-yun lived with congenital limb disabilities and used a wheelchair throughout his life, yet typed with the only two fingers that could still exert force to complete nearly forty books. He was elected to Academia Sinica’s 13th class of academicians in 1980 and received the sixth Tang Prize in Sinology in 2024, becoming the first strictly defined Taiwan-formed laureate; he donated the full NT$50 million prize to establish the Hsu-Sun Scholarship[^1]. His eldest sister Hsu Liu-fen was Wang Leehom’s grandmother, and his second younger sister Hsu Wan-ching was Lee Chien-fu’s mother: a Wuxi family headed by a navy rear admiral, linked at once to a University of Chicago classroom in Chinese studies, the office of the chair of National Taiwan University’s Department of History, the lyrics and melody of “Descendants of the Dragon,” and the kinship term “maternal granduncle-in-law,” which had been searched more than two thousand times without anyone clicking through[^2]. On August 3, 2025, he died in Pittsburgh at the venerable age of 95; when the Central News Agency interviewed him at 94, he said: “At the moment I breathe my last, I will still be learning.”[^3]

In June 2024, a Central News Agency reporter flew to Pittsburgh to interview Hsu Cho-yun, who had just learned he had been awarded the sixth Tang Prize in Sinology. At 94, he sat in a wheelchair, his hands bent inward, his eyesight so deteriorated that he could no longer turn pages by himself, his movements almost entirely dependent on the assistance of his wife, Sun Man-li[^4].

Yet every morning he still sat down in front of the computer and, using the only two fingers that could still exert force, kept typing, reading, and writing. The book he was working on that day was _Jingwei Huaxia_ — a history of ancient China of more than 300,000 Chinese characters, written over three years and revised eight times, ultimately typed entirely with those two fingers[^5].

When the reporter asked how he felt about winning the prize, he did not speak about the prize. He said: “At 94, I want to hand in my exam paper to society. I hope society will say to me: you have passed. But I cannot say to myself that I have passed. At the moment I breathe my last, I will still be learning.”[^3]

One year later, on August 3, 2025 (the early morning of August 4 Taiwan time), Hsu Cho-yun died in his sleep in Pittsburgh at the venerable age of 95. His final Weibo post, published on July 24, recalled 1938 during the War of Resistance, on the eve of the Battle of Taierzhuang, when Sichuan troops passed through Shashi; the next day, Wang Leehom, his grandnephew-in-law, posted a tribute on social media to his “seventh granduncle”[^6].

📝 **The trigger for rewriting this article was Google Search Console data, not the obituary news**: every week, English-language searches for “Hsu Cho-yun Wang Leehom” appeared more than two thousand times, all with zero clicks. Readers wanted to know the family relationship between Wang Leehom and this historian, but Taiwan.md previously had no bridge for them. This article’s scope expanded from the family genealogy to a Wuxi household headed by a navy rear admiral, the lyrics and music of “Descendants of the Dragon,” the University of Chicago’s social-scientific turn in historical methodology, and the long river of _China: A New Cultural History_ that “places China back into the world,” reopening an entry point for a new generation of readers.

## Twins on Xiamen’s Gulangyu, Wartime Exile Through Shashi, Hubei, and Chongqing

On September 3, 1930, Hsu Cho-yun and his twin younger brother Hsu Yi-yun were born on Gulangyu Island in Xiamen, Fujian[^7]. Their ancestral home was Wuxi, Jiangsu. Their father, Hsu Feng-tsao, was a rear admiral in the Republic of China Navy who had taken part in the 1911 Revolution; their mother, Chang Shun-ying, came from a Wuxi official-gentry family, was educated, and, beyond supporting her husband and raising children, served as the spiritual center holding the entire extended family together[^8].

Of the twins, one was physically sound at birth (the younger brother Hsu Yi-yun), while the other had congenital deformities of all four limbs, with hands and feet turned inward, and had to rely on two crutches even to walk with difficulty (the elder brother Hsu Cho-yun). Such a contrast between twins is medically extremely rare[^9]. For a Chinese family in the 1930s, it meant that through all the relocations, war, and exile of the coming decades, Hsu Cho-yun would be the child carried in arms, carried on backs, and cared for.

In 1935, his father was transferred, and the family moved from Xiamen to Shashi, Hubei. Two years later, the Marco Polo Bridge Incident broke out, and seven-year-old Hsu Cho-yun collided with one of the most turbulent eras in Chinese history. The family thereafter moved in wartime exile among Shashi, Hubei, Sichuan, and Chongqing, and this childhood of displacement became one of the underlying tones of his lifelong historical writing[^10].

📝 **His final Weibo post stopped at that moment on the eve of the Battle of Taierzhuang**: “I watched these young soldiers walk past me one by one. At that moment I turned from a child into an adult, vaguely knowing that these very young soldiers were passing through Shashi, heading straight from Xinyang to Taierzhuang, and in the end not one of them would be able to return home, not one would come back.” He added beneath it: “A child becomes an adult not by age, but by state of mind.”[^11] The post was dated July 24, 2025 — ten days before he died in Pittsburgh. He spent his life writing about ancient Chinese history, Han agriculture, and Western Zhou feudalism, but the beginning and end of his own life were both connected to that riverside road in Hubei in 1938.

Amid war, limited mobility instead became Hsu Cho-yun’s time for reading. He later called himself “a fortunate person”: “When I studied, I met good teachers; when I married, I met a good wife; even my congenital disability brought me benefits — more time to stay at the desk.”[^12]

In 1949, the Hsu family retreated to Taiwan with the Nationalist government, and the 19-year-old Hsu Cho-yun entered the Department of History at National Taiwan University. That generation of intellectuals who crossed the sea from mainland China to Taiwan with their learning brought academic traditions originally established in Beiping and Shanghai to Taipei, where they put down new roots. In that environment, Hsu Cho-yun built the foundations in Chinese studies that would allow him to converse with Western scholarship[^13].

## Under Herrlee G. Creel at Chicago: Bringing the Social Sciences into Chinese History

Hsu graduated from NTU’s Department of History in 1953 and earned a master’s degree from NTU’s Graduate Institute of Liberal Arts in 1956. That same year, he went to the University of Chicago for further study, lived at the Chicago Theological Seminary, and studied under the sinologist Herrlee G. Creel[^14].

At that time, American Chinese studies was undergoing a methodological transformation. Traditional “Sinology” centered on textual interpretation, philology, and evidentiary research; “China Studies,” by contrast, began to introduce tools from the social sciences, including sociology, anthropology, economics, and political science, treating China as an object that could be analyzed by modern academic disciplines. In 1962, the year Hsu received his doctorate, he stood precisely at this turning point[^15].

His doctoral dissertation was later revised into _Ancient China in Transition: An Analysis of Social Mobility, 722–222 B.C._, published by Stanford University Press in 1965. The book used quantitative methods to analyze social mobility during the Spring and Autumn and Warring States periods: which aristocratic families disappeared from the political stage, which emerging classes moved upward through military achievement or learning, and how the social structure loosened on the eve of Qin-Han unification. After reading it, the Harvard sinologist John K. Fairbank praised it as “a little classic”[^16].

📝 **This book was also a methodological seed for Taiwan’s historical profession**. When Hsu returned to Taiwan in 1962, he was 32. He first became an assistant research fellow at Academia Sinica’s Institute of History and Philology (concurrently from 1956, promoted to associate research fellow in 1962), and in 1964 he also became professor and chair of NTU’s Department of History (1964-1970)[^17]. During those six years, he did something that would later deeply transform historical studies in Taiwan: **he introduced the methods, schools, and issues of the social sciences into Taiwan’s history classrooms**. As department chair, he hosted workshops and international conferences, invited methodological debates from the American historical profession into NTU, and trained a group of students who would later hold important positions across the historical fields of Taiwan, China, and Hong Kong[^18].

The three major works he later completed — _Han Agriculture: The Formation of the Early Chinese Peasant Economy_ (English edition, University of Washington Press, 1980)[^19], _Essays in Search of Antiquity_ (Lien Ching, 1982)[^20], and _A History of the Western Zhou_ (Lien Ching, 1984)[^21] — can be read as three self-applications of this methodology:

| Work                            | Period Covered       | Methodological Tools                                                                                                                             |
| ------------------------------- | -------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| _Han Agriculture_               | Han dynasty          | Quantitative reconstruction of peasant life and the mutually reinforcing relationship between market-economic networks and intensive cultivation |
| _Essays in Search of Antiquity_ | Antiquity to Qin-Han | Evolution of the civil bureaucracy linking center/locality and politics/society as paired axes                                                   |
| _A History of the Western Zhou_ | Western Zhou         | Origins of “Huaxia” consciousness built through lineage groups                                                                                   |

He himself described the problems handled in these three books as the “three primary colors of Chinese culture”: three cross-sections of one historian’s continuous thirty-year inquiry into the same set of fundamental questions, rather than three unrelated studies[^22].

## Half a Century in Pittsburgh: A Big-History Method that Refused Sinocentrism

In 1970, Hsu Cho-yun was 40. He immigrated to the United States with his wife Sun Man-li and their eight-month-old only son, Leo Hsu, and joined the Department of History at the University of Pittsburgh[^23].

He spent 28 years at Pittsburgh (1970-1998), rising from visiting professor (1970-72) to tenured professor (1972-82), university professor (1982-98), and, after retiring in 1998, emeritus university professor until his death. The University of Pittsburgh retained his title for a total of 55 years[^24]. At the same time, he never left Taiwan’s academic world: from 1981 to 1997 he served as a corresponding research fellow at Academia Sinica’s Institute of History and Philology, and after 1989 he was successively appointed distinguished research fellow until 2006. Academia Sinica in Taipei, the Pittsburgh campus, and lecture itineraries across Taiwan, China, and Hong Kong — these three coordinates formed the map of the second half of his academic life[^25].

During the Pittsburgh period, he did two structural kinds of work: first, he continued to deepen the methodology of using the social sciences to enter Chinese history; second, he applied that methodology on a larger scale — looking not only at one dynasty, and not only at China itself, but placing China comparatively within the context of world civilizational history[^26].

📝 **The term “big history” was later most widely associated in Chinese with Ray Huang, but Hsu Cho-yun practiced a different kind of big history**: Huang focused on why China could not break out of traditional modes of governance (_1587, A Year of No Significance_; _China: A Macro History_), while Hsu focused on how Chinese civilization itself was shaped through contact with other civilizations — the steppe, India, Islam, the West — while also shaping others. His core conviction was a refusal of “Sinocentrism”: to understand China, one must place China back into the context of world history, rather than treating China as a self-sufficient, isolated, closed system evolving only according to its own internal logic[^28].

In 1989, he took part in founding the Chiang Ching-kuo Foundation for International Scholarly Exchange. The foundation had been proposed in 1986, when Hsu, Yu Ying-shih, Kwang-chih Chang, and other ethnic Chinese professors teaching in North America collectively wrote to President Chiang Ching-kuo; worried about the decline of overseas Chinese studies, they hoped Taiwan would fund an international academic grant institution to promote sinological research[^29]. After the foundation was established on January 12, 1989, Hsu served as chair of the North American Committee, a position he held until shortly before his death — 36 years in all[^30].

Among the students he trained during the Pittsburgh period were Wang Fan-sen, later director and vice president of Academia Sinica’s Institute of History and Philology, and Tu Cheng-sheng, who served as minister of education and director of the National Palace Museum[^31]. Together with contemporaries and long-term collaborators such as Hsing I-tien and Chen Jo-shui, “Hsu Cho-yun’s student circle” formed a highly influential network in historical scholarship across Taiwan, China, and Hong Kong from the 1980s to the 2010s.

## Family Genealogy: From Hsu Feng-tsao to Wang Leehom, Three Generations of Chinese-Language Cultural Elites

Hsu Cho-yun was the seventh of nine siblings — which is why Wang Leehom called him “seventh granduncle” in his tribute[^32].

Laid out as a family lineage, it becomes an overlooked map of postwar Taiwanese culture:

| Sibling Order                  | Name            | Main Identity                                                                                                                                                                                                                           | Link to the Next Generation                                                                                                                           |
| ------------------------------ | --------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Eldest sister                  | Hsu Liu-fen     | Graduate of Tsinghua University’s Department of Economics, chair of accounting and statistics at Taipei College of Business, author of _Principles of Accounting_ and _English-Chinese / Chinese-English Dictionary of Accounting_[^33] | → married Wang Hsin-ming → son Wang Ta-chung (neurosurgeon) → grandson **Wang Leehom**[^34]                                                           |
| Second younger sister          | Hsu Wan-ching   | Graduate of National Southwestern Associated University                                                                                                                                                                                 | → married Li Mo (former vice minister of education and economic affairs) → son **Lee Chien-fu** (original singer of “Descendants of the Dragon”)[^35] |
| Seventh (elder twin brother)   | **Hsu Cho-yun** | Academician of Academia Sinica, historian                                                                                                                                                                                               | (main subject of this article)                                                                                                                        |
| Seventh (younger twin brother) | Hsu Yi-yun      | Chemical engineering scholar, chair of the Executive Yuan Atomic Energy Council[^36]                                                                                                                                                    | —                                                                                                                                                     |
| (Others)                       | —               | —                                                                                                                                                                                                                                       | —                                                                                                                                                     |

In other words, Hsu Cho-yun held two roles within this family: he was Wang Leehom’s “seventh granduncle,” and he was also Lee Chien-fu’s uncle. Wang Leehom and Lee Chien-fu are cousins: Hsu Liu-fen was Wang Leehom’s grandmother, Hsu Wan-ching was Lee Chien-fu’s mother, and Hsu Liu-fen and Hsu Wan-ching were sisters.

The Hsu family was a Wuxi official-gentry household. The father, Hsu Feng-tsao, was a rear admiral in the Republic of China Navy; the mother, Chang Shun-ying, came from a scholarly family. In the next generation in Taiwan, the family spread into accounting, the vice-ministerial offices of the Ministry of Education, academia, the popular-music industry, and nuclear-energy policy. In the third generation, the line reaches the lyrics and music of “Descendants of the Dragon” in 1980, written and composed by Hou Dejian and first sung by Lee Chien-fu, the son of Hsu Cho-yun’s younger sister Hsu Wan-ching, and Wang Leehom, a representative figure of Chinese-language popular music in the 2000s — two branches of the same Wuxi family[^37].

📝 **This family line is worth writing about seriously not because of gossip**. It precisely shows how the cultural capital of a “second-generation mainlander” intellectual family that crossed to Taiwan with the Nationalist government in 1949 could, within three generations, be dispersed into several of postwar Taiwan’s most important fields: scholarship (Hsu Cho-yun), Ministry of Education policymaking (Li Mo), accounting as a profession (Hsu Liu-fen), nuclear technology (Hsu Yi-yun), and popular music (Wang Leehom and Lee Chien-fu). Roots in China, growth in Taiwan, academic training in the United States, and transmission across the Chinese-language world — one family, five outlets.

On August 5, 2025, Wang Leehom posted on social media to mourn this “seventh granduncle,” writing about the childhood of his father, Wang Ta-chung, a neurosurgeon and graduate of National Taiwan University’s medical school[^38]:

“One of my father’s warmest childhood memories was the bedtime stories his seventh uncle read to him. His seventh uncle, Hsu Cho-yun, was frail and often ill from childhood and congenitally disabled, but his inner world was extraordinarily rich, and his wisdom was beyond compare.”

“Although he was the seventh of nine siblings and had physical impairments, what he left the world was a towering silhouette.”

“His frame may have been thin and small, but his spirit was tall as a mountain; everything he left behind will never be forgotten. Mr. Hsu Cho-yun, may you rest in peace. Thank you, seventh granduncle. May God bless you with rest.”

The detail about bedtime stories was later amplified by many media outlets: every night, Hsu Cho-yun would invent a serial story for this nephew, Wang Ta-chung, many of them improvised, with lessons about life hidden inside[^39]. An uncle who could not run or jump with his legs held his little nephew through stories; decades later, that nephew’s son spoke to global Chinese-language audiences through popular music. From the rear admiral Hsu Feng-tsao’s generation to Wang Leehom’s generation of Golden Melody Award-winning singers, with a man who wrote Chinese history with two fingers in between, this line itself is a miniature history of 20th-century Chinese diaspora.

## _China: A New Cultural History_: Writing China as a Long River for an Age of Common Readers

In 2006, _China: A New Cultural History: The Turns and Developments of Chinese History and Culture_ was published by Hansheng Publishing in Taipei[^40].

This 270,000-character book was Hsu Cho-yun’s history of China written for “the age of common people.” In his preface, he wrote: “Today’s readers are different from those of the past. In this age of common people, most who have received a high-school education or above may take an interest in history. This book is a history written for this generation of Chinese people.”[^41]

📝 **To “popularize” scholarship is a higher demand, not a lowering of the threshold**. To write for high-school graduates while also earning the nod of university professors is a rare ability in any language. The strategy of _China: A New Cultural History_ is to abandon the linear chronicle of “dynastic succession” and instead make “the diffusion and contraction of culture” the central axis: Chinese civilization is like a great river, growing from a trickle into a torrent, absorbing tributaries along the way, while also changing course, flooding, and drying up.

His points of entry are the questions ordinary readers actually ask: What did common people in the medieval period eat? What differed between Yangtze River culture and Yellow River culture? Why did Taiwan appear in Chinese history only in the modern period? What did Confucius mean by “ren”? Why did China not develop Western-style industrialization? Each of these questions could be a popular essay on its own, but Hsu Cho-yun assembled them into a single book[^42].

After publication, _China: A New Cultural History_ circulated widely in Taiwan, Hong Kong, mainland China, and overseas Chinese communities, accumulating sales in the millions over two decades, with simplified-character editions successively introduced by SDX Joint Publishing and Guangxi Normal University Press in mainland China[^43]. It is a popular work, but it was also accepted by the academy. A history book that can be read by professors and by high-school graduates alike is a rare achievement in the 21st-century Chinese-speaking world.

He then used the same framework of “civilizational big history” to write three sister works in succession:

**_Self and Other: The Internal and External Boundaries in Chinese History_ (2009, China Times Publishing / Chinese University Press)**[^44] — This book directly addresses the historical problem of how China defined itself and how it defined others. Hsu proposed six systems for analyzing the formation and breakdown of “self/other” relations: interactions between China and other nations or peoples; interactions between China proper and peripheral groups; interactions between central regimes and local society; interactions between upper and lower social strata; market-economic networks; and cultural interactions between the mainstream “orthodox” order and “heterodox” challengers. He wrote with great restraint: “Regarding the ‘inside/outside’ and ‘self/other’ relations of complex systems, the most important point lies not in ethnic distinction, but in cultural cognition.”[^45] Against the tense political identities of Taiwan, China, and Hong Kong in 2009, this was a deliberate widening of historical scale.

**_Huaxia Discourse: The Transformation of a Complex Community_ (2015, Guangxi Normal University Press)**[^46] — This book pushed one step further: he argued that China should not be defined from a “fixed frame” — what China looked like in a particular dynasty or period — but should be understood from “process,” from how China changed. Concretely, he decomposed this into the dynamic interweaving of four variables: political power, economy, society, and cultural ideas.

**_Jingwei Huaxia_ (CITIC, 2023 / revised traditional-character edition, Hong Kong Joint Publishing, 2024)**[^47] — This was the final major work that the 93-year-old Hsu completed with two fingers after eight rounds of revision. When Xu Zhiyuan visited him in Pittsburgh in 2019 and returned in 2023, the book was still being written. The final traditional-character revised edition of 2024 added two long essays, about 20,000 characters in all, on the “Great Flood” and “interactions between the northern steppe and the Central Plains”[^48].

📝 **Taken together, these four books form one historian’s comprehensive reexamination of the concept “China”** — from the long-river metaphor of _China: A New Cultural History_, to the internal/external boundaries of _Self and Other_, to the processual definition in _Huaxia Discourse_, to the enlargement of spatial vision in _Jingwei Huaxia_, from continent to ocean. In his later years, Hsu Cho-yun explicitly wrote “Chinese history” as “the history of China’s relations with the world.” This was also the language of the 2024 Tang Prize citation: “Professor Hsu’s historiography grasps the large picture. His specialized studies of ancient China excavate essential problems in long-term history; his interpretations of general history emphasize cultural inclusiveness and exchange, seeking China’s position in the world.”[^49]

## Guide to the Works: Forty Books, Five Approaches

Hsu Cho-yun wrote nearly forty books in his lifetime, covering ancient history, Han social history, Western Zhou feudalism, overseas Chinese studies, problems of Chinese identity, and, in his later years, notes on contemporary America and personal self-settlement. Readers encountering him for the first time do not need to begin with the heavy academic monographs. The following guide suggests five approaches in reading order:

**1. Entry Point: Chinese Big History for Common Readers**

- **_China: A New Cultural History: The Turns and Developments of Chinese History and Culture_ (2006, Hansheng)**[^40] — His most widely circulated book. A long-river perspective on civilizational diffusion and contraction, offering within 270,000 Chinese characters a Chinese cultural history without dynastic boundaries.

**2. Advanced: The Internal and External Boundaries of Civilization**

- **_Self and Other: The Internal and External Boundaries in Chinese History_ (2009, China Times Publishing)**[^44] — Addresses the problem of Chinese identity through a six-system framework.
- **_Huaxia Discourse: The Transformation of a Complex Community_ (2015, Guangxi Normal University Press)**[^46] — Defines “China” processually, through the interweaving of political power, economy, society, and culture.
- **_Jingwei Huaxia_ (2024, revised traditional-character edition, Hong Kong Joint Publishing)**[^47] — Revised eight times, expanding the framework “from continent to ocean.”

**3. Academic Representative Works: The Social Sciences Enter Chinese History**

- **_A History of the Western Zhou_ (Lien Ching, 1984; second edition 1986; revised third editions 1990/1993; expanded Beijing SDX edition 1994; expanded new edition 2020)**[^21] — A study of the origins of “Huaxia” consciousness through lineage groups.
- **_Han Agriculture_ (English edition, University of Washington Press, 1980; later translated into Chinese as _Han Agriculture: The Formation of the Early Chinese Peasant Economy_)**[^19] — A quantitative reconstruction of Han peasant life and market-economic networks.
- **_Ancient China in Transition_ (English edition, Stanford University Press, 1965)**[^16] — Revised from his doctoral dissertation; a quantitative analysis of social mobility in the Spring and Autumn and Warring States periods; praised by Fairbank as “a little classic.”
- **_Essays in Search of Antiquity_ (Lien Ching, 1982)**[^20] — The evolution of center/locality civil bureaucracy from antiquity to the Qin and Han.

**4. Autobiography and Late Thought**

- **_The Journey of the Mind_ (Wen Hsing, 1964 / Biography Literature, 1979 / Xiamen University Press, 2015)**[^50] — A young autobiography written at 34, tracing his intellectual path from Xiamen to Taipei to Chicago.
- **_Sixty Years of Vicissitudes in America: Observations of a Chinese Person_ (Lien Ching, 2019)**[^51] — His observations from half a century in the United States and his diagnosis of American society.
- **_Go Inward, Settle Yourself_ (Beijing Daily Press, 2022, coauthored with Feng Junwen)**[^52] — Reflections written at 89, a book of self-settlement for an agitated age.

**5. Popularization and Interviews**

- **The five-volume _Hsu Cho-yun on History_ series**[^53] — A popularized version of big history.
- **_Hsu Cho-yun’s Ten-Day Talks: The World Order Today and Humanity’s Future_ (Hong Kong Joint Publishing)**[^54] — A late-life compilation of his thinking on the world order.

📝 **If you can read only one book, start with _China: A New Cultural History_**. If you want to understand his historical method, the preface to the expanded new edition of _A History of the Western Zhou_ is more powerful than the book itself. If you want to understand his overall view of China, _Self and Other_ is the most condensed. If you want to understand Hsu as a person, read _The Journey of the Mind_ together with _Go Inward, Settle Yourself_.

## The 2024 Tang Prize: Donating the Full NT$50 Million to Establish the Hsu-Sun Scholarship

On June 20, 2024, the Tang Prize Foundation announced the laureate of the sixth Tang Prize in Sinology: Hsu Cho-yun. The prize carried NT$50 million, including NT$10 million in research grant funding[^55].

He was the first Tang Prize laureate in Sinology who, under a strict definition, was formed in Taiwan. The previous five laureates — Yu Ying-shih (Harvard), Stephen Owen (Harvard), Hazama Naoki (Kyoto), Wang Gungwu (Singapore), and Erik Zürcher (Leiden) — had all completed secondary education and foundational university training overseas. Hsu Cho-yun, by contrast, crossed to Taiwan with the Nationalist government in 1949, graduated from NTU’s Department of History in 1953, and earned his master’s degree from NTU’s Graduate Institute of Liberal Arts in 1956; he was a scholar who had “truly grown up in Taiwan”[^56].

⚠️ **The Tang Prize citation directly identified the core of his historical work**: “Professor Hsu’s historiography grasps the large picture. His specialized studies of ancient China excavate essential problems in long-term history; his interpretations of general history emphasize cultural inclusiveness and exchange, seeking China’s position in the world. His writings reveal concern for the state, the nation, and humanity in the world; he is a historian of both erudition and public-minded moral commitment.”[^49]

But what received still broader coverage was his next act after winning: he donated the entire NT$50 million prize to establish the “Hsu-Sun Scholarship,” supporting young scholars around the world pursuing doctoral research in Sinology[^1]. The scholarship was jointly named for Hsu Cho-yun and his wife Sun Man-li, recognizing Sun’s support over 55 years of his academic career.

📝 **The meaning of this act was not only generosity**. For Hsu Cho-yun, who had taken part in founding the Chiang Ching-kuo Foundation in 1989 and long chaired North American grants for Chinese studies, “the cultivation of the next generation of sinologists” was one of his lifelong central tasks[^29]. Donating the entire Tang Prize converted “what my generation has accomplished” directly into “whether the next generation will have the chance to continue.” For a 94-year-old historian with failing eyesight and complete dependence on others for movement, this was the most concrete act of transmission he could make.

In late September 2024, Tang Prize Week was held in Taipei. Because of health reasons, Hsu could not attend in person and delivered his laureate lecture, “Finding a Way in a Confused World,” by video. That same month, Xu Zhiyuan’s _Thirteen Invitations_ organized the two interviews he had conducted with Hsu in Pittsburgh since 2019 into season 8, episode 1. On camera, Hsu spoke most about his unease with the current world: he saw the tearing apart of American society, China’s bewilderment, and Taiwan’s anxiety, and used the phrase “I cannot help but think of the late Qing” to describe the present international order[^57].

## An Academic Bridge Across the Strait: Keeping Distance Beyond “Sinocentrism”

One of Hsu Cho-yun’s most repeatedly cited positions in his later years was his critique of “Sinocentrism”[^58].

He argued: “I hope I will no longer hear talk of ‘China is formidable.’”[^59] This position made him “not Chinese enough” in the eyes of Chinese nationalists, and “too Chinese” in the eyes of some Taiwan-centered historians. Yet this was exactly the position he wanted: not choosing sides within any nationalist narrative, but using the longer time scale of “China’s relations with the world” to examine the political anxieties of the present cross-Strait situation[^60].

⚠️ **In September 2024, a remark he made in an interview with the Central News Agency was widely quoted**: “Trump’s ‘protection fee’ talk is like gangsters in the foreign concessions.”[^61] This was said in the days around his sixth Tang Prize laureate lecture. He compared the current American posture toward allies to the posture of foreign powers toward China during the treaty-port concession era of the late Qing. The analogy itself was an application of the “big history” method: anxieties over the current international order have historical counterparts when viewed at the scale of the long river.

Toward Taiwan, Hsu always maintained a special emotional attachment. In his 2024 CNA interview, he told the Taiwanese reporter[^3]:

“Taiwan makes me long for it and feel regret. I always hope that one day the waters over the Strait will be calm, that brothers on the two sides will shake hands, that neither side will remain hostile, and that there will no longer be divisions between them.”

“I do not regret it. I have fulfilled the responsibility I ought to fulfill for Taiwan.”

Before his death, he had already purchased a burial plot in Wuxi and had his gravestone engraved in advance, hoping to be buried with his parents after he died — “fallen leaves returning to their roots” was his choice for his own final destination[^62]. On the gravestone he quoted Lu You’s “To My Son”: “I only grieve that I cannot see the Nine Provinces united.” This line led commentators on both sides of the Strait to read different meanings into it: some took it as “regret over Chinese unification,” while others read it as a deeper sorrow that no political narrative could fully contain his life of displacement.

📝 **This position was extremely difficult**: he went to university in Taiwan, earned his doctorate in the United States, spent half a century in Pittsburgh, had millions of readers in China for _China: A New Cultural History_, and held the identity of an Academia Sinica academician in Taiwan. Each identity carried expectations for him, and no single identity could fully contain him. What he ultimately chose was: “I have fulfilled the responsibility I ought to fulfill for Taiwan.” This responsibility was defined by himself, crossed the levels of political position and national identity, and settled on “using the long scale of a historian to offer Taiwan, China, and Hong Kong a shared measure of time for understanding one another.”

Hsu Cho-yun repeatedly said throughout his life: “History tells us that any civilization, at the moment it is strongest, is also brewing its crisis. Conversely, at the moment it is weakest, it often also buries the seeds of revival.”[^63] He spent his life writing Chinese history, but what he truly cared about was the act of looking between the upstream and downstream of the long river. Dynastic strength or decline was only the surface. What a 95-year-old historian typed out with two fingers was a dose of temporal calm for an agitated age.

## Death at 95, Leaving a Long River for Future Readers

On August 3, 2025, Hsu Cho-yun died in his sleep in Pittsburgh, United States, at the venerable age of 95. His wife, Sun Man-li, was by his side[^64].

Academia Sinica issued a press release evaluating its 13th-class academician: “Academician Hsu Cho-yun was learned in both Chinese and Western traditions and was a globally renowned leader in the field of history. He specialized in ancient Chinese history, social history, and cultural history; his writings were abundant and deeply original. His vision was broad: with the perspective of world history, he explored ancient Chinese history without limiting himself to China.”[^25] Mainland China’s Taiwan Affairs Office made the unusual public statement that his passing was “deeply regrettable.” This event of simultaneous mourning on both sides of the Strait was itself the final annotation to Hsu Cho-yun’s position[^65].

On August 5, Wang Leehom posted on social media to mourn his “seventh granduncle.” That same day, Lee Chien-fu also posted in memory of this “uncle” — the original singer of “Descendants of the Dragon” and the songwriter of “Love Because It Is in the Heart,” descendants of the same Hsu family compound in Wuxi, saying goodbye across three generations to the same boy on crutches[^66].

In early August, the University of Pittsburgh’s Department of History issued a brief In Memoriam: “Renowned Historian Passes Away at 95.” The university where Hsu Cho-yun had spent 55 years sent him off with a four-word headline[^67].

Yeh Kuang-shih, former minister of transportation and a collaborator at the Chiang Ching-kuo Foundation, wrote a passage recalling his last meeting with Hsu Cho-yun: “Hsu Cho-yun was not only an excellent scholar; his conduct as a person and in handling affairs was extraordinary. He was an important driving force behind the founding of the Chiang Ching-kuo Foundation.”[^68]

Hsu Cho-yun left behind nearly forty books. The earliest, _The Journey of the Mind_, was published in 1964, when the author was 34; the last, the revised edition of _Jingwei Huaxia_, appeared in 2024, when the author was 94. Across 60 years, he used a pen — and later two fingers — to pursue the same long river of Chinese history.

✦ On the riverside road in Shashi, Hubei, in 1938, Sichuan troops passed before him on their way to Taierzhuang.
On the deck at Keelung Harbor in 1949, the flag of the Nationalist government fluttered in the wind.
In the University of Chicago library in 1962, he first used social-scientific methods to write about the Spring and Autumn and Warring States periods.
In the Academia Sinica meeting hall in 1980, he was elected to the 13th class of academicians.
In the display window of Hansheng Bookstore in 2006, the first printing of _China: A New Cultural History_ went on the shelves.
At the desk in his Pittsburgh home in 2024, he used two fingers to finish the eighth draft of _Jingwei Huaxia_.
On July 24, 2025, he wrote on Weibo: “A child becomes an adult not by age, but by state of mind.”
On August 3, 2025, he drew his last breath in his sleep.

“At the moment I breathe my last, I will still be learning.” He said this in an interview at 94; one year later, it came true.

What he left behind is a long river that continues to flow, without a final answer.
