In early 2019, he joined the Toronto Raptors. During the entire playoffs, he played only 27 minutes; in Game 3 of the Finals, he appeared for 51 seconds.[^2] But the Raptors won the 2019 NBA championship, and Lin became the first Asian American player to receive an NBA championship ring.

After winning the ring, no NBA team signed him again. At a testimony event, he broke down crying: "It feels like the NBA's kind of given up on me."[^12]

## "Coronavirus"

In 2021, Lin returned to the NBA system with the Santa Cruz Warriors, the Golden State Warriors' G League affiliate. During one game, an opposing player called him "coronavirus" on the court.[^13]

He responded publicly: "We are tired of being asked where we really come from, tired of having our eyes mocked, tired of being treated as invisible." His remarks prompted a formal G League investigation, and Warriors head coach Steve Kerr publicly voiced support.[^13]

That game condensed the entire Asian American condition in the COVID-19 era into a single nickname from an opponent's mouth.

## Returning to Taiwan

After leaving the NBA in 2019, Lin first played one season in China's CBA with the Beijing Ducks.[^14] In late 2022, he made a decision that shook Taiwan's basketball world: joining the New Taipei Kings of P. League+.

In the 2023-24 season, he scored 33 points in a crucial Game 6 playoff matchup against the Dreamers, leading the Kings to the first championship in franchise history.[^3]

The 2024-25 season was the inaugural year of the Taiwan Professional Basketball League (TPBL). At age 37, Lin won regular-season MVP, All-TPBL First Team, and All-Defensive First Team. Then, in Game 7 of the Finals, he scored 27 points with 5 rebounds and 4 assists, defeating the Kaohsiung Aquas 108-89 and becoming the first player in TPBL history to win both regular-season MVP and Finals MVP.[^3]

At the end of August 2025, he announced his retirement. On December 28, 6,800 fans at Xinzhuang Gymnasium watched the Kings retire his No. 7 jersey. Jay Chou appeared in person to pay tribute.[^15]

## An Illogical Trajectory

State champion, zero scholarships. Harvard graduate, undrafted. Cut twice in 15 days. Jersey sales surpassing Kobe. Fifty-one seconds in the NBA Finals. Double MVP in Taiwan at age 37.

Jeremy Lin's professional career never fit anyone's expectations - including his own. But precisely because it was illogical, this trajectory permanently changed the answer to the question of how far Asian players could go.
