# Jimmy Liao: The Yilan Illustrator Who Brought Picture Books to the World Stage After Beating Blood Cancer

> **30-second overview:** Jimmy Liao, born Liao Fu-bin on November 15, 1958, in Yilan, graduated from the Department of Fine Arts at Chinese Culture University and worked at an advertising agency for twelve years.[^1] Diagnosed with blood cancer in 1995, he turned to picture book creation full-time after recovery. In 1998, he published his first two picture books, _Secrets in the Forest_ and _The Smiling Fish_, in the same year, with _Secrets in the Forest_ being his debut.[^2] His best-known work, _Turn Left, Turn Right_ (2001), was adapted into a film (2003) co-directed by Johnnie To and Wai Ka-fai.[^3] Jimmy Liao Square officially opened in Yilan in late June 2013, drawing on three picture books—_Starry Starry Night_, _Sound of Colors_, and _Turn Left, Turn Right_—as its textual foundation.[^4] From March 28 to April 19, 2026, the Harbour City Art Gallery in Hong Kong hosted the 25th-anniversary special exhibition _Glimmers of Chance Encounters_.[^5]

## 1958, Yilan

On November 15, 1958, Jimmy Liao was born in Yilan.[^1] He loved drawing from a young age and studied in the Department of Fine Arts at Chinese Culture University, receiving formal art training. After graduation, he joined an advertising agency as a graphic designer, spending twelve years in commercial design and accumulating extensive visual design experience.[^1]

Those twelve years of advertising training formed the invisible foundation of Jimmy Liao's later picture book work. An advertising designer must convey a clear message with the fewest possible visual elements in the shortest possible time. This discipline gave Jimmy Liao's picture books their characteristic rhythm of "one emotion per page"—no redundant visual storytelling. His books may look "simple and warm," but behind that simplicity lies the precision of commercial visual design.

## Blood Cancer Diagnosis: From Advertising Art to Picture Book Creation

In 1995, at the age of 37, Jimmy Liao was diagnosed with blood cancer.[^1] Facing a life-or-death crisis, he decided during the long course of treatment to leave his advertising work behind and devote himself entirely to the painting and illustration he truly loved.

His recovery from blood cancer had two inseparable effects on his creative work. First, a complete restructuring of his sense of time: he shifted from the ad man's "week-by-week" time structure to the picture book creator's "year-by-year" time structure. Second, a shift in emotional themes: his picture books repeatedly engage with loneliness, waiting, hope, and missed connections—themes whose density and weight can only come from someone who has stood at the edge of death.

## Two Debut Picture Books in One Year: Sweeping All Three Major Book Awards

In 1998, Jimmy Liao published his first two picture books in Taiwan: _Secrets in the Forest_ and _The Smiling Fish_, with _Secrets in the Forest_ being his debut.[^2] Both works received the China Times Open Book Award for Best Children's Book, the Min Sheng Daily Good Books for Everyone Annual Best Children's Book Award, and the United Daily News Reader's Best Children's Book Award. Sweeping Taiwan's three major book review awards with a first work is an exceptionally rare achievement for a debut author.[^2]

These works immediately resonated in the Taiwan book market. Readers discovered that they could touch the hearts of adults: formally picture books, but substantively written for grown-ups. The category of "picture books for adults" was new to the Taiwan publishing market at the time—it was neither children's literature, nor pure literary fiction, nor comics. Jimmy Liao's work pioneered this in-between space.

## _Turn Left, Turn Right_ and Its Film Adaptation

_Turn Left, Turn Right_, published in 2001, is one of Jimmy Liao's most celebrated works. The story of two people who love each other but keep missing their chance encounters was later adapted into a film.

The film was **co-directed by Johnnie To and Wai Ka-fai**, starring Takeshi Kaneshiro and Gigi Leung, and was released in 2003 in Hong Kong, Taiwan, and other territories.[^3] It was not Johnnie To alone—Wai Ka-fai was co-director, and the two have a long history of collaboration in Hong Kong cinema; Wai Ka-fai also served as producer and screenwriter for the film.

Other film adaptations include _Sound of Colors_ (2003, directed by Ma Wai-ho, starring Tony Leung Chiu-wai and Miriam Yeung) and _Starry Starry Night_ (2011, directed by Tom Lin Shu-yu). Adaptations have also spanned musicals, animation, and television dramas, making Jimmy Liao **the Taiwan picture book author with the broadest cross-media adaptation footprint**.

## Jimmy Liao Square, Yilan: The First Public Space Dedicated to a Single Picture Book Author

Jimmy Liao has deep affection for his hometown of Yilan. In the spring of 2013, the architectural firm "Fieldoffice Architects" invited Jimmy Liao to collaborate on a public art space. **Jimmy Liao Square** officially opened in late June 2013, located about 200 meters south of Yilan Railway Station.[^4]

The square draws on three picture books—_Starry Starry Night_, _Sound of Colors_, and _Turn Left, Turn Right_—as its textual foundation, proposing the concepts of of "travel" and "fragmented landscapes of life."[^4] During the 2014 "Happy Yilan New Year" event, a flying train entered Doudoudang Forest, and giraffes climbed onto the rooftop balcony of Yilan Railway Station, linking Jimmy Liao Square, Doudoudang Forest, and the train station into an extended picture book universe.

Jimmy Liao Square is **Taiwan's first large-scale public art space themed around a single picture book author** and one of the most recognizable landmarks on Yilan's tourist map. Its significance goes beyond tourism: a county-level train station with no prior strong connection to art and culture was redefined as a "cultural space" through the work of a picture book author—a successful case of public art intervening in urban memory in Taiwan.

## 25 Years of Sustained Creation

From his first picture book in 1998 to the present, Jimmy Liao has continued to publish new works at a pace of roughly one to two books per year. Other representative works include _Sound of Colors_ (2001), _The Moon Forgets_ (1999), _A Flower Blooms in My Heart Every Day_ (1999), and _Starry Starry Night_ (2009).

Unlike many authors whose creative energy diminishes after a single breakout work, Jimmy Liao's output frequency and thematic range have remained steady over 25 years. This endurance is the fundamental reason he has been able to keep producing without being boxed in by a single style.

## 25th-Anniversary Special Exhibition _Glimmers of Chance Encounters_: Harbour City, Hong Kong

Officially authorized by the Jimmy Liao brand and co-organized with Homework Design, the special exhibition **_Glimmers of Chance Encounters_** was held from **March 28 to April 19, 2026** at the Harbour City Art Gallery in Tsim Sha Tsui, Hong Kong.[^5] Centered on the core theme of "states of being," the exhibition was divided into three zones: "Life Always Catches You Off Guard," "When You're Holding On Alone," and "Landscapes in the Glimmer of Chance," presented through layered progressions of two-dimensional artwork, light and shadow, and three-dimensional sculpture.

The choice of Hong Kong as the venue for the 25th-anniversary special exhibition echoes the historical connection of the _Turn Left, Turn Right_ film, which was directed by the Hong Kong team of Johnnie To and Wai Ka-fai: Jimmy Liao's work has a cross-generational readership base in Hong Kong, and this exhibition is a response to that cross-strait connection.

**Common framing → more precise reading:** Jimmy Liao is often positioned as a "warm, healing picture book author." But a more precise reading is that his work **engages with loneliness, waiting, and missed connections—themes that are not warm at all**. He simply wraps these themes in visual language that makes them bearable. The "healing" label obscures the true weight of his work's inner core. He does not avoid pain in his picture books; he makes pain readable.

> 🎙️ **Curator's note:** The 1995 blood cancer diagnosis was the true starting point of Jimmy Liao's career—not a continuation of his twelve years in advertising, but a complete redirection. Without that illness, he might still be the person doing graphic design in the advertising world, with picture books as a hobby.
>
> Publishing two picture books in the same year in 1998 and sweeping all three major book review awards—this debut was exceptionally rare in Taiwan's publishing history. It was not the typical path of "a newcomer gradually recognized by the market," but an explosive entrance where "the market recognized him in an instant."
>
> The success of Jimmy Liao Square established "**single author × public space × urban memory**" as a replicable cultural model. This model has since been repeatedly attempted in cultural promotion efforts across other counties and cities in Taiwan, but Jimmy Liao Square remains the most successful and most enduringly energetic case among them.

Jimmy Liao's picture book readership spans Taiwan, Hong Kong, mainland China, Japan, South Korea, and the broader Sinophone communities of Southeast Asia. He is **one of the Chinese-language picture book authors with the largest international readership base**. His works have been translated into English, French, German, Japanese, Korean, and other languages, giving the identity of "a picture book author from Yilan" a recognizable position in the global visual arts world.

From the 1995 blood cancer diagnosis, to the two debut books published in the same year in 1998, to _Turn Left, Turn Right_ in 2001, to Jimmy Liao Square in 2013, to the 25th-anniversary special exhibition at Harbour City, Hong Kong in 2026, Jimmy Liao's creative trajectory is a line that **begins with a personal life crisis and extends outward to the city and the world**. He is not a picture book author who exists only on the page; his work has grown legs, walking into city squares, into movie theaters, and into transnational exhibition spaces. The scope of this extension is the most concrete result of his 25 years of creative energy.
