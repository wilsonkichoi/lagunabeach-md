# Monaneng: Two Kinds of Blindness, One Book of Poems

![Landscape of Daren Township, Taitung County — mountains and ocean framing a Paiwan community, the Pacific visible in the distance. Monaneng was born in 1956 in the Aluwei settlement of this township (Anshuo Village).](/article-images/people/monaneng-daren-township-2011.jpg)
_Daren Township, Taitung County — the Aluwei settlement where Monaneng was born in 1956. Photo: Bernard Gagnon, 2011-06-08. [License via Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Daren_Township,_Taiwan.jpg) CC BY-SA 3.0._

> **30-second overview:** Maljaljaves Mulaneng (registered Han name: Tseng Shun-wang), a blind Paiwan poet born in 1956 in the Aluwei settlement of Daren Township, Taitung, is the **first indigenous poet to publish a collection of modern Chinese-language poetry in Taiwan**. _Beautiful Spikelets_ (《美麗的稻穗》), published by Morning Star in 1989, collects 30 poems and features a long preface by the writer Chen Yingzhen titled "The Poet of the Internal Colony of Taiwan." Monaneng did not lose his sight at birth — he became fully blind only after a car accident and concussion while working at a freight company in 1979. The lines in "Come, Let Us Drink" — "The slogan of eighteen million people seeking self-determination / cannot hear our sighs" — write into a single poem the nascent Taiwan independence movement, the Return Our Land movement of indigenous peoples, and the question of who counts as Taiwanese, all at the moment martial law had just been lifted. And yet Monaneng himself would later publicly oppose Taiwan independence, advocate for cross-Strait unification, and join the China Writers Association[^1]. There are two kinds of difficulty in reading him: physically, he cannot see; politically, many Taiwanese readers are unwilling to see his position.

## A Child of the Aluwei Settlement

June 3, 1956, Daren Township, Taitung County. A Paiwan settlement is called **Aluwei**, known in Chinese as **Anshuo Village**[^2]. A boy is born here. The community calls him **Maljaljaves Mulaneng**; the household registration office records the Han name **Tseng Shun-wang**[^3]. Later, when he writes poetry, he uses a romanized form of his indigenous name as his pen name: **Maljaljaves Mulaneng** — also rendered in Chinese characters as 馬列雅弗斯·莫那能.

He was not yet six when his mother died of tuberculosis[^4]. In middle school, his father went to prison after taking the blame for someone else's crime[^5]. Before the age of 16, he herded cattle back home in the village; after 16, he went to the city to work like a beast of burden[^4]. In 1972, at the age of 18, he was deceived by labor traffickers and taken to the city to work as a gravel laborer and porter[^5].

These details matter because many official literary archives write him up as a "blind poet who triumphed over adversity" — a convenient icon. His childhood was in fact a very common trajectory for indigenous mountain families in Taitung during the 1960s and 70s: a mother dead of tuberculosis, a father in prison, a childhood spent herding cattle, a teenage boy taken to a construction site by a broker. Every element of that story could be found in the lives of other indigenous youth at the time. He himself later wrote this trajectory into a poem called "Come, Let Us Drink," in memory of a friend from the settlement who never truly came home.

> 📝 **Curator's Note:** Monaneng did not encounter social issues through the act of writing poetry. He was first a direct participant in this trajectory, and only at the age of around 30 did he put the whole of it into verse. The sequence — experience first, poetry after — is essential to reading him clearly.

## A Car Accident Stole the Light

During a middle school health examination, he was told something: he had **retinitis pigmentosa**, a hereditary progressive disease causing the retina to degenerate. He would eventually go blind — it was only a matter of time[^6].

After middle school, he passed the entrance exam for the **Air Force Mechanical School**. He failed the medical screening upon reporting, because of the retinitis pigmentosa. His dream of the Air Force was over. He later considered the Taitung Teachers College and the Police Academy — both blocked due to his vision. For an indigenous youth from the mountains in the 1970s, the limited channels for upward mobility were closing one by one[^6].

Then came the trafficking in 1972. In 1977, his younger sister was sold into a brothel as a child prostitute by a cousin's husband. He borrowed money everywhere, waited outside the brothel in the dead of night, and rescued his sister; in the process he was beaten, and his eyesight deteriorated further[^7].

The decisive incident came in **1979**. He was working at a freight company when a car accident caused a severe concussion. He was in a coma in hospital for nearly two months. When he woke, his right eye was completely blind and his left eye had vision of 0.2[^8]. Wikipedia records that he was 23 at the time; the National Museum of Taiwan Literature dictionary says 24; interviews in _Indigenous Peoples' Horizons_ and _Observer_ magazine give ages of 26 and 27[^9]. Multiple versions circulate, but Monaneng's own oral account from 2022 clearly states: "1979, while I was working at the freight company"[^10].

Afterward, he went to the **Taiwan Blind Rehabilitation Center** to learn massage and Braille. He later ran a small massage parlor in Taipei, which he simply called "A-neng Massage"[^11]. He was not a "street masseur" — as the internet often mistakenly describes him — he had a physical shop. And it was in that shop, in Braille, that he wrote his poems. **But the poems did not begin with Braille.**

## Drinking at Yang Du's House, Chanting Poems into Being

The true origin of the poems is more complicated than the romantic image of "a blind man writing poetry in Braille."

In 1974 (some sources say early 1977), through a teacher at Tamkang University named Wang Chin-ping, he came to know a group of left-wing intellectuals: **Su Ching-li, Chen Yingzhen, and Chen Ku-ying**[^12]. Chen Ku-ying invited him to convalesce at his home for several months; the room was filled with books of left-wing thought, and Monaneng read them slowly with his half-blind eyes. At the end of 1978, he participated in his first political activity, helping Chen Ku-ying and Chen Wan-chen campaign for seats in the Legislative Yuan and National Assembly[^13].

In March 1984, at the home of **Yang Du** in Taichung, he was drinking, singing, and spontaneously chanting words in long riffs. The poet **Li Ji**, who was present, felt these should not be lost, and transcribed the oral utterances as poems. They appeared in the first issue of _Spring Wind Poetry Journal_ (《春風詩刊》) under the heading "Poems of a Mountain Person." The second issue carried what would become the most famous poem: "Come, Let Us Drink." This was the spring of 1984 — **a full five years before the poetry collection _Beautiful Spikelets_**[^14].

This sequence is worth reading slowly:

- Monaneng's poems **began as oral transmission** (drinking, singing, chanting)
- **Li Ji transcribed them** to produce the written text
- **Spring Wind Poetry Journal** published first (1984)
- **The Morning Star collection** came later (1989)
- **Chen Yingzhen encouraging Braille writing** was a later development, not the origin of the creative act

This Odyssey-like oral origin is the true basis for Chen Yingzhen's later characterization of Monaneng as "**the poet of the internal colony of Taiwan**" and for scholars comparing him to "**the Homer of Taiwan**." Homer's greatness lies not in his blindness, but in the fact that the tradition of oral epic lived on through him[^15].

## The Bell at Haishan Coal Mine, the Cane of the Indigenous Rights Association

At dawn on June 20, 1984, a coal-dust explosion struck the **Haishan Coal Mine** in Tucheng, Taipei County. **Seventy-two people died**, most of them Amis miners[^16].

In that Taiwan, the flow of indigenous people from eastern mountain communities to city mines, fishing boats, and construction sites was already commonplace, yet these people were nearly invisible in mainstream society. After the Haishan explosion, **Hu De-fu** organized a "Mountain Songs" benefit concert. In the process, he met Monaneng and others. That December, together with **Icyang Parod**, **Tung Chun-fa**, **Iwan Nogan**, and others, they founded the **Taiwan Indigenous Peoples' Rights Promotion Association** (TIPRPA, 臺灣原住民族權利促進會) — the first nationwide indigenous political organization in postwar Taiwan[^17].

Monaneng served in the TIPRPA as **convener of the Promotion Committee** and also as the **convener of the Tribal Task Force**[^18]. For the Amis workers who died at Haishan, he wrote the poem "Why?"

In the latter half of the 1980s, his figure — dark glasses, leaning on a cane — appeared at every pivotal moment of the indigenous rights movement[^19]:

- **September 1985:** Protest against the inauguration of the Wu Feng Temple in Chiayi; he organized the "No Myth, Give Us History" march.
- **January 9, 1988:** Taiwan's first ever "Rescue Child Prostitutes" street march. He wrote "When the Bell Rings — For Our Sister Survivors of Mountain Child Prostitution" in solidarity; the prototype was his own younger sister.
- **August 25, 1988:** The first "Return Our Land" movement march.
- **December 31, 1988:** The toppling of the Wu Feng bronze statue in front of the Chiayi train station.

⚠️ **Contested viewpoint:** Many Taiwanese readers under 30 today are familiar with the term "indigenous peoples" (_yuanzhumin_), know names like Tao and Truku, and understand the concept of "traditional territories" — all of this traces back to the street movements of 1984–1990. But over the past thirty years, as commemorative exhibitions, documentaries, and museums have packaged those movements as "historical turning points," it has become easy to forget how violently contested they once were. Monaneng's cane at the time was not a literary metaphor — it was the cane he actually depended on.

## Chen Yingzhen Writes the Preface: "The Poet of the Internal Colony of Taiwan"

In autumn of 1989, **Morning Star Publishing** printed _Beautiful Spikelets_ (《美麗的稻穗》) in Taichung[^20].

The title alludes to a song composed by Puyuma musician **Lu Sen-pao** (1910–1988) in 1958 — originally titled "Harvest Year" (〈豐年〉), later known as "Beautiful Spikelets" — written to comfort Puyuma soldiers serving on Kinmen during the 823 Artillery Bombardment[^21]. Lu Sen-pao wrote it for the Puyuma; Hu De-fu popularized it in the folk music movement of the 1970s; and **Paiwan** poet Monaneng appropriated it as his collection's title — the cross-ethnic borrowing itself was already a claim: the concept of "indigenous peoples" was being assembled, from above the level of individual nations, by this generation of activists.

The collection contains **30 poems**, organized into **5 sections**:

1. "Restore Our Names"
2. "Come Back, Shawmi"
3. "Song of the White Cane"
4. "We Will No Longer See the Dark"
5. Appendix

Chen Yingzhen wrote a long preface for the collection, titled "Monaneng — The Poet of the Internal Colony of Taiwan"[^22]. The same year, Monaneng received a cultural grant from the "Care for Taiwan Foundation"[^23].

"Restore Our Names" opens as follows[^24]:

> From "savages" to "mountain compatriots"
> our names
> have gradually been forgotten in the corners of Taiwan's history.
> From the mountains to the plains
> our fate — ah, our fate —
> is granted respectful treatment and concern
> only in the survey reports of anthropologists...

And it closes:

> If there comes a day
> when we are to stop wandering on our own land,
> please first restore our names and dignity.

In 1995, the Legislative Yuan passed amendments to the Name Act, formally granting indigenous peoples the right to register their tribal names. In the six years leading up to that moment, "Restore Our Names" was the poem most often read aloud at movement gatherings.

> 💡 **Did you know?** The first indigenous Taiwanese writer to write in Chinese was **not** Monaneng. In 1971, the Paiwan writer **Chen Ying-hsiung** (Kowan Talall) published the short story collection _Traces of Dreams Beyond the Borders_ (《域外夢痕》, Taiwan Commercial Press) — eighteen years before Monaneng. Monaneng's "first" is, strictly speaking, "**the first indigenous poet to publish a collection of modern Chinese-language poetry**" — poetry, not fiction, not prose. This distinction matters because the beginnings of indigenous Chinese-language literature in Taiwan were always plural, and should not be reduced to a single heroic narrative.

## That Line: "Eighteen Million People"

The most frequently cited and most contested poem in the collection is "Come, Let Us Drink" (〈來，乾一杯〉). It tells of a Paiwan friend named **Kara Bai** who traveled from the settlement to the city and then to a deep-sea fishing vessel — beaten in school as a child, dropped out of middle school, went west to work as a gravel laborer, went fishing, served in the military, shipped out on a deep-sea boat, and finally **died far from home in Cape Town, South Africa**[^25].

The poem contains this passage:

> The slogan of eighteen million people seeking self-determination
> cannot hear our sighs.
> Equality and fraternity, justice and righteousness
> abandoned us long ago.

In the late 1980s, "**eighteen million**" was the approximate population of Taiwan; "**self-determination**" was the core term of the _dangwai_ (outside-the-party) movement and later the founding discourse of the Democratic Progressive Party. Placing these four lines back on the timeline of 1984–1989 — the period spanning martial law's lifting, the height of the Return Our Land movement, and the self-immolation of _Freedom Era Weekly_ publisher Cheng Nan-jung in April 1989 — makes clear how much weight they carry. In these four lines, Monaneng addressed a direct challenge to the nascent Taiwan independence movement: **Does the "self-determination" you speak of include us?**

This challenge can be read in different ways:

- **A sympathetic reading:** Monaneng is speaking to the marginal position of indigenous peoples within the mainstream democratization narrative of the late 1980s. The Taiwan independence movement's framing of "eighteen million people" presupposed a Han-centric notion of "Taiwanese subjecthood," within which indigenous peoples were incorporated but not truly represented.
- **A critical reading:** Monaneng's opposition to the Taiwan independence movement remained outspoken as recently as 2022, indicating that the challenge voiced in the 1989 poem was already a long-held political position — and that position carries its own set of problems.

Both readings are valid. The key point is: this poem was **written in 1984** (Spring Wind Poetry Journal, second issue) — before the Democratic Progressive Party existed, before the Return Our Land movement had erupted. **These four lines existed first in Taiwan's literary history as a question; the answer remains suspended to this day.**

## A Pro-Unification Poet?

On June 16, 2010, the China Writers Association (中國作家協會) announced it had admitted **Monaneng, Chu Hsiu-chuan, and Chen Yingzhen** as its first three Taiwanese members[^26]. That same May, Renjian Publishing (人間出版社) released Monaneng's oral autobiography _The Experience of a Taiwanese Indigenous Person_ (《一個台灣原住民的經歷》)[^27]. Monaneng subsequently served as chairman of the **Chinatide Association** (夏潮聯合會) — an explicitly left-wing pro-unification organization[^1].

His public position is unambiguous: he advocates for cross-Strait unification, on the grounds that "**the more peaceful the Cross-Strait relationship, the more beneficial it is for indigenous peoples**"[^28]. Wikipedia characterizes his stance as: "long advocating that Taiwan's indigenous peoples are also Chinese, and that the cross-Strait peace brought by unification is most beneficial for Taiwan's indigenous peoples"[^1].

This position has left him in an awkward place within Taiwan's literary world since the 2010s. "Restore Our Names" continues to appear in middle school curricula and to be read aloud on Indigenous Peoples Day; yet the author himself traveled to Beijing and published in pro-unification journals. When "When the Bell Rings — For Our Sister Survivors of Mountain Child Prostitution" was selected for the 2019 middle school curriculum, a legislator objected to it as "indecent" and demanded its removal. The arguments against removal came from curriculum committee members and indigenous organizations — but Monaneng himself did not speak publicly in its defense[^29].

How should we read this double position? Three observations:

1. **The poems were written in the 1980s; the political position became public in the 2010s — thirty years apart.** "Come, Let Us Drink" (1984) asks whether "eighteen million people" includes indigenous peoples; in 2022 Monaneng says "the DPP has been hijacked by Taiwan independence." The latter is far more explicit than the former, but they are not the same statement.
2. **The pro-unification left is not Monaneng's personal idiosyncrasy.** His intellectual mentors — Chen Yingzhen, Chen Ku-ying, Wang Chin-ping, Su Ching-li, and that entire cohort of 1970s–80s left-wing intellectuals — were among the few voices in Taiwan of that era criticizing both Kuomintang authoritarianism and capitalist exploitation simultaneously. After Taiwan's nativist consciousness rose in the 1990s, they were gradually marginalized and drifted toward a Cross-Strait unification position. The intellectual community formed by the Chinatide Association, _Renjian_ magazine, _Straits Review_, and related publications traces a complex history: left-wing persecution during the Cold War, former White Terror political prisoners released from prison, and participants in the Protect Diaoyu movement returning to Taiwan and converging. Understanding Monaneng requires understanding this generational trajectory of the unification-left intelligentsia — not extracting him from it for isolated scrutiny. At the same time, understanding that trajectory does not mean endorsing its conclusions. Whether the anti-imperialism of the 1970s became, by the 2020s, support for China's sovereignty claims over Taiwan — and whether something essential was substituted in between — is another question that must be faced honestly.
3. **A political position does not cancel the literary value of the poetry, but nor should the literary value of the poetry cancel the political position.** That Monaneng's 1989 collection is written with great skill, and that he joined the China Writers Association in 2010 — **both are true**. Any account that tries to acknowledge only one of these is sanitizing.

> ✦ When _Observer_ magazine interviewed him in 2022, he put it even more directly: "**The DPP does not care about the interests of the working class or ethnic minorities, and has been hijacked by 'Taiwan independence,' heading down the dead-end road of splitting the country**"[^28]. This is an oral statement he made in a pro-unification publication. Readers who disagree with this judgment may disagree; but this is the position Monaneng explicitly stated in 2022.

## The Poems Keep Being Read; the Question Keeps Going Unanswered

After the 2019 controversy over the middle school curriculum selection of "When the Bell Rings," the poem stayed. "Restore Our Names" continues to be the emblematic poem of the indigenous name-restoration movement. "Come, Let Us Drink" continues to be rediscovered by new generations of Taiwanese poetry readers — typically in friend groups, independent bookstores, and documentary films about indigenous issues, not in middle school classrooms.

But one thing, as of 2026, no one has answered directly:

Thirty-seven years ago, a book of poems contained a blind man writing Chinese characters, asking in one poem: "**The slogan of eighteen million people seeking self-determination — does it include us?**" That question was radical in 1984. It remains radical in 2026. In those 37 years, Taiwan established an indigenous peoples' television channel, passed the Indigenous Peoples Basic Act, designated "Indigenous Peoples Day" as a national holiday, and wrote "traditional territories" into law — these are all good developments, all progress. But the question Monaneng was asking was not about these things. He was asking something more fundamental: **Are we genuine members of Taiwan's political community, or merely guests who have been invited in?**

That question has no answer. **The poem has not become history.** The poem continues to be the present.

And in the present, that blind poet — doubly blind: physically unable to see, and politically invisible to many Taiwanese readers who are unwilling to see his position — is still there. He turns 70 on June 3, 2026. The drink in "Come, Let Us Drink" has gone unfinished for thirty-seven years.

In the final section of the 1989 collection, "We Will No Longer See the Dark," he wrote this: **"If there comes a day / when we are to stop wandering on our own land / please first restore our names and dignity."** In 1995, when the Legislative Yuan amended the Name Act and indigenous peoples gained the right to register tribal names, half of that line came true. The other half — "**stop wandering on our own land**" — remains a question thirty years on. A question set down in Chinese characters by a blind man thirty-seven years ago, to which Taiwan in 2026 has still not delivered an answer.
