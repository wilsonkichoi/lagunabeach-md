# Chen Chien-nien: The Golden Melody King from the Police Station, Turning His Grandfather's Songs into Ocean

> **30-Second Overview:** Chen Chien-nien (Puyuma name Pau-dull), born August 1, 1967, in Nanwang, Beinan Township, Taitung County.[^1] He is the maternal grandson of Lu Senbao (Baliwakes, 1910-1988), the Puyuma song master and author of "Beautiful Ears of Rice."[^1][^2] After graduating from the 114th police officer class in 1986, he was assigned to Guanshan, Taitung, and went on to serve in the police for 30 years and 10 months.[^3] In 1999, at age 33, he released his first album of original songs, Ocean. At the 11th Golden Melody Awards in 2000, he defeated Jacky Cheung, Harlem Yu, David Tao, and Wang Leehom to win Best Mandarin Male Singer; at the same ceremony, "Myth," which he wrote for Samingad, won Best Composition.[^4][^5] At the moment he received the award, his full-time job was still police officer.[^3] In September of that same year, he requested a transfer to Lanyu and remained there until retiring in 2017.[^1][^6]

## Puyuma Pau-dull, and the Songs His Grandfather Left Behind

On August 1, 1967, Chen Chien-nien was born in Nanwang, Beinan Township, Taitung County: Sakuban, one of the eight Puyuma communities and among those that have most fully preserved Puyuma culture.[^1] His Puyuma name is Pau-dull. His father was Chen Kuang-jung; his mother was the daughter of Puyuma song master Lu Senbao (Baliwakes). Lu Senbao was therefore Chen Chien-nien's **maternal grandfather**, not his father.[^1][^2] This generational relation determines how the next thirty years of his music should be read. What his grandfather left behind were melodies remembered by an entire people; what the grandson had to do was receive them, hold them, and then write his own songs.

Lu Senbao was born in 1910 and graduated from Tainan Normal School, making him a rare Puyuma intellectual of the Japanese colonial period.[^2] After the war, he wrote a series of Puyuma-language songs: "Puyuma Mountain," "Ode to the Ancestors," "Beautiful Ears of Rice," "Thinking of Home," and "Love of Lanyu." Among them, "Beautiful Ears of Rice," later carried forward through Hu Defu's singing, became one of the first Indigenous songs heard by the outside world during Taiwan's folk song movement of the 1970s.[^2] Lu Senbao died of a cerebral hemorrhage in 1988. That year, his maternal grandson Chen Chien-nien was 21 years old, two years out of Taiwan Police College, and working as a police officer in Guanshan, Taitung.[^3]

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> Common online introductions often describe Lu Senbao as Chen Chien-nien's "father" or "paternal grandfather." Behind this error is, in fact, the seepage of Han Chinese ideas of lineage. For Mandarin readers, "father passes to son" is the presumed route of inheritance. But traditional Puyuma society is matrilineal, and the maternal grandfather is the true elder anchor in the family narrative. Chen Chien-nien's own wording is clear: he is Lu Senbao's "maternal grandson," and Samingad and Jiajia are his "nieces." Only when the kinship line is made clear does this line of inheritance become visible.[^1][^7]

## The Bangdi in Seventh Grade, the Guitar in Eighth Grade, and a 1984 Newcomer Award in Kaohsiung

Chen Chien-nien's musical starting point was not family tradition, but his school's Chinese orchestra. In seventh grade, his homeroom teacher founded a Chinese orchestra, and he learned the bangdi flute and nanhu.[^7] After entering eighth grade, with the campus folk song movement in full swing, he picked up the guitar and tried singing Lo Ta-yu's "Childhood." In high school in 1982, he and upperclassmen formed the "Four-String Choir," busking during China Youth Corps self-strengthening activities; his own composition "Once Felt" later became one of the songs the corps was required to teach.[^7]

In 1984, he entered the Kaohsiung Newcomer Original Song Competition, won a newcomer award, and recorded a compilation album with the other winners.[^7] He was 17 that year. But that path did not become a record contract. In 1986, he was admitted to the 114th police officer class at Taiwan Police College, Republic of China, and after graduation was assigned to Guanshan Township, Taitung.[^1][^3] He became a police officer.

> "I only wanted to have an album for keepsake!"[^8]

That was what Chen Chien-nien later told a Taiwan Panorama reporter when recalling the recording of Ocean. He had no ambition to become a singer; police work was the life he intended to live. But the songs kept being written and sung within Taitung's Indigenous communities. After thirteen years of daily police work, in 1999, at age 33, he encountered Trees Music.

## "Not Following the Usual Model of Pulling the Singer into a Taipei Studio"

Trees Music was founded in 1998. Its head, Chang Ssu-san, and producer Cheng Chieh-jen heard Chen Chien-nien in Taitung.[^9] The first decision they made was this: they would not pull the singer into a Taipei recording studio.

"Instead of following the usual model of pulling the singer into a Taipei recording studio, they moved the recording equipment to Taitung. Beyond recording the vocals, they also traveled through Taitung's valleys, streams, communities, and coastlines to gather sounds, pairing the most distinctively Taitung sounds with the songs."[^10]

The liner notes of Ocean include one record that turns recording technique into a kind of local gazetteer:

> "Source of the sound of ocean waves / Shanyuan coast, Dulan Bay, Taitung, 3:43 p.m., Yang's 25 degrees, southeast wind, clear skies, Green Island clearly visible in the distance."[^10]

This passage reads like a recording log and also like a local gazetteer: minute, temperature, wind direction, and visibility are all complete. A stretch of waves is annotated as historical material fit for citation. The Taipei industrial model of "singer plus arrangement plus session musicians" was pushed aside, replaced by "person plus place plus time plus one guitar."

In June 1999, Ocean was released under Trees Music catalog number TCM003.[^11] All 13 songs use guitar as the principal instrument. Chen Chien-nien wrote the lyrics, music, and arrangements himself, with a small number of tracks co-written with Lin Chih-hsing. Lin Chih-hsing is a Puyuma poet from Taitung who became known in the 1990s for his Indigenous-language poetry collection Tribal Rhythms, Village Affections: Betel Nut Poems. "We Are Compatriots," self-published in that poetry collection, was the poem Chen Chien-nien set to music and included on Ocean.[^12] Lin later described their collaboration this way in an interview: "Half of these songs are my life experience, and half are Chien-nien's life impressions."[^8]

What Chen Chien-nien wanted was not the R&B, ballad packaging, or layered synthesizers then fashionable in the Mandarin pop scene. What he wanted was for the sound of flying fish to be used like a synthesizer:

> "Just like the music of 'Betel Nut Brothers': only a few simple instruments, blended with the sounds of children and flying squirrels. That is the feeling I want."[^8]

After its release, Ocean was selected by the Association of Music Workers in Taiwan as one of the "Top Ten Albums of 1999."[^10] Years later, the album was ranked **number one** in the 1993-2005 section of Taiwan Popular Music: 200 Best Albums.[^10]

## Sun Yat-sen Memorial Hall: The Police Officer Among Four Heavenly Kings

April 29, 2000, Sun Yat-sen Memorial Hall. The 11th Golden Melody Awards ceremony.[^4][^5] The nominees for Best Mandarin Male Singer were Jacky Cheung, Harlem Yu, David Tao, Wang Leehom, plus Chen Chien-nien, a Puyuma police officer from Taitung.[^10] When the result was announced, David Tao, Wang Leehom, and Jacky Cheung all turned toward him to shake his hand and congratulate him.[^13] At the same ceremony, Chen Chien-nien also won Best Composition for "Myth," a song he wrote for Samingad, with lyrics by Lin Chih-hsing.[^4][^14] Samingad herself won Best New Artist that year.[^4]

Samingad is Chen Chien-nien's niece. Jiajia, or Chi Chia-ying, is Samingad's younger sister and also Chen Chien-nien's niece.[^14][^15] In other words, the 2000 Golden Melody Awards placed three generations of Puyuma music from the entire Nanwang community on the highest podium of the Mandarin pop world at the same time: Lu Senbao, absent and already deceased in 1988; Chen Chien-nien, Best Male Singer and Best Composition; and Samingad, Best New Artist.

How did international music criticism view that year? A Taipei Times preview published on April 27, 2000, quoted critics describing Chen Chien-nien's music as "sincere, pure and naturally touching," and said the nominations for Chen Chien-nien and Samingad represented "an affirmation of Aboriginal musical achievement."[^16] The outside world read the same signal: Indigenous music was no longer merely material to be "sampled" by pop lyricists, but a subject capable of standing onstage and winning mainstream awards on its own behalf.

But Chen Chien-nien, on that awards stage, was still a police officer by profession.

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> At the opening ceremony of the 1996 Atlanta Olympics, the German group Enigma's "Return To Innocence" sampled an Amis drinking song performed by Difang and Igay Duana. It was broadcast globally, and the subsequent royalties lawsuit was not settled until 1999. That was the largest instance of Taiwan Indigenous music being treated as "material" in the international commercial world. Four years later, when Chen Chien-nien and Samingad went onstage to receive Golden Melody Awards, the significance lay precisely along this line of contrast. Indigenous music again entered the mainstream, but this time the creators themselves stood under the brightest spotlight, rather than appearing as samples cut up by someone else.

## September of the Same Year: Requesting a Transfer to Lanyu

After winning an award, the most natural script would have been to sign with a major record company, go on television programs, mount concert tours, and become a star. Chen Chien-nien chose the opposite direction. In September 2000, less than five months after the Golden Melody Awards, he voluntarily applied to transfer from the main island of Taitung to Lanyu.[^1][^6]

Lanyu is an offshore island southeast of Taiwan proper. Its primary residents are the Tao, also known as the Yami, and its population is a little over four thousand.[^17] For a singer who had just won a Golden Melody Award, this was a form of self-exile to a place where the media could not call every day. Before his 2022 solo concert, Chen Chien-nien explained the atmosphere of that move to an ETtoday reporter: it was to "avoid the commotion."[^1][^13]

From September 2000 until his retirement in September 2017, he served intermittently on Lanyu for more than ten years in total, rotating through every police station on the island: Lanyu, Yongle, Dongqing, and Langdao. Before retirement, he was deputy head of the Lanyu Police Station.[^3][^6][^18] Outside police work, he hiked, dived, took photographs, learned Tao, and participated in the Tao flying fish festival and Indigenous rituals.[^6][^18] The police dormitory became his recording studio, and Lanyu's coastline became the source library for his next album.

In 2002, he released his second original album, Earth, issued by Trees Music. It reflected three years of creation across Lanyu, Taitung, and Taipei.[^19] On August 5, 2021, he released his third major original album, pongso no Tao, Tao for "Island of People," Lanyu's own name for itself. Its 17 songs all take Lanyu's natural environment as their creative source.[^20] At the 33rd Golden Melody Awards in 2022, pongso no Tao won Best Indigenous Language Album.[^20][^21]

That is to say, between the 2000 Best Mandarin Male Singer award and the 2022 Best Indigenous Language Album award lay 22 years. During those 22 years, he did not run off to become a major celebrity. He stayed in the police station, on Lanyu's beaches, and slowly wrote Lanyu into one long record.

## "We Are Compatriots": Lyrics Written in 1999 That Became a Song at a Disaster Relief Site in 2024

On Ocean there is one song whose meaning, heard from 2026, carries even more weight than it did at its 1999 release.

"We Are Compatriots," lyrics by Lin Chih-hsing, music by Chen Chien-nien. The core of the lyrics is this line: "Mountain people or plains people, we are all the people of this place; first inhabitants or later inhabitants, we are all residents of this place."[^12][^22]

The song was written in 1999, at the end of Lee Teng-hui's presidency, just after the Indigenous "name rectification movement" had replaced the old term "mountain compatriots" with "Indigenous peoples," and while the Legislative Yuan was still discussing the draft Indigenous Peoples Basic Law.[^17] Lin Chih-hsing distilled the everyday greeting "we are compatriots," used when Puyuma elders gathered, into a poem that accepted all later arrivals. Chen Chien-nien set it to brisk guitar and rhythm, avoiding a narrative of sorrow and giving it the tone of an invitation.

More than twenty years later, the song's afterlife became larger than when it was written. After the magnitude 7.2 Hualien earthquake on April 3, 2024, its lyrics were repeatedly quoted in disaster volunteer message groups, and the line "we are all the people of this place" became part of the spiritual undertone of cross-ethnic rescue work.[^23] The same song has also been repeatedly cited by a generation of younger political workers, including Audrey Tang, as one of the Taiwanese phrases most often invoked when discussing the coexistence of multiple identities.

> **✦** "Mountain people or plains people, we are all the people of this place."

When Chen Chien-nien wrote the music for this line, he was still wearing a police uniform during the day at the Guanshan Police Station in Taitung. As an agent of public authority, the people he dealt with every day were the mixed society of eastern Taiwan: plains people, mountain people, Han people, and Indigenous people. When "we are all the people of this place" became a song in his hands, its meaning was concrete. It was the eastern Taiwan society seen every day from the service window of a police station.

## Flag Platform, Police Motorcycle, Convention Center: Moving the Police Station Onto the Stage

Five years after retirement, Chen Chien-nien held his first large ticketed concert. On February 12, 2022, at the Taipei International Convention Center, 2,500 fans attended.[^13]

The stage was strange. There was a flag platform, a police motorcycle, and a Lanyu police station scene moved onto the stage. The concert opened with a flag-raising ceremony. Chen Chien-nien walked onstage in police uniform and gave this opening remark:

> "Because every police station has to raise the flag. The work of the police is to be loyal to the country and loyal to the leader. I share with everyone this heart of loyalty to the country."[^13]

Then he began to play and sing. He made a mistake in the first song, stopped, and told the 2,500 people in the audience:

> "I'm so nervous! Sorry, I'm so excited, because I haven't performed for a while."[^13]

A few songs later, he made another mistake and laughed at himself in a mixture of Japanese and Tao: "Sumimasen! I always make a mistake on the first song!"[^13]

What was special about this concert was not its scale: 2,500 people is a relatively modest number for a Golden Melody king from 22 years earlier. Its distinction lay in the design choice. Chen Chien-nien did not turn the stage into a medal show called "the Golden Melody king returns." He turned the stage into a police station. He put both of his past 30 years of dual identity, police officer plus singer, onto the stage at the same time, and let the police half raise the national flag first. The audience had to accept this contradiction in order to hear him sing.

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> For a rock narrative accustomed to taking an anti-establishment position, the sentence "the work of the police is to be loyal to the country and loyal to the leader" would normally be something to deconstruct in reverse. But when Chen Chien-nien said it, he was completely serious. This is why he cannot be absorbed into either side's narrative: he is neither a "rebellious Indigenous musician disguised as a police officer" nor "an obedient police officer within the system secretly making music." He is a person who believes in two things at once: loyalty to the land, loyalty to the state, and loyalty to the songs his grandfather left behind, all held side by side in the same body. Such coexistence is very ordinary in Taiwan's civil service system. So ordinary that, when he wrote something like "Ocean," the outside world was surprised that a person like this could write songs.

## After Retirement: The Second Half of Life

On September 1, 2017, Chen Chien-nien officially retired from his position as deputy head of the Lanyu Police Station, ending a police career of 30 years and 10 months.[^3][^6] In an interview with the Central News Agency on the eve of retirement, he said: "After retirement, I will return to a leisurely and natural life, but I will still continue on the path of music and create more and better music."[^3]

Several months later, he explained the deeper meaning of that decision to a Liberty Times reporter:

> "In the first half of life, I worked hard at my job. In the second half, I should spend more time with family and take good care of my health."[^6]

The retirement list he set out was also concrete: look after the family's land as a farmer; travel often to Lanyu to make ecological records; train his body to complete a cycling circuit around Taiwan, triathlons, full marathons, and ultramarathons.[^6] When he talked about Lanyu, he did not romanticize it: "Lanyu is full of conflicts of culture and values, and many things are very hard to change."[^6] Even his feeling when wearing the Tao people's traditional thong for the first time was stated directly: "The first time wearing it was really uncomfortable; the rough material chafes the skin."[^6]

Read together, these remarks show that he never packaged himself as a "spokesperson for Indigenous music" or a "Puyuma cultural ambassador." He acknowledged cultural conflict, acknowledged that Lanyu has problems that are difficult to change, and acknowledged that traditional clothing chafes when worn. Such details are often polished away in the usual media narratives about "diva-level Taiwan Indigenous singers," but Chen Chien-nien himself keeps them.

## Why This Person Matters to Taiwan

Placed back into the music history of late-1990s Taiwan, Chen Chien-nien's position is irreplaceable.

In 1996, Enigma's "Return To Innocence" sampled the voices of Difang and Igay Duana, triggering a lawsuit and prompting Taiwan society, on a large scale for the first time, to become aware of intellectual property issues in Indigenous music.[^24] In 1996, A-mei's debut album Sisters sold 4 million copies across Asia, and an Indigenous singer was truly seen by the mainstream market for the first time.[^25] In 1998, Trees Music was founded and opened another route through Chen Chien-nien, Panai, and Samingad: do not sign with a major company, do not leave the community.[^9] In 1999, Ocean was released; in 2000, it swept the Golden Melody Awards.[^4]

Across this timeline, Chen Chien-nien is the person who proved, at the highest commercial awards, that an aesthetic of "local recording, writing one's own words and music, playing one's own guitar, and not relying on commercial packaging" could work. Like A-mei, he is Puyuma and from Taitung, but he took the completely opposite path: A-mei went to Asia's largest stages; Chen Chien-nien retreated to Taiwan's farthest offshore island. Both paths are equally legitimate. But if Chen Chien-nien had not taken his path first, later Indigenous musicians such as Sangpuy, Suming, Panai, and ABAO, who won Golden Melody Awards after the 2010s, would have had one less precedent proving that one could be non-commercial and still receive the highest award.[^26]

A deeper layer is generational relay. Lu Senbao wrote "Beautiful Ears of Rice" in Puyuma in the 1950s, but it was not heard by the outside world until Hu Defu carried it forward in the 1970s;[^2] another thirty years passed before his maternal grandson Chen Chien-nien truly pushed that line onto the highest podium of Mandarin pop music in 2000. Three generations and fifty years later, that musical line was finally closed once. Chen Chien-nien later included "Beautiful Ears of Rice" on Ocean: in his own arrangement, he sang the song his grandfather had written.[^27]

> **✦** The grandfather left songs for a people; the grandson wore a police badge and wrote the ocean.

## Closing: The Guitar at the Police Station Window

On April 29, 2000, the day of the Golden Melody Awards, many of Chen Chien-nien's colleagues at the Guanshan Precinct and the Lanyu, Yongle, and Dongqing police stations where he had rotated later found out only afterward that the colleague who stood morning shifts and filled out incident reports with them was the Golden Melody king they had seen on television.[^3] What Chen Chien-nien himself did after winning was return to the police station for handover, then apply to transfer to the more distant Lanyu.[^1]

If you go to Lanyu today and ask local Tao elders about him, they may not say "Golden Melody king Chen Chien-nien." They may say "the police officer who could fix water and electricity" or "the deputy station chief who could play guitar."[^6] That is the position Chen Chien-nien chose for himself. The Golden Melody Award is a name others gave him; police officer is the position he answered to every day; the songs of his grandfather are the responsibility he played into the night. All three identities exist in one body at the same time, without coming in separate stages.

And that guitar, first plucked at home in Taitung in eighth grade in 1980, then recorded into Ocean at 3:43 p.m. on the Shanyuan coast of Dulan Bay in 1999, then carried on his back after the flag-raising ceremony at the Taipei International Convention Center in 2022, is still being played today in 2026.

Chen Chien-nien did not begin writing songs in 1999. He had been writing songs in Taitung's communities since the 1980s, writing for twenty years without being heard by the mainstream market, and then was suddenly discovered by the outside world in 2000. Nor did he only retire in 2017. He walked into a police station in 1986, made songwriting something he did after work, and did that for 30 years.

The songs his grandfather left behind were not something he forcefully "inherited." He simply, slowly, one song at a time, played them again himself.
