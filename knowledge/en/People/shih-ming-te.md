# Shih Ming-teh: Twenty-Five Years Behind Bars, He Finally Raised a Flag Against His Own Party

> **30-second overview:** Shih Ming-teh was imprisoned twice in his life, spending a cumulative total of over twenty-five years behind bars. After the 1979 Kaohsiung Incident, he refused to plead guilty at his military tribunal, instead requesting the death penalty, and was ultimately sentenced to life imprisonment. After his release, he helped build the Democratic Progressive Party (DPP), serving as party chairman from 1994 to 1996. In 2006, he launched the Million-People Anti-Corruption (Red Shirt) Movement against Chen Shui-bian, raising a flag against the very cause he had once fought for. On January 15, 2024, he died of liver cancer on his 83rd birthday.

## The First Prison Gate at Twenty-One

In 1962, Shih Ming-teh was arrested for involvement in political organization activities and sentenced to life imprisonment.[^1] He was twenty-one years old. Taiwan was still under the harsh grip of martial law, and the fate of political prisoners often depended on the will of the rulers rather than any legal procedure.

He spent thirteen years in prisons on Green Island and Taiwan proper. In 1975, Chiang Kai-shek died and the government declared a general amnesty, leading to Shih's release.[^2] He was thirty-four when he walked free—half his youth already spent behind iron bars.

## The Last Summer of the Dangwai Movement

In the late 1970s, Taiwan's Dangwai ("outside-the-party") movement found relatively more room to operate under the dual pressures of international scrutiny and domestic demands for reform. Shih Ming-teh became a core organizer, actively connecting reform-minded individuals across the island.

In 1979, he co-founded _Formosa Magazine_.[^3] With its calls for democratic reform and human rights protections, the publication quickly became the spiritual stronghold of the Dangwai movement, linking dissidents across Taiwan who had never before known each other. That summer and autumn, in retrospect, were filled with the undercurrents of an era approaching its end.

## Kaohsiung, December 10, 1979

On December 10, 1979—International Human Rights Day—_Formosa Magazine_ held a human rights rally in Kaohsiung. Crowds gathered, tensions escalated, and the event ultimately erupted into violent confrontations between riot police and demonstrators, an episode that became known as the "Kaohsiung Incident" (also called the _Formosa_ Incident).[^3]

After the incident, Shih Ming-teh went into hiding under an assumed identity, evading capture for several weeks before ultimately being apprehended.[^1] The Kuomintang (KMT) government launched a wave of mass arrests, charging the core members of _Formosa Magazine_ with sedition. Shih was listed as the mastermind.

## That Defense Brief Written for History

On March 28, 1980, Shih Ming-teh stood before the military tribunal to deliver his final statement.

He had originally prepared a 60,000-word "political testament" to read aloud in court. But just weeks earlier, the family of Lin I-hsiung had been massacred in Taipei—Lin's mother and twin daughters were killed, and his wife was seriously injured.[^4] Upon learning the news, Shih chose to set aside the 60,000 words and spoke directly:

**"Sentence me to death! I request it! I request it!**"[^5]

That 60,000-word defense brief was later published under the title _Shih Ming-teh's Political Testament_. In it, he wrote: "I do not expect a fair verdict from this worldly 'court,' but I firmly and without doubt believe that **the court of history will one day vindicate me!**"[^5]

The court's final verdict: life imprisonment. It was the second time Shih Ming-teh had been sentenced to life. In prison, he went on hunger strikes to protest the unjust trial and was subjected to forced feeding over 3,000 times.[^1] In 1990, as Taiwan's political environment evolved, he regained his freedom. His two imprisonments combined totaled more than twenty-five years.[^1]

> 📝 **Curator's note:** When Shih Ming-teh said "sentence me to death," on the surface it looked like self-destruction, but in the courtroom context it was in fact a rejection of the entire logic of the trial—he refused to acknowledge that the court possessed any moral authority to "sentence" him to anything. The accused, from a moral vantage point, looked down upon the tribunal. The reason the Kaohsiung Incident became a pivotal turning point in Taiwan's democratic transition owes much to this courtroom scene.

## Release, and the DPP's Founding Years

In 1986, while Shih Ming-teh was still in prison, the Democratic Progressive Party (DPP) declared its founding even before the ban on new political parties had been lifted.[^6] After his release, he devoted himself to building up the party and served as DPP chairman from 1994 to 1996.[^6] His stature derived in part from an unquestionable history of sacrifice, and in part from his rare commitment to principle.

In 2000, Shih Ming-teh voluntarily withdrew from the DPP—he was not expelled; he chose to leave on his own.[^2]

## 2006: Raising a Flag Against His Own Party

In 2006, corruption scandals surrounding DPP President Chen Shui-bian erupted one after another. Shih Ming-teh, in his personal capacity, launched the "Million-People Anti-Corruption Movement," standing on Ketagalan Boulevard in a red shirt and demanding the president's resignation.[^7] The Red Shirt Army sustained street protests in Taipei for over a month, drawing hundreds of thousands of participants.

The common criticism was that he had compromised his legacy and become a tool of the pan-Blue camp. Koo Kwang-min publicly called it a case of "**losing his integrity in his later years**."[^2]

There is a problem with this line of criticism: Shih Ming-teh's twenty-five years of imprisonment were never in service of a party—they were in service of a principle: that any politician, regardless of party affiliation, who walks the path of corruption must be opposed. When the DPP, after taking power, did things he found unacceptable, he opposed the DPP. The party changed positions; his logic did not.

He once put it this way regarding Taiwan's sovereignty: "**Taiwan should be independent, and in fact it has already been independent for over thirty years. Its current name is the Republic of China.**"[^5] For him, this sovereignty did not belong to any party to guard, nor did it need any party to speak for it.

## Later Years: That Principle Was Ultimately Vindicated

The Red Shirt movement ended without Chen Shui-bian's resignation, and Shih Ming-teh endured sustained attacks from DPP supporters for years, bearing the labels of "traitor" and "pan-Blue tool."

The conventional narrative was that he had lost his integrity and become a pawn of anti-Taiwan-independence forces. But subsequent developments offered a different reading—in 2009, Chen Shui-bian was convicted on corruption charges and actually sent to prison.[^2] The political judgment that the head of state was corrupt and should step down was ultimately confirmed at the judicial level. His methods were criticized, his timing was questioned, but the core argument—"a corrupt ruler must be opposed, regardless of party"—received a peculiar verdict from history: his conclusion was right; only he was in the wrong camp.

Afterward, Shih Ming-teh gradually faded from mainstream media attention. His health declined year after year, but he continued to give interviews from time to time, offering views on Taiwan's sovereignty and political trajectory, holding fast to a standard that never shifted for the sake of party.

## January 15, 2024

In the early hours of January 15, 2024, Shih Ming-teh died of liver cancer at Taipei Veterans General Hospital, aged 83.[^8] That day was also his birthday.

Born January 15, 1941; departed January 15, 2024.

In that defense brief written for history, he left these words: "Every era has its devotees. Devotees always play tragic roles... **The narrow, winding paths that devotees crawl through will surely be trodden into broad roads by those who follow. Devotees do not belong to today, but they will live on in tomorrow!**"[^5]

In 1980, that thirty-nine-year-old man stood in court and said, "Sentence me to death." In 2024, whether that narrow path has become a broad road—each Taiwanese person has their own answer. But he walked it to the end, birthday to birthday, a full eighty-three years.
