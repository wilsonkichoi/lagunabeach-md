# Bobby Chen: From Changhua, Debut Album 1988, Three Decades of New Year's Eve Shows Are His Calling Card

> **30-second overview:** Bobby Chen, born Chen Chih-sheng on October 29, 1958, in Xizhou Township, Changhua County, released his first solo album _Crowded Paradise_ in 1988, establishing himself in the Taiwanese music scene with a distinctive folk-rock style and poetic lyrics. Since 1994, he has held a New Year's Eve concert every year—thirty consecutive years without interruption—making it the longest-running annual ritual in Taiwan's music world. After undergoing oral cancer surgery in 2020, he recovered and returned to the stage; in 2025, he held his 31st New Year's Eve show, "Big Wave."

## October 29, 1958, Xizhou, Changhua

On October 29, 1958, Bobby Chen was born in Xizhou Township, Changhua County.[^1] His given name is Chen Chih-sheng. He grew up in the rural central Taiwan countryside—not the urban, loneliness-soaked Taipei that would later populate his music.

The rural backdrop of central Taiwan left an indelible imprint on Chen's music: a creative perspective steeped in乡土情感 (local, earthy sentiment) yet framed through an urban lens recurs throughout his lyrics. He set out from Changhua and settled in Taipei, and that migration left him with a permanent outsider's vantage point—able to see urban loneliness, able to feel it, but never fully belonging to it.

Early in his music career, he joined the "New Structure Chorus." In 1987, the group disbanded, and Chen began pursuing a solo creative path—a turning point that defined a singular voice in Taiwan's independent music.

In later interviews, Chen rarely dwelled at length on the moment the chorus dissolved. But the significance of that turning point is clear: a collective form ended, and he chose to continue—just as a single voice. That decision to "continue" is the foundational setting of Bobby Chen's entire career: his own time, his own direction, his own rhythm.

## 1988: _Crowded Paradise_

In 1988, Bobby Chen released his first solo album, _Crowded Paradise_.[^1] The album blended folk, rock, and blues elements into a sound distinctly at odds with the mainstream pop of the era, paired with intensely personal lyrics.

The mainstream of late-1980s Taiwanese pop was the sweet, sentimental ballad and the TV variety show. Chen's _Crowded Paradise_ was not on that track at all—his voice was hoarse, his lyrics poetic yet unassuming, not designed for mass radio play. This choice was commercially risky; in music history terms, it was a clear declaration of position.

He did not chase the television mainstream; he walked his own road. Works like "Leave the Sadness to Myself," "However," and "Kite" revealed his observations of urban loneliness and the details of everyday life—melancholic yet laced with humor, poetic yet grounded.

Chen's subsequent musical trajectory spanned folk, rock, blues, glove puppet show scores, and Hakka music, never confined to a single stylistic framework. This cross-genre quality is sometimes described as his "eclectic style," but a more precise reading is that his music has always been a direct output of lived experience, and life itself does not have only one sound. Form follows feeling, not the market.

## 1994: The First New Year's Eve Show

In 1994, Bobby Chen held his first New Year's Eve concert.[^2] It was small in scale from the start—a gathering of friends, on a Taipei street, counting down to midnight with a group of old companions.

That original format actually reveals the DNA of Chen's New Year's Eve shows: not a meticulously designed commercial concert, but an annual form of togetherness. The "smallness" of that beginning fits precisely with his definition of the event—people show up, they cross the last second of the year together, and that's it.

But this December 31st appointment continued. And continued for thirty years.

From that first street-gathering scale to later shows at the Taipei International Convention Center drawing thousands, Chen's New Year's Eve concerts grew in form, but the structure never changed: he on stage, the audience below, singing what he wants to sing, counting down at the moment that feels right. For thirty years, this core has never shifted.

## The Last Night of the Year, for Thirty Years

Bobby Chen's New Year's Eve concerts have their own logic: not a megashow, but a Bobby Chen-style gathering. He shares reflections on the year, sings new songs and old ones, talks and chats with the audience. The form of the performance itself is an extension of his musical philosophy.

The most widely known trait is that he does not count down at the stroke of midnight—he waits until the emotional arc of the singing reaches that moment, then counts together with the audience. He has said: "**Time is defined by yourself. I like to count down with everyone at the right emotional moment, following the order of the songs.**"[^5] That non-midnight countdown has become the signature of his New Year's Eve shows.

The common narrative is that Chen's New Year's Eve concerts attract die-hard fans through "unique style." But a more precise reading is this: for thirty years, he has never tried to "scale up" the event—the size can change, but the essence of the New Year's Eve show as "an occasion for togetherness" has never changed. This decision not to expand is itself anti-market logic, and it is precisely this quality that makes his New Year's Eve concerts an irreplaceable presence.

Chen's annual New Year's Eve concert has become a yearly coordinate for many Taiwanese on New Year's Eve—what draws people has always been its Bobby Chen flavor, nothing to do with scale.

Chen's New Year's Eve concerts occupy a position in Taiwanese music history that cannot be defined by a single framework of "commercial success" or "artistic achievement." They are a thirty-year interpersonal commitment—every December 31st, he and several thousand people in the same space, together at some emotional moment around midnight, counting down those final seconds. The persistence of this commitment is a record without parallel in Taiwan's music world.

## 2020: After Oral Cancer Surgery

In 2020, Bobby Chen faced the most severe reality of his career: oral cancer surgery.[^3] For a musician whose livelihood is his voice, the implications were profound.

He underwent treatment, recovered, and returned to the stage. The New Year's Eve appointment was not interrupted.

Within the narrative framework of "a singer who had oral cancer surgery," the returning Bobby Chen could easily be packaged as an inspirational story. But he has never described himself that way—his attitude toward music has always been: "**I'm not a very active independent figure or revolutionary, just someone who likes freedom and speaks rather straightforwardly. It doesn't matter how the audience misunderstands me—having come this far, isn't that what it means to be a free person?**"[^3] Rehabilitation and returning to the stage was a person who likes to sing continuing to sing—not a heroic comeback.

The impact of oral cancer surgery on a singer-songwriter is far-reaching on a technical level: vocal cord control, articulation clarity, and vocal range can all be permanently altered by surgery. Chen's post-return performances represent the expressive approach he found under his new vocal conditions—he did not wait to return to his "pre-surgery state," but continued singing from the state he was in now. This choice is more authentic than a rehabilitation success story.

## 2025: The 31st Show, "Big Wave"

In 2025, Bobby Chen held his 31st New Year's Eve concert, themed "Big Wave."[^4] The number itself is a statement—from 1994 to 2024, thirty years without stopping.

Before the concert, he described the theme of that year's show: "**Life is like ocean waves—rising and falling, you have to hold your breath.**"[^4] Coming from someone of his age, those words carry deeper weight—they are both a lyrical image and a personal interpretation of life's rhythm from someone who has survived oral cancer surgery and thirty years of New Year's Eve shows.

The "Big Wave" theme also applies to a description of his entire career: over thirty years, he has weathered periods of mainstream incomprehension, waves of commercial competition, and the trials his body handed him. Each wave made him rise and fall, and each time he found a way to continue within that rising and falling—not by resisting, but by holding his breath, waiting, then surfacing.

At this New Year's Eve show, he also did something he had not done in twenty-two years: performing solo, singing and playing guitar on stage in public. He said his wish for this year's show was "to find his way back to his original intention, to make himself cry."[^6] That original intention refers to the state of the very first New Year's Eve show in 1994—small venue, intimate, emotion in the moment. Thirty years later, he still remembers where he started.

"Returning to one's original intention" is sometimes commercial packaging rhetoric in the Taiwanese music scene. When Chen says it, he says it in the context of the 31st New Year's Eve show—someone who has held them for thirty consecutive years, who has never leveraged the New Year's Eve shows for a public listing, saying in front of cameras that he wants to cry. Those tears are not performance; they are a person's most honest statement to himself, on the thing he has done the longest.

He is now in his sixties, has undergone surgery, has weathered all those years of storms. He is still on stage.

## From Changhua to Taipei, to That Non-Midnight Countdown

Born in Xizhou, Changhua in 1958, first album in 1988, first New Year's Eve show in 1994—this line has run for thirty years. It is not a line drawn by commercial calculation, but a line drawn by a person following his own definitions of music and time.

Every year in Taiwan, hundreds of singers work hard to "stay visible," dozens try to "maintain market position." Neither of these verbs appears in Chen's vocabulary. His vocabulary is "sing," "freedom," "spend New Year's Eve with friends"—small verbs, long actions. Thirty years prove one thing: in the music industry, the most enduring presence often comes from the person who calculates longevity the least.

The common narrative is that Chen built a loyal fanbase by being "anti-commercial." But a more precise reading is: he never decided to be "anti-commercial"—he simply never left his original coordinates: a person from Changhua who likes freedom, speaks straightforwardly, and tells the truth through lyrics, who found that December 31st occasion in Taipei where he and his audience can end the year together.

By the 2010s, Taiwan's New Year's Eve concert market had become fiercely contested commercial territory: A-Mei, Mayday, free fireworks shows hosted by city and county governments. Chen's New Year's Eve shows do not compete with these, nor do they attempt to match their scale—his venue is the Taipei International Convention Center, the audience numbers several thousand, tickets are self-purchased, year after year. This choice of scale is his only form of large-scale public statement: he does not need year-end charts, does not need television broadcast, only needs those several thousand people willing to sing with him around midnight.

That non-midnight countdown is the smallest version of Bobby Chen's entire calling card: time, defined by himself. For thirty years, he has counted at his own pace, never aligning his countdown with anyone else's—this insistence explains more clearly than any lyrics what kind of person he is.
