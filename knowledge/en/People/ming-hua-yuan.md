# Ming Hua Yuan: Founded in Tainan in 1929, the Four-Generation Chen Family's Taiwanese Opera Empire

> **30-second overview:** Ming Hua Yuan was founded by Chen Ming-chi in Tainan in 1929 and relocated to Chaozhou, Pingtung in 1962.[^1] The second-generation leader Chen Sheng-fu introduced modern theater technology—stage rigging, water-screen projections, aerial flying acts—and his signature *Ji Gong the Living Buddha* series brought Taiwanese opera into the arena-scale theater format.[^2] In 1982, the troupe debuted at the Sun Yat-sen Memorial Hall; in 1990, it entered the National Theater.[^2] Eight sub-troupes operate under the banner: Tian, Di, Xuan, Huang, Ri, Yue, Xing, and Chen.[^3] In 2024, *Sanxi* (*The Curtain Call*) toured for the fourth time, with performances at the National Taichung Theater and the Weiwuying National Kaohsiung Center for the Arts, marking the troupe's 95th anniversary.[^4]

## 1929, Tainan

The story of Ming Hua Yuan begins in 1929. Chen Ming-chi founded the Taiwanese opera troupe in Tainan, where it started as a itinerant outdoor stage (*yetai*) company following temple festival circuits—a mobile folk troupe that traveled with temple celebrations.[^1]

In 1962, the troupe relocated to Chaozhou, Pingtung, putting down roots in southern Taiwan. Some sources incorrectly list Chaozhou, Pingtung as the founding location; the authoritative record is Tainan, 1929.

In its earliest years, Ming Hua Yuan was a temple-circuit outdoor troupe performing for temple festivals in the Tainan area. This performance model, dependent on the temple economy, was the primary means of survival for Taiwanese folk theater during the Japanese colonial period—troupes needed no fixed venue and sold no tickets, sustaining themselves through temple performance offerings and sponsorship from nearby merchants.

Within this structure, Chen Ming-chi built his troupe and, over the following three decades, established Ming Hua Yuan's reputation at temple stages across southern Taiwan. This audience base laid the groundwork for the second-generation leader Chen Sheng-fu's later modernization efforts.

## The Second Generation: Chen Sheng-fu's Theater Revolution

When Chen Sheng-fu took over, Taiwanese opera was under assault from television and film. Most troupes disbanded or transitioned to television opera. Chen Sheng-fu made the opposite bet: he insisted on live performance but upgraded stagecraft to modern-theater standards.

The essence of this choice was a counter-market gamble—television opera reached larger audiences at lower cost and lower risk, but Chen Sheng-fu was betting on "the irreproducible impact of being there in person." He saw not the crisis of television compressing Taiwanese opera, but a differentiation opportunity: when everyone was moving to television, the live theater became scarce.

He introduced lighting, sound systems, stage rigging, aerial flying acts, and water-screen projections, making every Ming Hua Yuan production a visual spectacle. The signature *Ji Gong the Living Buddha* series staged flying sequences on the proscenium, drawing audiences who had never before set foot in a Taiwanese opera performance. *The Super Dazzling Legend of the White Snake* and *The Immortal of Penglai* also wove traditional vocal technique with contemporary dramatic tension through modern adaptations.[^2]

The modernization process was simultaneously an audience reconstruction project: traditional temple-stage audiences came in with their outdoor-theater habits, and Chen Sheng-fu held them with visual spectacle; at the same time, new urban audiences stepped into a Taiwanese opera venue for the first time because of the "spectacular staging," encountering a traditional art form they would not have actively sought out. The overlap of these two groups is the structural reason Ming Hua Yuan could continue to fill seats in the television era.

## 1982 Sun Yat-sen Memorial Hall, 1990 National Theater

In 1982, Ming Hua Yuan debuted at the Sun Yat-sen Memorial Hall—a key milestone marking a traditional folk troupe's entry into a major performance venue.[^2] In 1990, it entered the National Theater. The journey from temple-stage scaffolding to the nation's highest performance hall took roughly 30 years.

The 1982 Sun Yat-sen Memorial Hall performance was a watershed in Ming Hua Yuan's institutional history: it proved that Taiwanese opera, long regarded as "popular entertainment," could hold its own in a formal performance venue. This "it belongs in a theater" argument made the 1990 National Theater debut not a leap, but a logical extension.

Ming Hua Yuan has also toured internationally. It performed at the Festival d'Avignon in France (P0⚠️ pending confirmation)[^5], as well as touring Japan, Singapore, and Malaysia, bringing Taiwanese opera vocal technique and movement to the world stage.

## Eight Sub-Troupes: Tian, Di, Xuan, Huang, Ri, Yue, Xing, Chen

Ming Hua Yuan currently operates eight sub-troupes: Tian (Heaven), Di (Earth), Xuan (Mystery), Huang (Yellow), Ri (Sun), Yue (Moon), Xing (Stars), and Chen (Celestial Bodies).[^3] Each develops its own style, managed collectively by the third and fourth generations of the Chen family. This branching family-stewardship model is rare in Taiwan's traditional performing arts world.

The eight-troupe structure allows Ming Hua Yuan to appear simultaneously in different regions and at different scales: the Tian and Di troupes handle large-theater productions, while the other sub-troupes cover smaller temple stages and school performances. This flexible deployment is the organizational foundation for Ming Hua Yuan's ability to maintain a high performance frequency across Taiwan. With third- and fourth-generation family members each managing a sub-troupe, the inheritance structure does not depend on a single successor but is distributed as a networked management system.

## 2024: The Fourth *Sanxi* Tour, 95th Anniversary

In 2024, *Sanxi* (*The Curtain Call*) toured for the fourth time, with performances at the National Taichung Theater and the Weiwuying National Kaohsiung Center for the Arts.[^4] This year also marked Ming Hua Yuan's 95th anniversary.

The fourth revival of *Sanxi* is itself a structural signal: a work that can be restaged repeatedly across different eras in different theaters means it touches on themes that belong to more than a single moment in time. Ming Hua Yuan's choice to stage *Sanxi* at the 95-year mark is a self-conscious temporal decision—using a story about "ending" to confirm that it is still "here."

The story of *Sanxi* is itself a metaphor for Ming Hua Yuan's situation: an old opera troupe facing the threat of dissolution amid changing times, resisting disappearance through the act of performing itself. Placed within the framework of Ming Hua Yuan's 95th anniversary, this theme carries a self-referential power—what they perform and what they are are the same thing.

From a temple-stage outdoor troupe in Tainan to eight sub-troupes performing at Weiwuying—in nearly a century, this Taiwanese opera family has come this far.

**Common framing → more precise reading:** Ming Hua Yuan is often described as "modernized Taiwanese opera," a label that risks underselling what happened. A more precise formulation: their modernization was a survival strategy, not an academic project to reform tradition. Chen Sheng-fu introduced stage rigging, lighting, and aerial acts to get more people through the theater door to see Taiwanese opera—the strategy worked, and the tradition was preserved, but the two stand in a means-and-end relationship that must not be reversed.

> 🎙️ **Curator's note:** Ming Hua Yuan is one of the most successful cases in Taiwan's traditional performing arts of resolving the "inheritance dilemma." The branching management of eight sub-troupes gives third- and fourth-generation family members each their own space to develop, rather than concentrating the pressure of succession on a single heir.
>
> From temple-stage *yetai* to the National Theater to Weiwuying, Ming Hua Yuan has spent 95 years proving that traditional performing arts do not have to choose between "purity" and "commerce"—Chen Sheng-fu's modernization found a practical coexistence between commercial success and traditional preservation, rather than a compromise between the two.
>
> Taiwanese opera served as a linguistic vehicle of cultural resistance against colonial suppression during the Japanese period, as an expressive field for local identity in the postwar era, and in 2024 as a formal art form entering the national performing arts center. Ming Hua Yuan's ninety-five years span the full evolution of all these identities.
>
> Chen Sheng-fu's most important contribution was not merely the introduction of lighting and stage rigging, but the proof that Taiwanese opera could upgrade its expressive form without altering its essence. This "upgrade rather than replace" model became a reference point for many subsequent modernization attempts across Taiwan's traditional performing arts.

From Chen Ming-chi at a Tainan temple stage in 1929 to the fourth-generation Chen family at Weiwuying in 2024—Ming Hua Yuan's four-generation inheritance is a complete demonstration of how Taiwan's traditional performing arts have found their place anew in every era.

The outdoor troupe did not become a museum; the theater upgrade did not dilute the tradition—this dual preservation is the rarest success path in the modernization of Taiwan's traditional arts.
