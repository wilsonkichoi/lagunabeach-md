# Chung Li-he: Meinong 1915, the Writer Who Fell in a Pool of Blood

> **30-second overview:** Chung Li-he was born on December 15, 1915, into a Hakka family in Meinong, Kaohsiung.[^1] He went to Northeast China in 1938 to make a living and returned to Taiwan in 1946.[^1] His major works include _Lishan Farm_ (novel), _The Native_ (short story, written in 1959), and _A Couple in Poverty_.[^1] He is known as "the writer who fell in a pool of blood" and is often called the "Father of Taiwanese Nativist Literature" (a title that is disputed; Lāi Hé is widely recognized as the father of modern Taiwanese literature).[^2] On August 4, 1960, he died of hemoptysis while revising a manuscript, at the age of 44.[^1]

## December 15, 1915, Meinong, Kaohsiung

Chung Li-he was born on December 15, 1915, into a Hakka farming family in Meinong, Kaohsiung. His birth name was Chung Lian-ho.[^1] Meinong preserves a rich Hakka culture; its landscape and language became the primary material for his later writing.

After graduating from Meinong Public School, he was admitted to Tainan Normal School but was unable to complete his studies due to family financial difficulties and returned home to farm. He labored during the day and read voraciously at night—works by modern writers such as Lu Xun, Mao Dun, and Lao She.

This self-directed literary training shaped the foundation of his writing: he lacked the structural awareness of a formally trained writer, but possessed a sensibility honed through lived experience. The traces of Hakka syntax and the rhythms of rural labor in his prose were never smoothed over by the anxiety of "learning to write properly"—instead, they became his most distinctive linguistic fingerprint.

## To Northeast China in 1938, Return to Taiwan in 1946

In 1938, at the age of 23, Chung Li-he went to Northeast China (Dalian, Shenyang, Beijing) to make a living, and was only able to return home after the war in 1946.[^1] Eight years of exile gave him a deeper understanding of what "native land" meant, and this experience later became important material for his writing.

During those eight years in Northeast China, Chung Li-he lived as a Taiwanese on "the motherland's" soil—only to discover that he was neither regarded as Chinese nor able to return to Taiwan. This condition of double marginalization gave him a visceral, embodied experience of the question of "belonging," which later transformed into his repeated interrogation of the concept of the "native land."

## _Lishan Farm_: A True Portrait of Rural Taiwan

_Lishan Farm_ is Chung Li-he's most important novel.[^1] Set in a Hakka farming village, it depicts the struggle of peasants surviving on barren land. Chung himself came from a farming family and had firsthand experience of rural poverty; his portrayals of peasants are not idealized, and the work is considered an important precursor to Taiwanese nativist literature.

The significance of _Lishan Farm_ in the history of Taiwanese literature lies not only in its early date—but in the fact that its rural writing lacks the detached gaze of an urban intellectual. He did not "go to the countryside to look around" and then write for urban readers; he grew from that land itself, writing about his own reality. This insider's perspective later became an important standard for Taiwanese nativist literature.

## _The Native_: A Short Story Written in 1959

_The Native_ is a short story Chung Li-he wrote based on his own experience of displacement and wandering.[^1] Written in 1959, it is an important work from the final stage of his life. In the story, the "native land" refers to a spiritual home; the place called Meinong is only the starting point.

As a spiritual concept, "native land" in Chung Li-he's writing carries a unique dialectic: he spent eight years in "China" yet felt no sense of belonging; only after returning to Meinong did he realize that the true native land is an emotional attachment to the soil and its language, not a geographic location. This reverse discovery gives his concept of the "native land" a unique philosophical depth in Taiwanese literature.

Chung Li-he revised manuscripts from his sickbed. There is a famous account that he said, "I want to finish writing my life before I die" (this quote has not yet been confirmed as originating from a primary documentary source).[^3]

Regardless of the quote's precise origin, it accurately describes a reality: in the last years of his life, his body was already severely ravaged by tuberculosis, yet he continued to write and revise. Writing was his race against time—in the final years of his life as tuberculosis consumed him, he kept writing and revising. This attitude later became the primary reason younger generations of writers held him in such esteem.

## August 4, 1960: Death by Hemoptysis at Age 44

On August 4, 1960, Chung Li-he suddenly coughed up blood while revising a manuscript. He was taken to the hospital but could not be saved. He was 44 years old.[^1]

(Note: Some articles begin by saying "his brief 45-year life"—this is a contradiction. From December 1915 to August 1960 is in fact less than 45 years; the correct age is 44.)

He came to be known as "the writer who fell in a pool of blood."[^1]

This epithet endures not only because it describes the manner of his death, but because it captures a genuine attitude toward life: in his final moments, he chose to continue doing what he believed mattered most. This attitude itself became the spiritual core that anyone discussing Chung Li-he must address.

## Distinguishing the Titles: Father of Nativist Literature (Not Father of Taiwanese Literature)

Chung Li-he is often called the "Father of Taiwanese Nativist Literature," but the title "Father of Taiwanese Literature" or "Father of Modern Taiwanese Literature" usually refers to **Lāi Hé** (1894–1943)—they are two distinct titles.[^2]

In 1976, the Chung Li-he Culture and Education Foundation was established. It holds the annual Chung Li-he Literary Award, which discovers new literary talent.

That a literary award was founded in the name of a writer who died in 1960 and has continued into the 21st century demonstrates that Chung Li-he's literary spirit carries enduring symbolic power within the tradition of Taiwanese nativist literature. His name has become the vessel for a certain standard: attention to the land, honest writing, refusal to conform to mainstream conventions.

**Common framing → More precise reading:** Chung Li-he is often described through a lens of "tragedy"—poverty and illness, dying at 44, falling in a pool of blood. But this framing can obscure the power of his work itself. His literature is not a monument frozen in suffering; it is the honest record of a writer with a clear sense of purpose documenting the reality of Hakka rural life. Tragedy was his circumstance, not his literature.

> 🎙️ **Curator's note:** If Chung Li-he's 44-year life is reduced to the image of "the writer who fell in a pool of blood," we lose the most important thing about him as a writer: how clear-eyed his observational perspective was, how honest his language was, and what specific literary foundations his work contributed to the establishment of Taiwanese nativist literature.
>
> He and Lāi Hé represent two different origins of Taiwanese literature: Lāi Hé's anti-Japanese spirit was political; Chung Li-he's rural writing was social. The two lines later converged during the Nativist Literature Debate of the 1970s, and he was already gone—his name was cited as the source of that tradition, a position later generations assigned to him.
>
> A 44-year life that left behind only a handful of books, but those handful of books established a standard: nativist literature is not a romantic nostalgia for a rural past, but an honest record of real life. Every time later generations invoke his name, they are confirming that this standard still holds.

From a Hakka farming home in Meinong, to drifting in the Northeast, to the soil of Meinong, to revising a manuscript in a pool of blood—Chung Li-he's life is an extreme case study of "how literature can be equivalent to life itself": he traded the last of his physical strength for the completion of his work.
