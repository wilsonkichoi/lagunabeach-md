# Pai Hsien-yung: Taipei People (1971) to the Youth Edition of The Peony Pavilion's 20-Year Tour

> **30-second overview:** Pai Hsien-yung was born in 1937 in Guilin, Guangxi; his father was Bai Chongxi, a senior Kuomintang general.[^1] The family relocated to Taiwan in 1952. He entered the National Taiwan University Department of Foreign Languages and Literature in 1956 and co-founded the magazine _Modern Literature_ with Wang Wenxing, Chen Ruoxi, and others. In April 1971, he published _Taipei People_, a collection of 14 short stories.[^1] _Crystal Boys_ followed in 1983. In 2003, he launched the planning for the Youth Edition of _The Peony Pavilion_, which premiered in Taipei in April 2004 and marked its 20th anniversary with a tour in 2024.[^2] He received the National Award for Arts in 2003.[^3] As of 2026, he remains active.[^4]

## 1937: Guilin; 1952: Taiwan

Pai Hsien-yung was born in Guilin, Guangxi, in 1937 and spent his early years moving with his father's military postings amid the upheaval of war.[^1] In 1952, the family relocated to Taiwan, where he attended Jianguo High School.

In 1956, he enrolled in the National Taiwan University Department of Foreign Languages and Literature, where he encountered the mentor who would shape his life: Hsia Tsi-an.[^1] During his university years, he co-founded the magazine _Modern Literature_ with Wang Wenxing, Chen Ruoxi, and others—a pivotal moment in Taiwan's modernist literary movement.

The significance of _Modern Literature_ went beyond that of a single magazine. It systematically introduced Western modernism (Kafka, Woolf, Joyce) to Taiwanese readers while engaging in dialogue with the May Fourth vernacular literary tradition. Much of Pai's later narrative technique was forged during the exploratory period of this magazine.

## April 1971: _Taipei People_

_Taipeh People_ was first published in April 1971.[^1] Its 14 short stories depict the lives of people who relocated from mainland China to Taipei: a fallen general, a disillusioned performer, a lonely elder—each bearing a different weight of historical memory.

Standout stories include "The Eternal Yin Xueyan" (an ageless socialite) and "The Last Night of Miss Chin" (a dance hall queen's farewell evening).[^1]

The 14 stories of _Taipei People_ are not a mere collection of independent tales but a suite structured around the theme of exile: exile from the mainland to Taiwan, exile from history into the present, exile from youth into old age. Each character is a variation on this theme. This suite-like, long-form architecture was groundbreaking in Taiwanese literature.

## 1983: _Crystal Boys_

_Crystal Boys_ was published in 1983.[^1] Set in Taipei New Park, it portrays the lives of a gay community marginalized by mainstream society. It was the first full-length novel in Taiwanese literary history to take this community as its central subject in a direct, sustained manner.

The historical significance of _Crystal Boys_ extends beyond literature. In 1983, it was the first work in the Taiwanese literary world to grant the gay community such complete narrative space—and to do so with empathy rather than pathologization. Pai's own sexual orientation became publicly known only after the book's publication, but the work had already positioned itself ahead of prevailing social attitudes.

"Taipei New Park" (today's 228 Peace Memorial Park) serves as a sanctuary for society's outcasts in _Crystal Boys_. The choice of location carries historical weight: the park bears the dual memory of political persecution and urban marginalization, becoming Pai's distinctive spatial symbol for writing Taiwan's postwar history.

## 2003 Planning, April 2004 Premiere: The Youth Edition of _The Peony Pavilion_

In 2003, Pai Hsien-yung launched the planning for the Youth Edition of _The Peony Pavilion_, participating in every stage from script adaptation to casting.[^2] The production premiered in Taipei in April 2004 and subsequently toured across Taiwan, mainland China, and Hong Kong, accumulating over 300 performances and reaching more than 600,000 audience members.

The significance of the Youth Edition lies in what it proved: the contemporary transmission of classical culture need not dilute itself through popularization. Through high-quality artistic presentation, it can draw younger generations in naturally. Pai chose young Suzhou Kunqu performers in their twenties—not as a market-driven compromise, but as a long-term investment in the vitality of Kunqu opera.

(Note: If a source states "began in 2004," this should be understood as the April 2004 premiere; the actual planning commenced in 2003.)

**Common framing → More precise reading:** Pai is often labeled simply as "the author of _Taipei People_," a tag that obscures the most important turn of his later career. After 2003, he devoted far more time to Kunqu preservation than to fiction writing. This was not the "pivot" outsiders describe. In his own words, it was "repaying a debt"—settling accounts with the Kunqu performances he had heard as a child in Shanghai.

## 2003: National Award for Arts

In 2003, Pai Hsien-yung received the National Award for Arts.[^3]

This award represented the Taiwanese literary world's formal recognition of his complete career arc—from modern fiction to Kunqu preservation. From his first student work in the 1950s to becoming a defining figure in Chinese-language literature half a century later, Pai's creative accumulation never ceased.

(Note: Neither the "Chungshan Literary Award" nor the "2018 7th Dream of the Red Chamber Award Jury Prize" can be confirmed. The latter is a hallucination—the 7th Dream of the Red Chamber Award (2018) was won by _Qingfuzi_ and _Spring Breeze_, not by Pai Hsien-yung.[^5])

## September 2024: 20th Anniversary, _Twenty Years of Peony Blossoms_

In September 2024, the 20th-anniversary tour of the Youth Edition of _The Peony Pavilion_ was launched.[^2] In November of the same year, _Twenty Years of Peony Blossoms_ was published, documenting two decades of performances.

In October 2025, National Taiwan University held a special exhibition on Pai Hsien-yung's literary works.[^4] As of 2026, Pai remains active.

The 2024 20th-anniversary tour spanned Taipei, Hong Kong, Suzhou, and other cities. _Twenty Years of Peony Blossoms_ records the full trajectory of this cultural project from its premiere to this major milestone.

Pai Hsien-yung continues to make public appearances in 2026, promoting Kunqu culture and literary education. This persistence is itself a declaration against disappearance.

> 🎙️ **Curator's note:** Pai Hsien-yung is one of the rare figures in Taiwanese literary history who began as a "modern novelist" and concluded as a "reviver of traditional culture." This arc is not a conventional career change but a deepening—the longing for what has vanished in _Taipei People_ and the guardianship of Kunqu in the Youth Edition of _The Peony Pavilion_ spring from the same question: how to keep precious things from disappearing.
>
> His success demonstrates that cultural preservation requires neither museumification nor popularization. What it requires is someone of sufficient artistic caliber and sufficient persistence, continuing to do it well enough.
>
> _Crystal Boys_ and the Youth Edition of _The Peony Pavilion_—one breaking silence in 1983, the other reviving the classical in 2004—appear radically different, yet both arise from the same deep commitment to protecting what has been marginalized.

From Guilin, Guangxi to Taipei New Park, from _Modern Literature_ to the Suzhou Kunqu Theatre—Pai Hsien-yung's six-decade trajectory is the portrait of a person who has never been willing to let go of "memory."
