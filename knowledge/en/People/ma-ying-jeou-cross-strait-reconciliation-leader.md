# Ma Ying-jeou: the Harvard S.J.D. who entered Chiang Ching-kuo's office as an English interpreter, later served eight years as an incorrupt president, and left behind cross-strait relations, Sunflower, and 22K

> **30-second overview:** Ma Ying-jeou (born in British Hong Kong in 1950) was the twelfth and thirteenth president of the Republic of China (2008-2016). In office, he brought about ECFA, direct cross-strait flights, and the 80-second handshake at the November 7, 2015 Ma-Xi Meeting; he also left behind 22K, the low-wage starting point an entire generation of young people still remembers, the Sunflower Movement, and the Era News poll record of 9.2%. After leaving office, he visited China twice, in 2023 and 2024. At the second Ma-Xi meeting in Beijing on April 10, 2024, he misspoke "Chinese nation" as "Republic of China" and immediately corrected himself. In May 2026, after family members petitioned for an auxiliary declaration, Ma himself recorded a video responding, "Lawyer, can this prove that I do not have dementia?" Ten years after leaving office, he is still in the headlines. Incorruptible, internationally minded, controversial in decision-making, controversial after leaving office: these several impressions still coexist in different proportions in different Taiwanese minds today.

At 3 p.m. on November 7, 2015, in front of a yellow backdrop on the lobby level of the Shangri-La Hotel in Singapore, yellow symbolizing the descendants of Yan and Huang, Ma Ying-jeou and Xi Jinping shook hands. The two addressed each other as "mister," and camera flashes went off for 1 minute and 20 seconds[^1]. This was the first meeting between the top leaders of the two sides of the Taiwan Strait in the 66 years since their political separation in 1949.

The same photograph produced two entirely different reactions in Taiwan the next day. Kuomintang supporters regarded it as a historic achievement that broke the ice across the strait. The Democratic Progressive Party and many young people saw it as a Hong Kongization experiment in conceding Taiwan's subjectivity[^2]. Looking back another decade later, Hong Kong's 2019 anti-extradition protests added another layer of meaning to the second interpretation; after tensions in the Taiwan Strait rose following Lai Ching-te's 2024 inauguration, supporters of the first interpretation found their voice again.

To understand why this person can be remembered in both ways at once, one has to begin with an earlier scene. In 1981, a 31-year-old National Taiwan University law graduate, newly returned to Taipei from Harvard with an S.J.D. dissertation titled "Trouble Over Oily Waters: Legal Problems of Seabed Boundaries and Foreign Investments in the East China Sea," was appointed by Chiang Ching-kuo as the president's English interpreter[^3][^4].

## The English interpreter in Chiang Ching-kuo's office: the dustless road of 1981-1996

![Official portrait of Ma Ying-jeou after taking office as the twelfth president in 2008, wearing a black suit and red tie with the Republic of China flag in the background](/article-images/people/ma-ying-jeou-mofa-portrait-2008.png)
_Official portrait of Ma Ying-jeou after taking office as the twelfth president on May 29, 2008. Photo: Ministry of Foreign Affairs, Republic of China / Presidential Office. [License via Wikimedia Commons (Government Website Open Information Announcement)](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Ma_Ying-jeou_MOFA_Presidential_Portrait_2008.png)_

Ma Ying-jeou was born on July 13, 1950, at Kwong Wah Hospital in Yau Ma Tei, Kowloon, British Hong Kong. His father, Ma Ho-ling, was a Kuomintang party worker who rose to become director at the Executive Yuan's National Youth Commission and deputy director of the KMT Central Evaluation and Discipline Committee. The urn holding Ma Ho-ling's ashes was inscribed with eight Chinese characters: "gradually transform independence into unification, comprehensively rejuvenate China"[^5]. Those eight characters were later often used by outsiders to explain Ma Ying-jeou's cross-strait political choices, though he himself rarely responded directly.

His educational path was the standard version for second-generation mainlander political elites of the era: Taipei Municipal Jianguo High School, National Taiwan University Department of Law, a KMT Sun Yat-sen Scholarship to the United States in 1974, an LL.M. from New York University in 1977, and an S.J.D. from Harvard University in 1981[^4]. His dissertation dealt with seabed boundaries in the East China Sea and foreign-investment disputes around the Diaoyutai Islands. It was both an extension of his participation in the Baodiao movement while studying abroad and the foundation of his later hardline position on Diaoyutai.

In 1981 he returned to Taiwan and, on the recommendation of Frederick Chien, entered the Presidential Office as Chiang Ching-kuo's English interpreter[^3]. In Taiwan in the 1980s, this job was a ticket into the political elite. After Chiang Ching-kuo died in 1988 and Lee Teng-hui succeeded him, Ma became minister of the Research, Development and Evaluation Commission of the Executive Yuan. He was ordered to establish the Executive Yuan's Mainland Affairs Task Force and served as its executive secretary, later helping formulate the Act Governing Relations between the People of the Taiwan Area and the Mainland Area and establish the Mainland Affairs Council and the Straits Exchange Foundation[^6]. The conceptual foundation of his later cross-strait line began to be laid at this time.

On February 27, 1993, Ma Ying-jeou became minister of justice[^7]. In office he vigorously promoted anti-gang campaigns and attacked organized crime and black-gold politics under the posture of "declaring war on gangs." The media framed him as an "incorrupt and courageous" reformer. The nickname "Teflon pan" began to emerge during this period[^8]. In the context of the 1990s, the term was praise, meaning he did not get stained by the dust of political spoils. By the late 2000s, the context had changed, and the term began to take on a negative meaning: that he avoided responsibility and was unwilling to make political judgments. The same term, two periods, two meanings.

> **📝 Curator's note**
> The common account says "Teflon pan" was a matter of personality. But if one looks closely at his path from 1993 to 2007, including Chiang Ching-kuo's interpreter, executive secretary for mainland affairs, head of the RDEC, minister of justice, and Taipei mayor, every position was high-exposure, high-prestige, and low in actual responsibility. "Teflon pan" may have been a deliberately chosen career strategy rather than a passive personality trait. By 2008, when he had to be president and had to make real political judgments, this dustless road instead became a burden.

## 5.22 percentage points and a special allowance case: the political ascent, 1998-2007

On December 5, 1998, Taipei held its mayoral election. Ma Ying-jeou won 51.13% of the vote (766,377 votes), defeating incumbent mayor Chen Shui-bian's 45.91%, by only 78,305 votes, or 5.22 percentage points[^9]. This narrow victory rewrote the balance of power between the KMT's non-mainstream faction (the native Taiwanese faction) and mainstream faction (the mainlander faction) in the late 1990s. It also made Ma Ying-jeou the KMT's next ready candidate after the party parted ways with Lee Teng-hui's line.

In 2002 he was reelected Taipei mayor with 64.11% of the vote[^10], still the highest vote share ever recorded in a Taipei mayoral election. In office he promoted expansions of the Muzha and Xinzhuang MRT lines, straightened sections of the Keelung River, and forcefully implemented a per-bag garbage-fee system. In mid-2000s Taipei, these were achievements the middle class could broadly agree on.

In July 2005, Ma Ying-jeou defeated Wang Jin-pyng with 72.36% of the vote to become KMT chairman[^11]. The Lien Chan era officially ended. But the knot left by this intra-party contest between Ma and Wang would, eight years later in September, become one of the most dramatic political confrontations in Taiwan's political history.

February 13, 2007, was a scene that can only be described with the words "the same day": in the morning, the Taipei District Prosecutors Office indicted Ma Ying-jeou in the special allowance case under the Anti-Corruption Act; in the afternoon, Ma held a press conference announcing that he would resign as KMT chairman and run for president in 2008[^12]. Indicted in the morning, declaring a run for the highest political office in the afternoon: this is a certain archetype of a legal professional entering politics. Ma's defense strategy was the "great reservoir theory": public and private accounts were mixed, money was fungible, and as long as the total inflows and outflows balanced, no crime had occurred. On August 14, the Taipei District Court acquitted him at first instance. On December 28, the High Court upheld the acquittal on appeal.

> 💡 **Did you know?** Ma Ying-jeou has been indicted three times in his life: the 2007 special allowance case, the 2014 Huang Shih-ming leak case, and the 2017 Three Media case. All three ultimately ended in acquittal. On December 31, 2025, the High Court's second-instance ruling in the Three Media case acquitted Ma Ying-jeou, Chang Che-chen, and Wang Hai-ching, while sentencing Tsai Cheng-yuan to three years and six months for breach of trust[^13]. A legal professional's win rate in the judicial arena and his win rate in the political arena are entirely different things.

That July, Ma launched his "Love Our Homeland, Move Forward - Long Stay" campaign strategy. The first stop lasted 18 days in central Taiwan, spanning fishing villages in Yunlin, Chiayi, and Tainan[^14]. Analysts later often regarded this strategy as one of the keys to his two-million-vote victory in 2008: it let a second-generation mainlander from Taipei who had never lived in central or southern Taiwan move into local households across the island. But before that strategy would lead him to the presidency, and before the most painful criticism he would leave behind eight years later, it first carried him into the presidential office.

## The room after the financial tsunami: how 22K became the ceiling

On March 22, 2008, Ma Ying-jeou and Vincent Siew won 7,659,014 votes (58.45%), defeating Frank Hsieh and Su Tseng-chang's 5,444,949 votes (41.55%)[^15]. This was the highest vote-share presidential victory since Taiwan's democratization, with a margin of more than two million votes. Voter expectations were clear: set political disputes aside, focus on the economy, and improve cross-strait relations.

In his May 20, 2008 inaugural address, Ma proposed the cross-strait framework of "no unification, no independence, and no use of force" and pledged to resume cross-strait negotiations on the basis of the "1992 Consensus." The most frequently quoted sentence in the original was: "Ying-jeou firmly believes that the ultimate key to resolving cross-strait issues lies not in disputes over sovereignty, but in lifestyle and core values"[^16].

But what he took on was not merely a promise. In September 2008, Lehman Brothers collapsed and the global financial tsunami arrived. Ma's 2008 campaign "633 platform" - average annual economic growth of 6%, unemployment below 3%, and per capita income of US$30,000 by 2016 - was something he admitted could not be achieved in the short term because of the financial tsunami when he was interviewed by Mexican media in September[^17]. By the end of his term, average economic growth was around 2.81% to 3.0%, unemployment was around 4%, and per capita income had not reached US$30,000.

In April 2009, under the impact of the financial tsunami, the Executive Yuan launched the "Program for College Graduates to Intern in Enterprises," commonly known as 22K. The government subsidized companies NT$22,000 per month in base salary plus NT$4,190 for labor and health insurance per person to hire new university graduates[^18]. The original intent was to relieve unemployment pressure. The actual result was that monthly income for employed people aged 20 to 24 fell from NT$23,351 in 2008 to NT$21,685 in 2009, a 7% decline. The program ran in two phases for two years and five months, from April 2009 to September 2011, and its regulations were formally abolished only on June 3, 2013.

How deeply 22K was related to Ma Ying-jeou's personal decisions remains disputed in Taiwan's economics community today.

> ⚠️ **Contested views**
>
> **The caused camp (DPP, labor movements, some scholars):** The government subsidy amount directly became a pricing anchor. Companies paid NT$22,000 and did not add more. Wages for 20- to 24-year-olds fell 7% in 2008-2009, corresponding to the rollout of the program. Throughout the 2010s, real wages in Taiwan barely moved. This number needs no interpretation.
>
> **The symptom camp (KMT, some financial scholars, the National Policy Foundation):** 22K was temporary relief after the 2008 financial tsunami. Average unemployment fell from 5.85% in 2009 to 4.18% in 2013. Structural wage decline was the combined result of globalization, China's magnetic pull, and industrial relocation; attributing it directly to a single policy is unfair[^19].

But even when the symptom camp acknowledges structural factors, it is indisputable that 22K became a generational symbol. University graduates at the time wrote online: "Our generation's youth was used to subsidize that 22K." The sentence was widely shared on social media and became a shared phrase of generational discontent. This impression lasted longer than the actual duration of the 22K policy itself and later became one of the keys to the formation of political consciousness among Taiwan's younger generation between 2014 and 2020.

That August, Typhoon Morakot arrived. The August 8 floods caused 681 deaths and 18 missing persons[^20]. Xiaolin Village was wiped out, with 474 people buried alive. The Ma administration's disaster response was criticized as slow, and the Liu Chao-shiuan cabinet resigned en masse in early September that year. This was the first major crisis of Ma Ying-jeou's presidency, and the beginning of the transformation of his "Teflon pan" nickname from praise into criticism.

## ECFA, two ledgers for one agreement: 2010-2013

On June 29, 2010, Straits Exchange Foundation chairman Chiang Pin-kung and Association for Relations Across the Taiwan Straits chairman Chen Yunlin signed the Economic Cooperation Framework Agreement (ECFA) in Chongqing[^21]. This was the core achievement of Ma Ying-jeou's cross-strait line. On the early-harvest list, mainland China gave priority tariff reductions to 539 Taiwanese goods, while Taiwan offered preferential tariffs on 18 mainland agricultural products. On January 1, 2013, tariffs on all listed products fell to zero.

Before ECFA was signed, on the afternoon of April 25, 2010, Ma Ying-jeou and DPP chair Tsai Ing-wen held a televised ECFA debate[^22]. It was the first time a Republic of China president held a policy debate with an opposition party chair. Ma's argument was that ECFA would prevent Taiwanese products from being marginalized when competing with ASEAN-plus-one in the mainland market; Tsai's argument was that ECFA would make Taiwan's economic structure overdependent on China and would sacrifice workers in specific industries, including traditional manufacturing and agriculture. Looking back ten years later, both sides of that debate were partly right.

ECFA's actual economic effects are still read through two ledgers. The first ledger is the official estimate from the Taiwan Institute of Economic Research's GTAP model: tariff-reduction benefits of about NT$30 billion and an estimated GDP increase of 1.65% to 1.72%. The second ledger is the distribution of benefits: the early-harvest list mainly benefited large firms in traditional manufacturing, while small and medium enterprises and the service sector felt no dividend; during the same period, Taiwan's trade dependence on China rose from 28.0% in 2010 to 30.5% in 2014, and kept rising until the Sunflower Movement broke out.

> **📝 Curator's note**
> ECFA is a textbook example of "aggregate vs. distribution." From the perspective of macroeconomists, the agreement added 1.65% to Taiwan's GDP; from the perspective of factory technicians and farmers, that number had nothing to do with their wages. The Ma Ying-jeou era's cross-strait economic policy was statistically successful, but what the statistics did not tell you would erupt at 9:20 p.m. on March 18, 2014. That night, hundreds of students rushed into the Legislative Yuan chamber.

## The official residence on a typhoon night and Era News's 9.2% poll: the 2013 Wang Jin-pyng case

On January 14, 2012, Ma Ying-jeou won reelection as the thirteenth president with 6,891,139 votes (51.60%), defeating Tsai Ing-wen and Su Jia-chyuan's 6,093,578 votes (45.6%)[^23].

The second term collapsed almost immediately. In April 2012, fuel prices rose 10.7% (from NT$32.4 to NT$35.5 per liter), and in May electricity prices rose 29.5%. Police estimated 55,000 people took part in the "May 19 Scold Ma" march[^24]. On April 27, a Taiwan Thinktank poll found that 44.1% of the public felt "Taiwan chose the wrong president." Ma Ying-jeou had been in office for barely more than a year since his reelection, and public opinion had already reversed.

On the typhoon night of August 31, 2013, Prosecutor-General Huang Shih-ming entered the presidential residence to brief Ma Ying-jeou. The report concerned Legislative Yuan Speaker Wang Jin-pyng's alleged lobbying in a judicial case involving DPP legislator Ker Chien-ming; the Special Investigation Division had intercepted call content. Between 11 p.m. and midnight that night, Ma called Huang Shih-ming for questioning. On September 1, Huang again entered the residence for a second briefing. On September 4, Ma suggested that Huang brief Premier Jiang Yi-huah. On September 6, the Special Investigation Division held a press conference releasing transcripts of the intercepted calls.

On September 8, Ma Ying-jeou held a press conference, with Vice President Wu Den-yih and Premier Jiang Yi-huah beside him. Reporters wrote this passage of his into history[^25]:

"If powerful people can all lobby and influence the judiciary, can ordinary people still expect the judiciary to safeguard fairness and justice?" "This is the most shameful day in the history of the development of democracy and the rule of law."

On September 11, the KMT's Evaluation and Discipline Committee revoked Wang Jin-pyng's party membership. On September 13, the Taipei District Court granted Wang Jin-pyng a NT$9.38 million provisional injunction preserving his party membership. Ma Ying-jeou won the moral high ground and lost political approval and intra-party unity.

On September 15, 2013, an Era News poll reported Ma Ying-jeou's governing satisfaction at 9.2% and dissatisfaction at 80.5%[^26]. This was one of the lowest records for any president since Taiwan's democratization. A TVBS poll in the same period showed 11%. Over the past decade, many media outlets have mistakenly attributed the 9.2% figure to TVBS; the correct source is Era News's September 15 survey that year.

> **📝 Curator's note**
> The common explanation is that "the September political struggle proved Ma Ying-jeou lacked political judgment." A more precise description may be: Ma Ying-jeou fought a political battle with a thoroughgoing lawyer's logic. From a lawyer's perspective, Wang Jin-pyng was suspected of lobbying, wiretap recordings corroborated it, and revoking his party membership according to party rules was procedurally correct. From a politician's perspective, the price of this battle was losing cooperation with the legislative speaker, losing party unity, and losing public support. The limitation of legal professionals in politics is not that they are not intelligent enough; it is that procedural justice and political reality are two different dimensions.

## Outside the Sunflower chamber, 150 injured in front of the Executive Yuan: civil society explodes in 2014

![During the student occupation of the Legislative Yuan chamber beginning March 18, 2014, large crowds gather outside the Legislative Yuan amid protest banners and slogans](/article-images/people/sunflower-2014-legislative-yuan.jpg)
_On March 18, 2014, during the Sunflower Movement, crowds gathered outside the Legislative Yuan. The March 18 Sunflower Movement began when students rushed into the chamber at 9:20 p.m. on March 18 and ended when they withdrew on April 10, occupying the Legislative Yuan for 24 days. Photo: Voice of America. [License via Wikimedia Commons (Public domain, VOA U.S. government work)](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Sunflower_movement_legislative_yuan.jpg)_

On the afternoon of March 17, 2014, KMT legislator Chang Ching-chung, convener of the Legislative Yuan's Internal Administration Committee, announced in 30 seconds that committee review of the Cross-Strait Service Trade Agreement had been completed[^27]. At 9:20 p.m. on March 18, about 300 students rushed into the Legislative Yuan chamber. On March 30, organizers of the Ketagalan Boulevard anti-service-trade march estimated 500,000 participants; police estimated 116,000. On April 10, the students withdrew. The service trade agreement has still not completed legislation.

The night in between was March 23. At 7:35 p.m. that evening, hundreds of students attempted to occupy the Executive Yuan. In the early hours of March 24, National Police Agency Director-General Wang Cho-chiun reported to Premier Jiang Yi-huah that attempts to persuade them to leave had failed. Jiang ordered dispersal in accordance with the law. Starting at midnight, police launched six waves of forced dispersal using water cannons and batons. The site was fully cleared by 5 a.m., leaving more than 150 protesters and police injured and bleeding[^28]. After a nine-year investigation, the Control Yuan concluded in a 2023 report that "police agencies carried out the dispersal using violent means that exceeded the principle of proportionality" and issued corrective measures against the Executive Yuan, Ministry of the Interior, National Police Agency, Taipei City Police Department, and Taipei District Prosecutors Office.

Jiang Yi-huah later insisted[^29]: "Not only do I not regret it, I feel I have never made a decision more correct than this one." Regarding accusations of attempted murder, he described them as "a malicious distortion of facts and baseless false accusations and abusive litigation." Ma Ying-jeou and Jiang Yi-huah were later sued for attempted murder; on September 15, 2020, they were acquitted at first instance.

Lin Fei-fan, one of the leaders of that movement, looked back ten years later in 2024 and said[^30]: "(Sunflower told everyone) Taiwan's fate is decided by ourselves. Because we blocked the service trade agreement, the Ma government could not sign the trade in goods agreement, could not sign a military confidence-building mechanism, and could not conduct political negotiations with the other side. These are our achievements, the results we created."

Four years later, on December 17, 2018, Ma Ying-jeou himself commented on Sunflower in a lecture at Soochow University this way[^31]: "(Sunflower) some people who think they love Taiwan are killing Taiwan... They are sinners of Taiwan." "Conniving at the failure to pass the service trade agreement made Taiwan stagnate."

The same movement, two completely opposite historical placements. Ten years later, neither side has conceded.

> **📝 Curator's note**
> If the story of Sunflower is written only as "students occupied the chamber, the Ma government used water cannons to disperse them, and party turnover followed," it is too thin. What truly made this movement a watershed for Taiwan's civil society was what came after: the Sunflower generation helped sustain Tsai Ing-wen's 6.89 million votes in 2016, her 8.17 million votes in 2020, and Lai Ching-te's 5.58 million votes in 2024. When Ma Ying-jeou said in 2018 that "they are sinners of Taiwan," those 21-year-old students were already 25 and were voting to decide the next president. All the contradictions of an eight-year presidency ultimately became the voter structure of the next three presidential elections.

On March 14, 2014, while receiving Lions Club leaders, Ma Ying-jeou described deer antler velvet as "the hair inside a deer's ears"[^32]. This verbal slip, together with the contemporaneous "Is ah-gei a person's name?" and "death grip" memes, completely collapsed Ma's image among younger generations. Nine months later, on November 29, 2014, in the nine-in-one local elections, the KMT suffered a major defeat, with the pan-green camp winning 14 cities and counties against the pan-blue camp's 8. In Taipei, Ko Wen-je won 853,983 votes (57.16%), defeating Sean Lien's 609,932 votes (40.82%)[^33]. On December 3, Ma Ying-jeou resigned as KMT chairman.

A year and a half remained before he would leave the presidency.

## From 80 seconds to 16 seconds: two Ma-Xi meetings and two post-presidency narratives

On November 7, 2015, Ma Ying-jeou was already a lame-duck president. In the previous year's nine-in-one elections, the KMT had lost so badly that it was left with only its base supporters, and after Eric Chu became party chairman as the KMT's 2016 presidential candidate, his line had grown distant from Ma's. At this moment, Ma flew to the Shangri-La in Singapore and shook hands with Xi Jinping.

The two sides deliberately addressed each other as "mister" to avoid formal titles. Ma Ying-jeou's opening remarks set out five points[^34]: "Consolidate the '1992 Consensus' and maintain the peaceful status quo... In November 1992, the two sides of the Taiwan Strait reached a consensus on the 'one China' principle, abbreviated as the '1992 Consensus.' The 1992 Consensus is the common political foundation for the two sides to promote peaceful development... People on both sides of the strait belong to the Chinese nation and are all descendants of Yan and Huang; they should assist and cooperate with each other and strive for the rejuvenation of China."

Supporters believed this was the first meeting between the top leaders on both sides of the Taiwan Strait since 1949, a historic breakthrough that reduced cross-strait hostility. Britannica wrote in its entry on Ma Ying-jeou[^35]: "The U.S. government was pleased with Ma as president because he reduced tensions in the Taiwan Strait by pursuing cordial relations with Beijing rather than provoking its leaders, and the Taiwan Strait was downgraded from its former status as the foremost flash point in the world."

Opponents saw it as a concession of subjectivity: addressing each other as "mister" to avoid the title "president of the Republic of China" amounted to accepting diminishment, and "people on both sides of the strait belong to the Chinese nation and are all descendants of Yan and Huang" echoed Xi Jinping's "one China" framing. After Hong Kong's 2019 anti-extradition protests, the perspective that "cross-strait economic integration may be a Hong Kongization experiment" led more people to reinterpret the photograph from November 7, 2015.

On May 20, 2016, Ma Ying-jeou left office and handed power to Tsai Ing-wen. After leaving office, he lost criminal immunity, and the Taipei District Prosecutors Office immediately reopened investigations into 24 criminal cases, including the Huang Shih-ming leak case[^36].

On July 27, 2018, the Ma Ying-jeou Foundation was established at the Ambassador Hotel Taipei. Former KMT chairs including Vincent Siew and Lien Chan attended. The foundation promoted the "Da Jiu Academy" youth camp and cross-strait youth exchanges.

From March 27 to April 7, 2023, Ma Ying-jeou visited China for the first time, a 12-day trip that made him the first former president to visit China after 1949. On March 28, at Sun Yat-sen Mausoleum in Nanjing, he inscribed eight characters: "struggle for peace, rejuvenate China"[^37]. The wreath prepared by the Chinese side referred to him as "former chairman of the Chinese Kuomintang"; Ma referred to himself as "former president." Throughout the trip, he repeatedly mentioned that "both sides of the strait belong to the Chinese nation."

From April 1 to 11, 2024, Ma visited China a second time. On April 4, in Huangling County, Shaanxi, he personally paid respects at the Mausoleum of the Yellow Emperor, his first in-person rite after previously having remotely performed the ceremony six times from Taipei. He presented flowers on the same stage as Chinese representative Song Tao and was placed fourth in order. While reading the ritual text, he choked up twice[^38].

At 4 p.m. on April 10, in the East Hall of the Great Hall of the People in Beijing, the second Ma-Xi meeting took place. The meeting lasted about one hour. In his remarks, Ma Ying-jeou misspoke "Chinese nation" as "Republic of China" and immediately corrected himself. He quoted Lu Xun's famous line, "After passing through all calamities, brothers remain; meeting with a smile, enmity melts away." After the meeting, Wang Huning hosted a farewell banquet at the Diaoyutai State Guesthouse, where Xi Jinping told Ma Ying-jeou, "You are welcome to come often in the future"[^39].

The handshake at the Shangri-La in Singapore lasted 1 minute and 20 seconds. This one at the Great Hall of the People in Beijing lasted 16 seconds[^40].

Taiwanese society remains divided over this China visit and Ma's entire post-presidency line:

> ⚠️ **Contested views**
>
> **The communication-channel camp:** Official cross-strait communication has been suspended since Tsai Ing-wen took office in 2016. The Ma Ying-jeou Foundation's "Da Jiu Academy" and youth exchanges are among the few remaining civil-society bridges. The 2024 second Ma-Xi meeting was timed around Lai Ching-te's inauguration (April 10, with Lai taking office on May 20) to give Beijing a "window"[^41].
>
> **The "selling out Taiwan" camp:** The DPP accused Ma Ying-jeou of "openly advocating unification with China," mentioning unification-related terms eight times during his 2024 China visit and betraying the Taiwanese people. Chen Chun-han, chair of the Taiwan Nation Alliance, criticized him this way: "Ma Ying-jeou's visit to China has sinister intentions... Democratic countries around the world that support Taiwan feel very worried"[^42].

On May 21, 2026, Ma Ying-jeou's wife, Chow Mei-ching, and his eldest sister, Ma Yi-nan, issued a joint statement hoping that Ma Ying-jeou could truly retire and that foundation affairs would be handed over to the board of directors. Ma Yi-nan, through a lawyer, filed a court petition for an auxiliary declaration, not a guardianship declaration, the two having different legal effects[^43].

The next day, May 22, Ma Ying-jeou himself recorded a video response, with the manuscript signed in his own hand. Holding the manuscript up to the camera, he said: "Lawyer, can this prove that I do not have dementia?" Speaking of his elder sister, he said: "I live even farther from my eldest sister. We live in Wenshan District; she lives in Neihu District. At most, we probably see each other once a month, and we are not that close." Speaking of the foundation: "These corrupt criminals must absolutely not try to use this reason, saying that I have dementia, and then do things they should not do."

The context behind the incident was that the Ma Ying-jeou Foundation's three-person investigative panel on the "financial discipline case," with Lee De-wei as spokesperson, was investigating Hsiao Hsu-tsen, the executive director, and Wang Kuang-tzu, the former executive director. Lee De-wei responded to the family statement by saying: "The three-person panel's investigation into this case will not stop; it will continue... The fact that Ma Ying-jeou's family members have come forward to issue a statement means that there are indeed questions about Ma Ying-jeou's health."

This news story has not yet run its course, and Taiwanese society has not yet formed a consensus.

## Those 80 seconds and 16 seconds, and the young man who returned to Taipei in 1981

On April 10, 2024, in the East Hall of the Great Hall of the People in Beijing, he misspoke "Chinese nation" as "Republic of China" and immediately corrected himself. Sixteen years earlier, in his inaugural address as the twelfth president, he had written: "Ying-jeou firmly believes that the ultimate key to resolving cross-strait issues lies not in disputes over sovereignty, but in lifestyle and core values."

On May 22, 2026, in a video, he raised his hand toward the camera and said: "Lawyer, can this prove that I do not have dementia?" That 31-year-old young man who returned to Taipei from Harvard in 1981 with an S.J.D. in the law of the sea and was appointed by Chiang Ching-kuo as his English interpreter was already forty-five years away from that camera.

The 80-second handshake at the Shangri-La in Singapore lasted 1 minute and 20 seconds. The one at the Great Hall of the People in Beijing lasted 16 seconds. The same person, two historical moments: Taiwanese society still remembers him in different ways.
