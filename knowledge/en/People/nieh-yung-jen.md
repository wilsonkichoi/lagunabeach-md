> **30-second overview:** Aaron Nieh (b. 1977) is the first Taiwanese designer to join the international graphic design alliance AGI and a three-time Golden Melody Award winner for Best Album Packaging. He has designed music covers for Yoga Lin, Sodagreen, Jolin Tsai, and Hebe Tien, as well as book covers for publishers including Rye Field, China Times, INK, and Linking. The 2014 Sunflower Movement "Democracy at 4am" New York Times ad and the 2020 "Taiwan Can Help" NYT crowdfunding campaign both came from his studio. Tsai Ing-wen's 2016 campaign key visual "Light Up Taiwan" and the key visuals for two presidential inaugurations were also handled by him and his team. In 2024, True Lie Workshop opened a second location in the Pier-2 warehouse in Kaohsiung, and from that year on won consecutive identity-system contracts for Taipower, CPC Corporation, the Tourism Administration, and the Ministry of Economic Affairs. On the day the new Taipower logo launched in 2026, accusations of "political patronage" erupted, and he issued a five-point public statement in response. His frequently repeated line—"Before he was designer Aaron Nieh, he was citizen Aaron Nieh"—has been recontextualized again and again from 2014 to the present.

If you lay out two decades of project records, Aaron Nieh's design work reads like a cross-industry inventory. In pop music: Sodagreen, Yoga Lin, Mayday, Hebe Tien, Jolin Tsai. In publishing: Rye Field, China Times, INK, Linking; book covers for Lo Yao-chun and Gan Yao-ming passed through his studio repeatedly[^1]. In commercial branding: 7-ELEVEN CITYCAFE, UNiqlo Tomorrow Taipei, CTBC Bank cards, Kuo Yuan Ye. In the public sector: the Control Yuan, Taiwan Design Research Institute, Ministry of Digital Affairs, and from 2023 onward the Ministry of Economic Affairs, Tourism Administration, CPC Corporation, and Taipower. Add the 2014 "Democracy at 4am" NYT ad at four in the morning, the 2020 anti-Tedros NYT front page that raised NT$10 million in eight hours, and the key visuals for Tsai Ing-wen's 2016 and 2020 presidential campaigns and inaugurations.

The horizontal breadth of this list is the factual arc of Aaron Nieh's twenty-year career, and it is also the real backdrop to the Taipower logo controversy of May 8, 2026. What critics were really asking was about the distinguishability of identity: when the same studio spans all these industries simultaneously, how is the outside world to judge which role is the "real" Aaron Nieh?

## From Mechanical Drafting to Industrial Design

Aaron Nieh was born August 16, 1977. He studied mechanical drafting at National Taichung Industrial Senior High School before enrolling in the Department of Industrial and Commercial Design at National Taiwan University of Science and Technology[^1]. In a 2024 interview with _Cheers_, he reflected on this period: "More important than getting the diploma was knowing I had actually learned something."[^2] This was a retrospective description after failing to complete three separate graduate programs, but the training in mechanical drafting was still at work two decades later when he took on government identity-system contracts involving standard-character measurement and structural scaling: his studio handles not just visual-concept design but also the engineering details of typography and print production.

## Starting with Album Covers: Three Golden Melody Awards and Two Decades of Music Layout

From 2002 onward, Aaron Nieh began taking on album cover design for pop music. His three Golden Melody Award wins for Best Album Packaging were: the 21st edition for Yoga Lin's _Senses Around_, the 25th for Sodagreen's _Autumn: Stories_, and the 26th for Yoga Lin's _Fiction_[^*1]. During the same period he also designed covers for Mayday, Jay Chou, Jolin Tsai, and Hebe Tien, and his work accumulated on record-store shelves into a recognizable visual language: white backgrounds, low chroma, negative space in typography replacing figurative illustration.

> ✦ "Designing a visual identity is about abstracting concrete things—expressing imagination in the simplest form. This is the highest guiding principle for designing a logo." — Aaron Nieh, 2015 ETtoday interview[^3]

The media began labeling his style "subtractive aesthetics," but Nieh pushed back against the label in a 2017 conversation on OKAPI[^4]:

> "My work is very composite and diverse. When a lot of people say, 'This is the Aaron Nieh style,' I think—really? Is it?"

Book-cover design had an even higher project density than records. He maintained long-term collaborations with Rye Field, China Times, INK, Linking, Two Fish, and Sotheby's postwar Asian art catalogues; an entire generation of Taiwanese literary authors—Lo Yao-chun, Gan Yao-ming—had their book covers handled by his studio[^1]. In the 2017 _BIOS monthly_ feature "Aaron Nieh's Trolley Problem," he discussed his criteria for taking projects: "I only do it when the proportion of values I identify with is high enough," "I don't take badly paying bad jobs, because the client's constitution is poor," "Setting politics aside, every project runs into this kind of thing."[^5]

## First AGI Member and "Unsafe Design"

In 2012, Aaron Nieh was invited to join the Alliance Graphique Internationale (AGI), the Swiss international graphic design alliance founded in 1952, becoming the first Taiwanese designer admitted[^6]. AGI has roughly 500 members worldwide, selected through nomination by existing members and approval by the full membership. In the years following his admission, more Taiwanese designers were invited: Wang Chih-hung (2015), Lin Hsiao-i (2017), Ho Chia-hsing (2017), Yeh Chung-yi (2023). As of 2023, Taiwan had eight AGI members[^6].

In 2013, Nieh served as a jury member for the Red Dot Communication Design Award in Germany; the same year he created the key visual for the 50th Golden Horse Awards, collaborating with Hong Kong photographer Wing Shya—the first time one of Taiwan's three major awards ceremonies used the anniversary number as the conceptual source for its logo. In a 2017 Red Point interview, he left a statement that has been widely quoted since[^7]:

> "Every piece is complete and mature, but very safe—missing a little bit of cool."

In another interview with _La Vie_, he extended this into a design philosophy: "A beautiful design doesn't necessarily get disseminated; a slightly controversial design can be amplified and spread," "Safe designs are easily forgotten, while unsafe designs get remembered at that particular moment."[^8] For him, "unsafe" became one of the metrics for judging whether a design would be remembered. This criterion partly explains why he took on civic-movement design projects, and partly explains why his influence in Taiwan's design world spilled over from record covers into politics, books, spaces, brands, and every other industry.

## The Four-in-the-Morning NYT Front Page and Taiwan Can Help

In the early hours of March 24, 2014, police cleared Sunflower Movement students from the Executive Yuan. On March 30, half a million people gathered on Ketagalan Boulevard. In between, a citizen-crowdfunded advertisement—"[Democracy at 4am](https://www.thenewslens.com/article/2866)"—appeared on the front page of the _New York Times_[^9]. Black background, white text, full-page typography; the subtitle came from American poet Emily Dickinson: "Morning without YOU is a dwindled dawn." The designer was not credited on the page, but the design community knew it was Aaron Nieh[^10]. The first phase raised NT$1.5 million in 35 minutes; the second phase raised NT$6.33 million in three hours[^11].

Six years later, in April 2020, WHO Director-General Tedros Adhanom accused Taiwan of personal attacks and racial discrimination during a press conference. The next day, Ray Du, Aaron Nieh, Watcher Lin Tzu-yi (of Watchout), Chang Shao-lin (of Miso Tour), and Chi Chi-chang (of Chi Chi Seven Seven) launched the [anti-Tedros NYT crowdfunding ad](https://www.zeczec.com/projects/thisattackcomesfromtaiwan)[^12]. It raised over NT$4 million in eight hours, ultimately reaching NT$10.28 million with nearly 27,000 sponsors[^13]. On April 14, the NYT ran the full-page ads "This Attack Comes From Taiwan" and "Who Can Help? Taiwan," with a blue-background image of a cave opening and the letters "WHO" embedded in the entrance to "Taiwan." In a Central News Agency interview, Nieh said: "Print media is only the starting point. Taiwan's desire to help the world must not be distorted by Tedros."[^11]

Both ads were done pro bono or at minimal fees. Both also pushed Aaron Nieh's position in Taiwan's design world from "one of the best album-cover designers" toward "visual spokesperson for civic events."

## "Light Up Taiwan" and Two Presidential Inaugurations

The 2014 NYT ad and the 2015 approach from Tsai Ing-wen's campaign office were less than a year apart. In an ETtoday interview, Nieh discussed the decision to take the campaign key-visual project:

> "The fact that they came to our studio means they already had a rough idea of what to expect, because there's no way we could produce anything flashy."[^3]

His collaboration with Chen Xun-chi, "[Light Up Taiwan](https://www.dpp.org.tw/media/contents/6532)," was the version Nieh himself said was "the least likely to be chosen" among six sketches: a circular gradient background with only the words "Light Up Taiwan," with motion graphics executed by White Light Motion[^14][^15]. The system was later carried forward to Tsai Ing-wen's 2020 re-election campaign and used for the 2016 and 2020 presidential inauguration key visuals, including commemorative beer cans, invitations, and a full suite of identity items[^14].

![Tsai Ing-wen campaign event, 2015–2016](/article-images/people/tsai-2016-campaign.jpg)
_A "Light Up Taiwan" campaign event for the Tsai Ing-wen presidential election, October 2015. Photo: MiNe (Flickr). [CC BY 2.0 via Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Tsai_Ing-wen_presidential_campaign,_2016_(22116261150).jpg).\_

In a 2016 conversation with cultural critic Chang Tieh-chih, Nieh put it in more abstract terms: "Because concrete things are stupid. If you want to establish stature, you'd rather be abstract."[^16] This explains why the "Light Up Taiwan" design did not take the "green bloc + Taiwan outline" route and instead chose a circular gradient with a single line of English. It also foreshadowed the Taipower logo storm a decade later, which would erupt precisely at the level of "typeface retention vs. redesign": he was always working at the layer of identity structure, with the symbol surface being secondary.

## Leaving for Belgium and London: Escaping "Too Much Comfort Zone"

In September 2018, Aaron Nieh flew to Antwerp to pursue graduate studies in graphic design at the Royal Academy of Fine Arts Antwerp; in 2019 he transferred to the Computing Arts program at Goldsmiths, University of London—completing neither degree[^2]. Together with his earlier unfinished program at National Taiwan University of Arts in applied media arts, this made three graduate programs left incomplete[^17].

But the motivation for leaving was described plainly in interviews after his return in 2019[^18]:

> "Staying in Taiwan's design world, too many things came too easily… It didn't seem like a very normal environment. I felt it was too much of a comfort zone."

The timing of his departure came after the 2014 Sunflower Movement, the 2015 "Light Up Taiwan" campaign, the 2016 and 2020 presidential inaugurations, three Golden Melody Awards for record packaging, and AGI membership. Before he flew out in 2018, he was already one of the most frequently named designers on any Taiwan design-world list. The two graduate stints in Belgium and London produced no diplomas, but the act of "resetting his coordinate system," by his own account, took place.

## A Beam of Light in the Pier-2 Warehouse and the Shape of the Studio

In July 2024, True Lie Workshop opened a second location in Kaohsiung's Pier-2 Art Center. The space was a converted First Bank Kaohsiung warehouse: 3.8-meter ceilings, exposed concrete walls, original beam-and-column structure preserved, original warehouse numbering fonts retained, monthly rent NT$42,000, 28 ping (approximately 93 square meters)[^19]. Nieh said he chose it because "everything just fell into place": the year before, while long-staying in Kaohsiung to curate the Taiwan Design Show, "he was moved by a beam of sunlight."

> ✦ "Everything just fell into place." — Aaron Nieh, 2023 ETtoday Property interview[^19]

The reason the studio has two locations is that the shape of the projects changed. In the early 2020s, state-enterprise and public-sector identity systems began appearing密集ly in his project mix: the Taiwan Design Research Institute rebranding (2020), the Ministry of Digital Affairs (2022), the Ministry of Economic Affairs and Tourism Administration (2023), CPC Corporation and Taipower (2024), the Youth Design Festival and Taichung Green Museumbrary (2025). These projects require teams, physical meeting spaces, and proximity to southern government agencies.

In 2025, True Lie Workshop had two works selected for the 2026 Golden Pin Design Award's Best Design of the Year: the [Taichung Green Museumbrary visual identity](https://www.openbook.org.tw/article/p-69710) (the building designed by SANAA and Liu Pei-sen; the studio handled the full brand extension) and the Taiwan Philharmonic (One Song Orchestra) series album packaging (_ExAvantGarde_ and _NewOldSchool_)[^20]. Both were delivered before the Taipower controversy broke, and by internal design-community assessment, they are among the rare public-sector identity projects that did not generate political controversy.

## Consecutive State-Enterprise Contracts and the 2026 Taipower Logo Storm

The list of public-sector identity systems True Lie Workshop took on from 2023 to 2026 is as follows[^21]:

| Year       | Contract                     | Issuing Agency               | Amount     |
| ---------- | ---------------------------- | ---------------------------- | ---------- |
| 2023       | Image identity system revamp | Ministry of Economic Affairs | Not public |
| 2023       | Image identity system        | Tourism Administration       | NT$1.48M   |
| 2024-01-15 | Identity optimization design | Taipower                     | NT$968,000 |
| 2024-05    | Logo optimization design     | CPC Corporation              | NT$980,000 |
| 2024       | 2025 Youth Design Festival   | Kaohsiung Cultural Affairs   | NT$650,000 |
| 2025       | Visual identity              | Taichung Green Museumbrary   | Not public |

Add to these the earlier Ministry of Digital Affairs (2022), Taiwan Design Research Institute (2020 rebranding), Control Yuan, and the Taiwan Pavilion at the Venice Architecture Biennale. Within a few years, True Lie Workshop became the most commonly named studio for "rebranding government agencies."

On May 8, 2026, the day the new Taipower identity system launched, KMT legislator Wang Hung-wei questioned in a hearing: "As the former president Tsai Ing-wen's 'court designer,' during the Tsai administration he also became a frequent winner of design contracts across government agencies and state enterprises."[^22] The Tainan City Party branch executive of the Taiwan People's Party criticized: "Change a font, tweak a logo, and happily pocket NT$960,000 of taxpayers' money."[^23] Political labels—"green camp crony," "political patronage," "bluebird" (derogatory for pan-green supporters), "transitional justice"—spread from NOWnews to eight Chinese-language media outlets[^24].

Later that day, Aaron Nieh posted a five-point statement on his personal Facebook page (which would eventually reach 31,000 likes, 2,355 comments, and 3,806 shares)[^25]. He opened by directly naming the framing: "A quick clarification first, to prevent deliberate politicization and derailment by opportunistic politicians and media." The statement's argument had three layers. The first was functional difficulty: "Because the gap between early-era drafting and contemporary applications is enormous, inconsistent identification has been a long-standing problem—cast-iron maintenance hole covers, transformer box spray-painting, extremely small print items, three-dimensional lettering signs—all requiring adjustments for different media."[^25]

The second layer was typographic-historical research. He pointed out that the original Taipower "臺灣電力公司" (Taiwan Power Company) six characters were not personally written in a single connected hand by Yu You-ren: "Their source is characters individually taken from Yu You-ren's calligraphy copybooks, then arranged, scaled, and angle-adjusted, followed by computer tracing and edge processing—not a custom piece written by Mr. Yu specifically for Taipower."[^25] The third layer was continuity: he said the new standard characters "still incorporate connections to the central palace (中宮) and terminal-point characteristics of Yu You-ren's original calligraphic copybook typeface," with the design goal of preserving visual DNA[^25]. The statement also responded to budget and "why is it always you" accusations: "A project around NT$1 million stretched over a year and a half… is indeed far below our market-rate quotes for corporate and brand work," "If someone can do better than us, surpass us and win the bid, I would genuinely be delighted and very happy!" "We really do very few government agency projects, Amitabha Buddha."[^26][^25]

> ✦ "Whatever the outcome in terms of public reception, we delivered our professional best, and we have a clear conscience." — Aaron Nieh, May 8, 2026, five-point statement[^27]

![The Taipower new standard-character design process publicly disclosed by Aaron Nieh, showing comparison of Yu You-ren's original calligraphic characters with the new typeface's central palace and terminal-point features](/article-images/people/aaron-nieh-taipower-logo-2026.jpg)
_The Taipower new standard-character design process disclosed by Aaron Nieh in his May 8, 2026 Facebook post: left, Yu You-ren's original calligraphic characters; right, the new typeface, with central palace and terminal-point feature comparisons clearly visible. Image source: [Mirror Media report](https://www.mirrormedia.mg/story/20260508edi031) (screenshot from Aaron Nieh's public Facebook post), fair-use editorial commentary reference._

At 14:36 that afternoon, China Times published a rebuttal by Huang Chih-yang, dean of the College of Humanities and Arts at Huafan University[^28]. Huang cited the January 1, 1965 article "Right Elder's Calligraphy and Taipower" from _Taipower Monthly_ issue 25, documenting that in 1953, then-Taipower general manager Huang Hui personally invited Yu You-ren to inscribe "臺灣電力公司" (Taiwan Power Company) and "服務所" (Service Office) for Taipower, and that Yu You-ren did indeed write them in person, only changing the original right-to-left format to left-to-right arrangement. Huang called Nieh's account "an evasive and highly unprofessional misleading phenomenon," stating: "Personally inscribing for Taipower is a historical fact; it cannot be dismissed as a commercial misjudgment or used to cleverly mislead the public."[^28]

Nieh did not respond further that afternoon to the evidence from the 1965 internal publication. On that day, the Taipower logo completed a full cycle in the news—from "political patronage accusation" to "designer's three-layer defense" to "typography historian's rebuttal using a 60-year-old internal publication." A logo controversy reaching into the axis of typographic history was, in Aaron Nieh's twenty years of design work, the first time it had happened at this intensity.

## "Citizen Aaron Nieh" and "Designer Aaron Nieh"

A frequently quoted line from Aaron Nieh comes from the June 2020 _La Vie_ interview[^8]: "Before he was designer Aaron Nieh, he was citizen Aaron Nieh." In the same interview, he also discussed the tension between commercial work and "cool":

> "I still love doing cool things, but commercial work can't satisfy me. Nobody pays me to do cool things. But I have to achieve psychological balance."[^8]

> "Those beautiful things are safe and harmless—but is that really my persona? No. I'm just being honest about my persona."[^8]

The self-declared ordering of "citizen before designer" was clear in the contexts of the 2014 Sunflower Movement, the 2020 Taiwan Can Help campaign, and the 2016 and 2020 Tsai Ing-wen campaigns and inaugurations: he used his designer's professional skills in service of civic events as a matter of duty. But the four consecutive state-enterprise contracts from 2024 to 2026 placed this ordering back into a commercial frame: critics asked, when a designer takes government-budget identity-system contracts, to what extent can the "citizen" identity remain independent of the commissioning party?

Nieh's own response, in the Mirror Media version of his May 8, 2026 statement, was: "This project has nothing to do with politics, and we have also worked with teams from different political parties—we have a clear conscience and gave it our all."[^29] He cited recent True Lie Workshop collaborations with "different-party teams": Taichung Green Museumbrary (Lu Shiow-yen, KMT), the Changhua Taiwan Design Show, and the Museum of Contemporary Art Taipei[^26].

> 📝 **Curator's note:** "Citizen before designer" is the ordering Aaron Nieh himself chose in the 2020 _La Vie_ interview. But "citizen" and "designer" in his career trajectory have always been the same profession, just presenting different angles on different projects. When he did the Sunflower NYT ad, he was both citizen and designer; when he did the Taipower logo, he was both designer and citizen. When critics ask "can you separate them," what they are really asking is about the weighting of the same profession across two commissioning axes: when civic-duty projects and commercial-government projects sit side by side on the studio's project list, by what standard does the outside world judge? This is a question Aaron Nieh's twenty-year career continues to generate. His own answers, the media's labels, critics' accusations, and typography historians' rebuttals were all still pulling in different directions as of 2026.

## The Name "True Lie Workshop"

The studio name "True Lie Workshop" (永真急制) itself contains a pun: "Yong-zhen" (永真) is his given name, while "ji-zhi" (急制) is a homophonic play on "設計" (design) and also references the speed and time-sensitivity of design work. After operating in Taipei for many years, a second location was added in Kaohsiung's Pier-2 in 2024. Nieh is often treated as a "personal brand," but in practice True Lie Workshop's twenty-year roster of collaborators includes Chen Xun-chi, White Light Motion (Hung Yu-tang), type designers (the Taipower project involved commissioning professional type designers for refinement[^25]), photographer Wing Shya (Golden Horse 50), and the multiple graphic designers he refers to as "colleagues" in interviews.

If you printed out True Lie Workshop's project directory—from Sodagreen's _Autumn: Stories_ and Yoga Lin's _Fiction_ to the anti-Tedros NYT front page, from Tsai Ing-wen's 2016 "Light Up Taiwan" to the 2025 Taichung Green Museumbrary, from the Taipower logo to the One Song Orchestra albums—it would span nine industries: pop music, publishing, books, commercial branding, civic movements, political campaigns, national identity, art spaces, and international exhibitions. This span itself is the real backdrop to every discussion that occurred when the Taipower controversy erupted on May 8, 2026: Aaron Nieh is one of the key nodes in the formation of Taiwan's visual language since the 2000s, a single studio that has done design for every industry.

In the final paragraph of that _BIOS monthly_ "Trolley Problem" feature, Nieh said: "Setting politics aside, every project runs into this kind of thing."[^5] Political projects have higher visibility, but the ethics of taking on work is essentially the same in every commercial project. He will continue to take on projects and continue to face the question of "citizen Aaron Nieh vs. designer Aaron Nieh." What this studio's twenty years tells the reader is that the question itself is the core of the work.
