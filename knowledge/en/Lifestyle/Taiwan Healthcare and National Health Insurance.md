# Taiwan Healthcare and National Health Insurance: World’s No.1 Supported by 12.61% on 99.6%

> **30‑second overview:** The National Health Insurance (NHI) launched on 1 March 1995; Ye Jin‑chuan famously said “the preparation time was actually only three days”[^1]. Thirty years later the coverage rate is 99.6% of the eligible population[^2], administrative costs have long stayed below 2%[^3], and the Numbeo Health Care Index has been world‑number one for six consecutive years[^4]. Yet the system also rests on three pillars: a 5.17% premium rate, a nursing turnover rate that reached a ten‑year high of 12.61%[^5], and a political taboo—household total income—that has gone untouched for three decades. This article moves from the “three‑day panic” of 1995 to the 2026 three‑shift nurse‑patient ratio, dismantling the consensus of “cheap NHI,” “the five specialties are all empty = low point value,” and “IC‑card world‑first,” to reveal how a successful institutional design has locked the political path for system updates.

## “The preparation time was actually only three days”

At midnight on 1 March 1995, Taiwan’s universal health insurance officially began. The outward narrative was “world‑record speed”: 96 % coverage and a single card that could be used at 18,000 contracted medical institutions across the island[^6]. The internal story, recalled by the first director of the Central NHI Bureau, Ye Jin‑chuan, in 2021, was “the preparation time was actually only three days,” “the NHI cards hadn’t even been printed yet,” “the information system was not in place,” and “it was a fire‑under‑the‑butt situation”[^1]. More than 20 million cards were rushed into production a month after launch; on 1 March the public had to use their ID cards to see a doctor.

If we pull the timeline back, the scheme had been in the making for 27 years. The Ministry of the Interior first drafted universal health insurance in 1968; the Executive Yuan set 2000 as the target launch year in 1986; the Economic Construction Council formed a dedicated planning group in 1988[^7]. On 28 February 1989, Minister Yu Kuo‑hua announced in the Legislative Yuan that the launch would be moved up to 1995; in July 1990 the planning work transferred from the Economic Construction Council to the Department of Health; after Hao Po‑tsun took over, the date was advanced again to 1994; in February 1991 the Department of Health established a NHI planning group; and in December 1993 the Central NHI Bureau preparatory office was inaugurated, with Ye Jin‑chuan appointed director[^7].

The legislative process was turbulent. On 16 July 1994, DPP legislator Shen Fu‑hsung staged a hunger strike to protest the NHI law[^8]; three days later the NHI Act passed its third reading; on 12 November labor groups staged the “Autumn Fight,” shouting “three‑no‑insurance”[^7]. The Central NHI Bureau Organization Act was passed on 23 December 1994; the bureau itself was established on 1 January 1995; and on 25 February 1995 Premier Lien Chien‑chung ordered that the launch proceed on schedule, making Ye’s “three‑day preparation” count from that point[^1].

The system’s skeleton was chosen in 1989 by a task force led by Harvard School of Public Health scholar William Hsiao, which compared models from the United States, United Kingdom, Germany, France, Canada and Japan and selected the Canadian‑style “single‑payer” model[^9]. Hsiao’s reason for picking the Canadian model was succinct: “the quality of services was very high”[^10]. The five attributes of a single‑payer system were later dissected by scholars as: a unified risk pool, a uniform benefit package, sufficient medical resources, a single purchaser setting the rules of the game, and a global budget to control medical inflation[^11]. These five pillars explain why Taiwan’s NHI still enjoys the world’s highest coverage rate after 30 years, and why any reform that touches one pillar inevitably moves all of them.

Satisfaction data from the first year are often mythologized: coverage surged to 96 % in 1995, but contemporaneous polls showed only 39 % satisfaction and 47 % dissatisfaction[^12]. “Universal support” is a post‑hoc narrative; at the time the public was wary of a brand‑new, top‑down payment scheme.

> 📝 **Curator’s note:** “Single‑payer” literally means “single insurer,” but those four words have locked the political possibilities for three decades. Because everyone is in the same pool, any decision that would change the premium rate, the benefit package, the cost‑sharing formula, or the way household total income is calculated has no “pilot” or “partial rollout” fallback. Either the whole system changes together, or it stays as is. This was written into the DNA when Hsiao selected the Canadian model in 1989, and thirty years later it remains both the NHI’s greatest strength and its biggest political brake.

## Lee Yu‑chun’s Four Characters

The phrase “the five specialties are all empty” became a shorthand for Taiwan’s medical woes in the 2020s: internal medicine, surgery, obstetrics‑gynecology, pediatrics, and emergency medicine could not recruit enough resident physicians. The coiner of the term was not Ye Jin‑chuan, nor the medical association, nor the Ministry of Health and Welfare—it was Yang‑ming University public‑health professor Lee Yu‑chun, who coined “four empties” (internal, surgery, OB‑GYN, pediatrics) in the summer of 2009[^13]. The media later added emergency medicine, turning it into “five empties.”

Professor Jiang Tung‑liang recalled in 2018: “In the summer of 2009, Inspector Huang Huang‑hsung began a comprehensive NHI audit. By 2011, when the audit report was released, the ‘four empties’ issue suddenly became urgent”[^13]. Ye Jin‑chuan wrote a counter‑narrative in 2018: “What the medical community calls the five empties actually originated at a conference where a dean reported a shortage of internal, surgery, OB‑GYN residents and called it ‘four empties.’ Then the term spread, emergency medicine was added, and the media exaggerated it into ‘five empties’”[^14].

The first official documentation of the term appeared in the Control Yuan’s correction act of 17 July 2012. The correction committee (Huang, Shen, Liu) wrote plainly: “The four specialties suffer ‘little money, many tasks, close to prison,’” “Medical disputes and the payment system have become two ‘tight‑ening curses’ that choke the development of the five specialties,” and “Most hospitals have resident physicians working over 300 hours per month; some hospitals with no residents at all require attending physicians to take on on‑call duties, sometimes reaching 400 hours per month”[^15].

Ye Jin‑chuan has consistently qualified his view of the “five empties.” In a 2018 article he wrote: “It is not that the five specialties are completely lacking physicians; the total number of physicians is sufficient”[^14]. This counter‑argument is rarely heard in mainstream discourse, yet it is crucial for understanding the later inference that “low point value = five empties.” If the total number of physicians is adequate, the “cannot recruit” problem must be about distribution, work environment, liability exposure, and on‑call systems—not a simple supply shortage.

## 5.17% Appears Twice

The political history of the NHI premium rate can almost be told in a single table. It started at 4.25 % in 1995; was raised to 4.55 % in September 2002; increased to 5.17 % in April 2010 under Yang Chih‑liang—a hike that sparked enough controversy that Yang resigned on 26 January 2011 after the second‑generation NHI passed its third reading[^16]; the rate fell back to 4.91 % with a 2 % supplemental premium when the second‑generation NHI launched in January 2013; it dropped to 4.69 % plus a 1.91 % supplemental premium from 2016 to 2020; and in January 2021, under Chen Shih‑chung, it was raised again to **5.17 %**, with the supplemental premium rising to 2.11 %[^17].

Thus 5.17 % has appeared twice. The first time was after Yang’s resignation in 2010; the second time was under Chen in 2021. Eleven years separate the two, and the system has come full circle. This steady state is the result of a dual lock: a single‑payer structure combined with a politically sensitive premium rate. Each adjustment incurs political cost; when that cost reaches its limit the rate is pulled back, only to be raised again when finances can no longer bear it.

The second‑generation NHI ultimately chose a “supplemental premium” as a compromise. The original proposal was a “household total income” levy—charging based on the household’s annual total income, theoretically the fairest method. The 2010 legislative process flipped the script. After the third reading, Yang declared: “The supplemental premium is a mistake,” “The second‑generation NHI was supposed to be funded by household total income, but the Ministry of Finance said it could not be implemented; later the Finance Committee legislators overturned the Environmental Health Committee’s decision and, in three days, introduced the supplemental premium idea”[^18].

Consequently, the 2013 supplemental premium targeted six categories of income for single‑item calculation: large bonuses, part‑time wages, freelance fees, dividends, interest, and rent[^19]. From day one the design had two flaws: single‑item calculation made it easy to split income to avoid the charge; and income sources outside the six categories (e.g., overseas income, crypto assets) were not captured. Fifteen years later, in November 2025, the NHI planned to shift from “single‑item” to an “annual settlement” system—charging once a year when cumulative income exceeds NT$20,000, raising the withholding cap from NT$10 million to NT$50 million, affecting an estimated 4.8 million people and injecting NT$10–20 billion into the fund[^20]. The Executive Yuan, however, temporarily halted the reform in November 2025, sparking backlash from stock‑holding and retired groups.

The phrase “household total income” has been a political taboo for three decades. Hsiao’s 1989 single‑payer prototype already pointed to a comprehensive‑income‑tax‑style billing; Yang’s 2010 push led directly to his resignation; and even the moderate “annual settlement” version was stopped by the Executive Yuan in 2025. Technically feasible, politically impossible. Any reform that tries to fold diverse income sources into a single base meets opposition in the Legislative Yuan.

> ⚠️ **Contested view:** The supplemental premium has long been criticized by the left as a “regressive tax.” Salaried workers have it deducted monthly, while capital‑gain earners are only hit when they receive dividends or rent, and there is a withholding cap. Yet the 2010 legislative saga was the opposite: left‑wing labor groups opposed “household total income” (fearing families would be double‑counted), while the right‑wing Ministry of Finance opposed it (citing administrative capacity), and the Environmental Health Committee’s proposal was overturned by the Finance Committee in three days[^18]. Who is the “escaped fat sheep” has shifted three times over thirty years, and no consensus has emerged.

## 39.8% Uses, 54.8% Pays

The Directorate‑General of Budget, Accounting and Statistics released Statistical Bulletin No. 231 in 2020, providing a rarely cited precise breakdown: people aged 65 + consume 39.8 % of NHI resources; those aged 15–64 consume 54.8 %; and those aged 0–14 consume 5.4 %[^21]. Compared with 2015, the 65 + share rose by 4.2 percentage points. Per‑capita NHI spending for the 65 + group exceeds NT$70,000, versus about NT$10,000 for younger groups—a seven‑fold gap[^21].

That seven‑fold gap, multiplied by Taiwan’s accelerating ageing curve, is the biggest financial clock the NHI has faced in thirty years. Ye Jin‑chuan warned in 2020: “The current system can at most sustain another five years”[^22]. Counting five years from 2020 lands us in 2025; in November 2025 the NHI passed a budget of NT$988.335 billion for 2026, a 5.5 % growth, with a safety‑reserve level of two months, allowing the 5.17 % premium to remain unchanged[^23]. On the surface it held, but the underlying mechanics were precarious.

The first framing to correct is the common claim that “the NHI is about to go bankrupt.” The NHI operates on a pay‑as‑you‑go basis; there is no “bankruptcy” concept. The accurate statement is whether the safety‑reserve level falls below the statutory minimum[^24]. In June 2025 the NHI estimated a safety reserve of 0.96 months; by November it was back to two months, thanks to a NT$3 billion government top‑up and a NT$18.1 billion reallocation from the civil service budget[^23]. The NHI Executive Secretary Zhou Shu‑wan’s November 19 2025 statement was technical: “The 115th year NHI total‑budget safety reserve is two months, not below the waterline, and the premium rate remains at the current 5.17 % without adjustment”[^23].

A more hidden support mechanism is the “point‑value guarantee of NT$0.95.” Taiwan’s NHI settles on a “global budget + point‑value float” system—providers’ reported points are divided by the fixed total budget and then converted into NT$; point values have fallen to NT$0.7–0.8 in some counties, earning the nickname “pay NT$0.3 for a whole service”[^25]. To appease the medical community, the government pledged from 2024 to guarantee the point value at NT$0.95, a policy that The Reporter estimated would add NT$70 billion in spending, with the Executive Yuan allocating NT$33.6 billion in subsidies (NT$18.1 billion from the civil service budget plus NT$15.5 billion from the surplus fund)[^25]. Zheng Shou‑hsia (Dean of NTU School of Public Health and first director of the restructured NHI Bureau) summed it up: “Guaranteeing the point value is tantamount to abolishing the global budget,” “Mathematically it adds up, but it does not solve the substantive problem”[^25].

Partial cost‑sharing was reformed on 1 July 2023: outpatient drug co‑payment caps were raised to NT$300 for medical centers and NT$300 for regional hospitals; emergency department co‑payment caps were set at NT$750 for medical centers and NT$400 for regional hospitals[^26]. The new regime affected 8.45 million people, generating an additional NT$3.273 billion in the first year. Public Television Service’s evaluation a year later was ironic: average outpatient visits per person rose to 14.3 per year—a four‑year high; emergency visits per person increased by 0.1 compared with the previous year[^26]. NHI Director Shi Chong‑liang emphasized that chronic disease cases were rising and injected NT$5.27 billion, but the head of the Medical Reform Association, Lin Ya‑hui, criticized the policy: “NHI is not commercial insurance; it emphasizes shared risk and volume‑based payment”[^26]. Treating partial cost‑sharing as a “demand‑suppression tool” runs into the opposite spirit of the NHI law.

## Low Point Value Is a Symptom, Not a Root Solution

Mainstream discourse attributes the “five empties” to a single variable: “NHI benefits are too low.” The derived inference—“low point value → low physician salaries → cannot recruit”—has become near‑consensus in medical politics from 2024 to 2026, but academia and the NHI itself offer five counter‑arguments that dismantle this single‑cause framing.

1. **Zheng Shou‑hsia’s “abolish the global budget” argument:** “Guaranteeing the point value is tantamount to abolishing the global budget,” “Mathematically it adds up, but it does not solve the substantive problem”[^25]. The global‑budget system was designed as a brake on medical inflation; guaranteeing the point value loosens that brake—money is injected, but utilization rises, pulling the point value down again in a perpetual subsidy loop.

2. **Gao Ruo‑xiang’s (Secretary‑General of the Taiwan Medical Workers Union) “trickle‑down effect does not exist” argument:** The union’s analysis of 13 private‑hospital financial statements found “staff cost ratio is weakly correlated with profit margin.” Hospital surplus is spent on construction, equipment, expansion—not automatically trickling down to front‑line staff wages. In other words, “government subsidizes point value → hospitals profit → staff get raises” does not hold in the data.

3. **Lee Yu‑chun’s own counter‑argument:** The coiner of “five empties” told The Reporter in 2024: “The point value is a symptom, not a root solution”[^25]. Ironically, the person who raised the alarm later argued that money alone does not fix the problem.

4. **Shi Chong‑liang’s (NHI Director) “target point value without guarantee” argument:** “We hope the target point value can reach NT$0.95, but not by guaranteeing it, otherwise medical services will be distorted,” and “Physician salaries depend on hospital management; operators must bear substantial responsibility”[^25]. The director returns the ball to hospital managers: the point value is a total budget; how it is allocated among physicians, nurses, and technicians is a hospital‑management issue, not an NHI issue.

5. **Ye Jin‑chuan’s 2018 statement “total physician numbers are sufficient”**[^14]. If the total supply is adequate, “cannot recruit” must be a distribution problem, not a supply problem. The real questions are “why do emergency physicians prefer private clinics?” “Why do obstetrics‑gynecology doctors shift to cosmetic surgery?”—not “why are physician salaries low overall.”

These five counter‑arguments combine into an uncomfortable conclusion: the framing “low point value = five empties” is simple, politically palatable, and mobilizing, but it does not withstand scrutiny. Real solutions involve medical‑liability reform, resident‑work‑hour caps, internal hospital allocation, specialty‑training pipelines, and overseas‑license recognition—each ten times more complex than tweaking the premium rate or point value.

> 💡 **Did you know?** The average Taiwanese person makes 18 outpatient visits per year, more than twice the OECD average[^27]. The same cold symptom may lead a patient to three different clinics and three prescriptions. This “doctor‑shopping” behavior is a long‑standing academic discussion. The cheap NHI comes at the price of structurally amplified utilization; amplified volume then pushes the point value down. “Cheap → overuse → point value falls → staff salaries can’t keep up” creates a self‑reinforcing loop, not a linear transmission.

## 12.61% Turnover, 60% Practice Rate

Nursing shortage must be read precisely: a nursing license does not equal active practice. In 2023, about 310,000 nurses held a license, but only 60 % were practicing—roughly 186,000 people[^28]. One‑third of newly hired nurses left within three months[^28]. Annual turnover rates (per hospital reports) were 11.12 % in 2019, 10.13 % in 2021, 11.73 % in 2022, and **12.61 % in 2023**—a ten‑year high; the same year the vacancy rate hit 9.05%, also a ten‑year high[^29].

Population ageing amplifies the issue. The average age of practicing nurses rose from 36.33 years in 2019 to over 39 years in June 2024[^28]. The proportion under 40 fell from >70 % in 2017 to <50 % in 2023. Flow‑out analysis (Han Xing‑wen / National Chengchi University fiscal‑tax study of 13 private hospitals) shows 30 % move to NHI clinics, 25 % to private clinics, 10 % to long‑term care, 15 % to other industries, and 20 % retire abroad[^28].

Global demand is expanding simultaneously. By 2030 the world will face a shortage of 4.5 million nurses; Taiwan’s 2023 practicing pool of 186,000 will need to grow to an estimated 241,000–260,000, a gap of 55,000–74,000 nurses[^28]. Australian registered nurses earn NT$1.44–2.20 million, two to three times Taiwan’s salary. Lin Hsiu‑chu (Vice‑President of the Taiwan Nurses Association) said Australian scholars told her directly: “Sorry, we have been aggressively recruiting Taiwanese nurses for several years”[^28].

Bed closures are the most visible indicator. Taiwan’s 22 medical centers report that NHI‑covered beds account for 92 % of total beds; post‑pandemic estimates put the closure rate above 30 %[^28]. Gao Jing‑qiu (Vice‑President of Wan‑Fang Hospital and former president of the Taiwan Nurses Association) put it bluntly: “This is the most severe loss in thirty years,” “Which hospitals would voluntarily report closures to the local health bureau? Hospitals don’t talk about closures, they say ‘load reduction’”[^28].

The political tug‑of‑war over closures erupted in June 2024. NHI Nursing Division Director Cai Shu‑feng publicly asked on 1 June 2024: “Which hospitals have closed beds?”[^28] The question was interpreted as the Ministry of Health and Welfare refusing to acknowledge closures; on 5 June the minister, Qiu Tai‑yuan, apologized to the Legislative Yuan for the incident[^30]. The same month, Zhao Lin‑yu (Director of Chiayi Christian Hospital’s Spine Center) told The Reporter in an interview: “Without operating rooms, do surgeons have a career?” “Our anesthesia‑nurse shortage means surgeons can only operate one day per week”[^28]. Luo Xiang‑yun (Director of the Emergency Medicine Department at Lin‑Kou Chang‑Gung) summed up the sentiment to patients’ families: “You may be angry with me, you may be rude to me, but you cannot be rude to a nurse!”[^28]

Three‑shift nurse‑patient ratio reform: on 1 March 2024 the Ministry of Health and Welfare announced an administrative order (day shift 1:6/1:7/1:10, evening shift 1:9/1:11/1:13, night shift 1:11/1:13/1:15); on 8 May 2026 the Legislative Yuan passed the third reading of a medical‑law amendment; on 12 May 2026 Premier Lai Ching‑te announced an accelerated rollout to 20 May 2027 in phases[^31]. Penalties range from NT$250,000–500,000 for regional hospitals, NT$500,000–1 million for district hospitals, and NT$1–2 million for medical centers[^31]. This is the largest structural improvement to nursing work conditions in thirty years, but scholars generally assess that “better hours ≠ automatic return” — conditions in Australia and Canada remain more attractive, and Taiwan’s nursing salary structure has not kept pace, so the three‑shift ratio can only slow the exodus, not reverse it.

## The 99.6% Blind Spot

The “99.6 % coverage” figure is often shortened to “universal.” Precisely, it is “99.6 % of the eligible population”[^2]. Beyond the eligible definition, three groups remain uncovered: lost migrant workers, unregistered newborns (so‑called “black‑baby” cases), and overseas nationals whose NHI was suspended.

Lost migrant workers lose NHI eligibility and often accrue unpaid emergency‑care bills, a long‑standing blind spot in Taiwan’s migrant‑worker health‑policy landscape[^32]. The Global Taiwan Institute’s January 2025 policy report noted that the overseas “stop‑and‑resume” mechanism had been treated as exploitation—people living abroad did not pay premiums, and only reinstated coverage when they returned to Taiwan for care[^33]. A reform on 23 December 2024 finally abolished the stop‑and‑resume mechanism, but the downstream cost calculation and retroactive enforcement remain political taboos.

The NHI database issue runs deeper. From its 1995 launch to 2024, the Taiwan NHI database accumulated 70 billion medical records and 3.4 billion medical images, supporting over 8,414 peer‑reviewed publications[^34]. The NHI and National Taiwan University Hospital signed an MOU to co‑build the world’s first image‑information‑based cardiovascular‑risk database[^34]. This is one of Taiwan’s greatest soft‑power assets in academia.

However, on 12 August 2022, the Constitutional Court’s 111th Constitutional Interpretation No. 13 ruled the database unconstitutional on three grounds: lack of an exit right, ambiguous regulations, and absence of an independent oversight mechanism[^35]. A three‑year deadline was set. On 2 December 2025 the “National Health Insurance Data Management Act” passed its third reading, granting an exit right[^36], marking the biggest corrective step for NHI data governance in thirty years.

Reading the two together: 99.6 % is an achievement of coverage; the database’s unconstitutionality is a side‑effect of using that coverage “too thoroughly.” One price of universal enrollment is that every individual’s medical record enters the same pool; thirty years later big‑data researchers extracted it for studies, and the public realized “I never consented.” The Constitutional Court’s decision essentially returns the “data right” to individuals. This is a major blow to academic research but a corrective for basic‑rights protection.

Another often‑overlooked blind spot is accessibility. The IDS (Island‑and‑Mountain‑Remote‑Area Medical Service Enhancement) program launched in 1999, covering 49 mountain/island areas plus 49 non‑mountain/island areas, providing designated outpatient clinics, 24‑hour emergency rooms, mobile medical teams, home‑care, and post‑referral transport[^37]. NHI covers “insured” (99.6 %), but not “accessibility.” Rural residents may have an NHI card, yet the nearest hospital is 50 km away, making the card’s practical value very different from that of urban dwellers. IDS is a remedial mechanism, not a systemic solution.

## From IC Cards to Databases, Too Fast and Too Far

Taiwan’s NHI IC card was fully reissued nationwide in 2004 and is often touted as a “world‑first.” The accurate version is: Slovenia completed a nationwide smart‑card deployment in September 2000, becoming the “world’s first” universal health‑smart‑card nation. The Slovenian Health Insurance Institute (HIIS) and the 2001 PubMed‑indexed Hriberšek paper document this[^38]. Taiwan became the second, the largest, and the most integrated case, lagging by three and a half years.

This correction is not meant to deny Taiwan’s achievement, but to adjust the narrative habit of “we are world‑first.” When that habit is internalized, it extends to “our data governance is world‑class,” a claim that ran into the 2022 Constitutional Court decision thirty years later.

> ✦ **Closing maxim:** The NHI IC card and the NHIRD have been at the world’s forefront for thirty years; but moving too fast, too far, and too deep often causes a system to stumble over its own achievements. The Constitutional Court’s 111th Constitutional Interpretation No. 13 signals a moment when a mature system must look back and make up the missing pieces, not a sign of systemic failure.

The “world‑first” framing also needs calibration on international indices. Numbeo’s Health Care Index placed Taiwan first for six consecutive years up to 2024, scoring 86/100 (South Korea 82.7, Japan 79.3, Netherlands 78.9, France 78.1), covering 4,119 cities and 43,700 respondents[^4]. However, this is a user‑perception index, not an institutional metric; respondents self‑select and the scores reflect subjective impressions. Bloomberg’s Health‑Care Efficiency Index actually ranked Taiwan 14th in 2018 (not #9); in 2020 the ranking jumped to the top 4 due to Covid‑19 re‑weighting; by 2024 the list had ceased updating[^39].

Academic citations are the real evidence. William Hsiao’s 2003 Health Affairs paper “Does Universal Health Insurance Make Health Care Unaffordable? Lessons From Taiwan” remains the most‑cited English‑language article on Taiwan’s NHI[^9]. Cheng (2015) wrote in Health Affairs: “Taiwan’s NHI stands out as a high‑performing single‑payer national health insurance system that provides universal health coverage to Taiwan’s 23.4 million residents based on egalitarian ethical principles”[^40]. Princeton economist Uwe Reinhardt (who died in 2017 and had been a NHI advisor since 1989) wrote in his own article: “I have not advocated the single‑payer model here because our government is too corrupt”[^41], publicly opposing a U.S. copy of the model.

As for the “Obama learned from Taiwan” narrative, no official delegation record exists. The accurate version is that Taiwan is the case most frequently cited by U.S. single‑payer advocates; scholars (Reinhardt, Cheng) and politicians (Bernie Sanders) form the citation chain, not a direct “Obama administration learning” episode[^41].

The private‑insurance market provides another counter‑evidence. A 2004 survey showed that household private‑health‑insurance purchase rate was 72.3 %; in 1993 (pre‑NHI) it was 63.9 %[^42]. The hypothesis that “universal NHI chokes the private‑insurance market” is reversed—nine years after NHI’s launch, private‑insurance penetration rose by 8.4 percentage points. Taiwanese are pragmatic: they recognize NHI’s strengths but also know that upgrades, self‑pay therapies, overseas emergency care, long‑term care, and targeted cancer drugs lie outside the NHI benefit package and require private coverage. This is a structural reason why the question “Is NHI enough?” has become increasingly common after thirty years.

## Three Sentences Combined

Thirty years on, the tiny card issued at midnight on 1 March 1995 has become an app‑based QR code. Yet the same system still rests on three pillars: 99.6 % coverage, a 5.17 % premium rate, and a 12.61 % nursing turnover rate. The first two are sources of Taiwanese pride; the third is a taboo that no one has dared to touch for three decades.

Ye Jin‑chuan said in 2020: “The current system can at most sustain another five years”[^22]. Yang Chih‑liang, before resigning in 2011 after the third reading, said: “The supplemental premium is a mistake”[^18]. Lee Yu‑chun, the originator of the “four empties” term, said in 2024: “The point value is a symptom, not a root solution”[^25]. Combined, these three statements are not pessimistic; they are the culmination of thirty years of implementation finally spoken aloud. A world‑first NHI requires a world‑first investment; but “investment” has never been just about money. It points to household total income, hospital internal allocation, resident‑physician work hours, medical‑liability burden, and data‑governance. Each is ten times harder than adjusting the premium or point value.

William Hsiao’s 1989 reason for picking the Canadian model was “the quality of services was very high”[^10]. Thirty years later, Taiwan enjoys the world’s highest coverage rate while shouldering the lowest point value and a 12.61 % nursing turnover rate. Success is not the opposite of flaw—success itself creates political‑path dependencies that lock in the very defects. To move forward, we cannot rely on another “three‑day fire‑under‑the‑butt” sprint; we must build on the first time in thirty years that anyone is willing to tackle the “household total income,” “hospital allocation,” “resident work‑hour,” “medical‑liability,” and “data‑exit‑right” taboos, one by one.
