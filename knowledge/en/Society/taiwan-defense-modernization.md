> **30-second overview:** The 2022 Russo-Ukrainian War changed Taiwan's defense logic. In 2024, mandatory military service was restored from four months to one year. In 2025, Han Kuang Exercise 41 ran for a record ten days and nine nights, mobilizing 20,000 reservists. That November, the Lai Ching-te administration proposed a NT$1.25 trillion special budget to procure 200,000 drones. But on that same list, most of the money still went toward expensive conventional equipment — tanks, fighter jets, and submarines. Former Chief of the General Staff Lee Hsi-ming's response: "If a tank can be called an asymmetric weapon, then what can't be called an asymmetric weapon." Taiwan is walking two defense paths at once, and they contradict each other.

At the end of October 2025, on the Hukou National Parade Ground, a row of M1A2T "Abrams" tanks sat on the concrete. Lai Ching-te stepped down from the reviewing stand to preside over the activation ceremony, announcing that the "most powerful tank on earth" had officially entered the ROC Armed Forces' combat order of battle. The first batch of 38 arrived in December 2024, the second batch of 42 in July 2025, and the final batch of 28 in the first quarter of 2026, for a total of 108 vehicles, deployed with the 584th Armored Brigade and the 269th Mechanized Brigade at Hukou[^1].

At the same time, former Chief of the General Staff Lee Hsi-ming was signing books at a bookstore. His book, _Taiwan's Winning Hand_, argues that Taiwan should not buy expensive large weapons but should instead invest in large numbers of cheap, mobile, concealable small systems. When asked how he viewed the Ministry of National Defense's use of "decisive victory on the beach" as justification for purchasing the M1A2T tanks, he said with a wry smile: "All I can do is smile wryly when I see this. If a tank can be called an asymmetric weapon, then what can't be called an asymmetric weapon."[^2]

That sentence is the most honest key to understanding Taiwan's defense in 2026.
