> **30‑second overview:** On 1 June 1981, a financial magazine called Commonwealth was launched on a martial‑law‑bound Taiwan that had just lost its place on the international stage; the first print run of 10,000 copies sold out in two days[^1]. The initiator was economist Kao Hsi‑chun, who, under the idea of “scholars serving the nation,” gathered Wang Li‑xing, Chang Tso‑chin and Diane Ying to start the magazine[^2]; Diane Ying, a former United Press International, New York Times and Asian Wall Street Journal reporter, was its editorial soul and later the person who kept the magazine alive after the 1986 split[^3]. Over four decades it grew from a magazine that “writes economics as stories everyone can understand” into Taiwan’s business‑and‑local‑governance ranking engine: the 2,000‑big, Top‑50, benchmark enterprises and Happy‑City lists silently defined what “success” meant for Taiwanese people. It is one of the most trusted media outlets in Taiwan according to a Reuters report[^4] and has won the Magsaysay Award and the SOPA Lifetime Achievement Award. Yet the very position of trust is also the place where it sits closest to capital and power. That public credibility and that ranking power come from the same distance.

## “If you want to ruin a person, make him run a magazine”

Diane Ying once said with a wry smile, “If you want to ruin a person, make him run a magazine.”[^5]

The speaker had visited Eileen Chang in Cambridge in 1968. At the time she was a penniless exchange student, slipped into Chang’s room in borrowed jeans, and talked for three hours. “After me, no one ever interviewed Chang again, but she accepted my interview and we talked for three hours. I think she appreciated the interview. We kept in touch for more than a decade, and when I later founded Commonwealth Magazine she even sent me a greeting card.”[^6] Ying’s worldwide interviews ranged from semiconductor pioneer Morris Chang to literary giant Eileen Chang, and she eventually found that the hardest interview to land was the one with the very magazine she herself had to carry.

The person who actually invited people to run the magazine was Kao Hsi‑chun.

In 1981 Taiwan was an island that had just been pushed off the international stage. It withdrew from the United Nations in 1971, the United States cut diplomatic ties in 1979, diplomatic allies were severed one by one, martial law had not yet been lifted, and the topics that could be discussed were tightly framed—yet the economy was about to take off. At that critical moment, Kao Hsi‑chun, a Ph.D. in economics from Michigan State University who taught economics at the University of Wisconsin‑River Falls, acted on his “scholars serving the nation” conviction and invited then‑editor Wang Li‑xing, United Daily News editor‑in‑chief Chang Tso‑chin, and Diane Ying to co‑found Commonwealth Magazine in 1981[^7].

> **📝 Curator’s note**  
> A magazine that “only talks economics, never politics” seemed the safest, least‑risky choice on an island where politics could not even be discussed. Yet from another angle it was one of the most political choices of the time. When Taiwan lost its UN seat, lost its U.S. diplomatic allies and was virtually invisible on the diplomatic stage, a group of scholars decided to use the language of “economics” to rewrite a story of agency that the island could still grasp. Over the next forty years, the reason Commonwealth could stand in the “most trusted” position was precisely because it chose a perspective that was far enough from power yet close enough to Taiwan. That distance was its starting point.

Ying’s own question was even more direct. She is often quoted as asking, “Why does Taiwan not have a financial magazine that everyone can understand?”[^8] The question stemmed from her experience as an Asian Wall Street Journal reporter: she saw good business news that could “turn stiff economic reporting into something readable for the general public.” She wanted to create a magazine that combined the substance of _The Economist_ with the writing style of _Time_.

Ying’s journalistic pedigree is solid: she worked for the Philadelphia Inquirer, United Press International, the New York Times and the Asian Wall Street Journal—all major international news agencies[^3]. A Taiwanese woman who had written for the New York Times and contributed to the Asian Wall Street Journal and then went on to launch a home‑grown financial magazine is a rare trajectory.

On 1 June 1981 Commonwealth launched. The first print run of 10,000 copies sold out in two days, and a second edition was printed three times within the first month[^1]. The inaugural issue featured a dialogue with Formosa Plastics founder Wang Yung‑ching and economist Wang Tso‑jung, debating whether Taiwan should pursue “development” or “stability”[^9]. In an era when Taiwan had almost no international voice, the magazine asked a fundamental question about the island’s own direction.

## Scholars Serving the Nation, and a Naming Trap

![Commonwealth Magazine’s logo, black background with white “天下” characters derived from Sun Yat‑sen’s calligraphy](/article-images/society/commonwealth-logo.svg)
_The logo of Commonwealth Magazine, the two “天下” characters taken from Sun Yat‑sen’s calligraphy “天下為公”. Logo: CommonWealth Magazine. Public domain (PD‑textlogo) via Wikimedia Commons._

The title “天下” (Commonwealth) is taken from Sun Yat‑sen’s calligraphic phrase “天下為公”; the English name _Common Wealth_ carries a double meaning—both “confederation, commonwealth” and “wealth belongs to everyone”[^10]. A magazine that mainly reports on private capital and corporate wealth bears a name that talks about “the public” and “the world.” That tension has always been present.

But the name “天下” also links to the most commonly mistaken pair of brands in Taiwan’s media history.

Many people think “Commonwealth Magazine” and “Commonwealth Culture” are the same company. They are not. Commonwealth Culture is a publishing house belonging to the Vision‑Commonwealth Culture Group, which is Kao’s; Commonwealth Magazine is published by the Commonwealth Magazine Group, which belongs to Ying[^11]. Both use the same Chinese name but are distinct entities. The split reflects a deeper historical division.

> **💡 Did you know?**  
> The next time you see a book published by “Commonwealth Culture” (from _From A to A+_ to various business best‑sellers), it is actually part of Kao’s Vision Group, not the Commonwealth Magazine Group that publishes _Commonwealth_. The two “天下” belong to two different owners and follow two different paths—one of the most frequently misidentified brand pairs in Taiwan’s publishing circle.

## Four People, Five Years, Two Paths

![Kao Hsi‑chun speaking at a book‑launch event, wearing a dark suit](/article-images/society/kao-hsi-chun-2023.jpg)
_Chairman of the Vision‑Commonwealth Culture Group, Kao Hsi‑chun. He invited the team to found Commonwealth under the “scholars serving the nation” idea, and after the 1986 split created Vision and Commonwealth Culture publishing. Photo: Office of the President of the Republic of China, 2023. CC BY 2.0 via Wikimedia Commons._

In 1986, five years after its founding, the Commonwealth founding team split.

The split was about editorial direction. Ying wanted a professional news outlet that put Taiwan first; Kao wanted to focus more on corporate management and even expand into mainland China[^12]. The same group of scholars saw two different futures for “whether this magazine should be a news organization or a knowledge‑publishing venture for the Chinese‑speaking world.”

Consequently, Kao and Wang Li‑xing led a faction that left in 1986 to launch _Vision_ magazine, adding the publishing arm founded in 1982 to form the later Vision‑Commonwealth Culture Group. Ying stayed behind and kept Commonwealth Magazine.

> **📝 Curator’s note**  
> The usual narrative about a startup split assumes “splitting = failure.” Commonwealth’s split was the opposite. The “Taiwan‑first, news‑professional, most‑trusted” Commonwealth that we now know is the result of that division: after handing the “corporate‑management + mainland‑expansion” road to Vision, the remaining path was crystallised and guarded. In other words, the cleanliness of Commonwealth’s brand is partly because the other half of its DNA walked away. An institution’s identity often becomes clear only after a separation, when the remaining people can finally describe it.

Both sides claim different numbers of founders. Commonwealth’s official history usually lists three founders—Ying, Kao and Wang Li‑xing—while Vision’s official history and Kao’s own recollection also include Chang Tso‑chin, counting four; Chang was then United Daily News editor‑in‑chief and served as deputy editor‑in‑chief at Commonwealth, a core participant[^13]. The shared origin is remembered with two different numbers, itself a footnote to the split.

## Who Are the “2,000‑Big”?

![Commonwealth Economic Forum 2023, Grand Hyatt Taipei banquet hall, speakers on stage, audience of industry, government and academia](/article-images/society/cwef-economic-forum-2023.jpg)
_The Commonwealth Economic Forum (CWEF) 2023 winter session, Grand Hyatt Taipei. Commonwealth invites enterprises, government and academia into the same room each year. Photo: Chien‑chih Jian / Office of the President of the Republic of China. CC BY 2.0 via Wikimedia Commons._

If you stop here, Commonwealth is just a well‑run financial magazine. What turns it into something else is its ranking work.

In 1986—the year of the split—Commonwealth published Taiwan’s first large‑scale corporate ranking, the “1,000‑big,” covering manufacturing and modeled after the U.S. *Fortune* 500[^14]. From that year onward, Taiwan’s business community received an annual “report card” that was taken seriously. A noteworthy detail: the now‑famous “2,000‑big” brand did not solidify until 2013, when Commonwealth expanded the list to manufacturing, services and finance, totaling 2,000 companies[^14]. From 1986 to 2012 the list was called “1,000‑big.”

Rankings go beyond that. The benchmark‑enterprise reputation survey has been conducted since 1994; today, more than thirty years later, it asks industry leaders and analysts to evaluate twelve top‑size, profit‑making firms on ten indicators—TSMC and 7‑Eleven have long occupied the top spots[^15]. The “Top‑50 Groups” survey was run three times—in 2000, 2020 and 2025; in 2025, Hon Hai (Foxconn) led with NT$7.39 trillion revenue, while the TSMC Group surged from NT$1.11 trillion to NT$2.97 trillion to claim second, with eight groups falling out of the list over five years[^16]. Chang‑ming Tang, president of Chang Kang University, described the list as a “NVIDIA‑Island ranking,” meaning Taiwan’s corporate rankings are increasingly driven by an AI supply‑chain[^16].

> **✦** From “1,000‑big” to “2,000‑big,” from “benchmark enterprises” to “Top‑50 Groups,” the magazine has spent four decades defining what “success” means for the whole society.

The common narrative is that financial media like Commonwealth act as a neutral third‑party referee, fairly scoring companies. The story is tidy, but it omits a crucial fact: the referee and the players often share the same room. Commonwealth decides who ranks where, which indicators to use, and what counts as “success”; the ranked companies are also the magazine’s biggest advertisers, forum partners and event sponsors. It is not an outside referee; it is the most influential scorekeeper inside the arena. The scoring can be highly professional and credible—most of the time it is—but the act of scoring itself is a form of power.

The most contested aspect of that power is not the corporate list but the city rankings.

## “A Pig on a Tree” and a Mismatched Poll

Since 2003 Commonwealth has conducted a “county‑city survey” and, from 2004, added “government‑performance satisfaction.” The project later evolved into the well‑known “Happy City” and “County‑Mayor Performance Satisfaction” rankings, which blend public opinion with expert assessment[^17]. The results are published annually; some local leaders are delighted, others furious.

In 2017, Liu Zeng‑ying, mayor of Lienchiang County, topped Commonwealth’s list and commentator Lu Chih‑jun launched a full‑scale attack. He questioned Commonwealth’s practice of overlaying “four major civic groups” satisfaction scores onto the poll, calling it a “fish‑scales‑in‑a‑sea of pearls” technique, and added, “If Commonwealth’s ‘poll’ could be trusted, even a sow could climb a tree.”[^18] The remark is harsh but points to a real issue: when an “objective ranking” mixes in subjective evaluations, does it remain objective?

A more systematic critique came from academia. Liu Jia‑wei, professor of public administration at Taipei University, raised five methodological concerns in 2024 about the county‑city survey: insufficient sample sizes for some counties (Lienchiang’s sampling error reached ±7.49 percentage points); mismatched supporting data; lack of transparency in evaluation dimensions; bias introduced by mixing subjective and objective indicators; and double‑counting of overall and sub‑scores[^19]. In 2019, when former Kaohsiung mayor Han Kuo‑yu fell to the bottom of the performance‑satisfaction ranking, his supporters flooded social media, prompting Commonwealth to issue a statement clarifying its methodology and timeline[^20].

> **📝 Curator’s note**  
> The paradox of rankings is that Commonwealth can conduct these surveys and people care about them precisely because the magazine is trusted—an untrusted list would never attract criticism. Trust gives its rankings weight; that weight magnifies scrutiny of its methodology. Lu Chih‑jun and Liu Jia‑wei are not attacking an obscure magazine; they are reacting because Commonwealth’s rankings appear in news, are used by local governments as performance metrics, and influence voter perceptions. Its credibility backs its rankings; its rankings push its credibility into the riskiest arena. The same distance yields trust on one side and power on the other.

Beyond rankings, Commonwealth has also pursued projects that run counter to the notion of “success.”

- 1996 “Boundless Sky” education reform special tracked five 15‑year‑olds to see where they ended up a decade and two decades later[^21];
- 2001 “319 Rural Forward” distributed over a million “township passports” encouraging Taiwanese to visit their most unfamiliar towns[^22];
- 2005‑2006 “Smile Taiwan” turned the same idea into a long‑term brand.  
  The Commonwealth Magazine Education Foundation’s “Hope Reading Project” sends books and trains teachers in remote areas, funded by sponsors such as TSMC and ASML[^23]. These initiatives are sincere and have left tangible impacts. Yet when the charitable money comes from companies that Commonwealth ranks, the “distance” narrows again. No scandal, just the structural reality of a highly trusted media outlet’s everyday operations.

## The Retracted Impeachment Article

The cost of credibility surfaced sharply in November 2015.

That year President Ma Ying‑jeou met Chinese President Xi Jin‑ping in Singapore—the so‑called “Ma‑Xi meeting.” Huang Cheng‑yi, a researcher at the Academia Sinica’s Institute of Law, submitted an article to _Independent Review @ Commonwealth_ calling for the Legislative Yuan to immediately impeach President Ma. After publication, the piece was taken down[^24].

What followed was even bigger than the retraction itself. Huang publicly protested; a dozen columnists—including Hao Ming‑yi, He Ming‑xiu, Wu Jie‑min and Zhang Juan‑fen—quit _Independent Review_ in dissent[^25]. Commonwealth responded that the removal was based on “presenting multiple viewpoints.” Senior writer Ma Yue‑lin defended the decision, describing it as a “clumsy, poor, wrong choice” but insisting outsiders should not claim the magazine was steered by Beijing or that “Taipei became Beijing”—the magazine’s stance is that it does not want Taipei to become Beijing, hence the caution[^26]. Sociologist Zeng Bo‑wen wrote that _Independent Review_’s success lay in the word “independent,” not in the Commonwealth brand; the magazine applied its print‑magazine editorial logic to a web‑based commentary platform, a mismatch that generated controversy[^27].

> **📝 Curator’s note**  
> The difficulty lies in the fact that the same action carries opposite meanings in two worlds. In traditional magazine publishing, the editor‑in‑chief has the right to decide what to publish—a professional gate‑keeping virtue. In an online commentary platform, authors assume “I can speak freely here,” and post‑hoc removal feels like a violation of speech. Commonwealth applied its most trusted editorial discipline to a space it arguably should not have governed with the same logic. Its greatest strength, when misapplied, became its biggest controversy. The closer editorial power gets to authors, the farther trust may drift.

> ⚠️ **Controversial view**: Whether the retraction was politically pressured remains unresolved. Commonwealth consistently denies any Beijing or external influence, framing the episode as an internal editorial mistake. Critics argue that even absent external orders, the extreme caution exercised on cross‑Strait topics indicates a self‑censorship climate. This article does not draw a conclusion—but records the incident because it illustrates how “the most trusted media grapples with a sharply political article” for everyone to see.

A similar editorial slip occurred later. In 2023, entertainer Huang Tzu‑chao’s column was edited without consent; Commonwealth issued a public apology, admitting it had not followed internal procedures[^28]. Once gate‑keeping power is in hand, the possibility of error accompanies it.

## Putting the Publisher’s Shares into a Trust

If the story stopped at the retraction, Commonwealth would be a case of “public credibility turned against itself.” It did not stop there; it took a contrary step.

In 2021 Diane Ying placed the majority of her shares into a trust and directed the magazine’s surplus to be reinvested back into the publication[^29]. This structural move built a firewall for editorial independence—by locking the publisher’s equity in a trust, the magazine’s direction becomes harder for any single shareholder or short‑term commercial interest to sway. A media outlet accused of being “too close to capital” thus fortified the wall between capital and the newsroom.

The same year marked Commonwealth’s 40th anniversary. On 11 August, the 80‑year‑old Ying announced a hand‑over. She did not pass the magazine to family but to six professional managers: Wu Ying‑chun, Cheng Yi‑yuan, Wu Wan‑yu, Yeh Yun, Chen Yi‑shan and He Qi‑yu; Wu Wan‑yu had served as editor‑in‑chief for more than a decade and was the core of content[^30]. Ying herself moved to chair the Commonwealth Magazine Education Foundation. Her hand‑over statement included the line, “All things have their season, a time for all seasons. After forty years of working with Commonwealth, I have decided to pass the baton to the next generation of professional managers, opening a new journey.”[^31]

The journalist who asked, “Why does Taiwan not have a financial magazine everyone can understand?” handed the magazine over and returned to the issue that first mattered to her—reading and education.

## Print Lights Out, Magazine Still Shines

When Ying handed over, print media worldwide was dimming. Commonwealth kept the magazine alive through a series of uncharacteristic moves.

On 15 March 2017 Commonwealth launched “All Access,” Taiwan’s first metered paywall—readers could access a limited number of articles for free, then had to pay, a model inspired by the New York Times[^32]. By the end of that year, paid subscribers topped 10,000 with a renewal rate of about 90 %[^33]. This was a bold gamble in Taiwan, where the industry still believed “online content should be free, monetised by ads.” Commonwealth went against the grain, betting that readers would pay for depth.

Commonwealth’s digital transformation contains two milestones often conflated. In 2017 its digital ad revenue first surpassed print ad revenue—a “format‑type” breakthrough[^34]. It was not until after the pandemic, roughly 2020‑2022, that total digital revenue and subscriber numbers truly overtook print; the 2023 early‑year transformation report officially announced the shift. The gap between format‑type and revenue‑type breakthroughs spanned several years.

The group’s publications also multiplied, all belonging to Ying’s Commonwealth Magazine Group: _Health & Wellness_ (1998) – the first Chinese‑world health‑management magazine; _Cheers_ (2000) – “Happy Workers”; _Parenting Commonwealth_ (2008); _Independent Review @ Commonwealth_ (2013); _Crossing_ (2015), launched by senior writer Zhang Hsiang‑yi, gathering global youth voices; and the seasonal _Smile Taiwan_ (2016)[^35]. A single financial magazine gave rise to a whole ecosystem covering health, workplace, education, overseas youth and local travel.

Then the lights went out. In December 2024 _Parenting Commonwealth_ printed its final paper issue (No. 133); in 2025 it transitioned to the fully digital “Parenting Commonwealth Premium”[^36]. Also at the end of 2024, Commonwealth released Taiwan’s first ESG impact report for the media industry, and the previous year it became Taiwan’s first news organization to conduct a carbon inventory[^37].

> **💡 Did you know?**  
> Commonwealth was Taiwan’s first news outlet with a paywall, later the first to complete a carbon inventory and publish an ESG impact report. A magazine founded over forty years ago on an isolated island asking “Where is Taiwan headed?” continues to demonstrate “the next steps for the whole media industry.”

The magazine’s reporting reach has not shrunk with the paper’s retreat. In 2023, when the world debated how AI would reshape life, Commonwealth secured an exclusive interview with OpenAI founder Sam Altman.

Ying received the Magsaysay Award in 1987—Taiwan’s first recipient of the award in the “news” category[^38]. Her acceptance speech summed up the magazine’s four‑decade journey: “Journalism, perhaps, is the best profession in the world, because a journalist is paid to learn, and life itself is a process of ceaseless learning.”[^39] In 2021 SOPA (Asian Excellence in News Awards) gave her a Lifetime Achievement Award, describing Commonwealth as a “well‑respected, highly trusted media organization” in Taiwan[^40]. In Reuters’ Digital News Report, Taiwan’s overall news trust sits at 27‑33 %; yet Commonwealth ranked 57 % in 2022, tied with _Business Weekly_ as the most trusted outlet[^4].

## The Same Distance

Returning to the wry remark: “If you want to ruin a person, make him run a magazine.”

More than forty years later, the magazine that was “ruined” into existence has become one of Taiwan’s most trusted media institutions. It ranks enterprises, scores cities, and silently defines what “success” means for an entire society. Its credibility gives those rankings weight; that weight pushes the magazine to the spot closest to capital and power. Critics like Lu Chih‑jun, scholars like Liu Jia‑wei, and the retraction episode have not arisen because the magazine performed poorly, but precisely because it performed well enough to be trusted, so every judgment is taken seriously.

Ying answered this paradox by putting the publisher’s shares into a trust and reinvesting surplus back into the magazine, pushing capital a little farther from the newsroom, then a little farther again. She cannot eliminate the distance—a magazine that reports on Taiwan’s business must inevitably stand near business—but she can choose to build more walls along that distance.

Next time you see “Commonwealth 2,000‑big” or a “Happy City” ranking on a convenience‑store shelf or a news ticker, consider who compiled it, how, and for whom. Then consider another layer: a magazine’s “most trusted” status is both a product of how close it stands to capital and how forcefully it guards that distance so trust is not lost.

Diane Ying once wore borrowed jeans to interview Eileen Chang, kept in touch for over a decade, and received a greeting card from her on the launch day. That was the magazine’s original shape—a journalist and the person she cared about, separated by a pure distance. Forty years later, the magazine has learned how to measure, how to guard, and how to navigate the increasingly complex distance between itself and Taiwan’s capital. The journey is not yet finished.
