# Democratic System: From the 1987 Lifting of Martial Law to Lai Ching-te in 2024 — Coordinates of Taiwan's 37 Years of Democracy

> **30-second overview:** Taiwan endured 38 years of authoritarian rule beginning with the 1949 martial law declaration. After martial law was lifted on July 15, 1987, the democratization process began.[^1] Through the first direct presidential election in 1996 (Lee Teng-hui) and the first party rotation in 2000 (Chen Shui-bian), a complete democratic constitutional system was established.[^2] On May 17, 2019, Taiwan became the first region in Asia to legalize same-sex marriage.[^3] On January 13, 2024, Lai Ching-te was elected the 16th president with 5.58 million votes (40.05%), running alongside vice-presidential candidate Hsiao Bi-khim,[^4] marking the first time the Democratic Progressive Party achieved three consecutive presidential election victories. In the Legislative Yuan election held the same day, the Kuomintang reclaimed its position as the largest party with 52 seats, the DPP won 51 seats, and the Taiwan People's Party secured 8 seats — no party held a majority.[^5]

## Why It Matters

Taiwan's democratic system represents not only a transformation of political institutions but also the possibility of realizing democratic governance within a Chinese-speaking society. The experience of a peaceful transition from authoritarianism to democracy provides an important reference for other developing nations. At the same time, the establishment of democratic institutions has ensured the fundamental rights of the people of Taiwan, becoming a cornerstone of Taiwanese identity and value systems.

## Overview

Taiwan's democratization began with the political liberalization of the 1980s, progressing through key stages including the lifting of martial law, the opening of party and press bans, comprehensive legislative reform, and direct presidential elections. Despite challenges along the way, through the wisdom of political elites and the participation of the people, Taiwan successfully established a constitutional system with five-branch governance — executive, legislative, judicial, examination, and control — and has achieved multiple peaceful transfers of power.

## Key Facts

- **Lifting of martial law:** On July 15, 1987, President Chiang Ching-kuo announced the end of martial law, concluding 38 years of martial law rule.
- **First direct presidential election:** The first direct presidential election was held in 1996; Lee Teng-hui was elected as the first popularly elected president.
- **First party rotation:** In 2000, DPP candidate Chen Shui-bian was elected president, achieving the first peaceful transfer of power.
- **Second party rotation:** In 2008, KMT candidate Ma Ying-jeou returned to power, establishing party rotation as a norm.
- **Third rotation:** In 2016, DPP candidate Tsai Ing-wen was elected, becoming the first woman to serve as president.
- **2024 election:** On January 13, Lai Ching-te (DPP) was elected the 16th president with 5.58 million votes (40.05%), running alongside Vice President Hsiao Bi-khim on the same ticket, marking the DPP's first three consecutive presidential victories.[^4] Legislative Yuan election results: KMT 52 seats (reclaiming largest party status), DPP 51 seats (losing its majority), TPP 8 seats — no party held a majority.[^5]

## In-Depth Content

### The Authoritarian Period

**The martial law regime** (1949–1987) forms the backdrop to Taiwan's democratization. After the Nationalist government relocated to Taiwan, martial law was imposed, restricting freedoms of assembly, association, speech, and the press, and establishing one-party rule. During the **White Terror**, political persecution events such as the February 28 Incident and the Kaohsiung (Formosa) Incident caused social trauma but also galvanized the drive for democratization.

**Economic development** laid the foundation for democratization. Rapid economic growth from the 1960s to the 1980s cultivated a middle class, and the expansion of education raised political awareness and participation. **International pressure** — including withdrawal from the United Nations and the severance of diplomatic relations with the United States — compelled the ruling authorities to consider the necessity of political reform.

### The Democratic Transition

**The political liberalization phase** (1980s): In his later years, Chiang Ching-kuo initiated political reforms, opening space for opposition activities. The founding of the Democratic Progressive Party in 1986 broke the ban on new political parties. Although the **Kaohsiung (Formosa) Incident** (1979) led to the imprisonment of democracy activists, the courtroom defense process galvanized public support for democracy.

**The institutionalization phase** (1990s): Lee Teng-hui advanced a "Quiet Revolution," using constitutional amendments to achieve comprehensive legislative re-election, direct election of provincial and municipal executives, and direct presidential elections. The **1996 presidential direct election** took place under the shadow of military threats from the People's Republic of China; the people of Taiwan demonstrated their commitment to democracy with a high voter turnout.

**The consolidation phase** (2000s–present): Multiple party rotations have been achieved, and the democratic system has gradually matured. The **Sunflower Student Movement** (2014) demonstrated the power of civil society in holding the government accountable.[^6] The **legalization of same-sex marriage** (May 17, 2019) made Taiwan the first region in Asia to achieve this milestone.[^3] The **2024 general election** marked the first time in Taiwan's democratic history that the same party won three consecutive presidential elections, while the Legislative Yuan saw a three-party split with no majority, forming a new political structure.[^4][^5]

### Institutional Characteristics

**Five-Power Constitution:** Based on Sun Yat-sen's theory of five-branch separation of powers, the government comprises the Executive, Legislative, Judicial, Examination, and Control Yuans. **Presidential system tendencies:** After successive constitutional amendments expanded presidential powers, a semi-presidential system with distinctively Taiwanese characteristics emerged. **Legislative system:** The Legislative Yuan is the highest legislative body, elected under a single-district, two-vote system.

**Local autonomy:** A three-tier government structure operates at the central, special municipality/county, and township/district levels. Special municipality mayors and county/city magistrates are directly elected by the people. **Referendum system:** A 2018 legal amendment lowered the threshold for referendums, strengthening the function of direct democracy.

### Party Politics Development

**Two-party competition:** The KMT and the DPP are the two major parties, representing different positions on cross-strait relations. **Minor party roles:** Parties such as the People First Party, the Taiwan Solidarity Union, and the New Power Party have exerted critical influence at specific junctures. **Electoral system effects:** The single-district, two-vote system favors larger parties, but the party-list proportional representation system preserves space for smaller parties.

**Political culture:** A culture of obedience from the authoritarian era has shifted toward a culture of participation, with civil society flourishing. **Digital politics:** Social media has transformed political mobilization, and the handling of disinformation has become a new challenge for democratic governance.

### Challenges and Outlook

**Cross-strait relations** continue to influence democratic political development, with the unification-independence debate persistently dividing society. The rise of **populism** has impacted rational political discussion. **Generational differences** in values and voting behavior between younger and older generations have widened.

**Institutional reform** demands — including legislative reform, judicial reform, and constitutional reform — aim to respond to public expectations. **Digital governance** initiatives, including open government and e-participation, are being promoted to enhance the quality of democracy.

## International Standing

Taiwan is hailed as a "democracy miracle" and has been consistently rated as "Free" by organizations such as Freedom House. **Democracy assistance:** Taiwan actively participates in international democracy promotion, sharing its democratization experience. **Value diplomacy:** Democracy, human rights, and the rule of law serve as core diplomatic values, enhancing Taiwan's international image.
