# Environmental Justice and NIMBY Disputes in Taiwan

Environmental Justice focuses on the fairness of distributing environmental burdens and benefits. In Taiwan, environmental problems arising during industrial development are often concentrated in specific areas and among specific groups, creating a phenomenon of "environmental inequality." From garbage incinerators and nuclear waste disposal to petrochemical industrial parks, these necessary but risky facilities consistently trigger NIMBY (Not In My Back Yard) disputes, reflecting deep-seated contradictions in the distribution of environmental risks within Taiwanese society.

## Theoretical Foundations of Environmental Justice

### Unequal Distribution of Environmental Burdens

The core concept of environmental justice is "distributive fairness." Ideally, environmental benefits (such as clean air and beautiful landscapes) and environmental burdens (such as pollution facilities and health risks) should be distributed evenly. In reality, however, environmental burdens are often concentrated in specific areas, typically in communities with economic disadvantages and limited political influence.

The distribution of environmental burdens in Taiwan is clearly uneven. The Liucheng Petrochemical Industrial Park is located in Maiqiao Township, Yunlin County, where the local economy is primarily based on agriculture and fisheries, and residents have low incomes. The Kaohsiung Coastal Industrial Zone is located in Xiaogang District, an area with high population density but limited political influence. This distribution model reflects the "principle of least resistance to capacity"—pollution facilities tend to be located where opposition is weakest.

### Procedural Justice and the Right to Participate

In addition to distributive fairness, environmental justice emphasizes "procedural justice," meaning that all affected groups should have equal opportunities to participate in environmental decision-making. However, Taiwan's Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) system has long focused on technical assessments while neglecting public participation.

Residents often learn about facility locations only after decisions have already been made, lacking opportunities for prior participation in decision-making. Even when public hearings are held, they often become mere formalities, with public opinion having little real impact on the final outcomes. This "decide first, communicate later" model is a major cause of NIMBY disputes.

### Intergenerational Justice and Risk Assumption

Environmental justice also involves fairness between generations. The half-life of nuclear waste lasts tens of thousands of years; the current generation enjoys the benefits of nuclear power, but the risks are borne by future generations. Similarly, industrial development brings economic growth, but the environmental costs may persist for decades.

The traditional territories of Indigenous peoples often become sites for waste disposal, such as the nuclear waste storage on Lanyu (Orchid Island) and garbage landfills in mountain townships. These decisions were often made during eras when Indigenous peoples lacked political voice, yet their impacts continue to this day, forming a "colonial environmentalism."

## The NIMBY Dilemma of Waste Management

### Incinerator Siting Controversies

The construction of garbage incinerators in Taiwan has been fraught with NIMBY protests. During the 1990s, the "one incinerator per county/city" policy was promoted, and almost every incinerator faced strong opposition. The resistance against the Xindian An-Keng Incinerator was the most intense; local residents formed a self-help association and engaged in a ten-year opposition movement.

Reasons for opposition included health risks, falling property values, and reduced quality of life. Dioxin emissions were the primary concern. Although modern incinerators have reduced dioxin emissions to extremely low levels, public fear of "toxins" remains difficult to eliminate. The mindset of "not in my backyard" is understandable, but garbage must still be disposed of somewhere.

The government attempted to resolve disputes using compensation funds. Townships hosting incinerators receive compensation fees of tens of New Taiwan Dollars per ton of garbage for local infrastructure projects. However, monetary compensation cannot fully alleviate concerns about environmental risks, and the fairness of the compensation mechanism is also questioned.

### Cross-County Waste Processing

Taipei City sends its garbage to New Taipei City for processing, while New Taipei City sends its garbage to Taoyuan, creating a phenomenon of "garbage travel." While this regional processing solves the immediate crises of each county and city, it also triggers new issues of unfairness.

Landfills in remote mountainous areas become the final destination for urban garbage. Mountain townships such as Wulai, Sanxia, and Shihden, with sparse populations and limited political influence, often become the proposed sites for garbage landfills. Local residents protest, asking, "Why must we bear the garbage produced by urban dwellers?"

The distribution of waste processing costs is also unfair. Counties that generate garbage pay processing fees, but the counties bearing the environmental risks often receive compensation that is disproportionate. The principle of "polluter pays" is difficult to implement in cross-county garbage processing.

### Class Differences in Recycling

The impact of waste reduction policies differs across socioeconomic classes. Middle-to-high-income families can purchase products with less packaging, resulting in relatively less waste. Low-income families often buy cheaper goods with more packaging materials, generating larger volumes of waste.

The burden of recycling is also uneven. Street scavengers are mostly economically disadvantaged elderly people who engage in dangerous and dirty recycling work for meager incomes. Middle-to-high-income families enjoy the benefits of a clean environment, while the labor costs of recycling are borne by the lower classes.

## Ethnic Controversies in Nuclear Waste Disposal

### The Lanyu Nuclear Waste Incident

The Lanyu Nuclear Waste Storage Site is a classic case of environmental justice controversy in Taiwan. In 1982, Taipower established a low-level radioactive waste storage site on Lanyu, but the radiation risks were not fully communicated to the Tao people residents beforehand. It was initially claimed to be a "fish canning factory," and the Tao people accepted the nuclear waste without full knowledge.

This event highlights multiple injustices: ethnic injustice (exploiting the弱势 status of Indigenous peoples), intergenerational injustice (nuclear waste impacts persisting for hundreds of years), informational injustice (concealing true risks), and procedural injustice (lack of genuine informed consent).

The Tao people's resistance has lasted for forty years. They demand the removal of nuclear waste and the restoration of their ancestral lands. "Nuclear Waste Out of Lanyu" is not just an environmental demand but also a claim for Indigenous self-determination. Although the government promised removal, difficulties in selecting a final disposal site have left the nuclear waste stranded on Lanyu.

### The Dilemma of Final Disposal Site Selection

High-level radioactive waste requires deep geological disposal in geologically stable areas. Taipower commissioned Chung Hsing Engineering to conduct geological surveys, and sites such as Jinshan, Wanli, and Penghu were all listed as candidates, but all faced strong opposition.

Public fear of nuclear waste is understandable, but the realistic problem that "someone must bear the burden" cannot be avoided. Countries like Sweden and Finland have resolved siting issues through volunteer communities, substantial compensation, and transparent decision-making processes. However, Taiwan's social trust foundation is weak, making it difficult to replicate these models.

The screening criteria for candidate sites also spark controversy. While technical safety is crucial, social acceptance is equally important. Selecting sites solely based on geological conditions while ignoring local public opinion inevitably triggers protests. Finding a balance between technical rationality and social rationality is a challenge in policy formulation.

### Fair Burden of Nuclear Plant Decommissioning

Taiwan's three nuclear power plants are undergoing decommissioning, generating large amounts of low-level radioactive waste. The disposal of this waste faces similar environmental justice issues. Nuclear plants are mostly located in northern Taiwan, with electricity primarily supplied to the Taipei metropolitan area, but waste disposal sites may be located in other counties and cities.

Decommissioning costs are borne by the entire population, but environmental risks are concentrated in specific regions. This model of socializing costs while localizing risks is a manifestation of environmental injustice in nuclear energy policy. How to make beneficiaries bear corresponding responsibilities requires innovative institutional design.

## Environmental Burdens of the Petrochemical Industry

### Health Risks of the Liucheng Industrial Park

The Liucheng Petrochemical Industrial Park in Maiqiao, Yunlin, is Taiwan's largest petrochemical base, with an annual output value exceeding NT$2 trillion. However, the park also brings severe environmental burdens: air pollution, water pollution, soil pollution, and health risks.

Research from the College of Public Health at National Taiwan University found that cancer incidence rates among residents near Liucheng are significantly higher. The incidence rates of lung and liver cancer are 20-30% higher than the national average. Incidence rates of childhood asthma and allergies are also significantly elevated. These health risks are primarily borne by local residents, while economic benefits are mainly gained by enterprises and shareholders.

Air pollution from Liucheng has a wide impact range. Pollutants such as PM2.5, sulfur dioxide, and nitrogen oxides spread with the wind, affecting neighboring counties such as Changhua and Nantou. Air quality monitoring by the Environmental Protection Administration shows that air pollution concentrations in Yunlin have long exceeded standards.

### The Dalinpu Relocation Controversy

The Dalinpu area in Kaohsiung is surrounded by petrochemical industrial parks, and residents have been exposed to air pollution for a long time. The government's push for the Dalinpu relocation plan has been slow, causing resident dissatisfaction.

Relocation compensation standards are a focal point of controversy. The government purchases houses at market prices, but residents believe the compensation is insufficient to purchase equivalent housing elsewhere. Elderly residents are reluctant to leave their hometowns, while young people worry about employment issues. Relocation involves the reconstruction of an entire community, not just the buying and selling of houses.

Living arrangements during temporary relocation are also a difficult issue. Schooling for children, medical care for the elderly, and commuting for work all need to be rearranged. The government's resettlement plan lacks detail, leaving residents with a lack of certainty about their future lives.

### Transparency of Environmental Monitoring

Environmental monitoring in the petrochemical industry is often conducted by the enterprises themselves, raising questions about the credibility of the results. Citizens demand that the government set up independent monitoring stations, but the professional threshold for interpreting monitoring data is high, making it difficult for the general public to judge.

The tracking and supervision mechanisms for Environmental Impact Assessments are weak. Many EIA commitments in industrial zones are difficult to verify post-facto, and penalties for violations are insufficient. Lack of confidence in government regulatory capabilities deepens distrust of industrial facilities.

Citizen monitoring movements have emerged. Organizations such as the Changhua Environmental Protection Alliance and Yunlin Environmental Protection Alliance have purchased their own monitoring equipment to conduct air quality monitoring. This citizen science compensates for the shortcomings of official monitoring but also reflects gaps in government regulation.

## Social Distribution of Environmental Risks

### Class and Environmental Exposure

Environmental risks in Taiwan are clearly correlated with social class. Housing near industrial zones is mostly occupied by low-to-middle-income families due to lower housing prices and rents. High-income families live in areas with better environments, such as hillside residential areas and seaside villas.

Occupational environmental exposure also shows class differences. Blue-collar workers such as factory operators, construction workers, and sanitation workers are exposed to higher environmental risks. White-collar workers work in air-conditioned offices, resulting in relatively lower environmental exposure.

Educational level affects environmental risk perception. Highly educated individuals are more likely to understand environmental risks and have greater capacity to take protective measures. Less educated individuals may underestimate risks or be unable to move away from high-risk areas due to economic considerations.

### Urban-Rural Environmental Disparities

Urban areas enjoy better environmental quality and public services. Public transportation is developed, green spaces are abundant, and medical resources are sufficient. Rural areas often bear the environmental burdens produced by cities: garbage landfills, wastewater treatment plants, and thermal power plants are often located in rural areas.

Rural environmental monitoring networks are sparse, making it difficult to detect pollution events in a timely manner. Insufficient medical resources make it difficult to address environmental health risks immediately. Farmers may continue to farm on polluted land due to economic considerations.

Environmental justice issues in the process of urbanization are also worth attention. Urban renewal often relocates low-income residents to areas with poorer environments. Soil remediation of industrial sites is often funded by the public sector, but the benefits of land value appreciation are gained by private entities.

### Ethnicity and Environmental Justice

Indigenous areas often become the proposed sites for environmental risk facilities. Garbage landfills, nuclear waste storage sites, and large reservoirs in mountain townships have all impacted the traditional territories of Indigenous peoples.

Water resource distribution also shows ethnic disparities. Indigenous communities in mountain townships often lack safe drinking water, while downstream urban areas have sufficient water supply. The benefits and cost-bearing of water resources are disproportionate.

Indigenous environmental knowledge and management systems have long been ignored. Traditional natural resource management methods may be more sustainable than modern scientific management, but they lack policy attention.

## Response Strategies and Institutional Reform

### Reform of the Environmental Impact Assessment System

The current EIA system prioritizes technology over society and needs to increase the weight of social impact assessments. The scope of assessment should include health risks, social impacts, and cultural impacts. The assessment process should offer more opportunities for public participation.

The composition of the EIA Committee needs to be diversified. In addition to technical experts, social science experts, local representatives, and NGO representatives should be included. The decision-making process should be more transparent, with clear procedures for handling public opinions.

The EIA tracking and supervision mechanism needs strengthening. Independent third-party monitoring institutions should be established to regularly review the implementation of EIA commitments. Penalties for violations should have a deterrent effect and not be merely symbolic fines.

### Risk Communication Mechanisms

Establish effective risk communication mechanisms that allow citizens to understand and participate in risk decision-making. Risk information should be presented in a way that citizens can understand, avoiding technical black boxes.

Establish permanent communication platforms, not just communicating when facilities are being built. Regularly hold community meetings, collect citizen opinions, and respond to public concerns. Building trust requires long-term effort.

The media plays an important role in risk communication. Science journalists need better professional training to accurately report environmental risk information. Avoid sensational reporting while not ignoring reasonable public concerns.

### Compensation and Feedback Mechanisms

Design fair compensation mechanisms that consider not only monetary compensation but also community development needs. The use of compensation funds should be transparent and open, truly used to improve the quality of life in the locality.

Establish a health fund system to provide health monitoring and medical guarantees for residents near environmental risk facilities. Regular health checkups, medical expense subsidies, and health risk research can all be included in the fund.

Consider establishing an "Environmental Justice Fund" to assist environmentally disadvantaged communities in improving environmental quality. Funding sources could include pollution fees, environmental taxes, carbon taxes, and other environmentally related revenues.

### Citizen Participation Systems

Strengthen the legal basis for citizen participation in environmental decision-making. Establish citizens' rights to environmental information, participation, and supervision. Government information should be proactively disclosed, and there should be clear procedures for handling citizen opinions.

Empower environmentally disadvantaged communities to participate effectively. Provide resources such as legal assistance, technical support, and organizational training so that disadvantaged communities can effectively participate in environmental decision-making.

Promote the application of participatory budgeting in environmental policies. Allow citizens to directly participate in the allocation decisions of environmental budgets, increasing the democratic legitimacy of policies.

## International Experiences and Insights

### The US Environmental Justice Movement

The US Environmental Justice Movement originated in the 1980s, focusing on the concentration of toxic waste facilities in African American communities. After thirty years of development, environmental justice has been integrated into the mainstream of federal policy.

The US Environmental Protection Agency established an Office of Environmental Justice to ensure that all policies consider environmental justice aspects. Federal government agencies must assess the impact on disadvantaged communities when formulating policies.

California's Environmental Justice Act (SB 535) stipulates that 25% of greenhouse gas reduction investments must be used for disadvantaged communities. This "beneficiary priority" principle is worth reference for Taiwan.

### Procedural Justice in Europe

The European Union's Aarhus Convention establishes procedural rights such as the right to access environmental information, the right of public participation, and the right to judicial remedy. Member states must establish corresponding legal systems.

Sweden's environmental court system allows citizens to effectively remedy environmental rights. Professional judges, technical experts, and civil representatives jointly hear environmental cases. Judgments have mandatory force, and both the government and enterprises must comply.

The Netherlands' Environmental Impact Assessment system emphasizes public participation. Citizens can not only express opinions but also influence the scope and methods of the assessment. Assessment results must respond to the concerns raised by the public.

### Risk Communication in Japan

Japan has vigorously promoted risk communication systems after the nuclear disaster. The government established a dedicated agency, trained risk communication experts, and established standardized communication procedures.

The handling of the Fukushima nuclear disaster experience shows that risk communication is not just a technical issue but also a trust issue. The government must acknowledge uncertainty and assume decision-making responsibility to build public trust.

Local governments in Japan play an important role in risk communication. The attitude and policies of local chief executives often determine the success or failure of risk communication.

## Outlook and Conclusion

Taiwan's environmental justice issues reflect deeper structures of social inequality. Solving environmental justice issues requires addressing multiple inequalities simultaneously: class, ethnicity, urban-rural divides, and generations.

Future challenges include: climate change exacerbating the uneven distribution of environmental risks, just transition processes during energy transition, social impacts of circular economy development, and international trade environmental justice issues.

Environmental justice is not just an ideal but a practical reality. Establishing fair, transparent, and participatory environmental governance systems, allowing all citizens to enjoy environmental quality and bear environmental responsibilities, is a necessary condition for Taiwan to move towards a sustainable society.

Environmental decision-making in a democratic society should be based on sufficient information, open debate, and procedural justice. There are no perfect solutions, but we can pursue fairer processes. The realization of environmental justice requires the joint efforts of government, enterprises, and civil society, and more importantly, the active participation of every citizen.
