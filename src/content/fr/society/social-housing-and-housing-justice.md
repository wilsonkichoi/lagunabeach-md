# Social Housing and Housing Justice

## 30-Second Overview

Housing is a basic human need and a significant government responsibility. Since 2016, Taiwan has implemented the "8 Years, 200,000 Social Housing Units" policy, pursuing housing justice through a dual-track approach of direct construction and lease management. This is not merely about building houses; it is a reconstruction of social values, returning housing from a "commodity" to its essence as a "right to housing."

**Keywords:** Social Housing, Housing Justice, Lease Management, Housing Act, Youth Housing, Vulnerable Housing

## Why It Matters

Housing is more than just shelter; it is the foundation for people to establish their lives, build families, and pursue dreams. With the price-to-income ratio reaching 15-20 times, many young people find themselves "unable to afford to buy, unable to rent well." Social housing has become a crucial tool for the government to achieve housing justice.

For Taiwan, the social housing policy represents:

- **Social Equity:** Ensuring that economically disadvantaged groups can enjoy decent living standards.
- **Intergenerational Justice:** Providing young people with opportunities to settle down without being burdened by heavy mortgages.
- **Urban Development:** Guiding urban regeneration and improving the environment of old communities.
- **Social Harmony:** Reducing social conflicts arising from housing issues.

## Current Status of Taiwan's Housing Issues

### The Housing Dilemma in the Era of High Property Prices

**Persistently High Price-to-Income Ratio**

- Taipei City's price-to-income ratio is approximately 15-16 times (2024 data).
- New Taipei City is around 12-13 times, and Taoyuan City is around 9-10 times.
- In contrast, the international reasonable standard is approximately 5-6 times.
- The average family needs to go without eating or drinking for 10-15 years to afford a house.

**Problems in the Rental Market**

- A high proportion of the rental market is informal ("black market"), with many landlords unwilling to declare taxes.
- Rent prices are opaque, and tenants lack bargaining power.
- Rental quality varies significantly, and tenant rights lack protection.
- Short-term leases are common, leading to insufficient housing stability.

**Housing Needs of Special Groups**

- **Youth:** Wages cannot keep up with housing prices; buying a house seems out of reach.
- **Single-parent Families:** Heavy economic burdens and limited rental choices.
- **Elderly:** Decreasing fixed income faces housing risks.
- **Persons with Disabilities:** Require barrier-free environments, with even fewer choices available.

### Background of Policy Evolution

**Early Housing Policies (1950-2010)**
Taiwan's early housing policies focused on "encouraging home ownership":

- **National Housing Policy:** Built National Housing (public housing) sold at preferential prices.
- **Home Purchase Preferential Loans:** Supported home buying through financial policies.
- **Tax Refunds for Repurchase and First-Time Buyer Incentives:** Tax incentives encouraged buying.
- **Problem:** The primary beneficiaries were the middle class, with limited benefits for vulnerable groups.

**Enactment of the Housing Act (2011-2016)**
The _Housing Act_ was enacted in 2011, establishing a new direction for housing policy:

- Recognized the "right to housing" as a basic human right.
- Established the legal basis for social housing.
- Promoted the rental subsidy system.
- However, implementation was insufficient, and the construction of social housing was slow.

## Content of Social Housing Policy

### The 8-Year, 200,000 Units Plan

After the new government took office in 2016, it proposed the "8 Years, 200,000 Social Housing Units" policy:

**Policy Goals**

- Build 200,000 units of social housing from 2017 to 2024.
- Direct Construction: 120,000 units.
- Lease Management: 80,000 units.
- Total investment amount: Approximately NT$440 billion.

**Execution Strategy**

1. **Central-Local Cooperation:** The National Housing and Urban Renewal Center (National Housing Center) coordinates planning.
2. **Diverse Land Sources:** State-owned land, urban renewal returns, donations, etc.
3. **Innovative Financing Mechanisms:** Housing funds, special budgets for the Forward-looking Infrastructure Program.
4. **Professional Team Execution:** Establishing specialized agencies to improve execution efficiency.

### Dual-Track Progress Model

**Track 1: Direct Construction**
The government directly builds, owns, and manages social housing:

- **Construction Goal:** 120,000 units.
- **Funding Sources:** Housing funds, special budgets.
- **Management Method:** Government direct operation or entrusted to private management.
- **Features:** Better quality control and the ability to plan complete community functions.

**Track 2: Lease Management**
The government cooperates with private landlords to match vulnerable tenants:

- **Target Units:** 80,000 units.
- **Operation Model:**
  - **Lease (Baozu):** The government rents private housing and sublets it to the vulnerable.
  - **Management (Daiguan):** Assists landlords in renting out properties and provides management services.
- **Advantages:** Activates idle housing and quickly increases the supply of social housing.

### Execution Statistics

**Statistics as of End of 2024**

- **Direct Construction:** Cumulative 96,000 units (Target achieved).
- **Lease Management:** Cumulative 68,000 units.
- **Total:** 164,000 units, with an achievement rate of approximately 82%.
- **Estimate:** The goal of 200,000 units will be smoothly achieved by the end of 2024.

**Regional Distribution**

- The Six Municipalities account for approximately 75%, mainly New Taipei, Taoyuan, and Taichung.
- **New Taipei City:** 32,000 units (Highest).
- **Taipei City:** 28,000 units.
- **Taoyuan City:** 21,000 units.
- **Taichung City:** 19,000 units.

## Design Philosophy of Social Housing

### Principle of Social Mixing

**Eligibility Design**

- **Vulnerable Families:** 30% guaranteed proportion.
- **General Families:** 70% (Youth, newlyweds, and families with children are prioritized).
- Avoids the concentration of poverty and promotes social integration.
- Regular lottery systems ensure fairness.

**Scope of Vulnerable Groups**
According to the _Housing Act_, definitions include:

- Low-income and lower-middle-income households.
- Families in special circumstances.
- Families with three or more minor children.
- Those who cannot return home after ending placement in residential care institutions or foster families.
- Elderly persons aged 65 and above.
- Victims of domestic violence or sexual assault and their children.
- Persons with disabilities.
- Persons infected with HIV or suffering from AIDS.
- Indigenous peoples.
- Disaster victims.
- Homeless persons.
- Others recognized by the competent authority.

### Community Facility Planning

**Essential Facilities**

- **Barrier-Free Environment:** Ramps, elevators, room designs.
- **Kindergartens:** Priority setup of non-profit kindergartens.
- **Long-Term Care Points:** Day care centers, care points.
- **Community Activity Spaces:** Meeting halls, reading rooms.
- **Commercial Facilities:** Convenience stores, laundromats, and other convenient services.

**Innovative Designs**

- **Youth Innovation Spaces:** Providing studios for youth entrepreneurship.
- **Shared Kitchens:** Promoting interaction among residents.
- **Rooftop Gardens:** Urban agriculture and environmental education.
- **Recycling Centers:** Promoting the concept of a circular economy.
- **Smart Communities:** Introducing IoT devices to improve management efficiency.

### Rent Burden Principles

**85% of Market Price Principle**

- Rent is set at 85% of the surrounding market rate.
- Avoids excessive subsidies that distort the market.
- Ensures affordability for general families.

**Tiered Subsidy System**

- **Tier 1 (Extremely Low Income):** Rent discounted to 30%.
- **Tier 2 (Low Income):** Rent discounted to 50%.
- **Tier 3 (Lower-Middle Income):** Rent discounted to 70%.
- **Tier 4 (General Households):** 85% of market price.

## Important Social Housing Cases

### Taipei City Jiankang Public Housing

**Basic Information**

- **Location:** Zhongshan District, Taipei City.
- **Units:** 1,400 units.
- **Completion:** 2017.
- **Features:** Taipei City's first large-scale social housing project.

**Innovative Design**

- Vertical green walls to improve the urban heat island effect.
- Setup of non-profit kindergartens and elderly day care centers.
- Youth innovation space "Jiankang Healthy Creative Base."
- Smart parking system and community App management.

**Social Impact**

- Changed the public stereotype of social housing as "slums."
- Stimulated the development of surrounding commercial districts.
- Served as a benchmark for social housing planning in other counties and cities.

### New Taipei City Zhonghe Youth Social Housing

**Basic Information**

- **Location:** Zhonghe District, New Taipei City.
- **Units:** 522 units.
- **Completion:** 2019.
- **Features:** Combines urban renewal with social housing.

**Planning Highlights**

- One building containing social housing, commercial areas, and office spaces.
- Setup of community-based long-term care institutions.
- Rooftop gardens and rainwater recycling systems.
- Received Diamond-level Green Building certification.

**Community Building Results**

- Established a "Youth Participation Group."
- Regularly held community festival activities.
- Established a community shared resource platform.
- Resident satisfaction surveys reached over 85%.

### Taoyuan City Bade Social Housing

**Basic Information**

- **Location:** Bade District, Taoyuan City.
- **Units:** 1,003 units.
- **Completion:** 2020.
- **Features:** The largest single social housing project in Taiwan.

**Design Features**

- Integrated with light rail transit construction.
- 300 motorcycle parking spaces and 50 car parking spaces.
- Commercial facilities include well-known brands like Fullmart and Starbucks.
- Complete community facilities: Kindergarten, long-term care center, activity center.

**Management Innovation**

- Introduced AI facial recognition access control systems.
- Community App integrates various services.
- Established a community volunteer system.
- Promoted the concept of a "cashless community."

## Lease Management Policy

### Operation Model

**Lease Model (Baozu)**
The government rents private housing through professional vendors and sublets it to vulnerable families:

- **Lease Term:** 3 years, extendable up to 6 years.
- **Government Rental Price:** 80-90% of market rate.
- **Sublease Price:** 60-70% of market rate.
- The government bears the risk of vacancy.

**Management Model (Daiguan)**
The government matches landlords with tenants and provides professional management services:

- Landlords provide housing, tenants pay rent.
- Professional vendors are responsible for screening tenants, collecting rent, and coordinating repairs.
- The government provides repair subsidies and tax reductions.
- Reduces the risk of renting for landlords.

### Execution Mechanism

**Participation of Professional Vendors**

- **Housing Service Enterprises:** Responsible for housing matching and management.
- **Real Estate Brokerage Firms:** Assist in rental market surveys and bargaining.
- **Property Management Firms:** Provide housing maintenance and customer service.
- Currently, approximately 200 vendors participate.

**Government Support Measures**

- **Tax Incentives:** Reductions in house tax and land tax.
- **Repair Subsidies:** Up to NT$10,000-30,000 per unit.
- **Home Safety Insurance:** Government pays for insurance.
- **Dispute Mediation:** Provides legal consultation assistance.

### Results and Review

**Execution Results**

- As of the end of 2024, lease management has matched approximately 68,000 units.
- Approximately 55,000 landlords participated.
- Approximately 27,000 vulnerable families benefited.
- Stimulated NT$120 billion in private housing investment.

**Challenges Faced**

- **Limited Landlord Acceptance:** Concerns about tenant quality and property damage.
- **Concentration of Housing Sources:** Mostly concentrated in metropolitan areas, with insufficient sources in rural areas.
- **High Rent Levels:** Still a burden for extremely vulnerable families.
- **Variable Management Quality:** Significant differences in service quality among different vendors.

## Policy Effects and Social Impact

### Quantitative Effect Analysis

**Supply Side Impact**

- Increased rental market supply by approximately 200,000 units.
- Slowed the rise in market rents.
- Improved transparency in the rental market.
- Improved standards for rental housing quality.

**Demand Side Impact**

- Solved housing problems for approximately 400,000-500,000 people.
- Increased the proportion of youth settling down.
- Increased housing stability for vulnerable groups.
- Reduced the pressure on families to buy homes.

**Industrial Side Impact**

- Spawned the housing service industry.
- Stimulated development in the construction and renovation industries.
- Created approximately 30,000-40,000 jobs.
- Promoted the volume of urban renewal projects.

### Social Impact Assessment

**Realization of Housing Justice**
The social housing policy allows more people to enjoy decent housing:

- **Spatial Quality:** New social housing quality is superior to general rentals.
- **Community Environment:** Complete planning of community facilities and functions.
- **Housing Stability:** Provides longer-term housing security.
- **Reasonable Burden:** Rent levels are relatively more affordable compared to the market.

**Promotion of Social Integration**
Mixing design breaks class isolation:

- Vulnerable and general families live together.
- Promotes mutual understanding among different groups.
- Avoids the concentration of poverty in specific areas.
- Improves the overall quality of life in the community.

**Driving Urban Development**
Social housing acts as a catalyst for urban regeneration:

- Improves the environmental quality of old communities.
- Stimulates surrounding commercial activities.
- Improves land use efficiency.
- Promotes the development of public transportation.

## International Experience Comparison

### Singapore's HDB System

**Policy Features**

- Government-led, large-scale construction of HDB flats.
- 85% of the population lives in HDB flats.
- Ethnic quota system to promote ethnic integration.
- Integrated with the pension system to encourage home ownership.

**Lessons for Taiwan**

- Long-term stable policy execution.
- Complete community planning concepts.
- Mechanisms for multi-ethnic integration.

### Netherlands' Social Housing

**Policy Features**

- Social housing accounts for 30% of national housing.
- Built and managed by housing associations (non-profit organizations).
- Income tiering system ensures fair distribution.
- Emphasis on architectural design and environmental quality.

**Lessons for Taiwan**

- Mechanisms for non-profit organization participation.
- Refined architectural design concepts.
- Considerations for sustainable development environments.

### Hong Kong's Public Housing

**Policy Features**

- 45% of the population lives in public housing.
- Divided into two types: Rental Public Housing and Home Ownership Scheme (HOS).
- Strict eligibility review for entry.
- Centralized large-scale development.

**Differences in Taiwan's Experience**

- Taiwan places more emphasis on social mixing.
- Avoids large-scale centralized development.
- Focuses on the completeness of community facilities.

## Future Challenges and Development

### Main Challenges

**Difficulty in Land Acquisition**

- Urban land is scarce and expensive.
- Low willingness of landowners to cooperate.
- Complex procedures for changing urban plans.
- Public opposition (NIMBY effect).

**Heavy Fiscal Burden**

- Rising construction costs.
- Long-term operational and management expenses.
- Financial pressure on housing funds.
- Crowding out budgets for other public constructions.

**Social Acceptance**

- Some people still hold stereotypes.
- Concerns about affecting surrounding property prices.
- Community integration takes time.
- Requires continuous communication and explanation.

**Complex Management and Operation**

- Difficulty in managing residents with different needs.
- High costs for maintaining community facilities.
- High turnover rate of residents.
- Requires a professional management team.

### Future Development Directions

**Continuous Policy Deepening**

- Establish a long-term and stable construction mechanism.
- Amend the Housing Act to strengthen legal basis.
- Establish social housing quality standards.
- Promote the formulation of a Social Housing Special Act.

**Innovative Financing Models**

- Introduce private capital participation.
- Develop Social Housing REITs.
- Promote the land trust system.
- Establish a circular utilization mechanism.

**Smart Management**

- Introduce IoT smart management systems.
- Establish big data analysis capabilities.
- Promote community App integrated services.
- Develop AI customer service systems.

**Sustainable Development Orientation**

- Introduce green building certification systems.
- Promote the concept of a circular economy.
- Develop community energy systems.
- Establish carbon-neutral demonstration communities.

**Regional Balanced Development**

- Promote social housing in non-urban areas.
- Combine with local industrial development needs.
- Coordinate with public transportation development.
- Promote urban-rural balanced development.

## Conclusion: Moving Towards Housing Justice

Social housing is not just a housing policy; it is the practice of social values. Taiwan began promoting the 8-Year, 200,000 Units Plan in 2016. Not only is it close to achieving targets in quantity, but it has also established a "Taiwan Model" in quality:

**Features of the Taiwan Experience**

- **Social Mixing:** Avoids poverty concentration and promotes social integration.
- **Dual-Track Progress:** Direct construction and lease management complement each other.
- **Quality-Oriented:** Emphasizes design aesthetics and community functions.
- **Localized Design:** Combines Taiwan's climate and cultural characteristics.

**Future Vision**
To ensure that everyone living in Taiwan, regardless of economic ability, can enjoy decent, stable, and dignified housing. This is not only the solution to housing issues but also an important step in building a fairer and more inclusive society.

The realization of housing justice requires the joint efforts of the entire society. The government provides the policy framework and resources, professionals contribute technology and creativity, and the public gives understanding and support. Only then can social housing truly become an important force for Taiwan's society to move forward.
