Late on the night of October 25, 1971, in the United Nations General Assembly Hall in New York. After Foreign Minister Zhou Shukai of the Republic of China finished reading the statement, he turned and stepped down from the green marble podium; the entire delegation filed out. From entering to leaving, it took less than 20 minutes[^1]. Half an hour later, Resolution 2758 was passed by a vote of 76 to 35. The "representatives of Chiang Kai-shek" were expelled, and the People's Republic of China officially took its seat for China. Taiwan, which had been a founding member of the United Nations (on August 24, 1945, Chiang Kai-shek signed the instrument of ratification for the UN Charter in Chongqing[^2]), suddenly became an outsider to international organizations.

> **Understand in 30 Seconds**: On the surface, this was the tragic exit of the Republic of China based on the principle of "Han and bandits cannot coexist"; in reality, Chiang Kai-shek had already accepted a compromise of "two Chinas" as early as early 1971, and the US State Department even praised Taiwan for showing "extraordinary flexibility"[^3]. The real key was Henry Kissinger's secret visit to Beijing in July of that year. While officials in Taipei and Washington were still in the dark, he had already revealed his hand to Zhou Enlai: the United States would not sincerely fight to keep Taiwan in the United Nations. Half a century later, the US, UK, EU, Australia, and the Netherlands have successively stated that the words "Taiwan" do not appear in Resolution 2758.

## From Founding Member to the Battle for the Seat

The Republic of China was a founding member of the United Nations and one of the five permanent members of the Security Council. On June 26, 1945, in San Francisco, T.V. Soong signed the UN Charter on behalf of the Republic of China first; on August 24 of the same year, Chiang Kai-shek signed the instrument of ratification in Chongqing[^2].

The problem arose after 1949: The People's Republic of China was established in Beijing, and the Republic of China government retreated to Taiwan, but both sides claimed to be the only legitimate representatives of "China." Starting in the 1950s, the UN General Assembly argued every year about "Chinese representation." In 1961, the United States proposed the "Important Question Resolution," defining "changing Chinese representation" as an "important question" requiring a two-thirds majority to pass, effectively blocking Beijing[^3].

Former Foreign Minister Ye Gongchao had a famous metaphor: "The 'One China' policy is like painting the floor of a room—once you finish painting, you step out of the room."[^4] This prophecy came true in 1970. At the 25th UN General Assembly, the "Expel Us, Admit the Bandits" proposal proposed by Albania won a simple majority for the first time (51 to 49). The Republic of China retained its seat, relying only on the two-thirds threshold of the "Important Question Resolution" to block it[^3].

The red light was on.

## Chiang Kai-shek's Hesitation: Two Chinas in the Diaries

In the past, scholars often blamed Chiang Kai-shek's stubbornness of "Han and bandits cannot coexist" for the exit from the UN. However, after the National Palace Museum opened the _Diaries of Chiang Chung-cheng_ and diplomatic archives in 2010, the historical image was rewritten.

Scholars studying these archives, such as Wang Hao, point out that on December 16, 1970, Chiang Kai-shek explicitly instructed the Ministry of Foreign Affairs at the National Security Council to study various UN regulations, "to do our utmost to maintain our legal status in the UN, but also to make the worst-case plan of exiting the UN"[^3]. In a diplomatic meeting on December 31, he more clearly told the group of ambassadors that he did not oppose studying the "dual representation" plan, but insisted on retaining the permanent seat on the Security Council[^3].

In other words, "Han and bandits cannot coexist" was a strategy to maintain legal legitimacy internally and negotiate with the US; privately, Chiang Kai-shek had already psychologically accepted "two Chinas." However, his red line was the Security Council seat—this represented the symbolic legal legitimacy of the Republic of China as a "great power."

On May 26, 1971, Vice President Yen Chia-kan presided over the National Security Council on behalf of Chiang. Secretary-General of the National Security Council Huang Shaogu and Foreign Minister Zhou Shukai proposed a surprising shift in plan: If a diplomatic ally wanted to establish relations with Beijing, as long as three conditions were met (not recognizing the CCP as the only legitimate government of China, not involving the territory of Taiwan of the Republic of China, and continuing to recognize our government as the Government of the Republic of China rather than a "Taiwan government"), diplomatic relations could be maintained[^3]. This was essentially accepting the arrangement of "two Chinas," and Chiang Ching-kuo expressed on the spot that he needed "to think about it further."

Chiang's diaries also reveal his inner torment. He wrote, "The ancient teaching of being the pillar in midstream and fighting alone is a glorious isolation," but a few days later changed it to "Weak country diplomacy cannot lack internal firmness and external flexibility; there should be firm decisions in the heart, but the external form has not yet reached the time for implementation, and should adapt to the environment and change according to circumstances"[^3]. On May 27, he was diagnosed with cardiac enlargement; by mid-June he was officially bedridden, recovering only by the end of July—this window of time coincided exactly with Henry Kissinger's secret visit to Beijing.

## Dual Representation: The Road Not Taken

In early 1971, the Nixon administration formally proposed the "Dual Representation Plan"—allowing the People's Republic of China to take the permanent seat on the Security Council while retaining the Republic of China's membership in the General Assembly[^5]. On April 23, Nixon sent special envoy Murphy (Robert Murphy) to meet Chiang Kai-shek. To get Chiang's nod, Murphy unilaterally promised "that the Republic of China would retain its Security Council seat"—this clearly exceeded the White House's instructions and authorization[^3].

Chiang Kai-shek finally softened. But he warned Murphy: If the Security Council seat was stripped, he "had no choice but to prefer breaking as jade rather than being preserved as tile."

The problem was that Murphy had no authority to make this promise. On May 27, Nixon told Kissinger and Secretary of State William Rogers the truth: "I really wish we had failed by sticking to principles, so they could get out." Kissinger more sinisterly suggested: Another way to "be defeated" is to delay and not decide, finally pretending to take a "two Chinas" stance and failing—"that also means we did everything we could."[^3]

This delaying strategy ultimately succeeded, "fooling Rogers and the US Ambassador to the UN Bush (the future President George H.W. Bush), as well as Taiwan's diplomats, and even many historians in later generations"[^3]. George H.W. Bush was a sincere supporter of the Dual Representation Plan that year, desperately lobbying for votes in the General Assembly hall until the last moment—he did not know he was being used as a gun[^6].

## Kissinger's July: The Backstab

On the afternoon of July 1, 1971, on the eve of his secret visit to Beijing, Henry Kissinger spoke with Shen Jianhong, the Republic of China's Ambassador to the United States. Kissinger solemnly said: "President Nixon's friendship with our President and our government is extremely deep... His determination to do his best to maintain our seat in the General Assembly is beyond doubt. President Nixon also absolutely will not consider any measures that would cause us to lose our Security Council seat; please rest assured."[^3]

Ten days later, what Kissinger said to Zhou Enlai in Beijing was completely opposite.

From July 9 to 11, Kissinger feigned a stomachache in Pakistan and secretly flew to Beijing to meet Zhou Enlai. According to later declassified dialogue records[^7], Kissinger proactively revealed the bottom line of the US's "complex dual representation" plan to Zhou Enlai: Beijing could join the UN with a simple majority and take the Security Council seat, but expelling the Republic of China required a two-thirds majority. He promised Zhou Enlai: "As long as China joins the United Nations, then obtaining a two-thirds vote to expel Taiwan is only a matter of time; the key lies in both China and the US knowing this arrangement in their hearts, reaching a tacit understanding, and waiting patiently."[^3]

Zhou Enlai was not grateful: "We have already waited 21 years." And warned: "Taiwan will also oppose it, and the voices of opposition will come from all directions." Kissinger raised his hands in surrender, admitting this "is a very good way to end this issue"[^3].

On July 15, Nixon publicly announced Kissinger's visit to China. Medium and small countries that were still观望 (观望) began to change their stances. From October 20 to 26, while the General Assembly was debating Chinese representation, where was Kissinger? He was in Beijing—this time for a public visit, paving the way for Nixon's visit to China the following year[^8]. Taipei diplomats described: "We fought bloodily on the front lines, while our allies drank tea in the enemy's capital."

Former Vice Minister of Foreign Affairs Qian Fu stated directly in a later interview: "The US preferred to sacrifice the Republic of China in order to woo the CCP."[^9]

## Those 17 Minutes: Zhou Shukai Steps Down from the Podium

The agenda on October 25 was very dramatic.

In the afternoon, Jamil Baroody, the representative of Saudi Arabia, proposed a compromise: allowing Beijing to take the Security Council seat, and Taiwan to remain in the General Assembly under the name "Republic of Taiwan," waiting for the two sides to resolve differences themselves[^7]. If this proposal had passed, Taiwan's fate might have been completely rewritten. But the Republic of China delegation insisted on "One China" and did not actively strive for it; the proposal was shelved.

At 9:47 PM, the vote on the "Important Question Resolution" took place—55 to 59, it was rejected. At this moment, the battle was decided.

Zhou Shukai immediately raised his hand to request the floor. He walked up to the podium and read a pre-prepared statement: "The Republic of China decides to exit the United Nations, which it participated in creating." He then led the delegation members, including Liu Kai (then Permanent Representative to the UN), out of the hall[^1][^10].

At 10:25 PM, Resolution 2758 was put to a vote: 76 in favor, 35 against, 17 abstentions, 3 absent. The resolution text was only four paragraphs long, with the key sentence being: "Decides to restore all the rights of the People's Republic of China... and immediately expel the representatives of Chiang Kai-shek from the seats they illegally occupy in the organization of the United Nations and in all its specialized agencies."[^11]

It is worth noting: The resolution text does not contain the word "Taiwan," nor does it define the territorial scope of "China"[^11]. This became the seed of international legal controversy for the next 50 years.

## Chiang Kai-shek's "Message to Compatriots Nationwide" and Domestic Impact

Early on the morning of October 26, Chiang Kai-shek issued the "Message to Compatriots Nationwide on the Republic of China Exiting the United Nations": "Based on our position of 'Han and bandits cannot coexist' and upholding the dignity of the Charter... we must reluctantly announce our exit from the United Nations organization, which we participated in creating with great hardship."[^12] The eight characters at the end, "Revere Yourself and Strengthen Yourself, Be Unfazed by Changes," were printed in textbooks and carved on school gates for the next 30 years.

But the impact within the island was far more cruel than the official language. Alumni of the National Taiwan University Law School, Hong Sanxiong and Chen Lingyu, recalled: "When the news reached the mainland, the dream of 'Counter-attack the Mainland, Liberate Compatriots' was shattered instantly, let alone 'Han and bandits cannot coexist' becoming empty talk."[^13] This defeat directly ignited the Taiwan University Student Movement and the "Save the Diaoyu Islands Movement" between 1971 and 1973. Students began to question the party-state mythology, and many future leaders of the non-KMT and democratic movements were politically enlightened in this storm.

In his 2021 book _1971 Mythical United Nations_, Yao Chia-wen stated directly that Chiang Kai-shek "preferred to be expelled rather than represent Taiwan"—if the Baloudi proposal had been accepted that year, and Taiwan had remained under the name "Taiwan," Taiwan's subsequent international situation would have been completely different[^14]. But Wang Hao's research points out: The problem actually lay in Nixon and Kissinger having already decided to sacrifice Taiwan; Chiang Kai-shek "could not save it no matter how flexible he was"—this is a story of two national leaders joining hands to eliminate an ally[^3].

## Echoes 50 Years Later: What Exactly Does Resolution 2758 Say?

The consequences of exiting the UN were domino-like. From 1971 to 1979, the number of countries breaking diplomatic relations with the Republic of China dropped sharply from 68 to only 22; Japan broke relations in 1972, and the US in 1979[^15]. Taiwan was excluded from the WHO, ICAO, and Interpol, and even passports were often misidentified.

However, since the 2020s, the international community has begun to re-examine Resolution 2758.

Starting in 2024, the US, UK, European Parliament, Australia, the Netherlands, and other countries have successively passed resolutions or statements, explicitly pointing out: Resolution 2758 only addressed "Chinese representation," did not address "Taiwan's political status," and did not authorize China to represent Taiwan[^16]. On April 23, 2025, the US publicly criticized China for "distorting" Resolution 2758 for the first time at the UN Security Council; on May 5, 2025, the US House of Representatives passed the _Taiwan International Solidarity Act_ without dissent, formally confirming this stance in the form of domestic law[^17].

Returning to those 17 minutes in 1971—Zhou Shukai's steps down from the podium locked Taiwan's international situation for half a century. But the resolution text itself did not completely close the door. The gray area left in the crack of the door is precisely the space that Taiwan's diplomats are working hard to expand today.
