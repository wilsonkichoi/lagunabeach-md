# Ming Hua Yuan: The Opera Empire That Grew from the Temple Stage

## A Four-Generation Theater Family

The story of Ming Hua Yuan begins in 1929, when Chen Ming-chi founded the Taiwanese opera troupe in Chaozhou, Pingtung. At the time, it was simply a traveling outdoor stage troupe that followed temple fairs. No one could have imagined that this rural opera company would one day perform at the National Theater and Taipei Arena.

From Chen Ming-chi to his son Chen Sheng-fu, and then to the grandchildren's generation including Chen Sheng-kuo and Chen Sheng-tsai, Ming Hua Yuan has spanned four generations. Chen Sheng-fu, the second-generation leader, was the pivotal figure who transformed Ming Hua Yuan from a traditional troupe into a modern theater company. When he took over in the 1970s, Taiwanese opera was under severe pressure from television and cinema, with audiences hemorrhaging away. Many troupes chose to disband or pivot to television opera, but Chen Sheng-fu went against the tide—he insisted on live performance while upgrading stagecraft to cinematic standards.

## A Modern Revolution in Traditional Opera

Ming Hua Yuan's greatest achievement is this: it made Taiwanese opera _spectacular_.

Traditional Taiwanese opera stages were rudimentary, and the pacing was slow—too slow for younger audiences. Chen Sheng-fu introduced modern theater technology: lighting, sound rigging, stage machinery, aerial flying rigs, and water-screen projections, bringing Taiwanese opera's visual impact up to Broadway musical standards. His signature _Ji Gong: The Living Buddha_ series featured performers genuinely soaring across the stage, leaving audiences stunned.

But Ming Hua Yuan's innovation went beyond technology. They also took bold creative risks with their scripts. _The Super Dazzling Legend of the White Snake_ reimagined the classic tale with modern elements and humor. _The Immortal of Penglai_ blended fantasy and wuxia. These works drew young people who had never seen Taiwanese opera into the theater—and when they came out, they exclaimed: "I had no idea Taiwanese opera could be this cool!"

## From Temple Stage to Taipei Arena

Ming Hua Yuan's production scale kept escalating. In 1982, they performed at the Sun Yat-sen Memorial Hall for the first time. In 1990, they entered the National Theater. In 2009, they staged a massive production at Taipei Arena. A troupe that once performed under makeshift canopies at temple entrances had entered a 15,000-seat mega-venue—an unprecedented feat in the world of traditional theater.

They also took their act abroad. Ming Hua Yuan has performed at the Festival d'Avignon in France, as well as in Japan, Singapore, Malaysia, and beyond. On the international stage, the vocal technique, physical expressiveness, and dramatic intensity of Taiwanese opera showed global audiences the power of Taiwan's traditional art.

## The Future of Taiwanese Opera

Ming Hua Yuan is now run jointly by the third and fourth generations of the Chen Sheng-fu family, divided into multiple sub-troupes—Tian, Di, Xuan, Huang, and others—each developing its own distinctive style. This "family business" model is unique in Taiwan's performing arts landscape.

Taiwan's traditional theater faces severe challenges: aging audiences, a shortage of new performers, and unstable government funding. But Ming Hua Yuan's four generations of practice have proven one thing: tradition is not a museum specimen. It can be alive, breathing, and compelling enough to sell tickets to young people. The key is not to abandon tradition—but to let tradition speak in a contemporary voice.
