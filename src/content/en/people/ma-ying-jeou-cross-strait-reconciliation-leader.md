# Ma Ying-jeou: Cross-Strait Peacemaker, or the President Who Gave Young People the 22K Wage

In October 2013, a polling number made Taiwan's media gasp collectively: sitting President Ma Ying-jeou's approval rating had dropped to single digits (9.2% in a TVBS poll) — one of the lowest approval ratings for any president in Taiwan's democratic history. Almost simultaneously, a meme circulated online: "Ma Ying-jeou: Taiwan's GDP has grown. Young People: Then why am I still making 22K?"

That gap, more than any biographical passage, captures Ma Ying-jeou's place in Taiwan's political history — he was simultaneously a historic architect of cross-strait relations and the most embarrassing symbol of an entire generation's wage stagnation.

## Family Background and Education

Ma Ying-jeou was born July 13, 1950, in Kowloon, Hong Kong. His father Ma Ho-ling was a KMT military officer; his mother Qin Hou-xiu was a normal school graduate. After the government relocated to Taiwan, both parents settled in Taipei. Ma Ho-ling applied strict discipline to his children and left behind letters full of nationalist sentiment, shaping Ma Ying-jeou's political identity in profound ways.

During his schooling years, Ma Ying-jeou attended Jianguo High School, then entered National Taiwan University's Law Department and earned a lawyer's license. In 1976 he went to the United States, earning a Master of Laws from New York University before entering Harvard Law School, where he earned his Doctor of Juridical Science (SJD) in 1981. His dissertation concerned legal issues related to the East China Sea seabed boundary. While studying abroad, he actively participated in the Protect the Diaoyu Islands movement — a history that was both the starting point of his early political activity and the source of his later hardline stance on the Diaoyutai Islands.

## Early Government Career: From English Secretary to Minister of Justice

After returning to Taiwan in 1981, Ma Ying-jeou joined the Presidential Office as English secretary to President Chiang Ching-kuo. In 1988 he transferred to the Research, Development and Evaluation Commission as Chairman, then was promoted to KMT Deputy Secretary-General, overseeing mainland affairs — a portfolio that gave him deep knowledge of cross-strait issues and set the direction of his future career.

In 1993 Ma Ying-jeou was appointed Minister of Justice. During his tenure he vigorously promoted anti-organized-crime campaigns, positioning himself as a reformer who "declared war on crime" by fighting organized crime and black-gold politics. The media constructed him as a "clean and courageous" reformer, and the nickname "non-stick pan" (Teflon) was established during this period.

> 📝 **But "non-stick pan" was not without blemishes.** In 2006, Ma Ying-jeou — then Taipei Mayor — was indicted by prosecutors on suspicion of misappropriating Taipei City mayoral special funds, triggering a political earthquake. The case was ultimately ruled not guilty in August 2007, and Ma used "the law is my greatest weapon" to clear his name, but the case left the first crack in his "never-stick" image, and the debate over "legally innocent does not equal morally unblemished" in Taiwan persisted for years.

## Taipei Mayor: Outstanding Record, But Planting the 22K Time Bomb

In 1998, Ma Ying-jeou defeated incumbent mayor Chen Shui-bian by a narrow margin and won the Taipei mayoral election, then won re-election in 2002 with 64.1% of the vote. During his tenure he promoted the expansion of the Muzha and Xinzhuang MRT lines, comprehensively remediated the Keelung River by straightening its course, and forcefully implemented the pay-per-bag garbage disposal system, visibly reducing waste. His shaping of Taipei is a record many residents still remember.

When he left the mayoral office in 2006, however, a criminal trial was waiting for him — the special funds case became a weapon of fierce DPP attack during the campaign season. Although he was ultimately acquitted, this judicial episode illustrated the increasingly scorching political environment and foreshadowed a presidency full of rough weather ahead.

## 2008 Victory: The Beginning of the Peak, and the Beginning of the Test

In 2008, amid the shadow of the global financial crisis, Ma Ying-jeou and running mate Vincent Siew won the election with a commanding 58.45% of the vote — more than two million votes ahead of opponent Frank Hsieh. This was the highest vote share in any presidential election since Taiwan's democratization. Voters' expectations were clear: set aside political disputes, focus on the economy, improve cross-strait relations.

What Ma Ying-jeou took on was not merely a promise — it was a set of commitments that was nearly impossible to fully deliver.

### 22K: How One Policy Hurt an Entire Generation

Under the impact of the financial crisis, in 2009 the Executive Yuan introduced the "College Graduates into Corporate Internship Program," subsidizing companies NT$22,000 per month to hire recent graduates. The original intent was to ease unemployment pressures. The actual result was a disastrously counterproductive effect: NT$22,000 rapidly went from being a "floor" to becoming a "ceiling." Many companies used it as justification to suppress starting salaries, and 22K became a collective shadow over Taiwan's college graduates.

"I spent four years in university and make about the same as a high school graduate," said college graduates of that era. The policy was discontinued in 2013, but its aftereffects were buried deep in Taiwan's wage structure. Throughout the entire 2010s, Taiwan's real wages barely moved, falling further and further behind neighboring South Korea and Singapore.

Critics pointed out that during Ma Ying-jeou's eight years, Taiwan's per-capita GDP grew at an average annual rate of approximately 2%, while South Korea's average annual growth exceeded 3% during the same period; Taiwan's real wages in 2015 were still below their 2000 levels — a number that needs no interpretation. It is a blunt record of generational inequality.

College graduates at the time wrote online: "Our youth was used to subsidize that 22K." The line spread widely on social media, becoming an emblem of generational discontent.

## Cross-Strait Relations: Historic Breakthroughs Alongside Enduring Controversy

Ma Ying-jeou's greatest historical legacy is unquestionably in cross-strait relations. He pursued a "economics before politics, easy before difficult" gradualist strategy, restoring in 2008 the institutional consultations between the Straits Exchange Foundation (SEF) and the Association for Relations Across the Taiwan Straits (ARATS) after a gap of nearly ten years. Direct cross-strait flights, mainland Chinese tourism, and academic exchanges were opened one by one, materially changing the landscape of civilian cross-strait interaction.

The Cross-Strait Economic Cooperation Framework Agreement (ECFA) signed in 2010 was his central achievement. The agreement reduced tariff barriers on hundreds of categories of goods and did produce some export growth for certain industries in the early period of implementation. But the questions raised by critics never disappeared: the primary beneficiaries were large enterprises in traditional manufacturing; small and medium enterprises and the service sector felt little benefit; Taiwan's trade dependence ratio with China continued rising — a warning signal from the perspective of policy diversification.

On November 7, 2015, Ma Ying-jeou flew to the Shangri-La Hotel in Singapore to shake hands with Chinese President Xi Jinping. The "Ma-Xi Summit" was the first formal meeting between cross-strait leaders since 1949. Ma Ying-jeou said afterward: "This is a historic moment." Xi Jinping called it "an important milestone in cross-strait relations." But critics pointed out that the meeting had Ma Ying-jeou — as Taiwan's leader — accept the "1992 Consensus" framework, amounting to a concession on Taiwan's subjecthood rather than a dialogue between equals.

## The Sunflower Movement: More Than "A Peaceful Conclusion"

On March 17, 2014, amid chaos and controversy, the Legislative Yuan's committee rushed through the review of the Cross-Strait Service Trade Agreement, triggering public outrage. On the night of March 18, students stormed the Legislative Yuan chamber and occupied it. The [Sunflower Movement](/society/太陽花學運) was underway.

Ma Ying-jeou's government initially took a hard line, declaring it would not negotiate with the occupiers and simply demanded students leave the chamber. On March 23, some protesters attempted to storm the Executive Yuan — and the government's choice at that moment became the most controversial node of the entire episode.

The following night (March 24), police conducted a large-scale forced dispersal of protesters surrounding the Executive Yuan, deploying water cannons and riot police. The scene was chaotic; dozens were injured and sent to hospitals, including multiple media reporters and ambulance volunteers. The violence of the dispersal sent a powerful shockwave through Taiwanese society. "The government used this kind of force against its own young citizens," social activists said. "We will never forget."

On March 30, more than 500,000 people marched on Ketagalan Boulevard — one of the largest demonstrations in Taiwan's history. On April 10, students left the Legislative Yuan. The service trade agreement has never completed its legislative process — Ma Ying-jeou's policy goal remains unfinished business.

> 📝 **The Sunflower Movement was not merely a protest against the service trade agreement — it was a comprehensive indictment of Ma Ying-jeou's governing style**: non-transparent decision-making, dismissal of public opinion, excessive tilt toward Beijing. The movement politically benefited [Tsai Ing-wen](/people/蔡英文) and the DPP, and completed the party transition in 2016.

## The Criticism: Where Did "Ma Incompetence" Come From

Ma Ying-jeou's supporters described him as clean, principled, and internationally minded. His critics' assessments converged on a different word: "incompetent."

The "Ma incompetence" label was not an attack on his personal character — it pointed to a specific decision-making pattern: hesitating in the face of crises, over-reliance on technocrats, insufficient political judgment. During the food safety scandals (the 2014 Tatung adulterated cooking oil case), the government's response was slow, criticized as "waiting for the problem to go away." On the nuclear power plant controversy, his proposed solution of suspending the Fourth Nuclear Power Plant satisfied neither supporters nor opponents.

The September 2013 "September Political Storm" — when he used his presidential authority to push for the revocation of Legislative Yuan President Wang Jin-pyng's KMT party membership, only to fail comprehensively in both administrative and judicial procedures — was widely seen by political analysts as the most emblematic misstep of his final term. "He fought a political battle in the most unpolitical way possible," political commentators observed.

When Ma Ying-jeou left office, media commentary broadly converged on one paradox: "His integrity was real, but his political judgment was insufficient to sustain that integrity." This observation appeared in different forms across different outlets, but the core observation was the same.

## Post-Presidency: Cross-Strait Positions Sparking New Controversies

After leaving office, Ma Ying-jeou did not fade from public life. In March 2023, he visited mainland China as a former president — the first former president to travel to China since 1949. He visited Nanjing, Wuhan, Changsha, Chongqing, and in his speeches repeatedly invoked "both sides of the strait belong to the same Chinese nation" — provoking fierce criticism in Taiwan, especially against the backdrop of the ongoing Russia-Ukraine war and escalating Taiwan Strait tensions. Critics argued his words and actions blurred Taiwan's sense of subjecthood.

His supporters argued he was maintaining a communication channel for Taiwan, preventing a complete rupture in cross-strait relations.

## Historical Standing: A Contradictory Legacy

Ma Ying-jeou represents a distinctive paradox in Taiwan's political history: his personal integrity is widely acknowledged in Taiwan's political scene, yet his administration left the 22K wage stagnation stamp on an entire generation; he presided over the most peaceful period in cross-strait relations' history, yet also ignited the Sunflower Movement — the movement that defined a generation's sense of Taiwanese identity; he promoted ECFA while also deepening Taiwan's economic dependence on Beijing.

These are not accidental contradictions — they arise from different facets of the same system of political beliefs. Evaluating Ma Ying-jeou depends largely on how you answer this question: was the direction Taiwan moved in during 2008–2016 right or wrong?

And on that question, Taiwanese society has yet to reach consensus.
