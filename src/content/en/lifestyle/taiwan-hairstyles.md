# Introduction to Taiwan Hairstyles

In Taiwan, hairstyles are not just a matter of personal style; they are a mirror reflecting social changes and cultural shifts. From traditional barber shops to modern designer salons, the scenery atop people's heads has evolved from authoritarian discipline to diverse freedom.

## Historical Evolution: From Discipline to Openness

### 1. Traditional Barbering and the "Suit Haircut"

Before the Japanese colonial era, male barbering in Taiwan relied mostly on street-side "barber poles" (剃頭擔子). The Japanese introduced modern barbering concepts and equipment, making the "Suit Haircut" (oil slick/side part) a symbol of the gentry and fashion of that era.

### 2. "Hair Regulations" During the Martial Law Era

During the Martial Law era after the Nationalist government came to Taiwan, schools and the military enforced strict regulations on hairstyles. Boys were required to have "buzz cuts" or "crew cuts," and long hair or sideburns were strictly prohibited[^1]. This regulation of the body persisted until after the lifting of Martial Law.

### 3. Liberation and Diversification

After the lifting of Martial Law in 1987, although school hair regulations did not vanish immediately, the social atmosphere began to shift. In 2005, the Ministry of Education officially abolished school hair regulations, marking the full return of students' rights to choose their own hairstyles. Since then, influenced by Hong Kong dramas, Japanese dramas, and the Korean Wave, Taiwanese hairstyles have entered an era of diverse development.

## Types of Barbering Culture

In Taiwan, options for hair cutting are highly diverse, reflecting different demographic needs:

- **Traditional Barber Shops**: Commonly found in older neighborhoods, offering full services including haircuts, shaves, and ear cleaning. They are important social venues for middle-aged and elderly men[^3].
- **100 NTD Quick Cuts**: Based on the Japanese model, these shops feature high efficiency, transparent pricing (usually 100 to 150 NTD), and convenient locations (malls, stations), making them popular among those seeking practicality and speed[^2].
- **Hair Design Salons**: Emphasizing design and personal style, stylists create looks tailored to the client's face shape and needs, providing advanced services like coloring and perming.

## Modern Trends

Popular male hairstyles in modern Taiwan include:

- **Undercut**: Shaved sides and back with length on top, clean and easy to maintain.
- **Korean Side Part**: Influenced by Korean idols, emphasizing the flow and volume of the fringe.
- **Ah-Ji Haircut**: A specific hairstyle with strong internet meme characteristics (see [[Ah-Ji Haircut]] for details).

## References

[^1]: The Ministry of Education of the ROC officially announced the total abolition of school hair regulations on July 24, 2005, and issued an official memo to all schools on August 9 of the same year (No. 0940108865), emphasizing that "student hair style is a basic human right, and school regulations shall not include hair style management in student discipline."

[^2]: The "100 NTD Quick Cut" model in Taiwan was initially inspired by Japan's QB House, featuring "no wash, no perm/dye, high efficiency." It entered a period of explosive growth after 2010 with its presence in major shopping malls and MRT stations.

[^3]: "Barber poles" (Tì-thâu-tàn-tsí) were a common mobile barbering service in early Taiwan street scenes. The master would carry a pole with a heater for washing water on one end and tools with a small stool on the other. It is a shared memory of old-style barbering for many elders.
