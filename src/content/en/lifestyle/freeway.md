> **30-Second Overview**
> Taiwan's first "expressway" was the MacArthur Highway, opened in 1964 and only 23 kilometers long, which retired from its expressway role in 1977 following the completion of the Zhongshan Expressway[^1]. The Zhongshan Expressway (National Highway 1), construction of which began in 1971 and was completed in full in 1978, was the first of Chiang Ching-kuo's "Ten Major Construction Projects" and Taiwan's first large-scale post-war engineering mobilization. Its 374 kilometers shortened the distance from Keelung to Kaohsiung to a one-day trip[^2][^3]. The Formosa Expressway (National Highway 3), launched in the 1990s, formed the backbone of the "Dual National Highways"; the Xueshan Tunnel, opened in 2006, at 12.9 kilometers was the fifth-longest highway tunnel in the world at the time, taking 15 years to excavate[^4][^5]. In late 2013, ETC distance-based charging was fully implemented, bringing the toll collector system into history, yet leaving behind controversial chapters such as the Far Electronic Toll Collection case and the Legislative Yuan's correction report[^6][^7]. Today, Taiwan's national highway network exceeds 1,000 kilometers, a public infrastructure on this island that everyone uses every day, yet few understand its historical depth[^8].

## A Post-War History Written in Asphalt

If there is one infrastructure project that can condense post-war Taiwan, it is likely the National Highway.

It involves more than just engineering: behind it lies the withdrawal of US aid in the 1960s, the narrative of "self-reliance" under the oil crisis and diplomatic retreat in the 1970s, the infrastructure race before the party alternation in the 1990s, the logistics upgrade after joining the WTO in the 2000s, and the public-private power struggle surrounding an eTag sticker in the 2010s[^2][^6][^9].

Every section of road is a fingerprint of an era.

## The MacArthur Highway: The Forgotten First

Taiwanese people are accustomed to saying that the Zhongshan Expressway is the first highway, but strictly speaking, the "MacArthur Highway" (Taiwan Provincial Highway 5-1), opened in 1964 connecting Taipei and Keelung, was truly the first. It was 23.4 kilometers long, had four lanes in each direction, a design speed of 80 km/h, and was the pinnacle of Taiwan's road engineering at the time. It was built with US aid support and named after the Korean War general MacArthur[^1][^9].

After the Keelung-to-Taipei section of the Zhongshan Expressway opened in 1977, the MacArthur Highway was merged into the general provincial highway system and downgraded to an "urban road." Its fate presaged one thing: in Taiwan, "highways" are eaten by higher-level "national highways"—this game of hierarchy has been played for fifty years[^1][^3].

## The Zhongshan Expressway: Head of the Ten Major Construction Projects

On August 14, 1971, construction of the Zhongshan Expressway began.

This was the first and largest of Chiang Ching-kuo's "Ten Major Construction Projects." Designed from Keelung to Kaohsiung, with a total length of 373 kilometers and four lanes in each direction, the completion date was politically set for October 31, 1978—Chiang Kai-shek's birthday—and named the "Zhongshan Expressway"[^2][^3][^10].

The difficulty of the engineering is still staggering to look at today: Taiwan did not have highway design specifications at the time, so the engineering team translated Japanese and American standards while building. Many bridge and embankment designs were "from scratch"[^10]. The Zhongsha Bridge, Yangmei Slope, and Houlong mudstone section each have their own engineering stories. In the oral history special edition _The Way of the Great Road_ compiled by the National Highway Bureau, engineers from that era recalled: "We were not just building roads; we were learning how to build roads."[^10]

> **💡 Did You Know**
> The initial plan for the Zhongshan Expressway was a "level crossing" design, not a true closed system. It was only during construction that it was changed to the "true highway" standard of a fully closed, grade-separated system—meaning many sections were redesigned after construction had begun, an extremely rare "changing specifications while running" in engineering[^4][^10].

## From the First Generation to the Third Generation of National Highways

The National Highway Bureau itself divides the development of national highways into three generations[^4]:

1. **First Generation (1971–1990)** — Represented by the Zhongshan Expressway, the design concept was "north-south vertical, shortest connection," with routes kept as straight as possible and avoiding urban areas.
2. **Second Generation (1990–2004)** — Represented by the Formosa Expressway (National Highway 3), it added "east-west horizontal" National Highways 6, 8, and 10, forming a grid network. Design began to consider environmental impact and integration with urban planning.
3. **Third Generation (2004–present)** — Represented by National Highway 5 (Jiang Wei-shui Expressway), engineering focus shifted to "overcoming terrain." The Xueshan Tunnel, Beiyi Straight Line, and Wuyang Viaduct are representative works of this generation[^4][^5].

Behind each generation lies a different political logic: the first generation was a political achievement project of an authoritarian regime; the second generation was a product of coordination between local factions and the central government during the process of democratization; the third generation is an engineering project under the "EIA era" where every meter of road must be explained to the public.

## The Fukuo Expressway: The Mocked "Political Road"

National Highway 3 (Formosa Expressway), planned in the 1990s, was mocked by public opinion as a "political road"—the reason being that the route wound along the foot of the mountains, bypassed many densely populated areas, and the density of interchanges was believed to "cater to local legislators"[^11][^12].

However, after the full line opened in 2004, the Fukuo Expressway took on one-third of the traffic in the western corridor, forming a "Dual National Highways" diversion with the Zhongshan Expressway, becoming one of the most important dual backbones of Taiwan's logistics system today. The political criticism of the past was gradually digested by time into the engineering virtue of "risk diversification."

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> The greatest engineering significance of the Fukuo Expressway is not the road itself, but that it "forced" Taiwan's designers to face slope engineering: cutting through the Linkou Plateau, crossing the Baguashan Range, and viaducts over the Ailiao Creek valley in Pingtung—these experiences were later used in the Xueshan Tunnel and the restoration of the Southern Cross Highway.

## The Xueshan Tunnel: The Cost of Being the Fifth Longest in the World

If there is a "most dramatic" chapter in the national highway network, it must be the Xueshan Tunnel.

Construction began in 1991, originally scheduled for completion in 1998, but was finally delayed until 2006—8 years longer than scheduled, with the budget increasing several times over[^5][^13]. The Xueshan Mountain Range is a "fragmented zone + high groundwater pressure + hard rock sandwiching weak layers" in geological terms. All three major tunnel engineering taboos were present. During construction, there were multiple instances of massive water inflow and landslides, resulting in 25 worker casualties, and the TBM (Tunnel Boring Machine) was even trapped in the mountain body and could not be retrieved[^5][^13].

In the year it opened, the 12.9-kilometer Xueshan Tunnel became the fifth-longest highway tunnel in the world and the second-longest in Asia at the time, remaining a benchmark in Taiwan's engineering history—and a warning: in the fissures of Taiwan's tectonic plates, every kilometer of tunnel is not a problem that money can solve[^5][^13].

## ETC: A Public War Triggered by a Sticker

If the Xueshan Tunnel was a "war of engineering," then ETC was a "war of systems."

In 2006, the National Highway Electronic Toll Collection System (ETC) went online for the first time, with Far Electronic Toll Collection obtaining a 20-year franchise right under a BOT model. The initial use of OBU infrared vehicle units had a long-term low penetration rate. On December 30, 2013, the National Highway fully implemented "electronic distance-based charging," and the manual toll stations went out of history[^6][^14]. The eTag sticker replaced the past return tickets and toll collectors.

The transition period was chaotic: at the beginning of the launch, there were repeated deductions,感应 failures, and gantry charging errors. A spokesperson for Far Electronic Toll Collection saying "This is the destiny that eTag must face" ignited public resentment[^15]. In July 2014, the Legislative Yuan passed a correction report, severely criticizing the Ministry of Transportation and Communications and the National Highway Bureau for being "subject to the vendor" and having unclear contract responsibilities[^7]. However, in April of the same year, an audit committee抽查 (spot-checked) 2.8 million transactions and found only 5 errors. After the system stabilized, Taiwan's full-road distance-based ETC became one of the few successful cases internationally[^16][^17].

> **⚠️ Controversial Viewpoint**
> A 2024 report by the National Policy Foundation points out that although Taiwan's ETC is viewed internationally as a "successful first case," the BOT contract handed the core database of national highway charging to a single private vendor, leaving structural problems that are difficult to supervise in reverse—whether this "success" is a victory for the government or the vendor remains controversial[^17].

## Highway Service Areas: A Type of Taiwanese Public Space

Few countries make highway service areas into a "scenic spot" like Taiwan does.

Qingshui, Xihu, Dongshan, Hukou—these names in Taiwanese people's memories are not just "places to use the restroom," but relay stations for junior high school graduation trips, dining venues for long-distance drivers, and display fields for souvenirs. Taiwan's commercialization model for highway service areas (OT outsourcing, introducing local specialties) differs from the "pure rest" design of most countries, being a Taiwanese public space co-created by the National Highway Bureau and private merchants[^8][^18].

## The Still-Growing Network

As of the end of 2024, Taiwan's national highway network consists of 10 national highways, with a total length exceeding 1,000 kilometers[^8]. New engineering is still underway: the widening of National Highway 1 from Wuku to Yangmei, the extension of National Highway 2 to Taiwan Provincial Highway 61, and the planning of National Highway 7 (Kaohsiung East Outer Ring)—these are no longer the命题 (propositions) of "north-south connectivity," but new-generation issues of "metropolitan area relief" and "passenger-freight separation"[^4][^19][^20].

## What a Road Can Tell You

Building a highway requires not just money, but how much a society is willing to bet on the future.

The Zhongshan Expressway bet that the "western corridor would become the manufacturing heart"—it was proven correct. The Fukuo Expressway bet that "dual backbones would diversify risk"—it was also proven correct. The Xueshan Tunnel bet that "east and west would finally be connected"—in the year it opened, Yilan housing prices doubled, triggering a relocation wave. ETC bet on "full-road distance-based fairness"—it achieved it, but also paid the price of institutional trust.

Next time you are stuck in traffic on the National Highway, look at the milestone on the side of the road. That is not just a kilometer count; it is the accumulation of Taiwan's collective decisions over the past fifty years.
