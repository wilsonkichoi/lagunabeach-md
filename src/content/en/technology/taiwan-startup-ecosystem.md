# Taiwan's Startup Ecosystem

## 30-Second Overview

Taiwan's startup ecosystem began taking shape in the 2000s and, after more than two decades of development, has formed a complete support structure encompassing startup accelerators, venture capital, government policy, and technical talent. From success stories like Appier and 91APP to well-known accelerators like AppWorks, Taiwan is transitioning from an OEM manufacturing base to an innovation-driven economy.

**Keywords**: startups, entrepreneurial ecosystem, venture capital, accelerators, digital transformation

## Why It Matters

A startup ecosystem is an important indicator of a country's innovative vitality and a critical engine for economic transformation and upgrading. For Taiwan, building a sound startup ecosystem is not only a necessary path to escaping the OEM mindset — it is a strategic imperative for maintaining competitive advantage in the global digital economy.

### Driver of Economic Transformation

Startups bring new business models and technological innovation, propelling Taiwan's transition from a manufacturing economy to an innovation economy.

### Revitalization of the Labor Market

Startups create high-skill, high-wage employment opportunities, attracting young talent to stay and develop in Taiwan.

### Enhancement of International Competitiveness

Through innovation and entrepreneurship, Taiwan can occupy a higher position in the global value chain and boost international competitiveness.

## The Development of Taiwan's Startup Ecosystem

### Phase 1: Germination (2000–2008)

**Recovery After the Dot-com Crash**
After the dot-com bubble burst in 2000, Taiwan's technology industry began reflecting on the limitations of a pure OEM model, and entrepreneurs started attempting to develop proprietary brands and services.

**The Early Startup Environment**

- Startup funding came primarily from personal savings or investments from family and friends
- Absence of professional VCs and accelerators
- Limited government policy support
- Market focus remained domestic

**Key Milestones**

- 2000: Wretch (無名小站) was established, becoming an early representative of Taiwan's internet entrepreneurship
- 2003: PCHome Online launched e-commerce services
- 2005: Wretch was acquired by Yahoo!, marking the first Taiwanese internet startup acquisition

### Phase 2: Takeoff (2009–2014)

**Improved Startup Environment**
After the global financial crisis, the government began emphasizing innovation and entrepreneurship, introducing multiple startup-friendly policies.

**The Significance of AppWorks' Founding**
In 2009, Jamie Lin (林之晨) founded AppWorks, Taiwan's first professional startup accelerator, marking the formal beginning of Taiwan's startup ecosystem.

**Government Policy Support**

- 2009: Ministry of Economic Affairs launched the "Innovation and Entrepreneurship Policy White Paper"
- 2010: National Development Fund began investing in startups
- 2012: "Startup Acceleration Program" was launched

**Rise of Entrepreneurial Culture**

- Growth of startup competitions
- Emergence of co-working spaces
- Increased media coverage of entrepreneurship

### Phase 3: Acceleration (2015–2019)

**Emergence of Unicorns**
During this period, Taiwan began producing companies valued at over US$1 billion, demonstrating the international competitiveness of Taiwanese startups.

**Key Success Stories**

- **Appier (founded 2012)**: AI-powered digital advertising and marketing technology company; listed on the Tokyo Stock Exchange in 2021
- **91APP (founded 2013)**: SaaS e-commerce platform; listed on the Taiwan Stock Exchange in 2021
- **Gogolook (founded 2010)**: Whoscall caller identification service; listed on Taiwan's Innovation Board in 2023

**Active Venture Capital**

- International VCs began paying attention to the Taiwanese market
- Domestic VC fund sizes expanded
- Government venture capital funds continued investing

**Southeast Asia Market Strategy**
Taiwanese startups began treating Southeast Asia as an important market, leveraging Taiwan's technical advantages and geographic position to develop international markets.

### Phase 4: Maturity (2020–Present)

**Capital Market Opening**

- 2021: Taiwan launched the "Innovation Board" and "Strategic Emerging Board"
- Lowered listing thresholds for startups
- Provided diversified funding sources

**COVID-19 Accelerated Digital Transformation Demand**
The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated corporate digital transformation, creating new opportunities for startups in B2B SaaS, remote work, e-commerce, and other sectors.

**Increased Internationalization**

- More Taiwanese startups listed overseas
- International VCs continued investing in Taiwanese startups
- Cross-border collaboration increased

## Analysis of Key Success Cases

### Appier: The AI Advertising Tech Unicorn

**Company Overview**
Appier was founded in 2012 by You Chi-han and Li Wan-ling, focusing on AI-driven digital advertising and marketing automation solutions.

**Key Success Factors**

- **Technological advantage**: Leading expertise in deep learning and AI
- **International market**: Focused on the Asia-Pacific market from inception
- **Product strategy**: Developed user-friendly products targeting SME needs
- **Funding support**: Received multiple rounds of international VC investment

**Key Milestones**

- 2014: Raised US$7 million Series A
- 2016: Series B at US$33 million
- 2019: Series C at US$80 million, achieving unicorn status
- 2021: Listed on the Tokyo Stock Exchange Mothers board

**Industry Impact**
Appier's success demonstrated that Taiwanese startups can compete internationally in the AI sector, catalyzing the development of Taiwan's AI startup ecosystem.

### 91APP: Taiwan's First SaaS Unicorn

**Company Background**
91APP was founded in 2013 by Ho Ying-chi, focusing on O2O (Online to Offline) and OMO (Offline Merge Online) solutions for the retail industry.

**Business Model Innovation**

- **SaaS subscription model**: Rare SaaS business model in Taiwan
- **All-in-one service**: Integrates e-commerce, membership, inventory, and payment functions
- **Brand clients**: Serves well-known retail brands including PXMart and FamilyMart

**IPO Performance**

- Listed on the Taiwan Stock Exchange in 2021 (stock code: 6741)
- Market cap exceeded NT$20 billion on the first day
- Became Taiwan's first publicly listed SaaS company

**Industry Significance**
91APP's success demonstrated the Taiwanese capital market's acceptance of the SaaS business model, setting a precedent for other B2B startups.

### Gogolook: A Mobile App Success Story

**Product Success**
The Whoscall caller-identification app has over 100 million users worldwide, making it one of Taiwan's most successful consumer applications.

**Technical Capabilities**

- Big data analytics
- Machine learning algorithms
- Global phone number database

**Commercialization Strategy**

- Freemium model
- Enterprise-grade solutions
- International market expansion

## Startup Accelerators and Incubators

### AppWorks: Leader of Taiwan's Startup Ecosystem

**Institution Overview**
AppWorks was founded in 2009 by Jamie Lin and is Taiwan's most influential startup accelerator and venture capital institution.

**Service Model**

- **Accelerator program**: Free 6-month accelerator services
- **VC fund**: Early-stage to growth-stage investment
- **Ecosystem building**: Building an entrepreneurial community network

**Portfolio Achievements**
The AppWorks ecosystem includes over 2,000 founders, manages US$386 million in assets, with investment cases including:

- 91APP: E-commerce SaaS platform
- Carousell: Singapore C2C e-commerce platform
- Lalamove: On-demand logistics platform
- Dapper Labs: NFT and blockchain technology company

**Areas of Focus**

- AI and machine learning
- Blockchain and Web3
- Southeast Asia market expansion

### Other Key Incubators

**TSS Taiwan Startup Stadium**

- Government-led startup incubation platform
- Provides capital, technology, and market matchmaking services
- Focuses on cultivating startups in AI, IoT, and biotech sectors

**TACC (Taipei Computer Association)**

- Promotes innovation and entrepreneurship activities
- Provides an industry matchmaking platform
- Assists startups in connecting with large enterprise resources

**University Incubation Centers**

- NTU Innovation and Entrepreneurship Center
- NTHU Innovation Incubation Center
- NCTU Industry–Academia Operations Center
- NCKU Graduate Institute of Creative Industry Design

## Venture Capital and Investment Environment

### VC Fund Structure

**Government-backed VCs**

- **National Development Fund**: Government-led entrepreneurial investment fund
- **Small and Medium Enterprise Credit Guarantee Fund**: Provides credit guarantees
- **MOST Value Creation Program**: Support for technology innovation and entrepreneurship

**Domestic Private VCs**

- **AppWorks Fund**: Taiwan's most active early-stage investment fund
- **Cherubic Ventures**: Focused on enterprise digital transformation
- **Anchor Taiwan**: Traditional VC transitioning to digital investment
- **WI Harper Group**: Well-known domestic VC

**International VCs**

- **Sequoia Capital**
- **Matrix Partners**
- **Lightspeed Venture Partners**
- **SoftBank Group**

### Investment Trends and Preferences

**Hot Investment Sectors**

- **Enterprise SaaS**: Growing demand for B2B software services
- **Fintech**: Financial technology innovation applications
- **HealthTech**: Health technology and digital medicine
- **AI/ML**: Artificial intelligence and machine learning
- **Web3/Blockchain**: Blockchain and digital assets

**Investment Stage Distribution**

- Angel and seed rounds: Many startups but smaller amounts
- Series A: Competitive, with reasonable valuations
- Series B and beyond: Increased participation from international investors

**Geographic Expansion Strategy**
Taiwanese startups broadly treat Southeast Asia as a key market, and many VCs also support startups' international expansion strategies.

## Government Policy Support System

### National-Level Startup Policies

**Asia Silicon Valley Plan (2016–2020)**

- Promoted IoT innovation and R&D
- Built an innovation and entrepreneurship ecosystem
- Strengthened connections with international innovation hubs like Silicon Valley

**Digital Nation and Innovative Economic Development Plan (2017–2025)**

- Built digital infrastructure
- Developed digital talent
- Promoted industrial digital transformation

**Forward-looking Infrastructure Development Program**

- Digital infrastructure
- Talent development
- Innovation and entrepreneurship support

### Startup-Friendly Regulations

**Closed Corporation System (2015)**

- Allows flexible equity design
- Simplifies equity transfer procedures
- Protects the rights of founders and investors

**Financial Technology Regulatory Sandbox (2017)**

- Provides a regulatory experimentation space
- Reduces regulatory risk for innovative financial services
- Promotes fintech development

**Innovation Board and Strategic Emerging Board (2021)**

- Lowered listing thresholds for startups
- Provided diversified funding sources
- Established exit mechanisms for startup investors

### Talent Development Policies

**Key Industry Talent Supply and Demand Survey**
The government conducts regular surveys of key industry talent needs as a basis for education policy adjustments.

**Industry-Academia Collaboration Programs**

- Encourages collaboration between universities and industry
- Provides internship and job matchmaking
- Cultivates talent needed by industry

**Attracting Foreign Talent**

- Simplified work visa applications
- Tax incentives
- Improved working environment for foreign professionals

## Challenges and Opportunities in Taiwan's Startup Ecosystem

### Key Challenges

**Capital Scale Limitations**
Compared to China and the United States, Taiwan's VC capital scale remains limited, making it difficult to support the rapid expansion of large startups.

**Market Size Limitations**
Taiwan's domestic market is limited in scale; startups must internationalize early, but doing so is costly and risky.

**Intense Competition for Talent**
Demand for high-tech talent exceeds supply; startups are at a disadvantage competing for talent against large corporations.

**Risk-Averse Culture**
Taiwanese society is relatively conservative with low tolerance for business failure, affecting the entrepreneurial atmosphere.

**Regulatory Adaptability**
Existing regulations were largely designed for traditional industries and lack adaptability to new business models.

### Development Opportunities

**Digital Transformation Demand**
COVID-19 accelerated corporate digital transformation, creating opportunities in B2B SaaS, digital marketing, and other sectors.

**Southeast Asia Market Potential**
Southeast Asia's rapid economic growth provides broad market opportunities for Taiwanese startups.

**Semiconductor Industry Advantage**
Taiwan's leading position in semiconductors gives AI chip, IoT, and related startups a technological edge.

**Manufacturing Sector Digitization**
Taiwan's deep manufacturing base provides startup opportunities in Industry 4.0 and smart manufacturing.

**Cross-border E-commerce Growth**
The global e-commerce market continues to grow; Taiwan has advantages in e-commerce technology and supply chain management.

- **Health management**: Wearable devices, health monitoring

## International Comparison and Positioning

### Comparing Asian Startup Ecosystems

**Taiwan's Advantages**

- High-quality technical talent
- Deep manufacturing industry base
- Democratic rule-of-law environment
- Superior geographic location

**Compared to Singapore**

- Singapore: Government-led, high degree of internationalization
- Taiwan: Strong private sector vitality, deep technical base

**Compared to Hong Kong**

- Hong Kong: Strong in financial services, high international connectivity
- Taiwan: Technology industry base, strong entrepreneurial culture

**Compared to South Korea**

- South Korea: Large corporate-led, strong government support
- Taiwan: Active SMEs, high innovation flexibility

### Global Startup Ecosystem Rankings

According to the Global Startup Ecosystem Report, Taiwan ranks approximately 6th–8th in Asia and approximately 20th–25th globally, indicating room for improvement.

## Future Trends and Outlook

### Near-term Trends (2026–2028)

**Web3 and Blockchain Applications**
As blockchain technology matures, more DeFi, NFT, and DAO applications are expected.

**ESG and Sustainable Development**
Environmental, social, and governance issues are receiving greater attention; green technology startups will attract more focus.

**Accelerated Enterprise Digital Transformation**
Traditional enterprises are accelerating digital transformation, creating opportunities for B2B SaaS startups.

### Medium-term Development (2028–2030)

**Widespread AI Technology Application**
As AI technology matures, applications across vertical sectors will become more extensive.

**Metaverse Ecosystem Building**
With advances in VR/AR technology, metaverse-related applications will develop rapidly.

**Cross-border Service Integration**
Deeper Asian regional integration will increase cross-border service demand.

### Long-term Vision (2030 and Beyond)

**Becoming an Asian Innovation Hub**
Taiwan has the opportunity to become an innovation hub in Asia in specific areas, such as semiconductor-related AI applications and precision manufacturing digitization.

**Unicorn Cluster Formation**
More unicorn companies are expected to emerge, creating a virtuous cycle in the startup ecosystem.

**Increased Internationalization**
The internationalization of Taiwanese startups will significantly improve, making Taiwan an important hub connecting Asian markets.

## Recommendations and Conclusion

### For Entrepreneurs

**Market Strategy**

- Plan for internationalization early; domestic focus alone is not enough
- Pay attention to Southeast Asia market opportunities
- Consider deep cultivation of niche markets

**Financial Planning**

- Plan for international funding early
- Prioritize cash flow management
- Build strong investor relationships

**Team Building**

- Prioritize recruiting internationally-minded talent
- Build a multicultural team
- Develop cross-disciplinary integration capabilities

### For Policymakers

**Regulatory Adaptation**

- Accelerate regulatory adaptation to new business models
- Establish more regulatory sandbox mechanisms
- Simplify cross-border operating regulations

**Capital Environment Improvement**

- Encourage long-term capital such as pension funds to invest in startups
- Build a more robust tax incentive system
- Support international VCs in establishing a presence in Taiwan

**Talent Development**

- Strengthen cross-disciplinary education
- Encourage industry-academia collaboration
- Improve working conditions for foreign professionals

## Conclusion

Taiwan's startup ecosystem has built considerable scale and influence after more than two decades of development. From the early days of Wretch to today's Appier and 91APP, Taiwanese startups have demonstrated the capacity for innovation in both technology and business models.

Looking ahead, the challenge for Taiwan's startup ecosystem is how to create greater international impact with limited resources. This requires the joint efforts of government, enterprises, academia, and entrepreneurs to build a more open, innovative, and internationalized entrepreneurial environment.

Taiwan possesses excellent technical talent, a deep manufacturing industry base, and an open democratic society — all important assets for developing a startup ecosystem. By leveraging these advantages and continuously improving the entrepreneurial environment, Taiwan has every opportunity to become an important innovation and entrepreneurship hub in Asia.
