# Taiwan Railway History

> **30-Second Overview:** In 1891, Taiwan built all of China's first passenger railway — and then the Japanese took over and found it so poorly built they had to tear it down and start over. From Liu Mingchuan's "tuberculosis line" to the 300 km/h high-speed rail of 2007, Taiwan spent 136 years stitching an island with no flat plains into a one-day living circle. In 2024, the HSR carried 78.25 million passengers in a single year; Taiwan Railways, after an accident that claimed 49 lives, finally completed its incorporation as a state-owned company. The tracks have been carved into every turning point this island has made.

## Davidson's Train Adventure

In 1895, American journalist James W. Davidson set foot on Taiwan and took a train from Keelung to Taipei. What he saw he recorded in his later work _The Island of Formosa_: passengers with second-class tickets squeezed into first-class carriages; some boarded with live chickens, live pigs, and piles of vegetables and pork; after a few miles the train began to wobble and shake — when it picked up speed, the passengers and livestock inside rocked left and right together.

That was the true face of Liu Mingchuan's railway.

In 1887, Taiwan's first governor Liu Mingchuan established the "Taiwan Railway Commerce Bureau" at Dadaocheng, hired German engineer Becker to survey the roadbed, and began building Taiwan's — and all of China's — first passenger railway. The Keelung-to-Taipei segment opened in 1891, extended to Hsinchu in 1893, covering a total of about 107 kilometers. The route crossed Shiqiu Ridge from Keelung, passed through Badu, Nangang, and Xikuo (Songshan), crossed Daqiao Head into Haishankuo (Sanchong), then climbed Guilun Ridge to Taoyuan, Zhongli, and finally Hsinchu.

It sounds like a magnificent starting point for modernization. The problem was that the line was genuinely not well built.

## The "Tuberculosis Line"

After the Sino-Japanese War Japan took possession of Taiwan; in June 1895 Governor-General Kabayama Sukenori personally boarded the train from Keelung to Taipei. The train wobbled incessantly, moved at an agonizing crawl, and the roadbed was loose and soft — the accompanying soldiers gave it a nickname: "tuberculosis line," meaning this railway was like a tuberculosis patient, taking three gasps for every two steps.

Japan's professional engineering team arrived in Keelung three months later. An on-site survey made the situation look even worse. Railway engineer Oyama Yasumasa found that for a stretch of the Taipei-to-Hsinchu segment, the ties had been pulled out by people and even the rails were missing. The stations were built of adobe and all had to be demolished and rebuilt. Most fatally, the route design itself was the problem: the stretch from Taipei through Sanchong and over Guilun Ridge to Taoyuan had grades so steep the train simply could not climb them.

Japan made a decision: tear it all down and start over.

North of Taipei, they abandoned the Shiqiu Ridge tunnel segment and rerouted through the flatter Sankeng direction, opening a new tunnel to Badu. The changes south of Taipei were even larger — the original route through Sanchong over Guilun Ridge was scrapped, replaced by a line through Banqiao, Yingge, and on to Taoyuan. This rerouting directly rewrote the fate of towns along the way: Sanchong lost the railway and fell quiet for decades; Banqiao gained the new line and began to rise.

Hidden within this is a historical controversy still debated today: who is "the father of Taiwan's railways"?

## Liu Mingchuan or Hasegawa Kin-nosuke?

In July 2020, the National Taiwan Museum's Railway Department Park opened; its interpretive panels identified Japanese colonial-era Railway Bureau chief Hasegawa Kin-nosuke as "the father of Taiwan's railways." Within a week the argument had exploded. Former legislator Tsai Cheng-yuan challenged: the first person to drive railway development in Taiwan was clearly Liu Mingchuan — how could that title go to a Japanese person?

The historical gray zone lies here: Liu Mingchuan did build Taiwan's first railway, but it was so poor that the Japanese tore it all down. Hasegawa Kin-nosuke spent nine years in Taiwan from 1899 onward, overseeing the construction of the longitudinal trunk line from Keelung to Kaohsiung totaling 404 kilometers, which opened in its entirety in 1908 — and this is the true foundation of the Taiwan Railways network that exists today.

One was a visionary, the other an executor. Who is the "father" depends on whether you think "starting" matters more, or "finishing." (The framing draws on the argumentative context of commentary by Chiang Ping-lun in the United Daily News Roaring Forum, https://opinion.udn.com/opinion/story/12705/4720975)

> **Curator's Note:** The "father of Taiwan's railways" dispute is on the surface a historical debate, but underneath it is the identity politics Taiwan's society is perpetually grappling with — the legacy of the Qing Dynasty and the legacy of [Japanese colonial rule](/en/history/japanese-colonial-era/) — which one "belongs to us more"? This question appears not only in the railway context but also in Japanese colonial-era architecture, irrigation infrastructure, and even the medical system.

## April 20, 1908: That Day at Taichung Park

On April 20, 1908, the full length of the longitudinal line opened. On October 24 of the same year, the Taiwan Governor-General's Office held a "Longitudinal Railway Completion Ceremony" at Taichung Park, and built a Japanese-Western hybrid-style double-pavilion as a rest house for imperial and noble guests — that pavilion still stands on the lake in Taichung Park today, one of Taichung City's landmarks.

The longitudinal line transformed Taiwan's spatial logic. Before the railway, Taiwan was a series of independent settlements clustered along river ports; after the railway, commercial districts rapidly grew up around train stations in cities like Taichung, Chiayi, and Tainan, turning them into regional centers. The train station determined which places would prosper and which would be forgotten — that pattern persisted for a hundred years.

The longitudinal line split into the Mountain Line and the Coastal Line. The Mountain Line ran from Zhunan through Miaoli and Taichung's mountain areas to Changhua, demanding high technical complexity; the old Mountain Line's Shengxing Station at 402 meters above sea level is the highest point of the Western Longitudinal Line. The Coastal Line ran along the western coast through Tongliao, Dajia, and Qingshui — relatively flat but with many sand dunes requiring special sand-prevention engineering. The two lines converged at Zhunan and Changhua, like the two strands of a necklace.

## The Railway at 2,274 Meters Above Sea Level

In 1912, another even more audacious railway was completed.

The Alishan Forest Railway climbs from the Chiayi city area to Alishan at 2,274 meters above sea level, covering 71 kilometers with an elevation gain of over 2,000 meters. To overcome this topography, engineers employed all four methods used by mountain railways around the world: spiraling, zigzag switchbacks (Z-shaped), helical loops, and alternating bridges and tunnels. The full line has over 50 tunnels and 77 wooden bridges.

It was originally built to haul Alishan's millennia-old cypress trees down the mountain for sale. After logging ended, this railway unexpectedly became Taiwan's most beautiful scenic route — taking the narrow-gauge train from the plains up to high mountain terrain, the window view shifts from betel nut groves to bamboo forests and then to cloud-wreathed coniferous forest, as if traversing Taiwan's vertical ecology in two hours.

The Ministry of Culture has listed the Alishan Forest Railway as one of Taiwan's 18 World Heritage Potential Sites. In 2018 the Executive Yuan established the "Alishan Forest Railway and Cultural Heritage Administration" to take dedicated responsibility for preserving this century-old industrial heritage. ([Source: Ministry of Culture](https://www.moc.gov.tw/en/News_Content2.aspx?n=398&s=14062))

## The Bento Is More Famous Than the Train

In 1949, Taiwan Railways began producing bento at railway restaurant operations at five stations: Kaohsiung, Tainan, Taichung, Taipei, and Songshan — for the convenience of long-distance passengers. Initially just ordinary rice-and-dish bento boxes, they gradually evolved into the iconic classic: the braised pork chop bento. ([Source: Taiwan Panorama](https://www.taiwan-panorama.com/Articles/Details?Guid=3cd2ae7a-f0e1-4aa2-8696-9af844ba112c&CatId=10))

By the 2020s, Taiwan Railways was selling over 10 million bento boxes a year. Taipei Main Station alone sells tens of thousands per day, with braised pork chop bento accounting for nearly 90 percent. Some people say Taiwan Railways is "a bento shop that got sidetracked into running trains" — and there's real bitterness in that joke: the quality of Taiwan Railways' service has long been criticized, but the reputation of its bento has never wavered. ([Source: United Daily News Time Archive](https://time.udn.com/udntime/story/122390/7012703))

The Taiwan Railways bento sells more than food. That aluminum box, that pork chop steeped through with dark braising liquid, the pickled cabbage and tofu stuffed into the corners — for many Taiwanese people this is the smell of a train journey, a kind of ritual for being "on the road."

## A Bet on "Zero Government Contribution"

By the 1990s, Taiwan's north-south transportation was already saturated. Sun Yat-sen Freeway and Second Freeway were clogged; domestic air routes had no seats to spare. The government decided to build a high-speed railway, putting it out for tender under a BOT (Build-Operate-Transfer) model.

In 1997, the Taiwan High Speed Rail team led by Yin Chi won the bid with the promise of "zero government contribution" — the largest BOT project in history. Total construction cost was approximately NT$513.3 billion, using the technology of Japan's Shinkansen 700T series trains. Service began in January 2007; Taipei to Kaohsiung in as little as 1 hour 35 minutes, at 300 km/h.

But the promise of "zero contribution" quickly became a financial nightmare. Initial ridership fell short of projections, and the enormous interest burden kept the company continuously in the red, with debt at one point reaching NT$400 billion. In 2009 Yin Chi resigned as chairperson and the HSR was nearly pushed to bankruptcy. Then-CEO Ou Chin-der forcefully resisted a bankruptcy proposal, arguing that a government buyout would be "harmful to society." ([Source: CommonWealth Magazine](https://www.cw.com.tw/article/5063616))

In 2015 the government led a financial restructuring, extending the concession period and adjusting the shareholding structure. The HSR finally survived. In 2024, full-year ridership reached 78.25 million, revenues for the first time broke NT$50 billion — reaching NT$53.19 billion — with average daily ridership of 214,000. ([Source: United Daily News](https://udn.com/news/story/7270/8757143))

From the brink of bankruptcy to NT$50 billion in annual revenue, the HSR's story is not a simple success narrative — it is a lesson in public infrastructure, private capital, and political maneuvering.

> **Curator's Note:** The HSR BOT case taught Taiwan one thing: "zero government contribution" sounds beautiful, but the risk in public infrastructure ultimately has to be borne by someone. Most high-speed railways in the world are government-funded; Taiwan needed twenty years to learn why.

## 49 Lives' Worth of Reform

On April 2, 2021 — the eve of the Tomb-Sweeping Day holiday — Taroko Express Train No. 408 collided with a construction vehicle that had slid down an embankment before the Qingshui Tunnel near Hualien, killing 49 people and injuring over 200. It was the worst accident in Taiwan Railways since 1948.

The accident investigation revealed not just a slid construction vehicle but the systemic problems that had accumulated in Taiwan Railways over decades: lax construction site management, a thin safety culture, organizational rigidity. Japanese JR West safety advisor Abe Seiji stated bluntly that by his standards, "JR West in eighteen years of reform after the Fukuchiyama accident has only earned 50 points; Taiwan Railways, about to be incorporated, has a safety consciousness still at the level of JR West before the accident occurred." ([Source: The Reporter](https://www.twreporter.org/a/taiwan-railway-3334-local-train-atp-neiwei-station))

On January 1, 2024, the Ministry of Transportation and Communications' Taiwan Railways Administration was formally reorganized as "Taiwan Railways Co., Ltd." — a state-owned corporation. The inaugural chairperson and president signed a _Safety Charter_ and posted it at the entrance of the Taiwan Railways company building. Incorporation was seen as the starting point for breaking the bureaucratic system and introducing corporate management — but how long it will take for the culture to change along with the organization, no one can promise.

## A Nervous System for an Island

Taiwan's railway map looks like a nervous system: the Western Longitudinal Line is the spinal cord, the High Speed Rail is the express channel running alongside it, the Eastern North-Link and South-Link lines are the nerve extensions reaching into Hualien and Taitung, and the Alishan and Pingxi lines are the capillaries.

This system grew from Liu Mingchuan's tottering "tuberculosis line" of 1891, passed through Japan's complete rebuild, through wartime bombardment and postwar reconstruction, through electrification and high-speed modernization, through a BOT gamble that nearly bankrupted the HSR, and through the painful reform bought with 49 lives.

When the National Taiwan Museum's Railway Department Park opened in 2020, a display board identified Hasegawa Kin-nosuke as "the father of Taiwan's railways." Up in the hills of Keelung's Anle District, Liu Mingchuan's Shiqiu Ridge Tunnel still stands — Taiwan's very first railway tunnel, brick walls thick with moss. As for today's MRT Zhonghe-Xinlu Line from Daqiao Head to Huilong, it runs almost exactly along the path of Liu Mingchuan's 1893 railway, only now the line has gone from the surface down underground.

A hundred and thirty years have passed. The path is still there. Just wearing different skin.
