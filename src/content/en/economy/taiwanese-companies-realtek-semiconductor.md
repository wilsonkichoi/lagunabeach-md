> **30-Second Overview:** In 1987, seven engineers used NT$2 million to found Realtek Semiconductor in Hsinchu Science Park, with a crab as their logo. In 1991 they released Taiwan's first independently developed network chip. By 2024 revenues exceeded NT$80 billion; Realtek is Taiwan's third-largest IC design company, and inside virtually every computer in the world, there may be a "Realtek crab" hiding.

If you've ever used a computer, you've probably dealt with a crab. This crab won't pinch you, but it will connect your computer to the internet, play music, and display images on screen. It hides in a corner of the motherboard, printed on a small black chip, under the brand name "Realtek."

This is the story of how seven engineers with NT$2 million changed the world.

## The Seven Warriors' NT$2 Million Bet

In 1987, Taiwan had just lifted martial law, the economy was taking off, and Hsinchu Science Park had only been established a few years. Seven engineers working for foreign companies gathered together to discuss a bold idea: could they design chips themselves?

At the time, Taiwan's tech industry was still in the OEM assembly stage; designing chips yourself sounded like a fantasy. But these seven engineers — later called Realtek's "Seven Warriors" — believed Taiwan's engineers had that capability.

They pooled NT$2 million and on October 21 established Realtek Semiconductor. The company's English name is Realtek — "Real" stands for reality, "tek" for technology, meaning "real technology."

There's an interesting reason for choosing a crab as the logo: crabs are small but highly adaptable, able to survive in all kinds of environments. This was exactly their hope for the company — finding its own way to survive in the intensely competitive tech industry.

## The Birth of the First Crab

The early days weren't smooth. Seven engineers crowded into a small office with no customers, no products — only passion and technical ideals.

The turning point came in 1991. At the time, Taiwan's computer industry was developing rapidly, but network chips all had to be purchased from American or Japanese manufacturers — not only expensive, but often with inadequate technical support.

Realtek's team spotted this opportunity and decided to develop Taiwan's first independently designed Ethernet controller. After two years of R&D, the RTL8002 was born — Taiwan's first network chip with fully proprietary intellectual property rights.

This chip's significance went beyond technology. It proved that Taiwan's engineers had the ability to design world-class products from scratch, and marked a key step for Taiwan's tech industry in moving from "OEM manufacturing" toward "independent R&D."

## Memories of the Crab Card Era

In the mid-1990s, personal computers began to proliferate and the internet gradually entered Taiwanese homes. At the time, getting a computer online required installing a "network card" — usually a green circuit board with various chips and connectors.

Because Realtek's network cards had a crab logo, Taiwanese computer enthusiasts nicknamed them "crab cards" (螃蟹卡). This name later became a nostalgic symbol representing the era of Taiwan's early internet development.

More importantly, Realtek drove the price of network cards from thousands of NT dollars (charged by American manufacturers) down to hundreds, letting more Taiwanese people afford computers and access the internet. This philosophy of "democratizing technology" later became an important characteristic of Taiwan's tech industry.

## The Cross-Industry Move to Sound Cards

After successfully breaking into the network card market, Realtek was not satisfied with the status quo. In the late 1990s, they began entering the sound chip field.

This decision seemed off-key, but actually had its logic. Network transmission and audio processing both involve signal processing technology; the technical foundation Realtek had built with network chips could be applied to the audio field.

Realtek's sound chips quickly penetrated the supply chains of major motherboard manufacturers. Taiwan's large motherboard companies — ASUS, Gigabyte, MSI — all began integrating Realtek's audio solutions into their products.

By the 2000s, Realtek had become one of the world's major PC sound chip suppliers. Almost every assembled computer had a Realtek chip running inside.

## The Invisible-But-Everywhere Little Crab

Today's Realtek is no longer the seven-person small company of its origins. As of 2024, company revenues exceed NT$80 billion, employees exceed 6,000 people, and it is Taiwan's third-largest IC design company.

But even more impressive is the penetration rate of Realtek's products. From home routers to laptop computers, from smart TVs to industrial controllers, Realtek's chips are virtually everywhere. Hundreds of millions of people globally use devices with Realtek chips every day.

This success comes from Realtek's "platform strategy." They don't design single products but develop technology platforms applicable to various products. One core technology platform can produce different products including network chips, sound chips, and display control chips.

## The New Battlefield of the WiFi Era

In the 2010s, with the proliferation of smartphones and the rise of IoT, wireless networks became a new battlefield. Realtek again demonstrated its adaptability, moving from wired networking into wireless networking.

The WiFi chips they developed are widely used in routers, smart appliances, industrial equipment, and other products. Especially in the WiFi 6 era, Realtek's solutions have found a good balance between performance and cost.

Interestingly, the crab logo chosen back then to represent "strong adaptability" truly proved prophetic. From 1980s network cards to 2020s WiFi 6, Realtek has found its position in each wave of technological change.

## Challenges in the US-China Tech War

In recent years, US-China technology competition has intensified, with semiconductors becoming strategic assets. Realtek, as an important global network chip supplier, also faces new challenges.

On one hand, the Chinese market is very important for Realtek, with large numbers of routers, TV boxes, and smart appliances using Realtek chips. On the other hand, US export control policies may affect Realtek's sales to certain Chinese customers.

But Realtek's response strategy remains returning to its original heart: focusing on technological innovation. They believe that as long as they continue providing the best technology solutions, they can find their survival space in a complex geopolitical environment.

## The Crab Kingdom 35 Years Later

From 1987 to 2024, Realtek has traversed 37 years. Some of the original Seven Warriors have retired, some have moved to other companies, but the crab kingdom they founded continues to grow robustly.

Today's Realtek is not just a chip design company but a technology platform. Their chips enable devices worldwide to connect to networks, play audio, and display images. Although consumers may not know Realtek's name, virtually everyone benefits from Realtek's technology.

This story tells us that sometimes changing the world doesn't require loud slogans or massive investment. Seven engineers, NT$2 million, a crab logo, plus 30+ years of persistence and innovation — that's enough to let a little crab crawl all over the world.

And this crab's story is still continuing.
