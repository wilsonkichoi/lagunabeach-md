> **30-second overview:** PanSci is a Taiwanese science website and community founded in 2011. It grew out of a scrapped project at the Taiwan Digital Culture Association. In its early days, it was sustained by Cheng Kuo-wei and Hsu Ting-yao visiting science bloggers, soliciting contributions, and building a community. By 2013, it had over 80 science bloggers and more than 2,780 articles. Later communication studies placed PanSci within discussions of the democratization of science communication in Taiwan, social media public participation, and cross-strait comparisons of popular science websites. By the 2020s, PanSci had woven together articles, the PanSci Academy, the "Sci Student" platform, video, advertising projects, e-commerce, and a YouTube MCN into a knowledge services network. Its core tension is clear: for science to remain part of public discourse, the platform must first survive within commercial projects and algorithms.

## The Project That Got Scrapped

In 2014, Cheng Kuo-wei looked back at PanSci's origins without writing it as a startup myth. He described a website left behind after a project failed.

He had joined the Taiwan Digital Culture Association, and to justify keeping another person on staff, Hsu Ting-yao took on a project to promote Taiwan's achievements in bioscience and medicine online. Cheng built it according to his own vision, and by the time he remembered to report progress to the client, the direction was completely misaligned with what the client needed. The project was scrapped, the salary source vanished, and PanSci was born.[^letter]

This origin matters because every choice PanSci made afterward can be read from here: its roots were not in a university, a government agency, or a research institute, but in blogs, citizen journalism, and online communities. That group discovered that Taiwan's public discourse lacked a place to put science back into social issues.

Cheng Kuo-wei has no science degree. In 2014 he described himself as "an unqualified editor-in-chief without a science background," and at the start the editorial team was just him. PanSci's earliest work was decidedly low-tech: visiting the small but passionate community of science bloggers in Taiwan and asking them to become columnists.[^letter]

> **The real challenge lies in how the room is designed: whether scientists, educators, readers, and editors can calibrate one another in the same space.**

## Science Journalism Was a Giant Black Hole

In 2013, when _Business Next_ wrote about PanSci, it offered an early and precise diagnosis: Taiwan's mainstream media lacked science coverage. Cheng Kuo-wei put it in stark terms: "Science journalism is a giant black hole."[^bnext]

This pointed to a concrete public problem. The Fourth Nuclear Power Plant, US beef, ractopamine, avian flu — every major public controversy of the early 2010s involved science, risk, statistics, institutional, and trust issues. When mainstream media had no stable science reporters, science entered public discourse in the form of wire-service rewrites, expert soundbites, pundit talking points, or online rumors.

PanSci's first position was therefore more like a missing intermediary layer in public issues. In March 2013, a "Popular Science and New Media" forum drew over 300 young people, with topics ranging from the physics of archery in films to the psychology of breakups. The same report noted that PanSci's readership was primarily high school and university students, making it a source of scientific knowledge for students.[^bnext]

This early scene is crucial: PanSci, before the short-video era, already treated "readers willing to stop and engage" as a prerequisite for science entering public life.

## A Room of 80 Science Bloggers

By 2013, PanSci no longer looked like a small website.

A science forum report from that year noted that PanSci had accumulated over 80 science bloggers, more than 2,780 articles, over 20,000 daily visitors, and more than 130,000 monthly visitors.[^life] For Taiwan's internet in the early 2010s, these numbers represented a kind of community density: a group of people willing to translate specialized knowledge into public language, with a fixed point of entry.

In 2014, Cheng provided another set of early figures: in its first year, PanSci had only one full-time editor, and an article getting 2,000 to 3,000 views was cause for celebration, with monthly traffic in the range of 20,000 to 30,000. Four years later, monthly page views exceeded 2.5 million, and Facebook likes surpassed 170,000.[^letter]

These numbers suggest that Taiwanese society had a latent demand for science reading — it just didn't necessarily look like a textbook or a research paper. The gap PanSci found was in turning science from "the answer" into "a method of discussion."

The official "About Us" page states the mission directly: PanSci invites researchers, educators, enthusiasts, and those affected by science to discuss science together, returning scientific development to Taiwan's public forum, and using rational thinking to examine the scientific dimensions of social issues.[^about]

PanSci's most frequently quoted line is also here:

> "Science is too important to be left to scientists alone."[^about]

This line is easy to read as a slogan, but its real difficulty lies in the second half: if science is to be opened up for broader discussion, then to whom should it be entrusted? Entrust it to the media, and you run into headlines and click counts; entrust it to the community, and you run into emotion and echo chambers; entrust it to schools, and you run into curricula and exams; entrust it to corporations, and you run into sponsorship and trust.

PanSci's fifteen years have essentially been a process of shifting between these answers.

## How Researchers Later Saw It

PanSci also entered the case files of communication researchers.

In 2016, National Chengchi University's academic repository included Shih Tsung-jen's study "Social Network Sites and Public Participation: A User Study of the 'Pansci' Facebook Page." Using an online survey with N = 1,160, the study found that Pansci use had a positive impact on both informational participation and civic participation. Among usage motivations, social motivation was associated with both types of participation, while entertainment, self-positioning, and information motivations were linked to civic participation.[^nccu]

In other words, PanSci's Facebook page was simultaneously spreading articles and training a subset of users to connect science content to public issue engagement.

In 2017, the _Communication and Society Journal_ published "A Preliminary Study on the Characteristics and Communication Effects of Popular Science Websites Across the Taiwan Strait," conducting a content analysis comparing China's Guokr and Taiwan's PanSci. The study found that both websites covered diverse science topics, but Guokr leaned more toward biomedicine and earth sciences, while PanSci placed greater emphasis on physics, information science, and mathematics. PanSci's articles and titles were more vivid and placed more weight on diverse opinions from different sources.[^airiti]

This comparison placed PanSci within the context of cross-strait civil society differences: even when both are popular science platforms, how articles are written, how sources are arranged, and how interaction is designed all reflect political and social environments.

In 2020, the Taiwan chapter in ANU Press's _Communicating Science: A Global Perspective_ wrote PanSci — a science blogging initiative begun in 2011 — into the history of science communication in Taiwan. The chapter called PanSci one of the largest and most important knowledge communities of science communicators in Taiwan and placed PanMedia's for-profit incorporation within the context of "building a knowledge ecosystem."[^anu>

> **Taken together, these three research perspectives clarify PanSci's position: as Taiwan moved from "experts teaching the public" toward "citizens discussing science together," it commercialized early and was observed by researchers early.**

## Advertising, Courses, E-Commerce — All Part of Science Communication

PanSci's official page does not hide its business model. It explicitly lists advertising projects, video production, event partnerships, e-commerce, and online course publishing.[^about]

This makes it very different from [[The Reporter]]. The Reporter places "freedom from advertising and click-rate interference" within the ethical commitments of nonprofit investigative journalism; PanSci from the start was more like a knowledge community company. It wanted to do public discussion, but also to take on projects, sell courses, run events, produce video, and sell products.

When PanSci was preparing to spin off as PanSci Knowledge Inc. in 2014, Cheng Kuo-wei called it a social enterprise, aiming to address an "ecological crisis of knowledge": problems at every stage of knowledge creation, transformation, dissemination, and application.[^letter] This framing was much larger — and much riskier — than "popular science website," because it pushed PanSci into the gray zone between media, education, and industry.

In 2018, TechNews reported that PanSci Knowledge had taken a stake in the crowdfunding platform flyingV, describing PanSci Knowledge as having started from PanSci and gradually expanding into multiple vertical media outlets, events, education, and physical spaces.[^technews] As of 2026, not every brand mentioned in that report can be assumed equally active, but it revealed PanSci Knowledge's ambition: to turn knowledge content into services, products, and platforms.

The 104 company page is more direct. PanSci Knowledge positions itself in the digital content industry, with capital of NT$30 million and 50 employees. Its main products and services include new media content, video production, digital advertising integrated marketing, video platform data analysis, online courses, e-commerce, and subscription-based learning platforms. The page also notes that the company obtained YouTube MCN qualification in 2023.[^104]

A 2022 PanSci Knowledge media kit stated the commercial language even more bluntly. The deck claimed PanSci averaged over 3 million monthly page views, a Facebook readership of 450,000, a YouTube readership of 220,000, and an Instagram readership of 84,000, and described its services as brand translation, information verification, infographics, and multi-channel marketing.[^saleskit]

This deck is a sales document, and precisely because of that, it is useful. It shows readers what PanSci sells externally: research, reporting, book summaries, experiments, animations, social media posts, and KOL collaborations, packaged as knowledge marketing services that brands can purchase.

> **Calling this "popular science polluted by commerce" is too quick. More accurately, PanSci treats commercialization as a survival question for science communication: without revenue, public science discussion can only burn on passion; once revenue depends on corporations and platforms, trust becomes something that must be re-proven every day.**

## That Linfengying Ranch Video

In 2016, this trust problem erupted in concentrated form.

PanSci accepted sponsorship from Wei Chuan and produced "PanSci Investigation Corps," bringing editors and investigators to Wei Chuan factories, the Linfengying Ranch, and the Central Research Institute to observe, then produced a video on the production process of Linfengying fresh milk. At the time, Wei Chuan/Uni-President was under intense scrutiny due to food safety scandals and boycott campaigns. After the video went live, many netizens criticized PanSci for accepting sponsorship from a controversial company, accusing it of whitewashing the brand.[^tnl]

The Central News Agency reported at the time that when Cheng Kuo-wei responded to the question "Should Uni-President be boycotted?" he said his answer was "I don't know." His meaning was closer to a methodology: before fully understanding, before collecting what he considered credible data, before finding the most original sources, one cannot pretend to know.[^cna]

A science media outlet here runs into a public trust problem. When the funder is the subject being discussed, readers care about test data but also ask: was the framing of the discussion, the interview setting, the data access, the video editing, and the timing of release shaped by the sponsor's interests?

When The News Lens organized the debate at the time, it placed it within the dilemma of media survival and native advertising: media need money to produce in-depth content, but whose money they take, how they disclose it, and whether they preserve the possibility of counterexamples directly affects trust.[^tnl]

This episode deserves a place in the PanSci entry because it made PanSci's core contradiction visible: when you say "use rationality and evidence to discuss the scientific dimensions of social issues," readers will turn around and demand you apply the same standard to your own commercial conditions.

## From Popular Science Articles to Classroom Question Sets

PanSci later did not stay only in media.

The PanSci Academy's official page positions itself as an online course platform under the PanSci Knowledge media group, using diverse learning channels, cross-disciplinary talent, and applicable topics to make knowledge spread more effectively.[^school] This was PanSci's first layer of transformation from "readers" to "learners."

Another product closer to the school setting is "Sci Student." In 2020, PanSci Knowledge, Nanyi Publishing, and _Scientific American_ (Taiwan edition) launched a popular science reading platform, using 10,000 popular science articles from the PanSci platform and 50 years of archives from _Scientific American_, then having educators rewrite them into short passages and questions suitable for junior and senior high school students.[^student]

This transformation is interesting. PanSci's early articles were often written for adult and university-level readers. With Sci Student, they had to be rewritten into roughly 400-word pieces aligned with curriculum progress that teachers could incorporate into morning reading or literacy-based question sets.[^student]

Once a science website enters the classroom, its readers become "people squeezed by curricula, exams, reading levels, and teacher preparation time." This shifts PanSci's publicness to a different setting: from the online public forum to science reading in schools.

This also allows it to be viewed alongside the [[Encyclopedia of Taiwan]]. The Encyclopedia of Taiwan is a state project to establish a definitive knowledge corpus; PanSci is a private platform that breaks scientific knowledge into articles, question sets, courses, videos, and services. The former asks "who has the authority to define Taiwan's knowledge"; the latter asks "how knowledge gets seen, read, internalized, and used further."

## Wednesday Morning Recording Day

In 2026, PanSci's most visible form may no longer be its website, but short-form video.

This direction has a traceable trajectory. A 2022 PeoPo citizen journalism report noted that a PanSci [monkeypox/Mpox explainer video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xmjHnr6u9BM) garnered over 2.2 million views on YouTube, and that the channel had accumulated over 400 videos and nearly 400,000 subscribers at the time.[^peopo][^yt-mpox]

From the same year's official channel video on [James Webb Space Telescope image analysis](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tTe-nwziwCw) to 2026's [PanSci NEWS EP46](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=x__seBgp4HE), one can see PanSci's video efforts testing formats across astronomy, medicine, everyday science, and science news.[^yt-webb][^yt-news46]

A Mirror Weekly profile reported that Cheng Kuo-wei, hitting a traffic plateau, began posting daily science news short-form videos. On recording days, colleagues select science news from sources such as _Nature_, _Science_, and Science Alert; Cheng requires scripts to simultaneously check the original paper and other high-quality science media coverage, then uses AI tools to help digest papers and evaluate script versions.[^mirror]

This workflow compresses fifteen years of PanSci's evolution: it started with blogs and articles, then moved to community, events, courses, e-commerce, and now must face short-form video platforms. Science communicators need to research, understand research methods, and write copy — but also understand pacing, camera work, memes, algorithms, and audience retention.

Mirror Weekly wrote that Cheng uses about 1,200 characters in three minutes to explain a scientific paper and tries to explain the research methodology so viewers can scrutinize whether the study holds up.[^mirror] This is actually PanSci's oldest ideal in a new format: the audience gets the conclusion and also sees "why you can question this conclusion."

But short-form video also makes the pressure more naked. Cheng spoke in the interview about traffic anxiety, saying that if science had not drifted away from society, there would be no need to enter the battlefield of traffic anxiety.[^mirror] This line could almost serve as a footnote for PanSci in the 2020s: science communicators enter the algorithm because that is where public discussion is already happening.

This also connects PanSci to the [[Taiwan YouTuber Industry and Culture]]. When knowledge content enters YouTube and short-form video, it faces the same update frequency, audience habits, platform rules, and commercial conversion pressures as entertainment creators — with an added layer: the cost of errors falls on public understanding.

## From Website to Knowledge Industry

PanSci's parent company, PanSci Knowledge, has also increasingly come to resemble a knowledge industry services provider.

The 104 company page states that it builds on content production experience, introducing data analysis, content production, and advertising monetization systems, with the goal of constructing a digital content industry oriented toward the future.[^104] The PanMedia website in 2024 announced the MARMOT system, describing it as a multi-channel analysis, revenue optimization, and precision targeting tool designed for explanatory news media and content creators, with partners including Foodnext and Plain Language Law.[^marmot]

This step moved PanSci beyond self-operated popular science, turning its accumulated data, video, monetization, and creator management capabilities into systems that other knowledge media can also use.

Its difference from 想想論壇 (Thinking Taiwan Forum) is also here. 想想論壇 is more of a political and democratic dialogue article platform; PanSci grew from a science article platform into a knowledge content supply chain. It is simultaneously a media outlet, a course company, a video team, a data tool, a creator network, and an advertising services provider.

This metamorphosis carries risks. The more a platform knows how to monetize content, the more it will be suspected of letting monetization logic determine content in reverse. But it also has practical significance: if public knowledge content cannot learn to survive within the platform economy, algorithms will not automatically keep good knowledge at the top.

## Admitting What You Don't Know

At its best, PanSci turns "not knowing" into a workable public state, refusing to package science as an unassailable answer.

In a 2022 LIS interview, Cheng Kuo-wei's declaration on scientific literacy was: "Admit that you don't know many things."[^lis] This line echoes both the 2016 Linfengying incident and the 2026 short-form video workflow. The task of science communication is to train society to tolerate uncertainty: when information is insufficient, evidence conflicts, and interests are entangled, people are still willing to slow down, check more, and admit they might be wrong.

PanSci's fifteen-year story has no clean success line. It has failed projects, community passion, commercial expansion, sponsorship controversies, educational products, short-form video anxiety, and the ambition to turn knowledge into an industry service.

If [[The Reporter]] answers the question "How should public truth be funded?" through nonprofit investigative journalism, PanSci answers a different question: How can public science survive across commercial platforms, school settings, and community algorithms?

The answer remains unstable as of 2026. But the questions PanSci leaves behind are clear: science is too important to be left without broad participation; it is also too fragile — hand it to the algorithm and it loses the thickness of public discussion.
