# The Semiconductor Industry

> **30-second overview:** TSMC began mass production of its 2nm process at the end of 2025, leading the world by 2–3 years. Its 3nm process utilization has already reached 100%, and advanced process revenue accounts for 69% of total sales. From Morris Chang's 1987 bet on the "pure-play foundry" model to today's status as the lifeline of global technology—Taiwan holds the most advanced chip manufacturing technology on Earth.

One afternoon in 1985, Minister without Portfolio Kuo-Ting Li tracked down Morris Chang, who had just returned to Taiwan to take up the presidency of the Industrial Technology Research Institute (ITRI). Li got straight to the point: "We want to build a very-large-scale integrated circuit manufacturing company. You're going to lead it."

Chang was taken aback. He thought he was only coming back to head the institute. Two weeks later, he was pulled into founding a company with a business model no one had ever tried before.

That conversation changed the world.

## 1987: An Unprecedented Bet

### The Prelude: RCA Technology Transfer

The story begins in 1973. That year, ITRI spent US$4.5 million to acquire integrated circuit technology from the American company RCA and sent 19 engineers to the United States for training. No one at the time imagined that this "tuition" would become the first cornerstone of Taiwan's semiconductor kingdom.

In 1980, ITRI spun off its technology to form United Microelectronics Corporation (UMC), giving Taiwan its first semiconductor company. But Li was not satisfied—UMC was too small, its technology could not keep up with international standards. Taiwan needed a bigger breakthrough.

### Morris Chang's Crazy Idea

On February 21, 1987, Morris Chang established Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC) in the Hsinchu Science Park, creating a business model the world had never seen: **pure-play foundry**.

The idea sounded insane at the time. Every semiconductor company in the world was vertically integrated—handling everything from design to manufacturing under one roof. How could a company only do manufacturing and not design? Would clients really hand over their most confidential chip layouts?

Chang's logic was simple: the semiconductor industry was growing increasingly complex. Design and manufacturing are two entirely different specialties. Rather than doing everything and excelling at nothing, it was better to focus on doing one thing—making chips—better than anyone else on the planet.

### The Ingenious International Partnership

TSMC's initial equity structure was carefully designed:

- Government investment: 48.3% (ensuring national strategic positioning)
- Private investment: 24.2% (Taiwanese capital participation)
- Netherlands-based Philips: 27.5% (bringing in international technology and customers)

Philips' involvement was critical. At the time, the semiconductor industry was dominated by the United States and Japan, and Europe urgently needed an alternative supplier. Philips didn't just invest—it also placed its own chip orders with TSMC, becoming its first major customer.

## The Foundry Model: Reshaping the Global Semiconductor Ecosystem

### A Revolution in Industrial Division of Labor

TSMC's foundry model triggered a great division of labor in the semiconductor industry:

- **IC design companies** focus on designing chips (e.g., Qualcomm, NVIDIA, MediaTek)
- **Foundries** focus on manufacturing chips (TSMC, UMC, GlobalFoundries)
- **OSAT (outsourced assembly and test) firms** handle back-end processes (ASE, SPIL)

This model allowed more companies to enter the semiconductor industry. In the past, only giants like Intel and IBM could afford the astronomical investment required to build a fab. Now, any startup with a great idea could design a chip and hand it to TSMC for manufacturing.

### Building a Trust Mechanism

The core of the foundry model is trust. Customers must believe that TSMC will not steal their designs, will not leak trade secrets, and will not compete with them.

TSMC established strict "trust rules":

1. **Technology neutrality**: Never design chips of its own
2. **Customer equality**: All customers receive the same technology and service
3. **Confidentiality agreements**: The highest level of information security protection
4. **Capacity allocation**: Fair distribution of advanced process capacity

These rules have been enforced for nearly 40 years, without a single exception.

## Process Technology: The Eternal Arms Race

### Chasing Moore's Law

In 1965, Intel founder Gordon Moore proposed "Moore's Law": the number of transistors on a chip doubles every 18 months. This law drove the semiconductor industry for over 50 years and became the racetrack on which TSMC competes with the rest of the world.

Starting from the 3-micron process in 1987, TSMC has been in relentless pursuit:

- **1990s**: 1.2μm, 0.8μm, 0.6μm
- **2000s**: 0.35μm, 0.25μm, 0.18μm, 0.13μm, 90nm
- **2010s**: 65nm, 40nm, 28nm, 20nm, 16nm, 10nm, 7nm
- **2020s**: 5nm, 3nm, 2nm

### The Arrival of the 2nm Era

In Q4 2025, TSMC began mass production of its 2nm process, with primary production lines at Baoshan Fab 20 in Hsinchu and Kaohsiung Fab 22. This represents a new frontier in human manufacturing—2nm is roughly the diameter of 20 atoms, approaching the theoretical boundary of physics.

TSMC estimates that by the end of 2026, its 2nm process will reach a monthly capacity of 100,000 wafers, with first-wave customers including Apple's A-series chips and NVIDIA's AI chips.

What is even more daunting for competitors: TSMC is already developing its 1.6nm (A16) process, with mass production expected in the second half of 2026. While the rest of the world is still chasing 3nm, TSMC is already sprinting toward the angstrom era (sub-1nm).

### The Cost of Technological Leadership

The R&D cost of advanced processes has grown exponentially:

- 28nm process: US$1 billion
- 7nm process: US$3 billion
- 3nm process: US$10 billion
- 2nm process: estimated at over US$20 billion

These astronomical figures mean fewer and fewer companies can keep up in this game. Currently, only TSMC, Samsung, and Intel remain in the advanced process arms race—and TSMC leads its rivals by 2–3 generations.

## The Sacred Mountain of the Nation: A New Geopolitical Chip

### The Silicon Shield Effect

TSMC's technological advantage has unexpectedly become Taiwan's "silicon shield" in geopolitics. The world's tech giants all depend on TSMC's advanced processes:

- **Apple**: core chips for iPhone, iPad, and Mac
- **NVIDIA**: AI training and inference chips
- **AMD**: CPU and GPU chips
- **Qualcomm**: 5G smartphone chips
- **Tesla**: autonomous driving chips

If TSMC halted production for one week, the global tech supply chain would be paralyzed. This strategic position gives Taiwan unprecedented influence in international politics.

### The Global Scramble

Precisely because TSMC is so important, countries around the world want to bring this technology to their own shores:

**Arizona, USA (2025–2028):**
A US$40 billion investment, with plans to produce 5nm and 3nm chips. But capacity is limited, and the symbolic significance outweighs the practical impact.

**Kumamoto, Japan (production began 2024):**
A partnership with Sony and Toyota, producing automotive chips at 22nm to 28nm. These are not the most advanced processes, but they are sufficient for Japan's automotive industry needs.

**Dresden, Germany (operational 2027):**
A partnership with BMW and Bosch, targeting the European automotive electronics market.

The key point across all these overseas fabs is this: **the most advanced process technology remains in Taiwan.** Cutting-edge technologies such as 2nm and 1.6nm will not be relocated in the short term. Taiwan remains the heart of global semiconductor technology.

## The AI Wave: An Unexpected Second Spring

### The Shift from Mobile to AI

Before 2020, TSMC's primary customers were mobile chip vendors. But after ChatGPT went viral in 2022, AI became the new growth engine.

AI training and inference require massive computing power, driving explosive demand for high-end chips. NVIDIA's H100 and H200 AI chips are all manufactured on TSMC's 4nm and 5nm processes. Apple's M-series chips have also incorporated more AI capabilities.

Data from 2024 shows that advanced processes (7nm and below) already account for 69% of TSMC's total wafer revenue—and this share continues to rise.

### The Breakthrough of CoWoS Packaging Technology

AI chips need to process enormous volumes of data. A single chip is no longer enough; multiple chips must be "stacked" together. TSMC's CoWoS (Chip on Wafer on Substrate) packaging technology has become a critical technology of the AI era.

NVIDIA's H100 chip uses CoWoS technology to package the GPU die together with high-bandwidth memory. The technical barrier is extremely high, and currently only TSMC can mass-produce it.

## Competitors: Samsung's Pursuit and Intel's Struggles

### Samsung's Korean Offensive

Samsung is TSMC's most formidable competitor, pursuing a completely different strategy:

- **Vertical integration**: a full chain from memory and foundry services to smartphone manufacturing
- **Massive investment**: US$230 billion from 2022 to 2026
- **Government support**: South Korea's "K-Semiconductor Belt" initiative provides full backing

However, Samsung still lags behind TSMC in advanced process yield rates. In 2023, Samsung's 3nm process yield was approximately 60%, while TSMC's exceeded 90%. In an industry where precision is everything, a yield gap of this magnitude is the difference between night and day.

### Intel's Transformation Struggles

Intel was once the global leader in semiconductor technology, but it was stuck at the 10nm node for years, missing the best window to compete with TSMC.

In 2021, Intel launched its "IDM 2.0" strategy, aiming to simultaneously operate its own chip design business and a foundry services division. But by 2025, Intel Foundry had not secured any major customers. The most ironic twist: some of Intel's own high-end chips designed in-house are now manufactured by TSMC.

## Taiwan's Semiconductor Ecosystem: More Than Just TSMC

### The Advantage of a Complete Supply Chain

Taiwan's semiconductor competitiveness does not come from TSMC alone—it comes from the entire ecosystem:

**IC Design**: MediaTek (top 3 globally), Novatek, Realtek, Himax
**Foundry**: TSMC (No. 1 globally), UMC, VIS
**OSAT**: ASE (No. 1 globally), SPIL, King Yuan Electronics
**Equipment & Materials**: Nanya Technology, Winbond, AWSC

This complete industrial cluster creates powerful synergies. A single chip can go from design to completion by making a circuit around Taiwan, without the need for cross-border shipping.

### The Talent Magnet of Hsinchu Science Park

Established in 1980, the Hsinchu Science Park has developed over more than 40 years into one of the most important semiconductor clusters in the world. The park houses over 500 companies with more than 170,000 employees.

Even more important is talent mobility. An engineer might spend a few years at TSMC, then jump to MediaTek to design chips, and later move to ASE to handle packaging. This talent circulation continuously raises the technical level of the entire industry.

## Future Challenges: Physical Limits and Geopolitical Risks

### The End of Moore's Law

2nm is already approaching the physical limits of silicon-based semiconductors. To continue scaling down, new materials (such as carbon nanotubes or graphene) or new architectures (such as quantum computing or photonic computing) may be required.

TSMC's "angstrom-class" processes (sub-1nm) under development present exponentially increasing technical difficulty. The cost per transistor may actually rise, challenging traditional economic logic.

### The Double-Edged Sword of Geopolitics

TSMC's strategic position is both a shield and a risk. The US-China tech war places Taiwan in the middle, requiring a delicate balance between two superpowers:

- **US pressure**: requiring TSMC to comply with technology sanctions against China
- **China market**: still a significant revenue source for TSMC
- **Supply chain risk**: the danger of over-concentration of production in Taiwan

### The Talent War

The world is competing fiercely for semiconductor talent. The US CHIPS Act, the EU Chips Act, and Japan's semiconductor strategy all offer massive subsidies to attract talent. TSMC must remain competitive in the global talent market.

## Imagining 2030: Beyond Moore's Law

### A New Era of Heterogeneous Integration

When transistors can no longer be shrunk, the semiconductor industry is turning to "heterogeneous integration"—combining chips of different functions within a single package:

- **Processor chips**: responsible for computation
- **Memory chips**: responsible for storage
- **Sensor chips**: responsible for sensing
- **RF chips**: responsible for communication

TSMC's 3D IC technology allows chips to be "vertically stacked," packing more functionality into a limited area.

### The Challenge of Quantum Computing

Google, IBM, IonQ, and other companies are developing quantum computing, claiming it can solve complex problems beyond the reach of classical computers. If quantum computing truly breaks through, it could upend the entire semiconductor industry.

But TSMC is also positioning itself. The company is collaborating with IBM on manufacturing technologies for quantum computing, preparing for the next wave of technological revolution.

## Conclusion: The Eternal Race of the Technology Island

Starting from Morris Chang's gamble in 1987, TSMC has spent nearly 40 years transforming Taiwan into the lifeline of global technology. This is not just the success of one company—it is a strategic miracle for an entire island.

Today, when you send a message on an iPhone, ask ChatGPT a question, or activate Tesla's Autopilot, you are using Taiwanese technology. Sixty-nine percent of the world's most advanced chips are manufactured on this small island.

But how long can this advantage last? Technological leadership has no finish line—every day is a new beginning. TSMC must continue to invest, continue to innovate, and continue to lead in order to maintain its position in this eternal technology race.

For Taiwan, the semiconductor industry is not just an economic pillar—it is a survival strategy in the era of globalization. As geopolitics grows more complex, technological advantage may be the most powerful talisman a small nation can possess.

Taiwan's story is still being written. And the next chapter will be the challenge of angstrom-class processes.
