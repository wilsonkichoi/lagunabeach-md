> **30-Second Overview:** Taiwan is struck by an average of 3.5 typhoons per year, which contribute nearly 50% of the island's total rainfall and represent its most severe source of compound disasters. Research from Academia Sinica shows that by the end of this century, only 1 to 2 typhoons may hit Taiwan annually, but rainfall intensity will increase by 40% and wind speeds by 10%—a "fewer but stronger" new normal has already arrived. Taiwan possesses both world-leading typhoon observation technology and centuries of typhoon literary tradition. But typhoons bring more than data and poetry—they bring real human lives: 71-year-old Xiaolin survivor Luo Panchunmei watched 442 relatives vanish; former Central Weather Administration director Wu Derong publicly debunked the "divine guardian mountain" myth; and DOTSTAR project leader Wu Junjie flew into the eye of a typhoon to experience it firsthand. We can predict a typhoon's path, but not destiny.

"Xiaolin is gone!"

On the morning of August 8, 2009, 71-year-old Luo Panchunmei stood on her second-floor balcony and watched everything disappear. The mountain that had been there just the day before—Xiantou Mountain—had collapsed under days of relentless rain. A debris flow like a yellow dragon surged down from the peak, swallowing streets, houses, and the village she had lived in her entire life. Her 462 relatives vanished into the mountains. [^1]

> **✦** "I cried until I couldn't cry anymore. Now the tears come less often. We have to move forward." — Luo Panchunmei, looking back a decade after Typhoon Morakot

That cry was one of the most devastating moments in four centuries of Taiwan's struggle with typhoons.

## Four Centuries of Wind and Rain: From Takou's Storm Flags to Alishan's Two Thousand Millimeters

Taiwan's history with typhoons is far longer than the Republic of China.

During the Tongzhi era of the Qing Dynasty (circa 1865), the British hung storm warning flags atop the Maud Hospital roof in Takou (modern-day Kaohsiung)—Taiwan's earliest meteorological warning system. The colors of the flags represented different wind force levels, telling ships at a glance whether it was safe to set sail. [^6]

At that time in Penghu, an official named Sun Yuanheng witnessed the terror of a hurricane firsthand. In 1705, he wrote _Song of the Hurricane_ (〈颶風歌〉), an 85-line poem recording the omens and devastating impact of the storm: "Autumn winds rise in a single night, the Hurricane Mother arrives from the west in a fury." [^35] Two hundred years later, his verses are still read anew before every typhoon.

Fast forward to July 31, 1996. Typhoon Herb struck Taiwan, and Alishan recorded 1,094.5 mm of rainfall in a single day—equivalent to Taipei's "entire year" of rain, dumped in one day. [^5] It was the first such event since the weather station was established in 1933. Years later, PTT forum users recalled: "Typhoon Herb flooded the entire first floor of our home." Another wrote: "Most of my father's life work—his fish ponds and farmland—was swept away." Herb was called "the 921 of typhoons"—urban and rural, every industry, all of Taiwan suffered together. Total losses were approximately NT$25.3 to 30 billion. [^5]

Five years later, in September 2001, Typhoon Nari lingered over Taiwan for 49 hours along an erratic path. The Taipei Weather Station recorded 425 mm of rain in a single day—a 105-year record. [^2] The Taipei Metro Bannan Line was completely shut down, with all 16 stations and the depot fully flooded. Former Bannan Line section chief Hu Zongli fled with the keys and several hundred thousand in operating cash, only to find all 12 stations under his management submerged overnight. [^2] Residents of Bishan Village in Neihu had spent a year pushing a recycling program; the typhoon brought 150,000 tons of garbage that completely paralyzed the entire recycling system. [^2]

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> Taiwanese typhoon memories are etched into everyday life on a deeper level. Thirty years ago, mothers would fill bathtubs with water the night before a typhoon, stock up on dry batteries and candles, and grandmothers would rush to the night market to hoard food. After the power went out, the whole family would cook a pot of canned mackerel tomato sauce noodles—anyone who grew up in the '70s and '80s remembers that taste, along with the evenings of trading instant noodle flavors with neighbors. [^17] As infrastructure improved, younger generations no longer experience water and power outages, and this generation's memories are fading.

## The "Divine Guardian Mountain" Myth: Shield or Pressurizer?

Whenever a typhoon approaches from the east, Taiwanese people instinctively look toward the Central Mountain Range, averaging over 3,000 meters in height. It is colloquially called the "divine guardian mountain," credited with weakening typhoons and protecting the western half of the island.

Former Central Weather Administration forecast center director Wu Derong has publicly refuted this claim many times. [^3]

> "If Taiwan were flat, the rainfall brought by Morakot would have been 'a world of difference.' It is precisely because the towering terrain forces warm, moist air to rise that extreme rainfall is unleashed on the windward side."

Scientific data supports his judgment. Typhoon Herb produced a three-day cumulative rainfall of 1,994 mm on Alishan—nearly two thousand millimeters. Morakot's total rainfall on Alishan exceeded 3,000 mm, setting a historical record. [^5] These numbers were not "blocked by the divine mountain" but rather "squeezed out by it."

> **✦** "Without the Central Mountain Range, Typhoon Herb would never have dropped 2,000 millimeters of rain on Alishan."

When typhoon circulation descends on the leeward side of the mountains, it often triggers hot, dry foehn winds. Taitung and Taichung regularly see temperatures spike after typhoons, causing severe crop damage. [^5]

[^3]: [Citing Morakot: Meteorologist Wu Derong says the Central Mountain Range is absolutely not a "divine guardian mountain"](https://tw.news.yahoo.com/%E8%88%89%E8%8E%AB%E6%8B%89%E5%85%8B%E7%82%BA%E4%BE%8B-%E6%B0%A3%E8%B1%A1%E5%B0%88%E5%AE%B6%EF%BC%9A%E4%B8%AD%E5%A4%AE%E5%B1%B1%E8%84%88%E7%B5%95%E9%9D%9E%E8%AD%B7%E5%9C%8B%E7%A5%9E%E5%B1%B1-015829806.html) — Yahoo News 2016 report, with Wu Derong using Morakot to explain how orographic rainfall amplifies precipitation.

This pattern—"the west is grateful, the mountains bear the brunt"—is itself a microcosm of class disparity, much like the typhoon day-off system: the same storm exacts vastly different costs depending on region, industry, and social position.

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> The title "divine guardian mountain" is fundamentally the perspective of western plain residents. For the mountainous windward areas, the Central Mountain Range acts as a pressurizer—wind is compressed into water and poured onto the windward slopes. This "west grateful, mountains bear the brunt" distribution is itself a microcosm of class disparity.

## Fewer but Stronger: The New Normal of Typhoons Under Climate Change

Statistics from 1951 to 2023 show that six years had zero typhoons forming before May. From 2020 to 2022, Taiwan recorded three consecutive years with no typhoon landfalls. [^1]

But behind the numbers lies a more alarming trend.

A collaboration between Academia Sinica's Research Center for Environmental Changes (RCEC) and National Taiwan Normal University used cloud-resolving models to simulate future scenarios: [^1] By mid-century (2040–2059), the number of typhoons affecting Taiwan will drop to 3–4 per year; by the end of the century (2080–2099), there may be only 1–2.

Frequency declines, but intensity surges.

[^1]: [Climate report warns: Typhoons hitting Taiwan by end of century may number only 1–2 per year, but with stronger winds and rain](http://www.cna.com.tw/news/ahel/202405080408.aspx) — CNA 2024 report, with Academia Sinica's RCEC using the HiRAM cloud-resolving model to simulate typhoon trends over the coming century.

The study also found that 6.5% of Morakot's extreme rainfall was amplified by anthropogenic climate change. [^1] In other words, without global warming, Morakot's rainfall might not have been so extreme. This is empirical evidence directly linking climate change to a single disaster event.

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> This data flips Taiwan's disaster preparedness thinking on its head. In the past, we allocated resources on the premise that "a typhoon could come any year." But going forward, Taiwan will face a polarized challenge: "in low-typhoon years, there's nothing to prepare for; in high-typhoon years, the events are extreme." When a typhoon does arrive, it brings an extreme event requiring a full year's worth of preparation—not just a few days of heavy rain.

## The NT$31.5 Billion Cost: Class Fractures Behind the Typhoon Day Off

In Taiwan, typhoons are not just natural disasters—they have also spawned a unique "typhoon day off" culture.

The system's origin lies in a tragedy. On July 30, 2001, during moderate Typhoon Toraji, teacher Xu Bilan of Qingshan Elementary School in Changhu County died after accidentally falling into a drainage ditch while protecting her students. President Chen Shui-bian personally visited her memorial, and the tragedy prompted the government to seriously reconsider decision-making standards for typhoon days. [^22] In 2013, the original operational guidelines were formally renamed the "Operational Guidelines for Suspension of Work and Classes Due to Natural Disasters," expanding the typhoon day off to cover various types of natural disasters. [^22]

> **✦** "Each day of work and class suspension costs over NT$31 billion."

This figure originated from a 2005 op-ed by former Environment Minister Peng Chi-ming, later recalculated by San San Chamber of Commerce chairman Lin Po-feng using 2023 GDP data, yielding a net loss of approximately NT$31.5 billion per day. [^4] The calculation subtracts output from continuous-operation industries like semiconductors and domestic demand markets, reflecting the true economic cost of a complete shutdown.

But this number itself conceals an injustice.

A yes123 job bank survey of 1,330 workers found that 81% had been required to work during typhoon days, with 65% pressured by supervisors. [^6] Wholesale and retail (88.6%), healthcare and biotech (86.7%), food and hospitality (85.7%), and agriculture, fisheries, and livestock (83.3%) were the five industries with the highest rates of showing up regardless of weather. [^6]

The more critical gap lies between classes. An FTNN News survey showed that 53.5% of workers received full pay, but 37.7% received no pay at all. [^7] Approximately 70% of workers in traditional industries had no protective measures. [^6] Nearly 800,000 migrant workers are concentrated in manufacturing and agriculture—precisely the industries with the highest attendance rates. Those on monthly salaries who receive no pay during suspensions see a direct impact on remittances sent home, and the constraints of employment contracts make it common for migrant workers to "dare not take leave." [^8]

96% of workers support legislating the typhoon day off as a paid holiday, but the Ministry of Labor has delayed修法 on the grounds that it "could severely impact essential services." [^6]

Academic research reveals another dimension: typhoon day-off decisions are deeply influenced by political considerations. A quantitative analysis covering 35 typhoon events from 2005 to 2015 found that, on average, each correctly granted day off increased vote share in re-election by 2%. [^5] Correct decisions boost re-election odds, while incorrect decisions carry no significant electoral penalty—explaining the political game of "waiting for neighboring counties and cities to announce first." [^5]

## The Storm Chasers: From Drones to Satellites, How Taiwan Stares Into the Eye of a Typhoon

If Luo Panchunmei's story represents the destructive power of typhoons, then Professor Wu Junjie's story represents humanity's courage to face disaster head-on.

Wu Junjie is a chair professor in the Department of Atmospheric Sciences at National Taiwan University and the principal investigator of the _Dropsonde Observation for Typhoon Surveillance near the Taiwan Region_ (DOTSTAR, or "Storm Chaser Project"). Launched in 2002, it was Asia's first large-scale typhoon research program. [^9]

On September 1, 2003, Typhoon Dujuan—the team's first official "storm chase." By the end of 2012, they had completed observation missions for 49 typhoons across 64 sorties, deploying 1,051 dropsondes over 334 total flight hours. [^9] This first-hand data reduced 24-to-72-hour typhoon track forecast errors by an average of 20%. [^9]

Typhoon Sinlaku (2008) featured a rare double eyewall structure. The Storm Chaser Project flew for three consecutive days; the entire multinational team flew for seven straight days. [^9]

> "I grew up in Taitung. I've chased them. You first feel the north wind, because typhoons rotate counterclockwise. Then suddenly there's no wind—that means you're in the eye. A few minutes later the south wind picks up—that means the eye has passed over you."

[^9]: ["Storm Chaser Project" at 20! Wu Junjie on Asia's first large-scale typhoon research program](https://scitechvista.nat.gov.tw/Article/C000003/detail?ID=ab691668-650c-4ffc-be83-866054a3e029) — Ministry of Science and Technology Sci-Tech Vista, a complete record of the Storm Chaser Project from its first flight into Typhoon Dujuan in 2003 through its twentieth year in 2023.

The Storm Chaser Project's accolades span the globe: the 2009 National Science Council "50 Scientific Achievements" award, selection as one of _CommonWealth Magazine_'s "100 New Lights of Taiwan" in 2010, and recognition by the UN WMO THORPEX decade-long program in 2014. [^9]

But storm chasing is only part of Taiwan's typhoon observation picture.

In 1998, Taiwan became the first country in the world to use the Aerosonde unmanned aircraft to observe typhoons. [^10] On October 16, 2001, it successfully penetrated the outer core region of Typhoon Haiyan for the first time. After the US banned exports in September 2001, Taiwan became the last country to possess and operate the Aerosonde. [^10]

The FORMOSAT-7 (Formosa Satellite 7) constellation, launched on June 25, 2019, elevated the observation perspective from "within the stratosphere" to "space." [^11] These six small satellites, developed in cooperation with NOAA, form the world's first operational radio occultation constellation focused on tropical and subtropical regions. They provide at least 4,000 to 5,000 atmospheric profile data points daily, with approximately 80% of the data penetrating below 1 kilometer in altitude—double the capability of the older FS3/COSMIC system. [^11]

## From Qing Dynasty Poets to AI: How Taiwan Expresses Typhoons

In Taiwan, typhoons have never been merely meteorological phenomena. They are also poetry, legend, and art.

During the Kangxi era of the Qing Dynasty (1705), Penghu official Sun Yuanheng wrote _Song of the Hurricane_ (〈颶風歌〉), an 85-line poem recording the terror of a hurricane. His contemporary Zheng Yongxi wrote in his poem _Hurricane_ (〈颶風〉): "Most heart-wrenching is the nearing harvest—the fields' grain may yet wither." [^35] When autumn typhoons arrive, farmers' harvests are threatened—people three hundred years ago worried about the same things people worry about today.

In Taiwanese literary history, Xiao Jizong (1915–1996) was a rare classical poet who took typhoons as his subject. In 1961, he wrote _A Playful Composition as Typhoon Pomela Passes_ (〈波蜜娜颱風過境戲賦〉), depicting Typhoon Pomela of 1961 in the classical form of a Yuan dynasty sanqu suite. [^36] The language is humorous and lively, weaving modern terms like "observatory" and "harbor of refuge" into ancient musical forms—the first dialogue between classical form and modern disaster.

In contemporary art, Lin Quanju's 2002 _Typhoon_ series uses piles of rice as its central image, depicting the cycle of farmers battling typhoons, typhoons destroying crops, and regeneration afterward. [^37] This collection, held by the Kaohsiung Museum of Fine Arts, replaces tragic accusation with "rebirth after destruction and the sentiment of human coexistence with nature." [^37]

_The Island of Great Winds_ (《大風之島》), which won Best Documentary at the 2025 Taipei Film Festival, uses "wind" as a metaphor for the Losheng Sanatorium preservation movement. [^38] The theme song _Dafengzi_ (〈大風子〉), composed by Wang Yujun, goes: "Losheng, Losheng / Branches stripped in protest / Yet broken branches still bloom." [^38] Wind, a symbol of natural disaster, extends into a metaphor for resilience in social movements.

## The Tribe's Weather Station: Indigenous Typhoon Wisdom

Modern meteorology is not the only way Taiwan predicts typhoons.

In the Shan-shen (Kakanami) Village of Laiyi Township, Pingtung County, Paiwan elders observe natural phenomena to judge weather changes. [^12] When village elders see stream water turning muddy and carrying soil, they alert the village: the mountains behind may collapse.

They have many more methods:

A rainbow appearing in the direction of the rising sun means the typhoon will weaken; a rainbow in the direction of the setting sun means a typhoon is coming. Large numbers of crashes crawling onto land, ant nests migrating en masse, earthworms surfacing in great numbers—these are all signals that a typhoon or earthquake is approaching. [^12]

During Typhoon Morakot in 2009, members of Shan-shen (Kakanami) Village in Taitung County noticed the stream water turning muddy, warned of landslide risk, and evacuated the entire village in time. [^12] This traditional wisdom became the last safety net in modern disaster management.

The Amis people of the Fengbin Harbor Tribe in Hualien County have their own sea-reading wisdom. Before a typhoon strikes, northerly winds blow; after it passes, southerly winds prevail. [^12] If black rocks are covered by large waves, it means the typhoon will sweep across the area. In the mythology of the Amis Matasping Tribe, the male god Sawarwar spins his body wildly and the female god Harongheng shouts loudly—they transform into wind, creating the destructive power and terrifying howl of the typhoon. [^13]

Among the Tao people of Orchid Island, place names encode disaster risk knowledge. "Ji-Rako a Poas" denotes a large-scale landslide area; "Ji-Igang" marks a flood danger zone. [^12] The Tao's semi-underground traditional architecture outperformed modern concrete houses during Morakot and Typhoon Tembin. [^12]

National Chengchi University professor Kuan Ta-wei's research notes that while indigenous traditional weather prediction is less precise than modern instruments, it reflects wisdom born from long-term observation of nature and coexistence with the environment. [^14] In edge cases where modern forecasts are inaccurate or delayed, this knowledge may become the last safety net.

## Our Home, Our Rescue: The Community Disaster Prevention Network That Grew from Morakot

The lessons of Typhoon Morakot taught Taiwan not only how to "predict" but also how to "respond."

Since 2010, the Water Resources Agency of the Ministry of Economic Affairs has promoted autonomous flood-prevention communities, establishing 335 by the end of 2015. [^15] The core philosophy of these communities is "our home, our rescue"—every neighborhood organizes its own early warning and rescue network.

Yilan City's Meizhou Community demonstrated the effectiveness of this model during Typhoon Soudelor in 2015. The patrol and early warning group discovered a dam at risk of breaching; emergency notification prevented the disaster from escalating. [^15] Keelung City's Dingxiang Village successfully executed a preventive evacuation during Typhoon Nesat in 2022. [^16] In Yunlin County's Huwei Town, when Typhoon Danas struck in 2025, a 32-member disaster prevention patrol team immediately went out to inspect and clear fallen trees as soon as the winds subsided. [^17]

Research by National Pingtung University of Science and Technology associate professor Wang Shitu documented the participation patterns of civil organizations after Morakot: 32 civil groups participated in reconstruction, spanning four areas: community mutual care, community function restoration, livelihood reconstruction, and community self-directed development. [^18] The study's conclusion: "Civil organizations are a force not to be underestimated in government disaster relief work." [^18]

## Fifteen Years After Xiaolin: What Typhoons Taught Taiwan

2024 marked the fifteenth anniversary of Typhoon Morakot.

Pan Yuanming, chairman of the Xiaolin Community Development Association, returned to the memorial shrine. He changed the ceremonial flowers from sunflowers to chrysanthemums—symbolizing longevity, conveying the ancestors' wish that their descendants live well. [^1]

> "Because it was a typhoon day, everyone was afraid. They didn't dare come back here."

Wulipu Permanent Houses, 90 homes built by the Red Cross for Xiaolin villagers, have an occupancy rate of only 30–40%. Livelihoods are hard to sustain, and the younger generation continues to leave. [^19] But some young people have chosen a different path.

Wang Minliang (Ah-Liang), secretary-general of the Riguang Xiaolin Community Development Association, founded the Da Man Wu Dance Troupe in 2011, leading Xiaolin people through grief with ancient songs and dance. The troupe received recognition at the 2019 Golden Melody Awards for Traditional Arts and Music. [^1] Five-year-old Pan Sichi has been listening to her mother sing ancient songs since she could remember: "I just kept listening, kept listening—listening to Mom sing, and I learned!" [^1]

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> The story of Xiaolin reveals a profound paradox: modernization has moved more people away from risk zones, but cultural roots have been severed in the process. The Da Man Wu Dance Troupe's attempt to "plant home back" through ancient songs is answering a more fundamental question: when the physical hometown can never be rebuilt, where should the cultural hometown be established?

## Coexisting with Uncertainty

In 2019, the tenth anniversary of Typhoon Morakot, Weng Ruiqi rebuilt a family with neighbor Yang Meilu, who had also lost loved ones, in the Wulipu Permanent Houses. He said: "Life has to go on." [^20]

[^20]: [Ruthless Waters, Human Love: Small Mercies Amid Misfortune for Xiaolin Survivors](https://www.cna.com.tw/news/aloc/201908070236.aspx) — CNA 2019 report on the tenth anniversary of Morakot, documenting the rebuilding stories of survivors like Weng Ruiqi in the Wulipu Permanent Houses.

Perhaps this sentence also captures the attitude of this island toward typhoons.

From Sun Yuanheng writing _Song of the Hurricane_ in Penghu during the Qing Dynasty to the Weather Administration using six AI models to generate 18 typhoon tracks in 2025; from the storm warning flags at Takou Harbor to FORMOSAT-7 reading 5,000 atmospheric data points daily from space; from Alishan's 2,000 mm of torrential rain to Xiaolin survivors learning to shed no more tears—four centuries on, Taiwan's understanding of typhoons is undergoing a deep transformation.

We are moving from "passive disaster avoidance" to "active adaptation." From "fighting typhoons" to "coexisting with uncertainty."

But this transformation is not yet complete.

335 autonomous disaster prevention communities cover only a fraction of Taiwan's neighborhoods. 37.7% of frontline workers labor without pay on typhoon days. 96% of workers support legislating paid typhoon days off, but修法 remains stalled. The new normal of fewer but stronger typhoons has already arrived, yet the distribution of disaster preparedness resources still overlaps with social class.

Robert Swinhoe wrote the word "rare" in Tamsui 160 years ago, describing a bird. [^21] Today, the typhoons we face are fiercer and more unpredictable than anything he could have seen.

[^21]: [100 Questions About Typhoons](https://www.cwa.gov.tw/V8/C/K/Encyclopedia/typhoon/index.html) — Official科普 column of the Central Weather Administration, Ministry of Transportation and Communications, with comprehensive explanations of typhoon formation, classification, forecasting, and history.

> **✦** "Without the Central Mountain Range, Typhoon Herb would never have dropped 2,000 millimeters of rain on Alishan."

Typhoon track forecast error has shrunk from 172 kilometers to 57 kilometers. But the moment Luo Panchunmei stood on her second floor watching 462 relatives vanish reminds us of one thing:

We can predict wind and rain, but not destiny.
