> **30-second overview:** In 2017, Yahoo TV launched Huni, marking the start of Taiwan's VTuber industry. From early corporate technology experiments, to over 3,000 Taiwanese VTubers having debuted by 2025, Taiwan has built a mature "Taiwan wave" virtual entertainment ecosystem. This has become a core medium bridging Gen Z and traditional culture, driving a Super Chat economy in the tens of millions of NT dollars — while individual creators still face high costs and high turnover. Olympic athletes have collaborated with them, and PTS's Taiwanese-language channel has created programming around them. [^1][^13][^14]

On July 7, 2017, a virtual girl named "Huni" with bright-yellow twin tails and tiny fangs officially debuted as a host on Yahoo TV, opening the curtain on Taiwan's VTuber industry [^4]. (Her fan page had launched on June 30.) At the time, it had been only six months since Japan's pioneer VTuber Kizuna AI first appeared, yet Taiwan was already throwing corporate resources into this experiment in the blending of real and virtual [^4]. "Huni is not a character — she is our vision of the future of media," the production team declared at the time. Nine years on, that vision has borne fruit: by 2026, Taiwanese VTubers (hereafter "Taiwan Vs") are no longer a self-enclosed niche community — they are cultural standard-bearers whose collaborations can involve Olympic athletes and whose appeal is enough for PTS's Taiwanese-language channel to create dedicated programming [^9]. By the latest data, top-ranking Taiwan V channels hold subscriber counts steadily in the tens of thousands to hundreds of thousands, led by names like Lee Ting (540,000 subscribers) and Almond Mil (440,000) [^1][^7].

## From Corporate Experiment to the Rise of the Indie Creator

Huni was born with a heavy corporate imprint. Yahoo TV needed a new, interactive form of host, and a virtual character's malleability — combined with the impossibility of "getting into a scandal" — fit the bill perfectly. Her character design was co-created by **Studio Reals** and **SAFE HOUSE T**; the production team used the then-cutting-edge Live2D technology to let flat-image artwork track the host's facial expressions and movements in real time [^4].

In 2018, Huni conducted an exclusive interview with director Mamoru Hosoda, becoming Taiwan's first VTuber to interview an internationally prominent animation director. She also appeared alongside Taipei Mayor Ko Wen-je to promote the World University Games, and in 2020 she appeared on screen at the same time as President Tsai Ing-wen on a show hosted by Tang Chi-yang. These "firsts" established an important precedent for Taiwanese VTubers: virtual characters can step out of the ACG (anime, comics, games) sphere and enter mainstream media and political spaces [^4].

> 💡 **Did you know?**
> Huni placed 118th in a global VTuber survey — ranking in the top tier among more than 4,000 VTubers worldwide — and has been called an "island ruler" by her fans. [^4]

However, the corporate-controlled model has its limits. In January 2020, the first-generation Huni (α Huni) "departed for the McIntosh Star" and ended her hosting duties, replaced by β Huni. In August 2023, β Huni also announced an extended hiatus. An IP's lifecycle seems always constrained by shifts in corporate strategy [^4].

As corporate IPs gradually faded, the industry's center of gravity shifted toward decentralized independent creators (indie). The development of Taiwanese VTubers can be divided into three key phases:

- **Early exploration (2017–2018):** Pioneered by Yahoo TV's Huni and Auris, opening the initial period of corporate-operated VTubers [^13].
- **Proliferation and corporate entry (2019–2021):** High-popularity indie creators like Almond Mil rose to prominence. Simultaneously, professional agencies like Spring Fish Creative and Meridian Plan moved in, establishing trainee systems similar to those of the entertainment industry [^12][^13].
- **Rapid growth and localization (2022–present):** Explosive growth in VTuber numbers. By 2025, over 3,000 Taiwanese VTubers have debuted, displaying high levels of community interaction and local character [^13]. However, the number actually active is far lower (a few hundred with stable followings), and many creators go on hiatus or "reincarnate" shortly after debuting, reflecting the industry's high turnover and its "sweatshop" realities [^14].

> 📝 Curator's Note: As corporate IPs gradually fade, the free spirits of indie creators have become the most resilient lifeline of the Taiwan V industry. Yet the high turnover and the hidden "sweatshop" reality also remind us that this path is far from smooth.

## Taiwanese Language Revitalization and the Cultural Path of Localization

When the mainstream assumed virtual idols were simply copies of Japanese culture, a group of Taiwanese creators chose the Taiwanese language (Tâi-gí) as their cultural fulcrum [^5]. In 2023, Chan Fang-yu, who had a background in the Department of Taiwanese Literature at NTNU, founded "Shin-siau" (神霄) and launched a VTuber team whose entire interaction takes place in Taiwanese. In her videos she introduces herself using Taiwanese romanization: "Tak-ke hó! Guá sī Li̍t-sim. Sī 18 hè Chúi-kan-á tsō ê bí-siàu-lú." (Hello everyone! I'm Rikushin. I'm an 18-year-old Aquarius beauty.) This was unprecedented in the Taiwanese VTuber world [^5]. Chan's motivation was straightforward: "Let VTubers speak Taiwanese." She believed that rather than forcing young people to learn Taiwanese through traditional coercive methods, it was better to use VTubers — a new entertainment format — to let audiences encounter Taiwanese culture naturally through entertainment. Shin-siau's VTubers discuss current events in Taiwanese, teach audiences how to say "emotional manipulation" in Taiwanese (khí-moo khà-iû), and even explain the cultural differences between formal banquets (辦桌) and running buffets (流水席) [^5].

This localization effort received government support. The Shin-siau team secured NT$900,000 in funding through the Ministry of Education's U-start Innovation and Entrepreneurship program, becoming that year's sole student startup selected from NTNU. They also collaborated with PTS's Taiwanese-language channel on programming, inviting the well-known VTuber Furry Rat to participate in the "Voice of Life" program — a single day's viewer count that climbed from single digits to 2,000. Additionally, the Shin-siau team partnered with Chunghwa Post to promote a mobile app and a stamp-collecting activity, bringing Taiwanese culture to a broader audience [^9][^13].

> 📝 Curator's Note: When technology becomes a vehicle for culture, Live2D is not merely an animation technology — it becomes a digital ark for an ancient language, carrying the possibility of cultural transmission and innovation.

## Diverse Forms: The Local Appeal of Animal VTubers

Within the Taiwan V ecosystem, VTubers with purely animal appearances form a presence that cannot be ignored. Compared with the emphasis on beautiful-girl/beautiful-boy personas, animal VTubers cut into the market with their harmless, approachable image [^8]. For example, the toucan VTuber **Caiki** not only sings in Taiwanese but has had an inflatable plush doll made, with hopes of meeting fans in a mascot costume in the future [^9]. Other examples — the bear character **Kuma**, the snowy owl **Hoshiba**, the overseas Chinese YouTuber **One Groundhog** — all showcase the creative range of Taiwanese VTubers in character design. This attempt to cross from the virtual into physical mascot costumes embodies Taiwan V creators' desire to break through the screen barrier and forge physical emotional connections with fans.

> 📝 Curator's Note: When "cute" is no longer the only answer, animal VTubers prove that approachability is the true pass for crossing dimensional barriers.

## Business Model: Emotional Economy and Cross-Domain Collaboration

The economic model of Taiwanese VTubers shows features markedly different from Japan's. In Japan, major agencies like Hololive and Nijisanji rely mainly on merchandise sales as their primary revenue (40–60% of income), with live events as a secondary source. But Taiwanese VTubers rely primarily on Super Chat donations and channel membership subscriptions, with merchandise sales relatively limited [^12]. Japan's VTuber giants — Hololive, Nijisanji — also command enormous fan bases and streaming data in Taiwan, and their technology and style exert a deep influence on the Taiwanese market, creating competitive-collaborative dynamics of "Taiwan V resistance" or hybrid persona settings.

This difference reflects the realities of market scale and consumption habits. According to Playboard statistics, in 2023, VTubers occupied 42 of Taiwan YouTube's top 100 Super Chat earners, with total Super Chat income reaching NT$33.52 million — 38% of the overall total [^12]. While this represents a scale of tens of millions in a single year, spread across hundreds to thousands of VTubers, individual income remains limited. Furthermore, YouTube's platform cut of roughly 30%, plus agency revenue sharing, means that many indie VTubers earn only a few thousand to tens of thousands of NT dollars per month — far below the imagined "more than a salaried employee" [^14]. Crowdfunding has therefore become an important means for Taiwanese VTubers to break through funding constraints.

For example, **Mizuki**'s 3D modeling crowdfunding campaign reached NT$8.74 million, and **Morisen Suzu**'s 3D modeling and live concert crowdfunding reached NT$8.68 million — both far exceeding their original targets. Backer founder Lin Ta-han observed: "VTuber operations require audience participation, which is similar in nature to crowdfunding. Any Taiwanese VTuber with a certain scale will use crowdfunding as a way to grow." [^12]

The social influence of Taiwan Vs is also demonstrated through diverse cross-domain collaborations:

- **Spring Fish Creative:** Specializes in IP storytelling, producing series like _Near-Extinction Team_ and _Age of Monsters_; its VTubers served as virtual ambassadors for the Paris Olympics [^15].
- **Meridian Plan:** Takes a premium approach; its **Mizuki** is one of Taiwan's highest Super Chat earners [^12].
- **Mojo Entertainment:** Uses a "budget, high-volume" strategy; its first-generation _EXITUS_ and second-generation _MeloNyx_ together have 13 members, building a trainee system similar to the entertainment industry [^12].
- **PTS Taiwanese-language channel:** Partnered with **Furry Rat** on the "Voice of Life" program [^9].
- **Nan-I Book Enterprise:** Launched its own VTubers and virtual classrooms, bringing virtual streamers into the educational space.

On the technology supply side, companies providing motion capture equipment — including **Ozora Electronics** and **Future Production** — as well as HTC VIVE Originals' metaverse platform **Beatday**, which allows VTubers to hold concerts in virtual reality, round out the ecosystem. Additionally, organizations such as the **Taiwan Virtual Influencer Association** have been established to promote industry standards and technology sharing, giving this "Taiwan wave" greater scale [^13].

> 📝 Curator's Note: In Taiwan, VTubers are not only entertainment — they are the intersection of culture and commerce, creating value through emotion and expanding boundaries through cross-domain collaboration. Yet the "sweatshop" reality behind them, the high costs, and the competitive-collaborative relationship with Japanese giants are all worth deeper reflection.

## Challenges and New Trends

The development of Taiwan's VTuber industry has not been smooth sailing. Talent loss is the biggest challenge: of the top ten Super Chat earners in 2021, **Hirahirako** and **No. 15** have graduated, and **Hoshimi Haruka** has left the original agency. Ideological conflicts and revenue-sharing disputes between agencies and creators lead to recurring waves of "graduations" [^14].

Technical barriers and funding requirements also limit the industry's scale. A character model (V-skin) and Live2D modeling typically cost between NT$100,000 and NT$200,000; 3D modeling can run as high as NT$500,000 [^14]. Many VTubers struggle to gain visibility, with an average career spanning only about six months after debut [^14]. All of this reflects the "sweatshop" side of the Taiwan V industry. Additionally, the "spending culture" (pressure after high-target crowdfunding campaigns), meme usage and Gen Z retention, and shifts in YouTube algorithm and Super Chat tax policy all pose operational challenges to VTubers.

The industry outlook remains optimistic, however. National Tsing Hua University Taiwan Literature Institute assistant professor Wang Wei-chih points out that VTubers possess three overlapping qualities: "the recognizability of an anime character, the real-time interactivity of a streamer, and the emotional connection of an idol," and that Taiwanese creators excel especially in "local language use, approachability, and meme deployment" [^13]. This localization advantage has also been recognized in international markets. **Furry Rat**, while residing in Japan, chooses Spring Fish Creative for legal and business support; **Almond Mil** has fans in both countries through a Taiwan-Japan hybrid persona. Taiwanese VTubers are not copies of the Japanese model; they have developed a distinctive form suited to local culture and market by building on the core concept [^13]. Taiwanese VTubers' cross-platform performance on Bilibili, Twitch, and elsewhere, as well as the return or dual-base activity of overseas Taiwanese creators, also strengthens their international connections.

New variables have emerged in 2025–2026: AI technology is deeply involved, with **MorphusAI** launching an AI VTuber brand that uses generative AI for real-time interaction [^6]. Although the scale of AI VTubers, their competitive/collaborative relationship with human VTubers, and technical limitations (such as real-time interaction quality and emotional connection differences) remain to be observed, the potential is substantial. At the same time, the spread of 5G networks has made HTC VIVE's Beatday metaverse concerts possible, allowing audiences to enter the venue as avatars, achieving a symbiosis of real and virtual [^13][^14]. A January 2026 _Taipei Times_ report noted that VTubers have become "the most effective cross-domain medium," unconstrained by physical bodies and real-world scenes, capable of instantly bridging different fields and demographics [^13]. Wang Wei-chih predicts that "brands self-building VTubers" will become a standard configuration for managing digital communities [^13]. Furthermore, the increasing accessibility of Live2D and 3D technology, as well as the development of open-source tools, have substantially lowered the barrier to entry for indie VTubers, becoming a key driver of the indie creator boom.

> 📝 Curator's Note: When virtual idols face real-world challenges, AI and the metaverse are opening new chapters for the Taiwan V industry — blurring the boundary between real and virtual, and testing the adaptability of creators and the industry alike.

## Conclusion: Guarding What Is Real Within the Virtual

From Huni's lone experiment in 2017 to the cultural landscape jointly constructed by more than 3,000 VTubers in 2025, Taiwan has proven that virtual technology can carry the most local of emotions. When a virtual girl speaking in Taiwanese about current events can draw thousands of simultaneous viewers, what we see is no longer just technological progress — it is the possibility of cultural transmission and innovation. The exterior is virtual; the soul is real. In an age when everything can be virtualized, Taiwan V creators are defining what "modern Taiwan" means in their own way.
