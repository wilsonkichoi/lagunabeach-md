# The Bamboo Hat

> **30-second overview:** Near Longdu Junior High School in Meinong, Kaohsiung, Lin Rong-chun has been shaving bamboo strips and weaving bamboo hats since his teens — over sixty years of work[^1]. Xinglin Township in Hsinchu County was once the headquarters of hand-woven bamboo hat production in northern Taiwan, with every household piling up moso bamboo and bamboo leaves in the street[^2]. During the peak years in Kenzi Village, Luzhu, Taoyuan, thirty households produced bamboo hats — over a hundred hats a day. When the Linkou brick kilns closed and nearby tea plantations shrank, the tea-picking workforce declined, and demand for bamboo hats fell sharply with it[^3]. In Dongshi, Taichung, the Hakka "libma" (笠嫲) hat uses bamboo shoot sheaths as the outer layer and dark-leaf bamboo strips for the inner frame — even the raw materials require specialized identification[^4]. In June 2024, the Legislative Yuan passed a third reading raising compensation for forgoing logging on indigenous reserve land to NT$60,000 per hectare — and 85% of Taiwan's moso bamboo comes from indigenous reserve land, meaning even the most upstream part of the supply chain is now structurally disrupted[^14][^15]. A Vietnamese coconut-leaf hat sells for under NT$70 on PChome[^16]; a hand-crafted bamboo hat costs more than ten times as much just in materials and labor. The hat has not disappeared from the fields. What has disappeared are the hands that make it — and the entire supply chain behind those hands.

## The Couple near Longdu Junior High

On March 12, 2017, blogger zoyo walked into an old house near Longdu Junior High School in Meinong, Kaohsiung, to visit a couple the local community called "the bamboo hat grandpa and grandma." The grandfather, Lin Rong-chun, was in his eighties, had started weaving bamboo hats in his teens, and by then had been doing it for over sixty years. His wife, Xiao Man-mei, handled the stitching. The two of them worked side by side on small stools in the living room[^1].

Lin Rong-chun shaped his bamboo strips using a special mold made of camphor wood — pressing thin strips around the mold in successive rings to define the form. This is the same technique used by a husband-and-wife pair in Fenglin, Hualien, whom Hakka TV in 2020 called "one of the last remaining" practitioners in their area[^5]. The Meinong couple were the same. In the photographs, you can see that the thumbnail on Lin Rong-chun's hand has been scored with dozens of fine grooves from the bamboo strips; beside Xiao Man-mei, a stack of bamboo leaves is pressed flat under a wooden board, waiting to be fitted into the inner frame.

Wikipedia's definition of the bamboo hat is brief: "a wide-brimmed hat woven from bamboo, conical in shape — the mature bamboo is split into strips to form the inner skeleton, and layers of bamboo leaves or strips are added and fixed with thread."[^6] That description takes ten seconds to write; making one hat takes hours.

By 2026, verifying whether the Longdu couple is still alive is not easy. Aside from zoyo's 2017 blog post, no mainstream media outlet has followed up on these two craftspeople — who were never officially registered as intangible cultural heritage preservers. Elsewhere in Meinong, the Guangjin Sheng paper umbrella shop, not far from Longdu, has accumulated extensive media coverage over twenty years because the second-generation craftsman, Lin Rong-jun, brought traditional oiled-paper umbrellas into a Jolin Tsai music video and a Louis Vuitton product line[^17]. **Whether a craft has a "cultural-creative crossover" determines whether it has a subsequent archive of news coverage** — and traditional rough-work bamboo hat making almost never has one.

## Hat Face, Hat Frame, and Dark-Leaf Bamboo Strips: The Anatomy of a Hat

The National Cultural Memory Bank's documentation of the Hakka "libma" (大埔Hakka: lib maˇ) from Dongshi, Taichung, breaks this seemingly simple conical hat into two components: the **hat face** and the **hat frame**. The hat face uses bamboo shoot sheaths — the outer layer peeled from bamboo shoots — pressed flat, sun-dried, and layered on top. The hat frame is the skeleton, made from thin strips split from "dark-leaf bamboo" (烏葉竹). Secondary materials include cotton thread and bamboo rings; tools include a chopper, a bamboo-splitting bridge, and a blade guide[^4].

This is not artisanal aesthetics. Dark-leaf bamboo and moso bamboo have different elasticity and fiber lengths. Different bamboo species are used for the frame versus the outer leaf layer, and masters distinguish them by feel and experience. The difference between a rough-work hat and a fine-work hat is laid out plainly in the records of Kenzi Community in Luzhu, Taoyuan: rough-work hats came from the Qin and Chen families of Tugong Creek; fine-work hats came from the Li family of Chitukeng — **two families, two distinct technical lineages**[^3]. Rough-work frames have thicker, fewer-layered strips, a wider brim, and are suited to threshing grain in the open field. Fine-work hats have dense weaving, more layers of finishing, and are lighter on the head — better for tea-picking or prolonged outdoor labor.

The variation in form is not romantic regionalism. It is function driving craft. A collection card in the National Cultural Memory Bank for a hat in the National Pingtung University of Science and Technology's agricultural implements museum notes explicitly that forms differ by use and region[^7] — a hat for threshing grain can measure over 60 centimeters across and is impractical on a motorcycle; a hat for tea picking is a size smaller and more tightly woven so rain cannot drip through the gaps into the tea basket. The camphor wood mold itself comes in different sizes, and a master typically has three to five molds — ranging from one _chi_ two _cun_ (about 36 centimeters) to one _chi_ eight _cun_[^16].

Stitching the hat is a separate process. The cotton thread Xiao Man-mei uses was once a thick spun cotton; today it is mostly replaced with nylon thread, for a straightforward reason — nylon does not rot in wet seasons, extending the hat's lifespan from two years to five[^5]. Traditional waterproofing used tung oil, brushed onto the outer bamboo-sheath layer and, when dry, forming a thin, translucent yellowish shell. This is the same treatment used on Vietnam's nón lá, the same process shared across the rice-cultivation cultures of East and Southeast Asia[^18].

## From the Zhushan Craft Institute to a Documentary: Other Masters Who Must Be Named

Telling the full story of the bamboo hat requires looking beyond the couple in Meinong. Taiwan's bamboo weaving has a much longer lineage, which must be traced back to the Japanese colonial era.

**Around 1937, the Taiwan Governor-General's Office established the "Zhushan Bamboo Craft Institute" in Nantou County.** This was the institutional starting point for Taiwan's bamboo craft industry — according to a history compiled by the Taiwan Bamboo Association, Taiwan has more than a hundred years of bamboo use, but "industrial organization is only about 80 years old"[^25]. Master bamboo weaver Huang Tu-shan entered the institute at the age of fourteen in 1939 to study fine bamboo work; he is one of the few living witnesses from that era. Over more than fifty years, he has trained over a thousand students, including Qiu Jin-duan, Lin Xiu-feng, and Tu Su-ying[^26]. **The bamboo hat is not an isolated craft — it is a branch of Taiwan's entire bamboo-weaving ecosystem.** That ecosystem has an institutional trunk in the Huang Tu-shan lineage, but the bamboo hat branch — a "utilitarian goods" branch — has never had an institute comparable to his.

Moving south to Longqi (龍崎), Tainan: in 2014, NTD TV (New Tang Dynasty Television) filmed 86-year-old Chen Lian-qin (陳連親), whose hands were covered in calluses but whose movements making hats remained deft — "the initial work on one hat takes more than an hour." In the same shot, Chen Yu-zi says, "When I was a child watching cattle, I peeled bamboo leaves for my mother to weave." **Longqi in Tainan is not a Hakka community — it is a Hoklo (Southern Min) farming village.** The bamboo hat craft there has been practiced since the Qing dynasty, placing it alongside Meinong and Xinglin among Taiwan's three main rough-work bamboo hat production regions. Also in Longqi, the five-generation "Baizhu Garden" run by Zhang Jun-bo has repositioned itself around teaching workshops rather than production, with a bamboo garden of over a hundred species[^28] — a different preservation path: shifting from producer to educator.

Returning north to the Hakka Public Communication Foundation: the second season of the 2022 documentary series _Passing It On_ (傳、傳) filmed **Wu Jin-yun (吳進運) and Xu Bao-mei (徐寶妹)** of Miaoli, a husband-and-wife pair who have been weaving bamboo hats for over fifty years[^29]. The following episode in the same series filmed "the joyful straw-cape man," Zhang Tian-fu, who says on camera, "This is the last one I'll ever make"[^30] — the most frequently heard line in Taiwan's traditional craft world in the 2020s. Further east in Fuli, Hualien, NTD TV filmed Xu Gui-zhu in 2016, fifty years into her craft of hand-woven bamboo hats, **selling each one for just NT$150**[^31] — "because it's a craft my mother left me, and inheritance cannot be interrupted." The Ministry of Education's Youth Development Administration listed Xu Gui-zhu as a featured craftsperson in the "Youth Community Engagement Action 2.0" program — a course called "The Fashionable Bamboo Hat for Your Head" — having students learn to identify moso bamboo and then weave a traditional hat from scratch[^32].

Placing these names together: Huang Tu-shan (born 1926, Zhushan), Chen Lian-qin (born 1928, Longqi), Lin Rong-chun (born around 1932, Meinong), Xu Gui-zhu (born around 1940, Fuli), Wu Jin-yun and Xu Bao-mei (born after 1940, Miaoli) — **Taiwan's "last generation" of bamboo hat masters was born in the 1920s to 1940s.** Those still living in 2026 are mostly between 85 and 100 years old. The generational gap is not approaching — it has already arrived.

## Xinglin, Kenzi, Meinong, Fenglin: A Contour Line Drawn Along Farmland

Xinglin Township in Hsinchu County "was once the headquarters of hand-woven bamboo hat production in northern Taiwan" — this is a direct quotation from the Council of Agriculture's publication _Rural Culture_ (農村與文化), June 2007. A journalist at the time described every household in the streets piled high with moso bamboo and bamboo leaves, elders and women bowed over them, several families producing five or six hats a day[^2].

The records of Kenzi Community Development Association in Luzhu, Taoyuan, are even more specific: "Before 1991, most residents made a living producing woven bamboo products. At peak, about thirty households in the village were engaged in hand production, with a daily output of over a hundred hats. But as Linkou brick kilns successively closed and the area of nearby tea gardens shrank (because tea-picking labor declined), demand for bamboo hats..." — the sentence trails off unfinished, because the association itself cannot say how the story ends[^3].

Placing these locations on a map reveals a striking line: Xinglin in Hsinchu, Kenzi in Luzhu, Taoyuan, Miaoli (where Yuanli's rush-grass hat weaving is a neighboring industry to bamboo hats)[^8], Dongshi in Taichung (the Hakka _libma_)[^4], Meinong in Kaohsiung (the couple near Longdu)[^1], Fenglin in Hualien (the "last remaining" couple reported by Hakka TV in 2020)[^5]. **This line nearly overlaps the distribution of Hakka communities across Taiwan** — but more precisely: it is Hakka communities where farming continued at significant scale. Hoklo farming villages also wore bamboo hats in the past. The National Museum of Taiwan History's collection piece 2002.005.0211 was collected from a Tainan rural area[^13], not a Hakka settlement. The National Cultural Memory Bank's records for Hoklo and Plains Indigenous agricultural implements all include bamboo hat forms[^7]; the difference is only in the degree of finish and material preference.

In other words: labeling the bamboo hat as "Hakka craft" is a strategic retrospective construction of the ethnic revival movements of the 1990s, not historical fact. **The reason a few elderly masters survived in Hakka communities until the 2020s is that agriculture in those areas — tea gardens, rice paddies, brick kilns — lasted comparatively longer.** Hoklo agricultural areas industrialized earlier, mechanized faster, and experienced the bamboo hat's decline earlier — leaving no one behind to make a final record.

## The Fracturing Bamboo Supply: On the Indigenous Logging Compensation Act

The disappearance of hat masters is the surface phenomenon. Deeper upstream lies the real problem: **the materials they need are no longer being harvested.**

According to data from the Forestry Bureau of the Council of Agriculture, 85% of Taiwan's moso bamboo comes from indigenous reserve land, primarily distributed across Fuxing District in Taoyuan, and Jianshi and Wufeng townships in Hsinchu County[^14]. On June 4, 2024, the Legislative Yuan passed the third reading of Article 6 of the _Act for Compensation of Logging Prohibition on Indigenous Reserve Land_, raising compensation from NT$30,000 per hectare to NT$60,000 per hectare[^15]. The legislative intent was to compensate indigenous landowners for losses caused by environmental protection restrictions on logging. But moso bamboo is a species that requires regular harvesting to remain healthy — if left uncut for three years, old culms collapse and new shoots cannot emerge, and the entire grove effectively becomes wasteland.

Cheng Yang-yi of National Central University, in a commentary published by the Environmental Information Center, put it sharply: "The more compensation, the more impoverishment" — after the increase, landowners are more inclined to collect compensation and not open their land for bamboo harvesting. Traditional indigenous logging crews lose their work; downstream bamboo weaving, bamboo utensils, oil-paper umbrella frames, and bamboo hat inner frames all lose their supply together[^15]. A group of young Atayal people organized the "Taoyuan City Fuxing Moso Bamboo Industry Development Association," advocating for the importance of periodic bamboo harvesting and developing experience programs and the "Qengay" Atayal bamboo accessory brand as a way forward[^19].

In April 2026, veteran journalist Chen Quan-xin wrote in a Hakka News column: "Every late spring, when wild ginger flowers open, that is when moso bamboo shoots push up through the soil..." — this Council of Hakka Affairs advisory committee member described the silent predicament of bamboo farmers in the Taoyuan–Hsinchu–Miaoli region: output declining year by year, young people not going up the mountains, large distributors' profits squeezed by Chinese dried bamboo imports[^20]. At the far downstream end of this supply chain are the bamboo strips in a hat master's hands.

**Master disappearance + bamboo supply fracture = dual structural extinction.** The Council of Hakka Affairs' official list of grant and subsidy programs includes entries such as "Hakka Cultural Renaissance Subsidy Guidelines" and "Promoting Hakka as an Official Language in Public Affairs Subsidy Guidelines"[^21] — but there is no comprehensive preservation program targeting a specific craft like the bamboo hat. The Ministry of Culture's registration of intangible cultural heritage in "bamboo weaving" has focused on individual artists: Lin Xiu-feng for fine bamboo weaving in Changhua[^11], Lin Huang-jiao for rush-grass weaving in Taitung[^12]. Individual preservation certificates exist — but **no one is protecting the entire supply chain.**

## Where Teaching Still Happens: A Living Map of Transmission Sites

The bamboo hat craft has not been formally registered by the Ministry of Culture as a national preservation technique in the way Lin Xiu-feng's fine-work bamboo weaving has been. Yet in the 2020s there are still scattered sites where the craft is kept alive:

- **Zhushan, Nantou | Huang Tu-shan Bamboo Weaving Studio:** Huang Tu-shan's son Huang Qi-xiang has inherited his father's craft, and the workshop is open by appointment from 09:00 to 12:00 daily[^26]. The National Taiwan Craft Research and Development Institute also offers "bamboo art workshop" weaving classes and has published _Introduction to Bamboo Object Weaving Techniques_ (Yang Zong-yu and You Kai-ting, 2020)[^33] — the most complete bilingual technical guide to the bamboo hat inner-frame process available.
- **Fuxing, Taoyuan | Luoma Bamboo Tribe:** Young Atayal people formed the Fuxing Moso Bamboo Industry Development Association, committed to preserving bamboo weaving culture and offering moso bamboo DIY experiences, alongside the "Qengay" bamboo accessory brand[^19]. The official "Explore North横" (北橫) tourism page positions this tribe as embodying "knowing bamboo is lasting happiness" — an elder's saying: "Wherever there are Atayal people, there is moso bamboo."
- **Dongshi, Taichung | Taichung Branch of the Forestry and Nature Conservation Agency:** The Dongshi Forestry Culture Park has hosted multiple bamboo-weaving experience courses since 2024, led by professional bamboo craft practitioners[^34], within the government's broader push for "domestic bamboo application and forest sustainability." The courses focus on small household items rather than complete hats.
- **Fuli, Hualien; Miaoli; Longqi, Tainan:** Xu Gui-zhu, Wu Jin-yun / Xu Bao-mei, Baizhu Garden's Zhang Jun-bo — the first two receive workshop students through the Ministry of Education's Youth Development Administration programs[^32]; the last emphasizes experience education[^28]. All follow the model of "final-generation masters + transition to experience workshops" — **not true occupational transmission, but a kind of cultural buffer.**

A distinction is important: bamboo weaving as a whole craft has the "Huang Tu-shan lineage" as a transmissible trunk, with second- and third-generation apprentice artists. But the bamboo hat branch is one of the nearly dead ends within bamboo weaving, with almost no next-generation professional practitioners — because the hat's market was consumed by imports long ago, and apprentices see no viable livelihood. Training institutes, experience courses, and documentaries are all important, but none of them can replace the fact of someone making a living from the craft.

## The Economic Reality: What Does a Hand-Crafted Bamboo Hat Actually Cost?

Search "Vietnamese bamboo hat" on PChome or Feebee price-comparison sites, and you find 1.4-_chi_ (about 42 cm) coconut-leaf hats selling for NT$68 to NT$115, many lacquered and suitable for painting, marketed as performance props or children's sun hats[^16]. Woven bamboo hats are priced slightly higher, but most are also below NT$200, and most products labeled "bamboo weaving" are actually made in Vietnam or China[^22].

Compare this to hand-production costs: a master can make at most five to six hats in a day[^2], with working hours starting at ten hours; materials (moso bamboo, dark-leaf strips, cotton thread, tung oil) aside, just counting minimum wage at NT$190 per hour times ten hours puts the **labor floor for a single hat near NT$400** — before materials, mold depreciation, and shipping. Traditional fully hand-crafted bamboo hats now retail mostly between NT$600 and NT$1,500. The main customers are no longer farmers but museum collections, television prop departments, traditional wedding gifts (a bamboo hat is customarily presented when thanking the matchmaker), and experience-workshop instructors.

In other words: **the competition for hand-crafted bamboo hats is not Vietnamese imports — it's that nobody needs a bamboo hat.** Farmers wear plastic caps (UV-blocking, washable, NT$20 each); tourists and children wear Vietnamese imports; a museum buys one and keeps it for twenty years. The only people who will pay NT$1,500 for a hand-crafted bamboo hat are heritage preservation units, creative brands (using them for wedding photo shoots or café décor), traditional performing arts groups, and a handful of elderly farmers. That market is far too small to support a young person entering the trade.

## The Thumbnail Scored by Bamboo Strips

Back to the living room near Longdu Junior High. The 2017 blog post ends: "Grandfather started weaving bamboo hats in his teens — more than sixty years, by the count."[^1] Lin Rong-chun has not won awards or been registered as an intangible cultural heritage preserver. He is not a national treasure-class craftsman — the titles given by the Council of Hakka Affairs and the Ministry of Culture have mostly gone to Lin Xiu-feng for fine bamboo weaving in Changhua[^11] and Lin Huang-jiao for rush-grass weaving in Taitung[^12]. He is not on the list.

But he is in the field. A hat he made was sold to a tea farmer in the next village, worn for five or six years, and when the brim frayed the farmer brought it back to him to mend. He mended it, and the farmer wore it for another five years. This cycle was never written into any collection database. The National Museum of Taiwan History's collection piece numbered 2002.005.0211 lists the creator as "unknown"[^13] — that is how most bamboo hats in Taiwan's museums are identified: unknown.

Not truly unknown. Simply, nobody at the time thought it was necessary to record a name. Bamboo strips were shaved from bamboo stalks, leaves were gathered from the bottom of bamboo clumps, cotton thread was bought from the general store, and the master squatted at the front door to make a hat and sell it to the neighbor who came by. No brand, no serial number, no author.

## Comparison: Vietnam's Nón Lá, Japan's Sedge Hat, the East Asian Cone

Vietnam's _nón lá_ is also a conical bamboo hat — but it is more pointed, lighter, and its outer layer uses palm leaves rather than bamboo shoot sheaths. Vietnam Airlines' travel guide describes it as part of "Vietnam's traditional costume," and its daily-use rate is far higher than in Taiwan[^18]. On streets in Ho Chi Minh City and Hanoi, women wearing _nón lá_ can be seen riding motorcycles, selling flowers, and carrying baskets every day; the formal _áo dài_ dress is often paired with a _nón lá_[^23].

What is the difference? Vietnam's _nón lá_ remains an everyday working object — masters have a market, and young people have a reason to learn. Taiwan's bamboo hat exited mainstream daily life in the 1980s; the masters who remain are "the last generation," not "the current generation." Japan's _sugegasa_ (菅笠, woven from sedge grass) is in a situation similar to Taiwan's: most prefectures use it only for traditional festivals, monks' pilgrimages, and Noh theater stages; some local governments have registered it as "intangible folk cultural property" and fund apprenticeship transmission[^24] — **a comprehensive preservation framework that Taiwan, as of 2026, still lacks.**

China's southern regions (Guangdong, Fujian, Zhejiang) have bamboo hat styles close to Taiwan's rough-work bamboo hats from Xinglin and Kenzi — from the perspective of ethnic history, these crafts crossed the Taiwan Strait with Hoklo and Hakka migrants and took root. But in their regions of origin the same double pressure of mechanization and youth disinterest applies — the conical hats of East Asia are different tributaries of the same river, all shallowing out together.

## Climate: The Final Blow

Typhoon Gaemi struck the Taoyuan–Hsinchu–Miaoli mountain region hard in July 2024. No official data on the area of downed moso bamboo was published, but Atayal bamboo-harvesting crews reported in early spring 2025 that "the harvestable yield is about 30% lower than in previous years."[^20] Extreme weather is lethal for bamboo that needs to be "mature, straight, and of uniform fiber" — toppled moso bamboo has damaged fibers and cannot be used for hat frames; it can only serve as fuel or be harvested for bamboo shoots.

Aging masters, shrinking market, broken raw material supply, climate disruption — four curves all bending downward simultaneously within the same decade. The conical shape remains; the skeleton technique survives in museum cards and memory banks. But the kind of person whose thumbnail has been scored by dozens of bamboo strip grooves — the youngest of them is already in their seventies today.
