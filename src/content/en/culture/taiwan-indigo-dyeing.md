# Taiwan Indigo Dyeing

## 30-Second Overview

In 19th-century Taiwan, indigo (the dye for indigo dyeing) was the third-largest export commodity after tea and camphor, making mountain towns like Sanxia rich overnight. As chemical dyes emerged, this industry almost completely disappeared in the 1920s. It was not until the late 1990s that the Council for Cultural Affairs and local craftspeople worked to revive the mountain indigo (Chinese indigo plant) of Sanxia and the indigo grass of Miaoli, finally bringing this deep shade of "Taiwan blue" back into everyday life.[^3]

---

## Once Upon a Time, It Was Taiwan's "Blue Gold"

In the 1640s during Dutch colonial rule, indigo was already an important trade commodity. During the Qing dynasty, the _Taiwan Prefecture Gazetteer_ recorded: "Indigo seed, planted to make blue dye. ... What is produced in Taiwan is especially fine."

Sanxia (formerly known as Sanjiaoyong) — with its clear stream water and moist valleys — became a cloth-dyeing center. In the mid-19th century, dyeing workshops lined its streets; the old street's red brick facades today still stand as historical testimony to the wealth generated by indigo dyeing.[^3]

---

## The Plant's Secret: Chinese Indigo and True Indigo

Taiwan indigo dyeing primarily uses two types of indigo plants:

- **Chinese indigo (mountain indigo):** Prefers cool, moist conditions; grows well at low elevations in mountain areas. Introduced by Hakka ancestors, it was the dominant variety in the north in the early period, producing a deeper ocean blue color.[^4]
- **True indigo (small indigo):** Prefers sunlight, drought-tolerant and moisture-tolerant, with a short growth period; common in the south, producing a lighter sky blue color.

Indigo plant leaves are green, but require microbial fermentation and oxidation to be transformed into blue-colored indigo dye. This process requires no heating at all — a reflection of the wisdom of humans living with nature.[^5]

### Traditional Process for Making Indigo Paste (From Plant to Dye)

1. **Planting and harvesting:** Indigo plants are mostly grown in shaded valleys or along streams. Chinese indigo is propagated by cuttings and can be harvested twice a year (early summer and late autumn). Fresh leaf stems are harvested in the morning before the dew has dried.[^4]
2. **Soaking and fermentation:** Leaves are placed in stone-built "indigo pits" (soaking pools) in the mountains, with clear water added, and soaked for 3–7 days (depending on temperature). The indican (indigo precursor) in the leaves breaks down under anaerobic bacterial action into colorless indoxyl.
3. **Beating and oxidation:** After fermentation, limewater (to raise pH) is added and the mixture is vigorously stirred with long rods — "beating the indigo." As oxygen enters, the indoxyl oxidizes into insoluble blue indigo, producing blue foam and precipitate.
4. **Settling and collecting:** Left to settle for several days, the upper waste liquid is drained, and the blue sludge at the bottom is scooped up. Filtered through cloth bags and dried, it becomes a paste or mud-like "indigo dye" that can be stored for a long time.

This is the "indigo making" stage. Next comes "dye building": placing the indigo paste in a dye vat, adding limewater and natural reducing agents (distillers' grains, rice bran, molasses), maintaining a temperature of 25–35°C and fermenting for 3–7 days. This reduces the indigo to soluble, yellow-green indigo white (the reduced form) — ready for dyeing cloth.[^5]

## The Magic of Indigo Dyeing: Methods from Vat to Fabric

Indigo dyeing uses a "reduction dye" technique. Its distinctive feature: when cloth is taken from the vat it appears yellow-green, but immediately turns blue upon contact with air. Multiple dips (5–30 times) are needed to deepen the color — a process like a "conversation" with the dye vat.

### 1. Traditional Reduction Vat Method (Core Technique)

Fabric is submerged in the reduction vat for 5–20 minutes, removed to oxidize, and the process is repeated. Dyed fabric carries a distinctive "fragrant blue" scent (with insect-repelling properties), and its color naturally changes over time, with light exposure and washing — it grows more soulful with use.

### 2. Tie-Dye / Shibori / Stitch-Resist Methods (Most Common Pattern Techniques)

Cotton thread, rubber bands, or needle and thread are used to tie, bind, and sew patterns (geometric, cloud, floral) onto the fabric. This blocks dye penetration; when untied, it reveals naturally gradated white-on-blue patterns. Sanxia's traditional applications included headcloths and aprons.

### 3. Clamp Dyeing / Board Dyeing (夾纈 jiā xié)

Wooden or metal boards clamp specific parts of the fabric to create symmetrical geometric patterns. Suitable for large-area designs; often incorporates Hakka floral fabric elements.

### 4. Batik / Wax Resist Dyeing (蠟纈 là xié)

Hot wax (beeswax, wood wax) is drawn onto the fabric as a resist. After dyeing, the wax is removed, leaving behind white or light-blue delicate textures. Teams such as Zhuoya Cottage often use this method for silk scarves and garments.

### 5. Paste Resist / Blue-Print Method (灰纈 huī xié)

A stencil is used to apply glutinous rice paste or soybean paste to the fabric as a resist, producing a blue fabric with white floral patterns.

### 6. Modern Sustainable Innovation

- **Cold dyeing:** Conducted entirely at room temperature — energy-saving and gentle on fibers.
- **Multi-vat gradient dyeing:** Different concentrations in different vats create rich layers of blue.
- **Natural mordanting:** Combined with iron filings and other materials to produce blue-green and blue-violet variations.
- **Yarn-first dyeing:** Yarn is dyed first, then woven into striped or plaid fabric.

Whatever the method, indigo dyeing is not mechanical reproduction — it is the collaborative creation of time, oxidation, and fabric fiber.

## Disappearance and Revival: Starting with a Silk Scarf

After chemical dyes emerged, natural indigo dyeing disappeared from Taiwan for more than 60 years.[^1]

In the 1990s, Sanxia's local history and culture workers and the New Taipei City government launched an effort to trace and recover the craft. In 2004, Cho Ming-pang and his wife Cheng Mei-shu founded "Zhuoya Cottage" in Sanyi, Miaoli. Cheng Mei-shu — a former home economics teacher — transformed herself: she personally planted indigo, built dye vats, developed techniques, and insisted on using her own cultivated Chinese indigo to revive this craft.[^6][^7]

Today, Taiwan blue has become a cultural calling card. When Czech Senate President Milos Vystrcil visited Taiwan in 2020, gifts included Zhuoya Cottage's indigo-dyed silk scarves.[^7]

---

## Why This Deserves to Be Said

Indigo dyeing is not just a color — it is the philosophy of Taiwan's people living in symbiosis with the land.

1. **Circular sustainability:** The entire process runs at room temperature, requires no chemical synthesis, the dye is biodegradable, and residue can be returned to the field as fertilizer. In an era of fast fashion, it offers a gentle and authentic alternative.
2. **The layers of time:** Colors naturally change with use — each mark is a life impression. It is "alive."
3. **The memory of land:** The mountain mist of Sanxia, the moisture of Miaoli, the sweat of the craftsperson — all woven into the fabric.

As craftspeople say: "Indigo dyeing is alive — it breathes in the dye vat, and grows old on the cloth."

This shade of Taiwan blue reminds us: slow work, coexistence, and respect for nature — these are the truest luxuries and the deepest sustainability.
