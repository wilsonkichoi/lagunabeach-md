## VERIFIED FACTS

### 1. Birth, Birthplace & Family Background

- **Full name:** 蔡英文 (Cài Yīngwén); Paiwan name: Tjuku (meaning "sun-shaped and round," also connotes "daughter of a chief")
- **Born:** 31 August 1956, Mackay Memorial Hospital, Zhongshan District, Taipei City
- **Father:** 蔡潔生 (Tsai Chieh-sheng), b. 1918 (Japanese colonial era). Initially worked as a mechanic in Manchuria (Dalian) during WWII, returned to Taiwan after Japan's defeat. He operated an **auto repair shop** serving US military personnel in Taiwan, later also invested in real estate, becoming wealthy.
- **Father's heritage:** Hakka Chinese descent — family originally from Meixian (嘉應州), Guangdong, but by Tsai Chieh-sheng's generation they had shifted to speaking Taiwanese Hokkien (福佬客). His mother was **Paiwan indigenous** from Shizi, Pingtung County.
- **Mother:** 張金鳳 (Chang Chin-fong) — the last of Tsai Chieh-sheng's four wives/partners.
- **Siblings:** Tsai Ing-wen was the **youngest of 11 children** (蔡潔生 had 11 children across four families).
- **Indigenous heritage (VERIFIED):** Through her paternal grandmother, Tsai has **one-quarter Paiwan (排灣族) ancestry**. Her great-grandmother on the paternal side was Paiwan. She is the **first Taiwanese president of indigenous descent** and the second of Hakka descent (after Lee Teng-hui). Her Paiwan indigenous name is Tjuku; she also holds a Tao (達悟) name, Dugang, from a visit to Orchid Island.
- **SOURCE CAVEAT:** Some sources say it was the grandmother who was Paiwan, some say great-grandmother — the zh.Wikipedia and ETtoday (2016) place the Paiwan lineage at the **great-grandmother (曾祖母) level** on the paternal side, meaning roughly one-eighth indigenous ancestry. The ZH Wikipedia explicitly states "四分之一排灣族血統" (one-quarter), which would put the Paiwan ancestor at the grandmother level. **The ZH Wikipedia figure of one-quarter is the most commonly cited in Taiwan-language sources.**
- **Hakka name:** Tshai Yîn-vun
- **Ancestral home:** Fangshan/Fenggang (楓港), Pingtung County

### 2. Education

| Degree         | Institution                                                                   | Year                      | Notes                                                 |
| -------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| LLB (法學士)   | National Taiwan University (NTU) Law                                          | **1978**                  | Graduated from the Department of Law (法律學系法學組) |
| LLM (法學碩士) | Cornell Law School (Cornell University)                                       | **1980**                  | Master of Laws                                        |
| PhD (法學博士) | London School of Economics and Political Science (LSE) / University of London | **1984** (degree awarded) | Thesis submitted/examined **1983**                    |

- **PhD thesis title:** _"Unfair trade practices and safeguard actions"_ (〈不公平貿易行為與保障措施〉)
- **Thesis URL:** https://etheses.lse.ac.uk/3976/1/Tsai__unfair-trade-practices-and-safeguard-actions.pdf
- **Doctoral advisor:** Michael J. Elliott (Michael Elliott)
- **Field:** International trade law / Law and economics

#### Thesis Controversy (VERIFIED DETAILS):

- The physical thesis was not deposited in LSE's library for **35 years** — it only became available in 2019 when Tsai provided a personal facsimile copy and later a digital copy to Taiwan's National Central Library.
- In October 2019, **LSE issued an official statement** confirming: "Dr Tsai was correctly awarded a PhD in Law in 1984." LSE cited: (a) Senate House Library records confirming receipt of a copy; (b) the thesis appearing in the IALS index _Legal Research in the United Kingdom 1905–1984_ (published 1985).
- Critics (notably media personality Dennis Peng / 彭文正) alleged the degree was fraudulent due to the missing physical copy. **LSE definitively rejected this claim.**
- STATUS: The controversy continues in some Taiwanese/overseas Chinese media but the **LSE's institutional position is that the degree is legitimate**.

### 3. Career Before Politics (1983–2000)

- **1983–1984:** Adjunct lecturer, Soochow University (東吳大學) Law School
- **1984–1990:** Associate professor, National Chengchi University (政大) Law School
- **1991–1993:** Professor, Soochow University Law School graduate program
- **1992–2000:** Advisor, Ministry of Economic Affairs — International Economic Organizations section (WTO affairs); she served as **Taiwan's trade negotiator for WTO/GATT** accession talks
- **1993–2000:** Professor, NCCU College of Commerce (International Business and Trade)
- **1993–2000:** Member, Fair Trade Commission (公平交易委員會)
- **1994–1995:** Chief researcher drafting the **Hong Kong and Macau Relations Act (香港澳門關係條例)**
- **1994–1998:** Advisory committee member, Mainland Affairs Council (MAC)
- **1999–2000:** Advisory committee member, National Security Council
- **Key role under Lee Teng-hui:** One of the **chief drafters of the special state-to-state relations doctrine (兩國論)** — Lee's July 1999 statement defining cross-strait relations as "at least special state-to-state relations." This was a landmark and controversial assertion of Taiwan's sovereignty.

### 4. Mainland Affairs Council (MAC) Chair, 2000–2004

- **Appointed:** 20 May 2000, by President Chen Shui-bian
- **Served under three premiers:** Tang Fei (唐飛), Chang Chun-hsiung (張俊雄), Yu Shyi-kun (游錫堃)
- She was appointed by the DPP despite being a KMT-era technocrat — she did **not join the DPP until 2004**.
- Resigned from MAC: 20 May 2004 (end of Chen's first term)
- During this period she was also a **Political Affairs Commission member (政務委員)** of the Executive Yuan.

### 6. Presidential Elections — Exact Figures

#### 2012 Presidential Election (LOSS)

- **Date:** 14 January 2012
- **Result:** Ma Ying-jeou (KMT) won with **51.6%** vs. Tsai's **45.6%**
- **Margin:** approximately **800,000 votes** (roughly 6 percentage points)
- Tsai resigned as DPP chair the day after the loss.
- It was widely expected to be a close race; the margin was described as "surprisingly large."

#### 2016 Presidential Election (FIRST WIN)

- **Date:** 16 January 2016
- **Result:** Tsai won with **56.1%** (6.89 million votes); KMT's Eric Chu got **31.0%** (3.81 million votes); PFP's James Soong got **12.8%** (1.58 million)
- **Victory margin:** 3.08 million votes — the second-largest winning margin in Taiwan's direct presidential election history (after 1996)
- DPP simultaneously won the **Legislative Yuan majority** for the first time (68 of 113 seats)
- **Historic significance:** First woman elected president of Taiwan; first woman president in the Chinese-speaking world
- **Inaugurated:** 20 May 2016

#### 2020 Presidential Election (RE-ELECTION)

- **Date:** 11 January 2020
- **Result:** Tsai won with **57.1%** (8.17 million votes) — a **record-breaking vote total** in Taiwan's history
- Opponent Han Kuo-yu (KMT) received 38.6% (5.52 million); James Soong (PFP) 4.3%
- **Victory margin:** 19.5 percentage points over Han
- **Context:** The 2019 Hong Kong protests and China's increasingly coercive posture dramatically boosted Tsai's support. Having looked likely to lose after the 2018 midterm defeats, the "Hong Kong factor" rehabilitated her standing by making "one country, two systems" a salient threat. Tsai forcefully aligned with Hong Kong protesters and made Beijing's Taiwan policy central to her campaign.

### 7. Key Policies as President

#### Same-Sex Marriage (VERIFIED)

- Taiwan became **the first country in Asia to legalize same-sex marriage** on **24 May 2019**
- **Legislative milestone:** Bill passed on 17 May 2019; signed by President Tsai on 22 May; took effect 24 May
- **Constitutional foundation:** On 24 May 2017, the Constitutional Court ruled the existing marriage law unconstitutional and gave the Legislative Yuan 2 years to comply
- **2018 referendum complication:** Voters approved referendums against recognizing same-sex unions within the Civil Code; the government responded by creating a **separate special law** rather than amending the Civil Code
- **526 couples married** on the first day of legalization
- Tsai had announced her personal support for same-sex marriage in **November 2015** during the campaign

#### Indigenous Peoples Apology (VERIFIED)

- **Date:** 1 August 2016 (Indigenous Peoples' Day, 原住民族日)
- First formal government apology in Taiwan's history for treatment of indigenous peoples
- Apology admitted 400 years of "brutal violations" of indigenous rights through "armed invasion and land seizure" by successive regimes
- Acknowledged: erasure of indigenous history; erosion of languages and cultural identities through assimilation policies; harm to Yami/Tao (達悟) tribe from nuclear waste storage on Orchid Island; insufficient implementation of the Indigenous Peoples Basic Law
- Representatives from all 16 recognized indigenous groups attended

#### Pension Reform (2017–2018)

- **Problem addressed:** Pension funds projected to go **bankrupt as early as 2030** due to generous legacy benefits (including a controversial 18% preferential interest rate on deposits for retired civil servants)
- **Reform enacted:** June 2017, Legislative Yuan passed the DPP pension reform package
- **Changes:** Gradually reduced income replacement ratio (from very high levels to 30–60% depending on years of service); phased out the 18% interest rate over 30 months
- **Backlash:** Large protests by retired military personnel, civil servants, and teachers; protesters packed Ketagalan Boulevard and marched on the Legislative Yuan
- **Tsai's response:** Apologized to affected groups while insisting reforms were fiscally necessary; she maintained this was essential to prevent total fund collapse

#### Energy Policy / Nuclear Phase-Out

- **Policy goal:** By 2025 — 50% natural gas, 30% coal, 20% renewables; **zero nuclear by 2025**
- **Achievement:** The last nuclear reactor (Chinshan/核三廠 Unit 2) shut down in 2025, technically achieving the "nuclear-free homeland (非核家園)" goal
- **Controversies:**
  - Renewable energy targets fell behind schedule year after year
  - Multiple major blackouts during Tsai's tenure: August 2017 (八一五大停電), May 2021 (兩次停電), March 2022 (三○三全台大停電)
  - Heavy reliance on natural gas (~50% after nuclear phaseout) with only ~10–14 days of reserve storage
  - International trend toward nuclear renaissance (post-COP) put Tsai's anti-nuclear position under increased scrutiny
  - After Tsai, Lai Ching-te's government began evaluating restarting nuclear plants, indicating policy softening

### 8. Cross-Strait Relations Under Tsai

- **China's response to Tsai's 2016 election:** Beijing suspended all official cross-strait communication channels immediately after her inauguration (she refused to explicitly endorse the "1992 Consensus")
- **"1992 Consensus" dispute:** China demanded Tsai accept this formula; Tsai acknowledged the 1992 talks but refused to accept Beijing's framing that it implied "one China"
- **Grey-zone military pressure:** Since 2016, PLA aircraft incursions into Taiwan's Air Defense Identification Zone (ADIZ) dramatically increased. Before the Pelosi visit (August 2022), such incursions were relatively rare; after it, they became daily. In all of 2022, **564 PLA aircraft crossed the Taiwan Strait median line** — more than 24 times all known crossings prior to Pelosi's visit
- **Pelosi visit (August 2-3, 2022):** US House Speaker Nancy Pelosi visited Taiwan — the highest-ranking US official visit in 25 years. She met with President Tsai. The symbolic image: two women, Speaker of the US House and Taiwan's president, side-by-side. China responded with unprecedented military exercises surrounding Taiwan, including ballistic missile launches over Taiwan and a 23x spike in cyberattacks. This visit permanently changed the military status quo in the Strait.
- **McCarthy transit (April 2023):** Tsai met with US House Speaker Kevin McCarthy in California during a transit — China again responded with military exercises
- **Result of Tsai era:** Cross-strait relations reached their lowest point in decades; Taiwan-US relations deepened significantly

### 9. COVID-19 Response

- Taiwan activated its epidemic command center in **January 2020** before WHO declared a global emergency
- Closed borders to China travel early; implemented mass quarantine enforcement using cell phone tracking
- **No national lockdown** was ever imposed
- By September 2020: Taiwan had recorded only **489 total cases and 7 deaths** in a population of 23.8 million — with no locally acquired cases for 135+ consecutive days
- The "Taiwan Model" was praised globally as a pandemic management exemplar, combining digital contact tracing, masking norms (inherited from SARS 2003 experience), rapid border controls, and transparent communication
- Health Minister Chen Shih-chung was the public face; Tsai gave him full political backing
- **Time Magazine:** Named Tsai among the 100 Most Influential People of 2020, substantially due to the COVID response

### 10. Personal Life

- **Unmarried, no children** — Taiwan's first unmarried president
- **Political issue:** Shortly after her 2016 election, China's state agency **Xinhua** published an article criticizing her for being a "single woman" and therefore supposedly "emotional and erratic" — widely condemned as sexist; the criticism backfired internationally
- **Cats:**
  - **蔡想想 (Think Think / Xiǎngxiǎng):** Female gray tabby; found by DPP legislator Hsiao Bi-khim during disaster relief after a typhoon near Hualien's Heping Station in 2012; Tsai adopted her and named her
  - **蔡阿才 (Ah Tsai / Ācái):** Male orange-white tabby; Tsai found him beside a pineapple field at Bajigao tribe in Taitung in June 2015; the farmer gave him to her
  - The cats appeared extensively in her 2015–2016 campaign (Facebook posts featuring them received ~50% more engagement than policy posts)
  - She also adopted three retired guide dogs (Bella, Bunny, Maru) and later two more dogs (Lele, Pineapple)
  - Upon leaving office in May 2024, Tsai took 2 cats and 4 dogs from the Presidential Office residence

- **Time 100 (2016):** Named to Time's 100 Most Influential People list in April 2016, shortly after her election
- **Time 100 (2020):** Named again, prominently for COVID-19 leadership; Taiwan was featured on Time's cover

### 12. 2024 End of Term

- **Term limit:** Under ROC Constitution, a president can serve maximum two 4-year terms; Tsai completed both terms
- **Final day:** 20 May 2024 — handed over to Lai Ching-te (賴清德), who had served as her Vice President (2nd term)
- **Tsai's endorsement of Lai:** Tsai actively campaigned for Lai in the January 2024 presidential election, calling it "a choice between Taiwan's continued progress or decline"
- **Lai's victory (13 January 2024):** Lai Ching-te won with ~40% of the vote (three-way race with KMT's Hou Yu-ih and TPP's Ko Wen-je); this marked **the first time any party won three consecutive presidential terms** in Taiwan's democratic history

## NOTES FOR ARTICLE WRITER

1. **11th child vs. youngest:** Multiple sources confirm she was the youngest of 11 children. "Eleventh child" and "youngest" are both accurate.

2. **Father's business:** He ran an **auto repair shop (汽車修理廠)** serving US military, **then** moved into real estate. Do not describe him as solely a real estate developer.

3. **Indigenous ancestry fraction:** ZH Wikipedia cites "one-quarter Paiwan," which would place the Paiwan ancestor at grandmother level. ETtoday (2016) says great-grandmother (曾祖母) — which would be one-eighth. The "one-quarter" figure is more widely cited in Taiwanese sources; use with a note about the uncertainty.

4. **NTU:** She graduated in 1978 (LLB), not 1980. Cornell LLM was 1980. LSE PhD degree awarded 1984 (thesis 1983).

5. **MAC chair:** She served under Chen Shui-bian, NOT under Lee Teng-hui. Her role under Lee was as a WTO trade negotiator and drafter of the two-states doctrine — she was a technocrat appointed by KMT before joining DPP.

6. **DPP chair first elected:** 19 May 2008 — she defeated Koo Kwang-ming. She was DPP chair three separate times (2008–2012, 2014–2018, 2020–2022).

7. **2012 loss margin:** ~800,000 votes / ~6 percentage points. Not a close race.

8. **Same-sex marriage:** Tsai signed it 22 May 2019; effective 24 May 2019. She announced personal support in November 2015. The Constitutional Court ruling was May 2017.

9. **Cats' Chinese names:** 蔡想想 (Think Think) and 蔡阿才 (Ah Tsai). Both carry the presidential surname 蔡.

10. **Post-presidency:** Since May 2024, she has been active as an unofficial diplomatic envoy (visited Europe in 2025).
