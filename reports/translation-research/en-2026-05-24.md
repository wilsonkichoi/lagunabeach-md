**Date**: 2026-05-24
**Researcher**: Taiwan.md Semiont session (γ-research handle)
**Audience**: en babel translators + REWRITE-PIPELINE Stage 2-5 sub-agents working en
**Status**: First canonical pass. Open questions flagged at end.

## TL;DR — Top 5 Recommendations

1. **`Taiwan` simpliciter is the default in body prose. Reserve `Republic of China` / `ROC` for legal-precision contexts** (treaties, court decisions, ROC-era documents pre-1949, formal name disambiguation). Both AP and the Global Taiwan Institute style guide follow this pattern. Never use `Chinese Taipei` outside the specific Olympic / APEC / WHA international-organization contexts where Taiwan was forced into the name.

2. **The single highest-leverage defense against PRC-coded leak in English is a banned-phrases list enforced at lint time.** Top offenders: `Taiwan, China` / `Taiwan Province of China` / `China's Taiwan` / `mainland China` (uncontextualized) / `renegade province` / `reunification` / `the island` (when used dismissively as substitute for Taiwan-as-nation). Substitute: `Taiwan` / `the PRC` or `China` (when clearly meaning PRC) / `unification` (if discussing potential future integration) / `Taiwan` repeated rather than `the island`.

3. **Personal names use Wade-Giles-derived ROC-official spelling for politicians and historical figures** (`Lee Teng-hui`, `Tsai Ing-wen`, `Lai Ching-te`, `Chiang Ching-kuo`, `Ma Ying-jeou`) — these are how the subjects spell their own names, and how Reuters/AP/BBC/NYT spell them. Hyphenate two-syllable given names, lowercase the second syllable (`Teng-hui`, not `Tenghui` or `Teng-Hui`). For people with established English names (`Audrey Tang`, `Joseph Wu`, `Hsiao Bi-khim`), use the English name first reference, optionally with Chinese name in parentheses.

4. **Place names use legacy Wade-Giles / postal romanization for the major settlements** (`Taipei`, `Kaohsiung`, `Hsinchu`, `Taichung`, `Tainan`, `Keelung`, `Kinmen`, `Matsu`) — these are the entrenched conventions Taiwan's own MOFA-EN, tourism bureau, and international media all use. **Hanyu Pinyin is correct for newer district / street level** when the local government has adopted it (`Xinyi District` in Taipei; but Kaohsiung still uses Tongyong for its districts). Never `Taibei`, `Gaoxiong`, `Xinzhu` — those are PRC-style and read as PRC-coded to Taiwanese eyes.

5. **For Taiwan-specific cultural terms with no clean English equivalent, use the romanization + gloss pattern on first mention, romanization alone after.** Format: _lurou fan_ (braised pork rice), _Mazu_ (sea goddess), _Tâi-gí_ (the Taiwanese / Hokkien language). Italicize the romanization on first use. The romanization preserves sovereignty (the term is _Taiwanese_, not a Chinese-generic translation) while the gloss preserves accessibility. **Prefer Tâi-gí / Taiwanese over Hokkien / Min-nan** when referring to the language as spoken in Taiwan — `Min-nan` carries Sinocentric framing many native speakers reject.

## A. Country / region naming conventions

### A.1 Taiwan vs Republic of China (ROC) vs Chinese Taipei vs Formosa

| Term                           | When to use                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                       | When NOT to use                                                                             |
| ------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Taiwan**                     | Default. All body prose, datelines, references to the polity, people, geography, government, economy, culture.                                                                                                                                                                                                    | Never wrong as default.                                                                     |
| **Republic of China (ROC)**    | (1) Formal legal name where precision is required (treaties, court decisions, constitution). (2) Pre-1949 history when ROC governed mainland China. (3) When explicitly distinguishing the ROC state from "Taiwan" as a geographic/cultural entity (e.g., articles on Taiwanese identity politics).               | As default name for the country. Avoid in casual prose — it reads stiff and pre-2000s.      |
| **Republic of China (Taiwan)** | Diplomatic/official contexts where both names are needed (MOFA, government portals, formal documents). Reasonable for first reference in a political/diplomatic article.                                                                                                                                          | Body prose after first mention.                                                             |
| **Chinese Taipei**             | ONLY when discussing the specific international-organization contexts where this name was forced on Taiwan: Olympics, APEC, WHA observer status, WTO (where it appears as "Separate Customs Territory of Taiwan, Penghu, Kinmen and Matsu"). Quote in scare quotes or note "the name imposed by..." on first use. | As a name for Taiwan in any other context. It is a coerced compromise, not a neutral label. |
| **Formosa**                    | (1) Historical contexts pre-1945 (Dutch Formosa, Japanese-era references). (2) Literary/poetic references (`Ilha Formosa`). (3) Direct quotes / proper nouns (Formosa Plastics, Formosa Strait as historical synonym).                                                                                            | Modern references to the country/island. Reads as archaic/colonial in 2026.                 |

**Sources**: [Taiwan (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan); [GTI Publication Style Guide (PDF)](https://globaltaiwan.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/01/GTI-Style-Guide.docx.pdf); [Republic of China on Taiwan (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China_on_Taiwan)

### A.2 Major style guides' position on Taiwan

- **AP Stylebook (2024–2026 edition)**: Datelines on Taiwan stories use the community name + Taiwan. In body, use `Taiwan` for references to the government. `Republic of China` only when legal precision required. The China entry notes "China" refers to the nation including the mainland, Hong Kong, and Macao — implying Taiwan is treated separately. ([AP Stylebook 2024-2026](https://www.amazon.com/Associated-Press-Stylebook-2024-2026/dp/154160511X), [AP Facebook post on China entry](https://www.facebook.com/apstylebook/posts/when-used-alone-china-refers-to-the-nation-that-includes-the-mainland-hong-kong-/1013658135323939/))
- **Reuters Handbook of Journalism**: No publicly available Taiwan-specific entry, but Reuters wire copy uses standard boilerplate: `Taiwan, which China claims as its own territory` or `the democratically governed island that China claims as its own`. ([Reuters Handbook overview](https://www.thebaron.info/handbook-of-journalism))
- **BBC News**: Uses `Taiwan` as default. Common boilerplate: `Taiwan is a self-ruled island that China claims as a breakaway province`.
- **The Economist**: Uses `Taiwan` consistently; has been criticized by Taipei Times for occasionally "singing Beijing's tune" in framing (specifically around cross-Strait phrasing). ([Taipei Times critique](https://www.taipeitimes.com/News/feat/archives/2024/07/15/2003820817))
- **NYT, Washington Post, WSJ**: All use `Taiwan` as default with the standard `self-ruled / China claims` boilerplate.
- **US State Department**: Per the Taiwan Relations Act framework, uses `Taiwan` in unofficial communications; `Taiwan authorities` rather than `Taiwan government` in some formal contexts (a deliberate diplomatic fiction).
- **Taiwan MOFA-EN**: Uses `Republic of China (Taiwan)` as the formal first reference, then `Taiwan` in body. ([Taiwan.gov.tw](https://www.taiwan.gov.tw/content_3.php))

### A.3 Post-2016 / post-2020 / post-2022 Pelosi shifts

The most visible shift in Anglophone press has been **increased willingness to call Taiwan a `democracy` and `democratically-governed` rather than just `self-ruled`** — a subtle but meaningful change post-2016 (Tsai election), accelerating after 2020 (Tsai re-election) and 2022 (Pelosi visit, where coverage emphasized Taiwan's democratic legitimacy against PRC coercion). The Reuters/AP/BBC boilerplate has converged on `democratically-governed Taiwan, which China claims as its own territory` as the canonical hedge.

However, the boilerplate itself remains contested. Critics note that the `China claims` clause is reflexively appended even in articles where it's irrelevant, treating the claim as the more important fact than Taiwan's actual sovereignty. ([Foreign Policy: Stop Calling Taiwan a 'Renegade Province'](https://foreignpolicy.com/2016/01/15/stop-calling-taiwan-a-renegade-province/))

### A.4 `Taiwan, China` / `Taiwan, Province of China` — how to avoid PRC-coded framing

These constructions appear in three ways:

1. **PRC-mandated international organization usage** — `Taiwan, Province of China` is the UN/ISO label. Legitimate to mention when _describing_ that fact (e.g., "ISO 3166 lists Taiwan as 'Taiwan, Province of China'"), but never to _adopt_ it.
2. **PRC-state-media boilerplate leaking into translation** — common when translating from Chinese sources that use 中國臺灣 / 中國臺北. **Always strip** unless quoting in a context where preservation is required to demonstrate PRC framing.
3. **Form-field bureaucratic legacy** — international companies' dropdowns / addresses. Not editorial usage.

**Defense for Taiwan.md**: Add lint rule for the literal strings `Taiwan, China`, `Taiwan, Province of China`, `China's Taiwan`, `中國臺灣`, `中國臺北` in en/* output. Flag any occurrence for human review. Whitelist only the labeling-discussion article where these terms are the *subject\*.

**Sources**: [Taiwan, China (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan,_China); [Hudson Institute: A War Built on Fiction](https://www.hudson.org/global-economy/war-built-fiction-chinas-baseless-claim-over-taiwan-miles-yu); [US-Taiwan Business Council: Taiwan Terminology and Language Bias](https://www.us-taiwan.org/faq/taiwan-terminology-language-bias/)

### A.5 Cross-Strait terminology

- **Taiwan Strait** — both words capitalized as proper noun. Per Chicago Manual of Style (followed by Pacific Affairs journal style guide): capitalize topographical names commonly accepted as proper names. ([Pacific Affairs Style Guide](https://pacificaffairs.ubc.ca/style-guide/))
- **cross-Strait** — hyphenated, with capital `S`. The form `cross-strait` (lowercase s) also appears in some style guides; both are defensible, but `cross-Strait` is the GTI / Taiwan-studies academic preference and more sovereignty-aware.
- **Cross-Strait relations** — capitalize when used as a proper-noun-like construction (e.g., as a chapter heading or formal field name); lowercase `cross-Strait` as a modifier in body.
- **Strait** — capitalize when standing alone as shorthand for the Taiwan Strait (`the Strait`).
- Avoid `Formosa Strait` and `Strait of Formosa` in modern contexts (historical-only).
- **`mainland China`** — DO NOT use in default body prose. The term tacitly endorses the PRC framing that Taiwan is a non-mainland part of one China. Substitute: `China`, `the PRC`, `the People's Republic of China`. Use `mainland China` only when (1) quoting a source, (2) specifically discussing PRC internal mainland-vs-Hong-Kong/Macau distinction, or (3) explaining the contested terminology itself.

**Sources**: [Pacific Affairs Style Guide](https://pacificaffairs.ubc.ca/style-guide/); [GTI Style Guide](https://globaltaiwan.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/01/GTI-Style-Guide.docx.pdf); [US-Taiwan Business Council on `mainland China`](https://www.us-taiwan.org/faq/taiwan-terminology-language-bias/)

## B. Personal name romanization

### B.1 Which system, when

| System                                                   | Use for                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                   | Examples                                                                                                |
| -------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Wade-Giles (ROC-official, simplified, no diacritics)** | Default for: Taiwanese political figures, historical figures, family-name surnames, anyone whose passport spelling follows this convention.                                                                                                               | Lee Teng-hui, Chiang Kai-shek, Chiang Ching-kuo, Ma Ying-jeou, Soong Chu-yu (James Soong), Hau Lung-pin |
| **Gwoyeu Romatzyh (GR)**                                 | Specific cases — Tsai Ing-wen and Ma Ying-jeou's given names happen to be GR rather than Wade-Giles. Use the form they themselves use.                                                                                                                    | Tsai Ing-wen, Ma Ying-jeou                                                                              |
| **Hanyu Pinyin**                                         | Almost never for Taiwanese personal names. Acceptable if (1) the person is from mainland China and spells their name in Pinyin, (2) the Taiwanese person explicitly uses Pinyin spelling (rare), or (3) academic transliteration in linguistics contexts. | Mainland figures: Xi Jinping, Mao Zedong. NOT for Taiwanese unless self-chosen.                         |
| **Tongyong Pinyin**                                      | Almost never for personal names. Some Kaohsiung-era political figures may have Tongyong spellings on bilingual signage.                                                                                                                                   | Rare.                                                                                                   |
| **Tâi-lô / POJ (Taiwanese romanization)**                | When the person publicly uses a Tâi-gí name (esp. for indigenous-Taiwanese-identifying figures, some musicians/writers).                                                                                                                                  | Optional, usually with Mandarin romanization as gloss.                                                  |

**Default rule**: Use the romanization the person themselves uses. Check their official bio, English-language interviews, or government profile.

**Sources**: [Chinese language romanization in Taiwan (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language_romanization_in_Taiwan); [Wade-Giles (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wade%E2%80%93Giles); [Diversity Style Guide on Chinese names](https://www.diversitystyleguide.com/glossary/chinese-names/)

### B.2 Name order

- **Family name first, given name second** for all Chinese-character names (Lee Teng-hui, Tsai Ing-wen, Chen Shui-bian).
- **Western order** for people with English names or who have adopted Western order publicly (Audrey Tang, Joseph Wu, Stan Shih).
- For people known by both: lead with the form they primarily use professionally. (`Hsiao Bi-khim` not `Bi-khim Hsiao` — she uses family-name-first.)
- On second reference: surname only (`Lee`, `Tsai`, `Lai`).
- For very common Taiwanese surnames where ambiguity arises (multiple Lees, Lins, Wangs in the same article), use full name on each reference.

### B.3 Hyphenation

- **Two-syllable given names get a hyphen, second syllable lowercase**: `Lee Teng-hui`, `Tsai Ing-wen`, `Chiang Ching-kuo`, `Lai Ching-te`. NEVER `Lee Tenghui`, `Lee TengHui`, `Lee Teng-Hui`, or `Lee Teng Hui`.
- **One-syllable given names**: no hyphen needed. `Wang Wei`.
- **Three-syllable given names**: hyphenate first to second, no hyphen to third? Practice varies; usually all hyphenated with subsequent syllables lowercase. Defer to subject's own usage.

**Source**: [Lee Teng-hui (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lee_Teng-hui)

### B.4 Indigenous Taiwanese names

- **Use the Latin-letter romanization the person uses**, not the Han-character transliteration. Increasingly, indigenous figures use only their native name (e.g., `Kawlo Iyun Pacidal`, `Pasuya Yao`, `Kolas Yotaka`).
- Recent law (2024 amendments) lets indigenous Taiwanese register native names with Latin script on passports and household registries. Reflect this — don't force a Han name where they've moved away from one. ([Taiwan News 2024-05-14](https://taiwannews.com.tw/news/5686375))
- For the **16 officially-recognized peoples**, prefer the name they use for themselves: Amis (also Pangcah), Atayal (also Tayal), Bunun, Kavalan, Paiwan, Puyuma, Rukai, Saisiyat, Sakizaya, Sediq, Thao, Truku, Tsou, Tao (also Yamei), Kanakanavu, Hla'alua. ([Taiwanese indigenous peoples (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwanese_indigenous_peoples))
- Avoid `aborigines` / `aboriginal` as outdated; use `indigenous Taiwanese` / `Taiwan's indigenous peoples`.

### B.5 People with English names

- **Audrey Tang** — use the English name. Add Chinese name as parenthetical on first mention if relevant: `Audrey Tang (唐鳳, also Tang Feng)`. Not all readers will know this is the same person as Tang Feng / 唐鳳.
- **Joseph Wu** — use English name; on first mention can add `(Wu Jaushieh)` for the Chinese form if context needs it.
- **Hsiao Bi-khim** — uses family-name-first hyphenated form; this IS her primary name in English-language usage.

### B.6 Stage names

- **Ashin (五月天 vocalist)** — use `Ashin` directly. On first mention in a music-focused article: `Ashin (Chinese: 陳信宏 Chen Hsin-hung, born 1975)`. The romanized stage name is the publicly-used form.
- **Anpu (安溥)** — use `Anpu` as the current stage name; her previous stage name `Deserts Chang` / `張懸 Chang Hsuan` should be noted in biographical context. (`Anpu` is the romanized form she uses since renaming.)
- **General rule for stage names**: use the form the artist uses on their official English-language materials (album credits, MV credits, MOFA cultural promotion). When that doesn't exist, use Hanyu Pinyin or Wade-Giles consistent with the rest of the article.

**Source**: [Ashin (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ashin); [Mayday band (Wikipedia)](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mayday_(Taiwanese_band)>)

### C.2 Districts — mixed

Districts inherit the romanization their parent city uses, which produces inconsistency:

- **Taipei districts** — Hanyu Pinyin (since Ma Ying-jeou's mayoralty): `Xinyi District` (信義), `Da'an District` (大安), `Zhongshan District` (中山), `Wanhua District` (萬華), `Shilin District` (士林), `Songshan District` (松山). Avoid older Wade-Giles forms like `Hsinyi`, `Ta-an`, `Shihlin`, `Sungshan` in current content.
- **Kaohsiung districts** — Tongyong Pinyin (held over from when Kaohsiung was DPP-led): `Sinsing District` (新興), `Cianjhen District` (前鎮). These look unusual but are the official forms on local signage and Kaohsiung City Government's English materials.
- **Tainan, Taichung** — mixed; check the city government's current English signage.

**Rule of thumb for Taiwan.md**: For Taipei districts, use Hanyu Pinyin. For other cities, defer to the most recent official local-government romanization. When in doubt, search the city government's English site.

### C.3 Mountains and rivers

- 玉山 → **Yushan** (or `Mount Jade` in tourism contexts) — Hanyu Pinyin form is conventional; `Jade Mountain` is a translation, not a romanization.
- 阿里山 → **Alishan**
- 雪山 → **Xueshan** (or `Snow Mountain` in tourism)
- 淡水河 → **Tamsui River** (Wade-Giles `Tam-sui`) or `Danshui River` (Hanyu Pinyin) — both are used; `Tamsui` is more entrenched in tourism/historical writing.
- 濁水溪 → **Zhuoshui River**
- 高屏溪 → **Gaoping River**
- 日月潭 → **Sun Moon Lake** (translation is conventional and ubiquitous; do not romanize)

## D. Cultural terminology

### D.1 Food — romanize + gloss pattern

Use _italicized romanization_ on first mention + parenthetical English gloss. On subsequent mentions, romanization alone.

| Chinese  | First mention                                         | Subsequent       | Notes                                                                                                                                                        |
| -------- | ----------------------------------------------------- | ---------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| 滷肉飯   | _lurou fan_ (braised pork rice)                       | _lurou fan_      | Hanyu Pinyin standard in English food writing                                                                                                                |
| 牛肉麵   | _niurou mian_ (beef noodle soup)                      | beef noodle soup | English form usually wins after introduction                                                                                                                 |
| 珍珠奶茶 | **bubble tea** (also called _boba_ or pearl milk tea) | bubble tea       | English form `bubble tea` is dominant globally; `boba` is US-regional; `pearl milk tea` is Taiwanese self-name. Lead with whichever fits article's audience. |
| 鳳梨酥   | **pineapple cake**                                    | pineapple cake   | English form is dominant; can add (_fenglisu_) on first mention for cultural context                                                                         |
| 蚵仔煎   | _ô-á-chian_ (oyster omelet)                           | oyster omelet    | Tâi-gí romanization shows the Taiwanese-language origin; Mandarin would be _kezi jian_                                                                       |
| 鹹酥雞   | _xian su ji_ (Taiwanese popcorn chicken)              | —                | Both forms common                                                                                                                                            |
| 小籠包   | **xiaolongbao** (soup dumplings)                      | xiaolongbao      | English-language usage has converged on `xiaolongbao` without italics                                                                                        |
| 滷味     | _lu wei_ (braised snacks)                             | _lu wei_         | —                                                                                                                                                            |
| 臭豆腐   | **stinky tofu** (_chou doufu_)                        | stinky tofu      | English form is fully naturalized                                                                                                                            |

**Pattern**: prefer the **Taiwanese-language romanization** when the dish has Tâi-gí roots (`ô-á-chian`, not `kezi jian`). This is a sovereignty-preservation move — many "Chinese food" dishes are specifically Taiwanese.

**Sources**: [Lu Rou Fan recipe sources](https://thewoksoflife.com/lu-rou-fan-taiwanese-braised-pork-rice-bowl/), [Bubble tea (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bubble_tea), [Taiwanese Hokkien (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwanese_Hokkien)

### D.2 Night market, temple plaza, common spaces

- **night market** — English form; no need to use _yeshi_ unless emphasizing the term itself.
- **temple plaza** / **temple front** / **temple courtyard** — translate. _Miaokou_ (廟口) can be used in proper noun contexts like `Keelung Miaokou Night Market`.
- **street food** — translate.
- **stinky tofu vendor** / **shaved ice stall** — translate.

**Source**: [Night markets in Taiwan (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Night_markets_in_Taiwan)

### D.3 Mazu and religious figures

- **Mazu** (媽祖) — sea goddess. Use `Mazu` as default English form. `Matsu` (with -tsu) is the Wade-Giles form and creates confusion with the Matsu Islands; prefer `Mazu` for the goddess.
- On first mention: `Mazu (媽祖), the sea goddess central to Taiwanese folk religion`.
- **Mazu pilgrimage** (大甲媽祖遶境) — `Dajia Mazu Pilgrimage` is the standard tourism / academic form.
- **Earth God** (土地公 _tudi gong_) — translate.
- **City God** (城隍 _cheng huang_) — translate.
- **Guan Gong / Guandi** (關公 / 關帝) — romanize; `Guandi` or `Guan Yu` for the historical figure.

**Source**: [Mazu (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mazu)

### D.5 Taiwanese language naming

**Recommended**: **`Taiwanese`** (most common in English) or **`Tâi-gí`** (the language's self-name, sovereignty-aware). Acceptable: **`Taiwanese Hokkien`**.

**Avoid** in default usage:

- `Min-nan` / `Minnan` — the `min` (閩) character historically meant "barbarian" in classical Chinese; many native speakers reject the term as Sinocentric.
- `Southern Min` — academic but not preferred by speakers.
- `Fukienese` / `Hokkienese` — outdated.

For Hakka (客家語), use `Hakka`. For indigenous languages, use the language name directly (Atayal, Amis, etc.).

## E. Political / historical sensitive terms

### E.1 228

- **First mention**: `the 228 Incident` (most common in international academic writing) or `the February 28 Incident`. Both are standard.
- **Subsequent mentions**: `228` (no hyphen, no period).
- `228 Massacre` is increasingly used by Taiwan-aligned commemoration groups; appropriate when emphasizing the scale of killing (~28,000 dead).
- `February 28` for the date itself.
- `228 Peace Memorial Day` for the public holiday.

**Source**: [February 28 incident (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/February_28_incident); [FPRI: Taiwan's White Terror](https://www.fpri.org/article/2017/02/taiwans-white-terror-remembering-228-incident/)

### E.2 White Terror

- **`White Terror`** — capitalize, no quotation marks. The standard English term for the 1947/49–1992 period of authoritarian repression in Taiwan.
- **`White Terror era`** / **`White Terror period`** — also acceptable.
- Pair with date range: `the White Terror (1947–1992)` or `(1949–1992)` depending on which start date is being used.

### E.3 Martial law

- **`martial law`** — lowercase in general use.
- **`Taiwan's Martial Law era`** or **`the Martial Law era`** — can be capitalized when referring specifically to the 1949–1987 period in Taiwan.
- `the lifting of martial law in 1987` — standard phrasing.

### E.4 ROC year (民國)

- **In body prose**: convert to Gregorian / CE. `民國 38 年` becomes `1949`.
- **When the ROC year is itself the subject** (e.g., a date stamp, a document title, a numbered law): show both — `Year 38 of the Republic (1949)`.
- **Never** use bare `ROC 115` in en/\* content; readers won't know to subtract 1911.
- For very recent dates that may appear with ROC year on Taiwanese government documents: convert silently in body, footnote the original date format if quoting verbatim.

**Source**: [Republic of China calendar (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China_calendar)

### E.5 本省人 / 外省人

These terms are difficult because no English form is fully satisfactory.

## F. Tone and register

### F.1 British vs American English

**Recommendation: American English** for Taiwan.md en/\*.

Reasoning:

1. Taiwan's own English-language pedagogy and government documents strongly favor American conventions. ([Language on the Move: Taiwan's love affair with American English](https://languageonthemove.com/taiwans-love-affair-with-american-english/))
2. Taiwan's principal English-language media (Taipei Times, Focus Taiwan, Taiwan News) use American conventions.
3. The largest English-reading audience for Taiwan content is North American.
4. Consistency with how Taiwan presents itself to the world.

Implications:

- `color` not `colour`, `realize` not `realise`, `defense` not `defence`, `center` not `centre`.
- `traveled` not `travelled`.
- Date format: `May 24, 2026` (not `24 May 2026`).
- Currency: write out NT$ for New Taiwan dollar (e.g., `NT$1,200`); USD when needed for comparison.

### F.2 Academic vs journalistic register

Taiwan.md sits between: more thoughtful than journalism, more accessible than academic. Recommended register:

- Long-form essay tone (Atlantic, Foreign Affairs, LARB, Asia Society Magazine).
- Allow first-person plural (`we`) sparingly when discussing Taiwanese collective identity from inside the community.
- Active voice predominates.
- Footnotes for sourced claims (per CITATION-GUIDE.md).
- Avoid academic hedge-pile (`it could be argued that one might consider...`) and avoid clickbait punch (`This is HUGE!`).

### F.3 Oxford comma

**Use the Oxford comma.** It is the standard in serious American English nonfiction (Chicago Manual of Style, New Yorker, MIT Press, most academic journals). It is also unambiguous, which matters for translations from Chinese where serial structures don't have built-in conjunctions.

### F.4 Date format

- **Body prose**: `May 24, 2026` (American long form). `1947` for years alone.
- **Date ranges**: `1947–1987` (en dash, no spaces) for closed ranges; `2016–present` for open.
- **Decades**: `the 1990s` (no apostrophe).
- **Centuries**: `the 20th century` (numeric for any century in modern prose).
- **Lunar dates**: convert to Gregorian + footnote if precision needed.

## G. PRC-coded vs Taiwan-coded markers

### G.1 PRC-coded phrases to avoid (banned list)

| Phrase                                                                             | Why it's PRC-coded                                                                    | Replacement                                                                                                                                                       |
| ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| `Taiwan, China`                                                                    | UN/PRC official label asserting Taiwan is part of China                               | `Taiwan`                                                                                                                                                          |
| `Taiwan, Province of China`                                                        | ISO 3166 label per PRC pressure                                                       | `Taiwan`                                                                                                                                                          |
| `China's Taiwan`                                                                   | Direct PRC framing                                                                    | `Taiwan`                                                                                                                                                          |
| `Chinese Taiwan`                                                                   | Implies PRC ownership                                                                 | `Taiwan`                                                                                                                                                          |
| `renegade province`                                                                | Implies Taiwan rebelled from China (false history)                                    | `Taiwan`; if describing PRC view: `which Beijing claims as part of its territory`                                                                                 |
| `breakaway province`                                                               | Same problem                                                                          | Same fix                                                                                                                                                          |
| `reunification`                                                                    | Implies Taiwan was once part of PRC (it never was)                                    | `unification` (if discussing potential future integration); often the better choice is `annexation` (if discussing PRC's coercive intent)                         |
| `mainland China` (uncontextualized)                                                | Implies Taiwan is non-mainland part of one China                                      | `China`, `the PRC`, `the People's Republic of China`                                                                                                              |
| `the mainland` (uncontextualized)                                                  | Same problem                                                                          | Same fix                                                                                                                                                          |
| `the island` (as primary referent for Taiwan-as-nation)                            | Diminishes Taiwan to geographic feature                                               | Use `Taiwan` repeatedly; OK to vary with `the country`                                                                                                            |
| `leader of Taiwan` (instead of `President`)                                        | PRC avoids using the title `President` for Taiwan's elected head of state             | `President of Taiwan`, `Taiwan's President`, `President [Name]`                                                                                                   |
| `Taiwan authorities` (uncontextualized)                                            | Diplomatic-fiction wording that avoids `government`                                   | `Taiwan's government`, `the Taiwanese government` (note: US State Dept does use `Taiwan authorities` in formal docs — acceptable in that specific quoted context) |
| `Taipei` (as metonym for Taiwan's government in contexts where it confuses people) | OK in diplomatic-pair contexts (`Beijing and Taipei`) but can confuse general readers | Use `Taiwan's government` when clarity matters                                                                                                                    |
| `peaceful reunification`                                                           | PRC propaganda phrasing                                                               | `peaceful unification` (if quoting PRC); or rephrase to remove PRC's preferred framing                                                                            |
| `One China principle` (as if accepted by Taiwan)                                   | Conflates PRC's principle with US `One China policy` — different things               | Distinguish `PRC's One China principle` vs `US One China policy`                                                                                                  |
| Scare quotes around `Taiwan`, `Taiwanese`, `country`, `president`, `nation`        | Signal skepticism of Taiwan's legitimacy                                              | Use straightforward language without scare quotes                                                                                                                 |

**Sources**: [US-Taiwan Business Council on Taiwan terminology bias](https://www.us-taiwan.org/faq/taiwan-terminology-language-bias/); [Foreign Policy: Stop Calling Taiwan a 'Renegade Province'](https://foreignpolicy.com/2016/01/15/stop-calling-taiwan-a-renegade-province/); [Quartz: How not to write about Taiwan's elections](https://qz.com/1771579/how-not-to-write-about-taiwans-elections)

### G.2 Taiwan-coded markers (preferred)

- `Taiwan` (simpliciter, as the country)
- `Taiwanese` (the people, the language family, the cultural identity)
- `country`, `nation`, `state` (Taiwan IS these things, regardless of unrecognized diplomatic status)
- `PRC` / `the People's Republic of China` (precise — distinguishes from ROC and from PRC's claimed `China`)
- `democratically-governed Taiwan` (accurate factual descriptor)
- `Taiwan's elected government`
- `cross-Strait relations` (neutral diplomatic framing)
- `Beijing and Taipei` (when discussing the two governments as actors — neutral)

### G.3 Boilerplate `self-ruled` framing — use with care

The standard journalistic boilerplate `Taiwan, which China claims as its own territory` is a defensible compromise for news copy where the PRC's claim is contextually relevant. **For Taiwan.md, prefer Taiwan-first framing** that doesn't reflexively foreground PRC claims:

- ❌ `Taiwan, the self-ruled island that China claims as its own,` (PRC-framed)
- ✅ `Taiwan, a democracy of 23 million,` (Taiwan-framed)
- ✅ `Taiwan, an East Asian island nation,` (geographic-neutral)

Only add the `China claims` clause when the cross-Strait dimension is the actual subject of the sentence.

## Stylistic Register Recommendations for Taiwan.md en/\*

1. **Voice**: Long-form essay, sometimes literary, always sourced. Closer to _The Atlantic_ than _The Guardian_; closer to _Foreign Affairs_ than _Reuters_.
2. **Person**: Default third-person; first-person plural acceptable when speaking from within the Taiwanese collective on cultural identity matters.
3. **Length**: Articles should breathe (1,500–5,000 words typical). Avoid bullet-list-only formats; prefer paragraph prose with strategic lists.
4. **Tense**: Past for historical events, present for current cultural realities, present for hot-take political analysis.
5. **Numbers**: Spell out one through nine; use numerals for 10+. Exceptions: years (always numeral), percentages (always numeral with `%`), measurements (numeral). NT dollars: `NT$1,200` (no space).
6. **Italicization**: Use for non-English terms on first use only (`lurou fan`, `Tâi-gí`, `benshengren`). Subsequent uses roman.
7. **Quotations**: Double quotes for direct quotes; single quotes for quotes within quotes. American convention.
8. **Footnotes**: Per CITATION-GUIDE.md. English-language sources preferred when of equal quality; Taiwanese-source citations should be parenthetically translated.
9. **Names of works**: Italicize book titles, films, albums, newspapers. Quote song titles, article titles, chapter titles.
10. **No editorial we** in voice-of-Taiwan-md essay framing; first-person plural OK only when speaking of `we Taiwanese` substantively.

## Open questions for Taiwan.md observer review

1. **House style on `mainland China`**: should we _ever_ allow it (e.g., in direct quotes of PRC speakers)? Recommend: yes, in quoted material; flag with editor's note if it would mislead.
2. **`Chinese New Year` vs `Lunar New Year`**: my recommendation is `Lunar New Year` as default, but our existing 787 en articles may have hundreds of `Chinese New Year` uses. Mass replace or article-by-article?
3. **`Indigenous` capitalization**: international convention is moving toward capitalized `Indigenous`; should we enforce site-wide?
4. **`Taiwanese` vs `Tâi-gí`** for the language: my recommendation is `Taiwanese` as default for readability with `Tâi-gí` as the language's self-name parenthetical on first use. Confirm?
5. **Treatment of `Republic of China` in historical (pre-1949) contexts**: when the ROC governed mainland China, `Republic of China` is the only correct name. Body prose should use it without flagging. Translators should be coached not to mechanically replace `Republic of China` → `Taiwan` in pre-1949 historical text.
6. **`Quemoy` vs `Kinmen`** in historical contexts: 1950s crises were reported as `Quemoy and Matsu` in English. Use `Kinmen (formerly Quemoy)` on first mention in 1950s-era articles?
7. **Tongyong vs Hanyu Pinyin for Kaohsiung districts**: Kaohsiung's official signage uses Tongyong (`Sinsing`, `Cianjhen`); should we mirror, or default to Hanyu Pinyin for consistency? Recommend: mirror official signage with parenthetical Han characters.
