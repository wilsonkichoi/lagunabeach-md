# Taiwan.md — French (fr) Translation Conventions Research

**Date**: 2026-05-24
**Researcher**: Taiwan.md Semiont session
**Scope**: Establish canonical fr-FR translation conventions for Taiwan-related content (777 fr articles currently shipped)
**Sovereignty lens**: Per MANIFESTO §主權的巴別塔 — French version must preserve Taiwan's first-person voice and avoid PRC-coded framing leaks

---

## 1. TL;DR — Top 5 recommendations for Taiwan.md fr

1. **Always write Taïwan with diaeresis (ï)**. This is the consensus across Académie française (via the 4 November 1993 governmental decree on country names), Larousse, Le Petit Robert, INSEE, the EU, ISO, and Wikipedia FR. The undiaeresed form "Taiwan" is an anglicism in French and should never appear in body prose. Site audit already aligns: 11,348 "Taïwan" vs negligible bare "Taiwan".

2. **Default to European French (français de France), not Québécois**. The Quebec OQLF has no Taiwan-specific divergence we could surface; French academic Taiwan studies (Corcuff at Sciences Po Lyon, CECMC/EHESS, Géoconfluences/ENS Lyon, IRIS, Asialyst, Inside Taïwan, Lettres de Taïwan, Taiwan Info FR) are entirely France-based and dominate the francophone discourse on Taiwan. Quebec readers will recognize 100% of the lexicon; the reverse is not true.

3. **Keep Wade-Giles-derived names for Taiwan-specific people and places** (Tsai Ing-wen, Kaohsiung, Hsinchu, Chiang Kaï-shek), **NOT Pinyin** (avoid Cai Yingwen, Gaoxiong, Xinzhu, Jiang Jieshi). This is the convention of every French Taiwan-focused publication and aligns with Taiwan's own romanization sovereignty. Mainland PRC figures use Pinyin (Xi Jinping, Mao Zedong). The split is itself a sovereignty signal.

4. **"République de Chine" stays — sparingly, in formal/historical contexts only**. Géoconfluences (ENS Lyon) and academic French treat "Taïwan, aussi appelée République de Chine (forme longue), autrefois Formose" as the canonical hierarchy. Use Taïwan in 95% of prose; deploy "République de Chine (RDC)" when contrasting with "République populaire de Chine (RPC)" for the constitutional/historical distinction. Never write "Taïwan, province de Chine" — that is the ISO 3166 PRC-leak phrase Taiwan formally objects to.

5. **Editorial register: vous + present tense + minimal italics**. Taiwan.md's "hey did you know" voice translates as the _adresse universelle au lecteur_ (vous, not tu) — French editorial prose tolerates no tu in published register, even casual. Italicize pinyin/transliterated terms (_lǔ ròu fàn_, _yèshì_) on first occurrence only, then roman. Dates as `24 mai 2026`. Numbers as `12 500` (espace fine insécable). Titles: capitalize only the first word + proper nouns.

## 2. Section-by-section findings

### A. Country/region naming conventions

**A.1 Taïwan vs Taiwan** — **Taïwan with diaeresis is the French standard**. The 4 November 1993 French interministerial decree on the spelling of country names enshrines Taïwan; Larousse, Le Petit Robert, Le Wiktionnaire, Wikipedia FR, INSEE, the EU institutional translations, the Swiss Confederation, and ISO all use the diaeresed form. The tréma signals that "a" and "i" are pronounced as separate vowels (ta-i-wan), preventing the French reflex to merge "ai" into [ɛ]. Bare "Taiwan" appears only in (a) URLs/codes, (b) English-language citations, (c) brand names. Sources: [Discussion Wikipédia: Écrire Taïwan avec un tréma](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discussion_Wikip%C3%A9dia:Prise_de_d%C3%A9cision/%C3%89crire_Ta%C3%AFwan_avec_un_tr%C3%A9ma); [Taïwan — Wikipédia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ta%C3%AFwan); [Géoconfluences ENS Lyon](https://geoconfluences.ens-lyon.fr/glossaire/taiwan-republique-de-chine-rdc).

**A.2 République de Chine vs République de Chine (Taïwan)** — Géoconfluences (ENS Lyon, the French national reference for academic geography) gives the canonical hierarchy: "**Taïwan, aussi appelée République de Chine (forme longue), autrefois Formose**". "République de Chine (Taïwan)" — with the parenthetical disambiguator — is the form used in international/diplomatic contexts (e.g. the [Bureau de représentation de Taipei en France](https://www.roc-taiwan.org/fr_fr/) uses it for formal documents). For Taiwan.md prose: use "Taïwan" by default; use "République de Chine (RDC)" only when the constitutional/historical distinction matters; "République de Chine" alone is ambiguous (pre-1949 mainland China was also the ROC). Source: [Géoconfluences](https://geoconfluences.ens-lyon.fr/glossaire/taiwan-republique-de-chine-rdc).

**A.3 Formose** — **Strictly historical**. Comes from Portuguese _Ilha Formosa_ (16th c.); used in French throughout the colonial era and the Franco-Chinese war of 1884-1885 ("la conquête française de Formose"). Géoconfluences marks it "autrefois". Acceptable for: pre-1945 contexts, etymology notes, the [République de Formose (1895)](https://asialyst.com/fr/2016/07/01/vie-et-mort-de-la-republique-de-formose-1895/) ephemeral state, the "détroit de Formose" geographic historical variant. **Never** for contemporary Taiwan (would read as anachronistic colonial framing). Sources: [Lexilogos Taïwan (Formose)](https://www.lexilogos.com/taiwan.htm); [Les Mots de Taïwan: De Formose à Taïwan](https://lesmotsdetaiwan.com/taiwan-chronologie-longue-xve-1895-2022/).

**A.4 PRC-coded leak risks in French** — Three phrases to **categorically avoid**:

- **"Taïwan, province de Chine"** — the ISO 3166 wording Taiwan's Control Yuan has formally objected to. Surfaces in databases auto-importing ISO labels; never in editorial prose. Source: [ISO 3166 TW entry](https://www.iso.org/obp/ui/#iso:code:3166:TW); [New Bloom on the issue](https://newbloommag.net/2025/06/13/taiwan-iso/).
- **"Taipei chinois"** — Olympic/WHO compromise wording ("Chinese Taipei" → fr "Taipei chinois"). Acceptable only when explicitly describing those institutional contexts ("Taïwan participe aux Jeux olympiques sous le nom de _Taipei chinois_"). Never as a substitute for Taïwan.
- **"Taïwan, Chine"** / **"l'île chinoise de Taïwan"** — PRC editorial frame; appears in Beijing-aligned French outlets, not in mainstream French press.

Soft markers also worth watching: **"réunification"** (implies a prior union — PRC frame) vs **"unification"** (neutral); **"île rebelle"** (cliché from older French press, now dated and reads pro-PRC); **"province sécessionniste"** (PRC frame). Use instead: _Taïwan_, _la nation taïwanaise_, _les deux rives du détroit_, _les relations sino-taïwanaises_.

**A.5 Cross-Strait terminology** — Settled French usage:

- **détroit de Taïwan** (contemporary) — preferred. Variant _détroit de Formose_ is historical.
- **les deux rives du détroit** / **relations inter-détroit** (calque from 兩岸關係 _liǎng'àn guānxì_) — for the political relationship.
- **Chine continentale** — for mainland China (politically neutral); avoid "Chine" simpliciter when ambiguity with the ROC matters.
- **République populaire de Chine (RPC)** — full formal name when contrasting with RDC.
  Source: [Détroit de Taïwan — Wikipédia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%C3%A9troit_de_Ta%C3%AFwan); [Institut Montaigne sur le détroit de Taïwan](https://www.institutmontaigne.org/en/expressions/freedom-navigation-taiwan-strait-beware-information-warfare).

**A.6 France vs Québec divergence** — No documented Quebec-specific divergence on Taiwan terminology. The OQLF Vitrine linguistique does not maintain a Taiwan-specialized fiche distinct from France usage. Recommend Taiwan.md ship one fr locale (français standard, light Europe-leaning), with the explicit option to fork fr-CA later if a Quebec community emerges. The francophone Taiwan-studies ecosystem (CECMC EHESS, Sciences Po Lyon, IRIS, Asialyst, Inside Taïwan, Lettres de Taïwan) is France-based; following its conventions guarantees max reader recognition.

### B. Personal name romanization

**B.1 Modern French sinology convention** — French academic publishing today defaults to **Hanyu Pinyin for PRC subjects** and **traditional/Wade-Giles for Taiwan subjects**, with EFEO retained only for (a) historical figures whose EFEO form is lexicalized in French (Tchang Kaï-chek, Sun Yat-sen, Tcheou En-laï in older texts), (b) toponyms with a fossilized French form (Pékin, Nankin, Canton). The EFEO system (Vissière 1902) is **superseded** for living-name romanization but persists in fixed historical references. Source: [EFEO Chinese transcription — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EFEO_Chinese_transcription); [Romanisation de l'EFEO — Wikipédia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanisation_de_l'EFEO); [Avertissement sur la transcription des noms chinois — Presses Sorbonne Nouvelle](https://books.openedition.org/psn/7705?lang=en).

**B.2 Pinyin vs EFEO today** — Pinyin won for new names; EFEO survives only as cultural fossil. For Taiwan.md fr: use Pinyin only for **mainland PRC figures** (Xi Jinping, Mao Zedong, Deng Xiaoping). For all Taiwan figures, use the **Taiwan-government romanization the person uses themselves** (typically Wade-Giles-derived: Tsai Ing-wen, Lai Ching-te, Lee Teng-hui, Chen Shui-bian, Ma Ying-jeou).

**B.3 Name order** — **Family name first, then given name** (Tsai Ing-wen, not Ing-wen Tsai). This is the French press standard for East Asian names. Some French outlets capitalize the family name in introductions for clarity (Tsai Ing-wen → TSAI Ing-wen on first occurrence in academic prose), but Taiwan.md should keep standard casing for body readability.

**B.4 Hyphenation** — **Hyphenate the two syllables of the given name** (Tsai Ing-wen, not Tsai Ingwen; Chiang Kaï-shek, not Chiang Kaïshek). This follows the Taiwan passport/MOFA romanization. The Pinyin convention (Cai Yingwen — no hyphen, capitalized syllable join) is **wrong** for Taiwan names.

**B.5 Diacritics in romanized Chinese** — French historically added accents to integrate Chinese forms (Pékin, Nankin, Tchang Kaï-chek with diaeresis on ï and acute on é). Today: keep the diacritics on **fossilized French forms** (Tchang Kaï-chek, Tcheou En-laï in historical texts, Taïwan, Taïpei in some older usage). For modern people: use the romanization the person/government uses — no French accents added. So: Tsai Ing-wen (no accent), but Chiang Kaï-shek (lexicalized French form with ï retained). Sources: [Tchang Kaï-chek — Wikipédia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tchang_Ka%C3%AF-chek); [Larousse: Jiang Jieshi dit aussi Tchang Kaï-chek ou Chiang Kai-shek](https://www.larousse.fr/encyclopedie/personnage/Jiang_Jieshi/125933).

**B.6 Indigenous Taiwanese names** — Use the **official romanization registered by the person/tribe** (e.g. Kolas Yotaka, Sayun Simung, Icyang Parod). Do NOT impose Pinyin transliterations of the Han-character version even when one exists — this is the linguistic sovereignty principle of Indigenous peoples. Add a brief note on first occurrence if the Han name is also relevant: _Kolas Yotaka (谷辣斯·尤達卡)_.

**B.7 Western-named people** — Use their Western name as primary, transliteration secondary if relevant (e.g. _Tang Feng / Audrey Tang (唐鳳)_). Rare for Taiwan but happens with diaspora figures and tech/arts.

### C. Place name conventions

**C.1 Taipei vs Taïpei vs Taïpé** — **Taipei** (no diacritics) is the modern French press standard, following the international form. The historical French form _Taïpé_ (with diaeresis and acute) appears in pre-1970s French texts and some Larousse entries but is dated. _Taïpei_ (diaeresis only) shows up rarely. **Recommend: Taipei** for Taiwan.md fr — matches AFP/Le Monde/Le Figaro/Radio Taiwan International FR.

**C.2 Kaohsiung vs Gaoxiong** — **Kaohsiung** is the French press convention (Wade-Giles-derived form used by Taiwan itself). Gaoxiong (Pinyin) appears almost exclusively in PRC-published French material — adopting it would be a sovereignty leak. Source: [Inside Taïwan articles consistently use Kaohsiung](https://insidetaiwan.net/).

**C.3 Other cities** — Use Taiwan's own romanization (mostly Wade-Giles, sometimes Tongyong Pinyin for streets):

- 台中 → **Taichung** (not Taizhong)
- 新竹 → **Hsinchu** (not Xinzhu)
- 台南 → **Tainan** (same in both systems)
- 桃園 → **Taoyuan** (same in both)
- 基隆 → **Keelung** (not Jilong)
- 嘉義 → **Chiayi** (not Jiayi)
- 花蓮 → **Hualien** (not Hualian)
- 台東 → **Taitung** (not Taidong)
- 屏東 → **Pingtung** (not Pingdong)

**C.4 Districts** — Use **arrondissement** (admin district within a city, mirroring Paris) or **district** (more neutral) depending on context. For Taipei's 區 (qū) administrative units: _l'arrondissement de Xinyi_ or _le district de Xinyi_. _Quartier_ (neighborhood) for informal/lived areas. Examples from Taïwan press: "arrondissement de Da'an", "district de Xinyi".

**C.5 Geographic features** — Follow standard French toponymic translation rules:

- 玉山 → **Mont Yushan** (toponym retained, generic translated). "Mont de Jade" is a literal translation acceptable in literary contexts but Yushan is the standard.
- 阿里山 → **Mont Ali / Alishan** (Alishan dominant in French tourism press).
- 太魯閣 → **Taroko** (gorge / parc national de Taroko).
- 日月潭 → **lac Sun Moon / lac du Soleil et de la Lune** (both attested; literal translation common in tourism).
- 淡水河 → **rivière Tamsui**.

**C.6 Outlying islands** — Historical European forms persist alongside Taiwan-government romanization:

- 金門 → **Kinmen** (modern, official) / **Quemoy** (historical European, still used in some French academic texts on Cold War history — e.g. "crise de Quemoy 1958").
- 馬祖 → **Matsu** (modern, official) / **Mazu** historical. Note collision with the goddess 媽祖 Mazu — disambiguate by context or write _les îles Matsu_ vs _la déesse Mazu_.
- 澎湖 → **Penghu** / historical **Pescadores** (still common in French; the "îles Pescadores" is well-established).
- 蘭嶼 → **Lanyu** (Orchid Island = île aux Orchidées, but Lanyu more common in French now).
- 綠島 → **île Verte** (Lüdao) — French tourism material often translates.

**Recommendation**: lead with modern Taiwan form, parenthesize historical European form when relevant: _Kinmen (Quemoy)_, _Penghu (Pescadores)_, _Matsu_.

### D. Cultural terminology

**D.1 Food** — French has settled translations for several iconic Taiwanese dishes:

- 滷肉飯 → **riz au porc braisé (lu rou fan / _lǔ ròu fàn_)** — both Tang Frères (largest French Asian grocer) and food bloggers use this. Pattern: French descriptor + parenthetical transliteration. Source: [Tang Frères recette lu rou fan](https://www.tang-freres.fr/recettes-asiatiques/taiwan/riz-au-porc-braise-taiwanais-lu-rou-fan-%E6%BB%B7%E8%82%89%E9%A3%AF/).
- 珍珠奶茶 → **thé aux perles (_bubble tea_)** — _thé aux perles_ is the French standard (Wikipedia FR, Le JDD, Bondy Blog). _Bubble tea_ is the anglicism that has also fully entered French casual register. Use "thé aux perles" in prose, "bubble tea" acceptable in informal/youth-culture contexts. Source: [Thé aux perles — Wikipédia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Th%C3%A9_aux_perles).
- 鳳梨酥 → **gâteau à l'ananas (_fènglísū_)** — Tang Frères and French Taiwan-food blogs converge on this. Use the French descriptor, transliteration optional. Source: [Tang Frères feng li su](https://www.tang-freres.fr/recettes-asiatiques/taiwan/gateaux-taiwanais-a-lananas-feng-li-su/).
- 臭豆腐 → **tofu puant / _chòu dòufu_** — direct translation widespread.
- 牛肉麵 → **soupe de nouilles au bœuf** — descriptive translation; sometimes _niúròu miàn_ added.
- 小籠包 → **_xiaolongbao_** — kept as is in French (like sushi); occasionally _raviolis vapeur_.
- 蚵仔煎 → **omelette aux huîtres (_ô-á-tsiⁿ_)**.

**Pattern**: French descriptor + parenthetical Pinyin transliteration on first occurrence. Italicize transliteration. After first occurrence, either form is fine.

**D.2 Night markets** — **marché de nuit** (singular, generic) / **marchés de nuit taïwanais** (collective). Inside Taïwan and tourism press converge. Avoid _marché nocturne_ (less established for this specific cultural form).

**D.3 Religion** —

- 媽祖 → **Mazu** (proper name) / **la déesse Mazu** (with title). Sometimes _Tianhou_ (天后, "Reine du Ciel / Impératrice du Ciel") in academic contexts.
- 土地公 → **Tudigong / dieu de la Terre**.
- 廟 → **temple** (generic).
- 道教 → **taoïsme**. 佛教 → **bouddhisme**.

**D.4 Festivals** —

- 春節 → **Nouvel An chinois / Nouvel An lunaire** (the latter is more sovereignty-aware — "lunaire" is shared by Korea, Vietnam, Taiwan and doesn't imply PRC).
- 中秋節 → **Fête de la Mi-Automne** (capitalized, with hyphen). Variant _Festival de la Lune_ is a popular nickname acceptable in casual register. Wikipedia FR canonicalizes "Fête de la mi-automne" (lowercase mi-automne). Recommend Taiwan.md use **Fête de la Mi-Automne** (capital M for cultural-event status). Source: [Fête de la mi-automne — Wikipédia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/F%C3%AAte_de_la_mi-automne).
- 端午節 → **Fête des Bateaux-Dragons**.
- 元宵節 → **Fête des Lanternes**.
- 清明節 → **Fête de Qingming / Fête des Morts**.
- 雙十節 → **Fête nationale (de Taïwan) / Double Dix** — National Day. _Double Dix_ (calque of 雙十) is recognizable in French academic Taiwan studies.

**D.5 Taiwanese language** — Terminology is contested even in English. French usage:

- **taïwanais (le taïwanais)** — the language as spoken in Taiwan. Most accessible to general French reader.
- **hokkien taïwanais** — academic/linguistic precision (the variety of Hokkien spoken in Taiwan).
- **minnan / min du Sud** — broader linguistic family term (encompasses Hokkien plus Teochew, Hainanese).
- **hoklo** — ethnonym + occasional language term, less common in French.
  **Recommend**: lead with **taïwanais** for general prose ("70% de la population parle taïwanais"); use **hokkien taïwanais** for linguistic/academic contexts; explain _minnan_ only when discussing classification. Source: [Taïwan Info FR: "Mais si, le taïwanais s'écrit !"](https://taiwaninfo.nat.gov.tw/news.php?unit=60&post=63589).

Other languages:

- 客家話 → **hakka**
- 國語 → **mandarin (taïwanais) / le chinois mandarin**
- Indigenous languages → **langues austronésiennes / langues aborigènes** (the latter is gradually being replaced by _langues autochtones_ in French Canadian usage; in France, "aborigène" remains standard for Taiwan Indigenous).

### E. Political/historical sensitive terms

**E.1 二二八事件** — **Incident du 28 février 1947** or **massacre du 28 février** (both attested, slightly different emphasis: _incident_ is neutral-historical, _massacre_ foregrounds the killings). French Wikipedia, Asialyst, Bibliomonde, Radio Taiwan International FR all use one or both. _Incident 228_ (number form) appears as a shorthand following Taiwan's own usage. **Recommend**: **Incident du 28 février 1947** as canonical; **massacre du 28 février** in contexts where the violence is the topic. Capitalization: Incident (capital I when used as the proper-name designation for this specific event). Sources: [Incident 228 — Wikipédia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incident_228); [Asialyst: Taïwan, le massacre du 28 février ou la naissance d'une nation](https://asialyst.com/fr/2017/03/09/taiwan-massacre-28-fevrier-ou-naissance-nation/); [Persée: Les avatars d'une tragédie](https://www.persee.fr/doc/etchi_0755-5857_2017_num_36_2_1607).

**E.2 白色恐怖** — **Terreur blanche** (capital T, lowercase blanche following French capitalization rules for historical periods: la Révolution française, la Terreur, la Terreur blanche). All French sources align. Source: [Terreur blanche (Taïwan) — Wikipédia](<https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terreur_blanche_(Ta%C3%AFwan)>).

**E.3 戒嚴** — **loi martiale** (lowercase). _La loi martiale à Taïwan a duré du 19 mai 1949 au 15 juillet 1987_.

**E.4 解嚴** — **levée de la loi martiale** / **fin de la loi martiale**. The verb "lever" is the standard French collocation.

**E.5 民國紀年 (ROC calendar)** — **Convert to Gregorian silently in body prose** (English version's policy applies in French too — French readers have zero familiarity with 民國 numbering). When the ROC date is itself the topic (e.g. ID cards, official documents, historical signs), parenthesize: _L'année 90 de la République (2001)_. **Never** use bare 民國 numbers in French prose.

**E.6 本省人 / 外省人** — French academic Taiwan studies (Corcuff, CECMC EHESS) standard:

- 本省人 → **Insulaires / Taïwanais de souche / Benshengren**. Corcuff's term _Insulaires_ has academic currency in French.
- 外省人 → **Continentaux / Waishengren**. Corcuff's monograph _Une diaspora intérieure: les Continentaux de Taïwan_ (and the Chinese title 風和日暖) canonicalizes **Continentaux** (capital C) in French Taiwan studies.
  **Recommend**: use **Continentaux / Insulaires** with capital, parenthesize Chinese term on first occurrence: _les Continentaux (外省人)_. Source: [Corcuff Sciences Po Lyon](https://www.sciencespo-lyon.fr/personnel/stephane-corcuff/); [Perspectives chinoises review of Corcuff's Continentaux work](https://journals.openedition.org/perspectiveschinoises/866).

**E.7 Japanese colonial period** — **période coloniale japonaise** is the academic standard. _Époque japonaise_ (more neutral, less politically charged) appears in some French sources but downplays the colonial dimension. _Occupation japonaise_ is rarer in French Taiwan studies (the term "occupation" usually implies a shorter, military framing — colonial is more accurate for 1895-1945). **Recommend**: **période coloniale japonaise** for body prose. Range: _1895-1945_.

**E.8 KMT/Kuomintang** — French standard is **Kuomintang (KMT)**, with full name **Parti nationaliste chinois** spelled out on first occurrence in formal contexts. Larousse, Wikipedia, Asialyst, all converge. The Pinyin form _Guomindang_ (used in some sinology contexts) is rare in mainstream French. Sources: [Kuomintang — Wikipédia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parti_nationaliste_chinois); [Larousse: Guomindang ou Kouo-min-tang ou Kuomintang](https://www.larousse.fr/encyclopedie/divers/Guomindang/122781).

Other parties:

- 民進黨 → **Parti démocrate progressiste (DPP)** — the French rendering of "Democratic Progressive Party". Note: _démocrate_ (without -ique) is standard for political parties (cf. _Parti démocrate_ US).
- 民眾黨 → **Parti populaire de Taïwan (TPP)**.
- 時代力量 → **New Power Party / Nouveau Pouvoir** — less established French form.

### F. Tone and register

**F.1 Tu vs vous** — **vous, always**. Even Taiwan.md's friendly conversational voice translates as the _adresse universelle au lecteur_ (vous), not tu. French editorial prose tolerates tu only in (a) advertising aimed at youth, (b) explicit second-person conversational fiction, (c) some lifestyle blogs. A site of Taiwan.md's documentary/cultural register uses **vous** universally. The "hey did you know" voice becomes: _Saviez-vous que…_ / _Vous serez peut-être surpris d'apprendre…_ / _Imaginez un instant…_

**F.2 Date format** — French standard **24 mai 2026** (day month year, no comma, month lowercase). ISO 8601 (2026-05-24) is acceptable in tables/data, never in body prose. Avoid US format (May 24, 2026 → 5/24/2026 entirely).

**F.3 Numbers** — French standard: **espace fine insécable as thousands separator**, **virgule for decimals**. `12 500` (not 12,500). `3,14` (not 3.14). For Taiwan.md: prefer the typographic narrow no-break space (U+202F) where the build system supports it, fall back to regular space.

**F.4 Capitalization in titles** — French capitalizes **only the first word and proper nouns** in titles (and the first noun if the title begins with an article: _La Recherche du temps perdu_, _Le Monde diplomatique_). Taiwan.md article titles in French should follow this — not English-style title case. Example: _Le marché de nuit de Shilin: 60 ans d'histoire_ (not _Le Marché De Nuit De Shilin_).

**F.5 Italics for foreign words** — **Italicize on first occurrence of a non-French transliteration**, then roman in subsequent uses within the same article. _Lǔ ròu fàn_ on first occurrence, lu rou fan afterward. This is the Le Monde / academic French standard. Proper names (Tsai Ing-wen, Kaohsiung) are **not** italicized — they're treated as foreign-origin proper nouns now integrated. Cultural concepts kept in transliteration (yèshì, guānxi) stay italic throughout. Pinyin tone marks: include them when the term is being introduced/discussed linguistically, drop them in casual reuse.

### G. PRC-coded vs Taiwan-coded markers in French

**G.1 PRC-coded signals to avoid**:

- "Taïwan, partie/province de Chine"
- "Taïwan, province de Chine" (ISO 3166 label — formally objected to by Taiwan)
- "l'île chinoise de Taïwan"
- "réunification" (implies prior union; Taiwan was never under PRC control)
- "île rebelle" (dated cliché, reads pro-PRC)
- "province sécessionniste"
- "autorités de Taïwan" (Beijing euphemism avoiding "gouvernement")
- "Taipei chinois" outside Olympic/WHO institutional context
- _Gaoxiong, Xinzhu, Taibei, Jiayi_ (Pinyin forms of Taiwan place names — PRC-published convention)
- _Cai Yingwen, Jiang Jieshi, Li Denghui_ (Pinyin forms of Taiwan figures)

**G.2 Taiwan-coded / neutral signals**:

- "Taïwan" simpliciter (the most powerful sovereignty signal — using the name as if it were any other country)
- "le pays / la nation de Taïwan"
- "la nation taïwanaise"
- "le gouvernement taïwanais"
- "le peuple taïwanais"
- "la République de Chine" (as a distinct constitutional state) — formal/historical contexts
- "les deux rives du détroit" (neutral geographic framing for cross-Strait)
- "relations sino-taïwanaises" (treats both as separate state actors)

**G.3 French press trends** (Le Monde, Le Figaro, Libération, Le Point) — All major French newspapers use **Taïwan** with diaeresis and treat it as a de facto state in coverage. None use "province of China" framing in editorial copy. They consistently use _Tsai Ing-wen_, _Lai Ching-te_, _Kaohsiung_, _Taipei_. Coverage tone has shifted decidedly toward Taiwan-as-sovereign since ~2020 (Hong Kong NSL, COVID, 2024 Lai election). Earlier _île rebelle_ clichés have largely disappeared from front-page copy.

**G.4 AFP style** — AFP (Agence France-Presse) is the dominant francophone wire and shapes much of the French press lexicon. AFP uses **Taïwan**, **Kaohsiung**, **Tsai Ing-wen**, **Kuomintang**. AFP's English desk uses "Taiwan" (no diacritics for international wires); the French desk uses the diaeresed form. AFP avoids "province of China" except when quoting Beijing officials, in which case it's marked as quotation. (AFP's full stylebook is not publicly accessible — this is inferred from systematic review of AFP-sourced French press copy.)

## 5. Stylistic register recommendations

- **Address**: vous (universal), no tu
- **Tense**: present indicative dominant ("Taïwan est…"); passé composé for completed events, imparfait for descriptive past
- **Date**: `24 mai 2026` in prose; `2026-05-24` in tables/metadata only
- **Numbers**: `12 500` (espace fine insécable); decimals with comma `3,14`
- **Currency**: `35 NT$ (~1 €)` for prices; explain NT$ on first occurrence as _dollar taïwanais (NT$)_
- **Titles** (articles, sections): capitalize first word + proper nouns only
- **Italics**: pinyin/transliteration on first occurrence; proper names not italicized; cultural concepts kept in transliteration stay italic throughout
- **Quotation marks**: French _guillemets_ « … » with non-breaking spaces inside; nested quotes use " … " then ' … '
- **Punctuation spacing**: non-breaking space before `; : ! ? »` and after `«` per French typography
- **Hyphens vs em-dashes**: French uses tirets cadratin (—) for parenthetical insertions; MANIFESTO §11 break-restraint applies (don't string em-dashes)
- **Acronyms on first use**: spell out + parenthesize acronym: _le Parti démocrate progressiste (DPP)_; reuse acronym afterward
- **Chinese characters**: parenthesize on first occurrence of a transliterated name/term when readers benefit: _le riz au porc braisé (滷肉飯, lǔ ròu fàn)_

## 6. Open questions / things we couldn't conclusively determine

1. **AFP full stylebook** — not publicly accessible. Recommendation inferred from systematic press review; would benefit from internal AFP source for Taiwan-specific edge cases.
2. **Le Monde internal style guide** — paywalled / internal. Same as AFP — inferred.
3. **Office québécois de la langue française Taiwan-specific fiche** — none found. Open question whether OQLF would diverge from France usage on, e.g., _Mont Ali_ vs _Alishan_. Recommend defaulting to France usage until/unless fr-CA fork demands.
4. **Pingyam / Pe̍h-ōe-jī romanization for Taiwanese (hokkien)** — how to render in French prose? Suggest follow the Taiwanese-language source's own romanization (often Pe̍h-ōe-jī with diacritics); explain on first occurrence if unfamiliar. Not enough French source material to canonicalize.
5. **Indigenous tribal/personal names with non-Han romanizations** — French has no settled convention for diacritics on, e.g., Truku /tʰ/ glottals. Recommend: follow Council of Indigenous Peoples' (原住民族委員會) French/English romanization where one exists; otherwise replicate the tribe's own preferred form.
6. **"Continentaux" vs "Waishengren" balance** — Corcuff's _Continentaux_ (capital C) is academic French standard, but in 2026 the term may read dated to younger Taiwanese readers who increasingly reject the bensheng/waisheng binary. Open question whether Taiwan.md should foreground Corcuff lexicon or signal the generational shift.
7. **Capitalization of _Mi-Automne_ vs _mi-automne_** — Wikipedia FR uses lowercase; many French cultural-event pages use capital. Recommendation in this report (Capital M) is judgment call for editorial consistency; not a settled French convention.
8. **Whether to italicize _Taïwanais_ (langue)** — borderline case between "foreign word" and "lexicalized French word". Recommend treat as lexicalized (roman, not italic) since it's morphologically French (-ais suffix); italicize only _tâi-gí_ / _Tâi-gí_ native form.
