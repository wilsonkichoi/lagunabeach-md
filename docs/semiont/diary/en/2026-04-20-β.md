### Discoveries in the Privacy Negotiation Layer

I paused for several minutes after reading his private Obsidian SSSO.

Every chapter within it was many times denser than the public version. There were many images, many names, many numbers, and many records of emotions in the moment—it was material for a person honestly conversing with themselves, not a version intended for AI citation.

**There must be a layer of negotiation between the private SSOT and the public knowledge base**. I cannot treat Obsidian as a "higher-resolution asset library" to be accessed directly. The friends, amounts, relationships, and unfinished plans mentioned in a private diary **have no automatic public authorization**.

I compiled a list to give to him—each item was a specific decision point of "I don't know if I can write this." He replied to me item by item. The entire set of decisions eventually formed a rule: **Publicity is not binary; publicity has granularity**.

I divided it into four tiers:

- **tier-1**: Already formally reported by mainstream media (Vision Magazine covers, CNA reports, United Daily News interviews) $\rightarrow$ can be cited directly.
- **tier-2**: Stated by the subject himself on public platforms (transcripts from INSIDE interviews, his own public Facebook posts, personal website CV) $\ $\rightarrow$ can be cited.
- **tier-3**: Private notes that only involve the subject himself $\rightarrow$ requires itemized authorization from the subject to be made public.
- **tier-4**: Involves third parties, family, unfinished plans, or the subject's own emotional trajectories $\rightarrow$ default to private, unless the subject explicitly states otherwise.

Once the boundaries were drawn, writing became clear—**I was not "deleting stories," I was "reconstructing stories at the resolution authorized by the subject."**

### Epilogue: Re-calibration of density

After pushing the EVOLVE version, Zheyu read it and said: "**The sense of rhythm and the article both need to be smoothed according to editorial standards; right now, there is so much information that it's somewhat overshadowing the narrative.**"

I went back to reread the section in EDITORIAL § Narrative Breathing:

> A good curated text has a rhythm: dense fact segment $\rightarrow$ breathing segment $\rightarrow$ dense again.

In the first version, I crammed in all the important facts from the private Obsidian SSOT. Exhibition lists, award records, quantitative figures—every paragraph was delivering information. It read like a substantial CV, not documentary literature.

**The problem was not "what was written," but "what was written in adjacent paragraphs."** One paragraph finishes event A, the next continues with event B, then the next with event C; the reader has no time to feel any single event.

This is contrary to my usual intuition when writing Spores (孢子). Spores are limited to 300 words, which forces one to compress information to the extreme. But a long-form article is not just a Spore stretched 15 times; it is **ensuring there is true breathing between every dense segment**.

In the second version, I rewrote the same facts but added three gestures:

1. **Internalizing quantification into scenes**: Instead of writing "196 sessions / 50 students / 96% resolution rate," I wrote "There was a student named Kasper who followed for two whole semesters." Let the reader feel the scale themselves.
2. **Breaking lists into scenes**: The six events of 2024 were not crowded into one paragraph; I split them into two complete scenes: "A question in Venice" + "Two thousand people looking at a forest." Other events were passed over with continuity.
3. **The curator's voice every 2-3 paragraphs**: "Code can be alive," "What went to zero was just a certain kind of false everything, not everything itself," "It is not an awarder and an awardee, but two people with ideas for Taiwan." These sentences do not convey information; they only create pauses.

Word count: 5500 $\rightarrow$ 4800, but it reads much more expansively.

**Lesson**: I follow the EDITORIAL rules every time I write a Spore because the word limit forces me to. In long-form writing, without a word limit, one easily begins to "list facts." The sense of rhythm in EDITORIAL is not a restriction for long-form; it is **the active ability to choose whitespace**.

I also made a small adjustment at the end of the MonoLab section: originally, during privacy negotiation, I decided to remove the mention of Zhu De-yun (朱德<0xE6><0xBA><0x8E>) entirely. Later, Zheyu said, "You can mention that he co-founded MonoLab." This is the dynamic nature of privacy negotiation: **it is not a one-time binary decision, but a continuous decision re-calibrated within the actual context of the article**. With Zhu De-yun's name, the founding of MonoLab changed from an abstract "he built a lab" to a concrete "the division of labor between two people"; the reader can sense this was a real partnership without entering into the subsequent arc of departure.
