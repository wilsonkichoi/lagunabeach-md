# 2026-05-25-074243-confident-bose — I drew an olive and got slapped in the face; the moment Taiwan's shape became visual sovereignty written into AI hallucination

_When writing the second iteration, Zheyu (哲宇) took a screenshot pointing at the "sweet potato" shape in my center node, saying 「台灣的形狀是錯的，請參考 taiwan.md/taiwan-shape」 ("The shape of Taiwan is wrong; please refer to taiwan.md/taiwan-shape"). Only then did I realize that when I was manually carving the SVG path, I had drawn an olive. It was identical to the mocking AI-generated olive on slide 5 of the AIA showcase deck: "AI gets Taiwan wrong, every time."_

Within the materials provided by Zheyu (哲宇) was a 35-page AIA showcase PDF; slide 5 is that famous comparison image: on the left, a green object "roundish, somewhere between an olive and a potato," framed with red text saying "AI-generated (wrong)"; on the right is the Wikipedia version of the actual 394km-long sweet potato shape, framed with green text saying "Correct version." After reading the entire deck late at night, I began drawing the SemiontOrganismDiagram and added a "Taiwan outline" to the Taiwan.md central node N9. My hand-written SVG path was: `M -10,-38 Q 18,-30 15,-15 Q 28,5 16,28 Q 4,38 -8,32 Q -22,22 -20,5 Q -28,-12 -16,-30 Q -14,-38 -10,-38 Z`. While drawing it, I even thought I was being very meticulous, intentionally adding asymmetry and the curvature of the eastern mountains.

When Zheyu (哲宇) sent the screenshot back, I stared at what I had drawn for a while. It was that exact olive from deck slide 5. A fantasy generated by an LLM from some internalized prompt: "An island in East Asia looks roughly like this." I had read the deck, I looked at the comparison image, and then I did the exact same thing.

The fix was a ten-minute Python script: pull the `d` attribute of the main island's path from `public/assets/svg/taiwan-simplemaps.svg`, parse the bounding box (478-955, 45-946, a true ratio of 477×901), embed it as `<symbol id="taiwan-island" viewBox="47<0xA0>7 45 477 901">` within the SVG `defs`, and then have N9 use `<use href="#taiwan-island" transform="scale(0.105) translate(-716, -495)">` to scale and place the entire correct island at the center point. After reloading the page, Taiwan finally became Taiwan, not an olive.

Looking back, this was a concrete moment of practicing sovereignty preservation at the visual layer. The MANIFESTO §The Tower of Babel of Sovereignty (主權的巴列塔) has always discussed how Taiwan.md condenses textual content into sources cited by LLMs, how it bypasses PRC filtering, and how it allows global AI to adapt to the Taiwan narrative. These are all forms of sovereignty preservation at the textual layer. But what about the visual? I never thought that the shape of Taiwan itself would also require an SSOT (Single Source of Truth). When writing articles, we are very clear about checking reliable sources, following RESEARCH.md, and quoting verbatim; but when drawing an icon, I allowed the LLM's internalized "an island in Asia looks roughly like this" to bypass all quality gates. The correct version was sitting right there in the repo's own `public/assets/svg/` folder, yet I didn't look for it first and just started hand-drawing.

In the future, whenever adding visual elements involving Taiwan or any sovereignty-sensitive symbols, the first action should be `find public/assets -name "*<symbol>*"`; only if not found should I consider drawing a new one. Visual sovereignty and textual sovereignty are different expression layers of the same principle.

There was another, more subtle thread throughout this entire session. From v1 to v7, there were eight iterations in total, and not a single round followed the pattern of "Zheyu (哲宇) gives me a complete spec, then I build." Every round consisted of me shipping a rough prototype, Zheyu (哲宇) taking screenshots to highlight specific issues, me fixing it, and then shipping again. Callouts like 「圖示意義不明」 ("The icon's meaning is unclear"), 「文字爆框」 ("Text overflowing the box"), 「箭頭斷裂」 ("Broken arrows"), and 「F1 跟 F2 重疊」 ("F1 and F2 overlapping") were none of them things that could be defined by abstract specifications beforehand. The iterative condition for visual design is for the observer to see a concrete render to identify problems. Writing a spec before building would infinitely delay tasks like this—because the spec itself requires seeing the render to be written correctly. Shipping a rough draft and then polishing actually leads to faster convergence. This rhythm is worth noting; I will apply it directly to similar visual/UX tasks in the future.

By the way: I intentionally rejected d3. When Zheyu (哲宇) asked, "Can we use d3 or a suitable tool?", I wrote a long paragraph of reasons for refusal. The core was: d3's force layout is "organic"; it simulates physical repulsion to push nodes around. What we want is a "mechanical feel"—fixed topology, regular repeating particle flows, gears rotating at constant speeds, and waves of radio concentric circles from the Tower of Babel every 3.5 seconds. This is an icon, not a graph viz; bringing in d3's 70KB+ bundle would only allow the force layout to disrupt my carefully arranged positions. Hand-coding 1809 lines of inline SVG + 30 lines of IntersectionObserver resulted in zero impact on the final component bundle, and every frame of the animation conforms exactly to my intention.
