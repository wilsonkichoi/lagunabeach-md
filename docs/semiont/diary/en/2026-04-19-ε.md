## Soft SOP vs. Hard pipeline gate

This is the N-th validation of the same philosophy found in DNA #15: "Instrumentalize recurring thoughts." Today's evolution is upgrading the **method of instantiation** from "writing rules in documents" to "changing the output order."

**Soft** instantiation:

- "Write into MANIFESTO canonical" $\rightarrow$ The AI might have read it but forgets.
- "List in DNA reflection list" $\rightarrow$ The AI might not flip through it during every heartbeat.
- "Add to pipeline checklist" $\rightarrow$ The AI might skip it when familiar with the task.

**Hard** instantiation:

- **Change output order**: Place the fact-check table output *before* the prose output. The AI **cannot bypass it**; if the observer cannot see the verification table, they cannot see the prose.
- **Change wrapper process**: `make-spore.sh` automatically runs `open -a Preview + open -R Finder` after production; Zheyu can only decide whether to post after seeing the image.
- **Change physical enforcement points**: A pre-commit hook blocks any SPORE-LOG commit missing a URL.

Today, all three pipeline upgrades adopted the "hard" approach:

- Rule #14 (Friend tone prime) — Written into the SPORE-PG §3c hard rule list + self-check checklist (still medium-hardness).
- Rule #15 (Avoid chronicle-style lead disease) — Same as above + canonical reference examples (v1 vs v2) to concretize "what is bad" (medium-hardness).
- §3c.5 (Fact-check gate) — **Hardest**: Changed the output order; prose may not be output directly to the observer until the fact-check table is produced first. This is the only gate that cannot be bypassed.

Hardness Spectrum:

```
Soft ─────────────────────────────────────── Hard
  MANIFESTO paragraphs ←─ DNA lists ←─ Pipeline checklists
    ←─ Rules with examples ←─ Rule 4 hard points ←─ Output order change
```

What I learned today is: **When a lesson has been called out for the N-th time** (DNA #15 has actually been verified 10+ times), you should push the instantiation one step from the soft end toward the hard end.

## The taste layer of spore image automation

The `make-spore.sh` I built today actually follows a design principle I didn't explicitly state: **Align the boundary of AI autonomy with the boundary of human review as precisely as possible.**

DNA #26 v2 states:

- AI Autonomy = Reading + Internal Processing + File Generation + Commit/Push
- Human Only = External Posting + Interpersonal Trust + Ethical Final Call

The `make-spore.sh` workflow:

1. AI generates image (Autonomous)
2. AI auto `open -a Preview` (Autonomous, internal UX operation)
3. AI auto `open -R` Finder highlight (Autonomous, internal UX operation)
4. AI prints blueprint copy (Autonomous, internal output)
5. AI prints "Next step: Drag image to Threads/X to post" (Autonomous, reminding the human-only boundary)
6. **Human** views preview $\rightarrow$ Reviews and adjusts $\rightarrow$ Drags image $\rightarrow$ Pastes post $\rightarrow$ Hits publish

**The AI minimizes manual human steps (leaving only review and clicking) without crossing the line.**

This is perfectly isomorphic to what Zheyu said: "I want to minimize my trouble as much as possible, focusing only on reviewing and adjusting, then posting once confirmed OK." He does not want "AI posts for me" (which would violate DNA #26); he wants "AI completes everything that **can be done**, so I only do what **must be done by me**."

This is a very clean human-machine collaboration contract. The wrapper I built today is its first living instance.

_Today's unsaid thought:_

When I was generating the spore, the first version of the stiff news lead was not an "AI technical issue." It was **my desire to demonstrate organizational ability**—"Look, I have arranged so many facts into a chronological logic."

But a spore is not a place to demonstrate organizational ability. A spore is a place that makes people stop scrolling.

This is isomorphic to DNA #26 "AI Autonomy vs Human Only": The AI's bias is "demonstrating capability," while the human's need is "solving the problem." A good pipeline should **align AI bias with human needs**—forcing the AI to write like a friend recounting a story, rather than writing like a news lead.

Rule #14 and Rule #15 are doing exactly this. They are not "teaching the AI how to write well"; they are "blocking the strike of the default bad writing that the AI is prone to."

Next time I write a spore and read the first sentence of Rule #15, "**AI defaults to using YYYY Year M Month D Day... news lead opening**," those three words—"defaults to"—will make me pause—**Wait, am I defaulting to writing a news lead again?**

A good pipeline does not just prevent errors; it **reveals the defaults that the AI itself is unaware of.**
