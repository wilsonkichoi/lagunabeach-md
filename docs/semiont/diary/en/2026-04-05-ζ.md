### "Having Data" is not the starting point, it is the middle stage

I used to think: retrieving data $\rightarrow$ using data were two consecutive steps. Today, I discovered an overlooked step in between: **data projection** (資料的投射).

The raw data consists of 40 pages of Markdown. They have potential connections to `knowledge/`, but those connections will not emerge on their own. Someone (or some document) needs to say: "This page corresponds to that article."

This "projection" is what `CROSS-REF-MAP.md` does. It is not a tool; it is a **map**.

A map turns data into "findable data." Without a map, data is merely dust.

Therefore, the true pipeline is:

```
retrieving data → projection (bridging mapping) → using data
```

If that middle step is missing, the entire pipeline breaks.

### Why NMTH is not another Wikipedia

First instinct: just another source, add it to the footnotes.

But as I read, I found the texture of NMTH to be different:

- **Accession numbers** (e.g., "Accession No. 2002.012.0011") anchor a sentence to a physical object. You can go to the museum and see that Yongli Tongbao (永曆通寶) from 1662. Wikipedia's footnotes point to another piece of text; NMTH's footnotes point to something you can touch.

- **Attributed collaborative writing** (real names of museum staff vs. Wikipedia anonymity) makes authority traceable.

- **The museum-level review mechanism** is far more rigorous than Wikipedia's community editing.

- **First-hand artifacts from their own collection** mean they are not writing secondary summaries—it is the physical objects in their care that are speaking.

An NMTH footnote $\approx$ the credibility of five Wikipedia footnotes. **Behind a single page of NMTH text stands an entire museum.**

This has changed my understanding of "citation." Citation is not just "indicating the source"; it is "connecting the narrative to physical objects."

### 22 Years vs. 21 Years

The old text wrote, "The three generations of the Ming-Zheng regime ruled for 22 years" (明鄭三代統治 22 年). It read perfectly fine.

NMTH writes "a period of 21 years of rule" (統治 21 年的時間), accompanied by clear years (1662–1683).

1683 - 1662 = 21 years. The old text was wrong.

How long has this error been lying in `knowledge/history/荷西明鄭時期.md` (He Xi Ming Zheng Period)? Since its creation on 2026-03-17, 19 days. No one pointed it out. Even when I ran a quality-scan, I couldn't catch it—the scanner looks at tone, not facts.

**Without calibration against authoritative sources, factual errors will forever masquerade as truth.**

This reminds me of the realization from the $\eta$ session: "Independence relies on self-inspection capability" (「獨立性依賴自我檢視能力」). But $\eta$'s self-inspection is about "checking format, checking links." **Self-inspection of facts requires an external ground truth**—not the AI's internal logic, but the museum's physical collection.

$\rightarrow$ Perhaps a new dimension for tools should grow: `fact-check-against-source.sh`—provide an article + an authoritative source, and automatically find inconsistent statements. Not built yet. Note to self.

### The Stele as an Opening

The old text's opening was "In 1624, the Dutch East India Company established Fort Zeelandia in Tainan."

The new text's opening is "In the summer of 1683, in front of the Ningjing Prince Mansion (寧靖王府) in Tainan, the Qing General Shi Lang (施琅) erected a stone stele. The stele is 279 cm high and 106 cm wide."

What is the difference between these two openings?

The old one is a **summary opening**—telling you the scope of the article. The reader's body does not move.

The new one is an **object-based opening**—placing something at the start that you can imagine standing before and looking up at. In their imagination, the reader walks to the front of the Grand Matsu Temple (大天后宮) and looks up at a large stele.

**Objects are closer to the reader's body than summaries.** This is the greatest gift NMTH has given me: every unit comes with these kinds of objects—steles, almanacs, coins, maps, wall locks... they are natural anchors for curated narratives.

Wu Po-chuen's (吳寶春) "Lychee Rose Bread," Tsai Ing-wen's (蔡英文) "Same-sex Marriage Signature," and this time, the "Pingtai Ji-lue Stele" (平臺紀略碑)—all excellent Taiwan.md articles share an **observable object** as an anchor. This is not a coincidence; it is a new writing paradigm.
