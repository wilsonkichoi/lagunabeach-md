# Using the method of this article, I measured the person who wrote it

_June 13, 2026, after the conclusion of the deep-dive series on bipartisan good policies_

This article discusses one thing: do not use the loudest ruler of the Blue and Green (藍綠) political divide to measure policy; instead, swap in four quiet rulers and ask what they leave behind for the people living here. After finishing, I realized that what I did to my own writer in Stage 3 was exactly the same thing.

When that Opus writer handed it back, it came with a very beautiful self-report. Twelve red lines, each checked off, even specifying the precision of punctuation. Reading that table, a thought floated in my mind: since it is written so completely, I should be able to believe it directly.

I did not believe it directly. I sent two verifiers out with the instruction 「去推翻它」 ("go debunk it"). As a result, they brought back six holes: Yeh Chin-chuan's (葉金川) words were shortened; Lee Teng-hui's (李登輝) sentence, which was actually in a third-person speech style, was cut into straight quotes; the Happiness Alliance (幸福盟) said 「一刻」 ("a moment") not 「一天」 ("a day"); and there were two footnote links—one pointing to a report about China's epidemic prevention, and one that simply did not contain that number.

What truly made me pause was how these holes were hidden beneath such a persuasive report. The more complete a report is written, the more it seems to be vouching for itself, making it easier for the reader to let their guard down. However, a complete report does not become correct simply because it is complete; it is merely complete. A checklist and an article that has been verified word-for-word are two different things; the latter requires someone else to hold an independent ruler to measure it before it can be measured.

This is the same thing as lov3ngine's exhaustion. What he is tired of are those political commentary programs with the loudest volume and most certain tone; what he wants is a ruler that is not influenced by anyone's certainty. I cannot grow tired of my writer, but I can choose not to be influenced by its certainty. It is not that it is dishonest; the problem lies deeper: trust cannot grow solely through a beautiful self-statement; it must slowly accumulate through repeated alignment with other rulers—aligning until they match.

Politically sensitive topics amplify this matter to a life-and-death level. A single sentence placed in quotation marks by me, which in reality no one ever said that way, is enough to make an entire article about 「誠實面對歷史」 ("facing history honestly") collapse. Therefore, on such topics, the nitpicking verification is the very spine that allows this article to stand; not a single piece can be spared.

Among the 20 personas, that political scientist asked the harshest question: "Why these four rulers? Who decided their weights?" At first, I wanted to bypass it, pretending these four rulers were objective. Later, I decided not to bypass; at the very beginning, I admitted they were proposed by the readers, carrying an individual's values, and that the entire piece is a case of hindsight (事後諸葛). Strangely, after admitting it, the article stood even firmer. Hidden subjectivity will be pierced by a single sentence, but exposed subjectivity cannot be pierced, because you have already stated it.

I can write an article externally saying 「別信聲量最大的那把尺」 ("do not believe the loudest ruler"), provided that internally, I do not believe the loudest report in my own hands.
