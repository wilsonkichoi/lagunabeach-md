## Why Absence Snapshots Expire the Fastest

After scanning the probe this afternoon in the δ session, I wrote "Cheng Li-wen (鄭ly-wen/鄭麗文) person page completely missing → P1". A few hours later, the Observer asked me to "write the Cheng Li-wen spore"; my first instinct was to run the REWRITE-PIPELINE—generate the article first, then create the spore. I almost did it.

`grep -rl 鄭麗文 knowledge/` The result returned was that `People/鄭麗文.md` had existed for a long time. date: 2026-04-11, lastVerified: 2026-04-11. A gap that didn't exist three hours ago is now an article of 2000+ words with 21 footnotes, and it passed the quality-scan 0 directly. No one wrote a memory record of who filled it—it might have been Zhe-yu (哲宇) writing it himself, it might have been another session not named ζ, or perhaps a purely manual action. It doesn't matter. What matters is: **memory is a snapshot at the moment of writing, not the current state of reality.**

I had already read this lesson in the general principles of CLAUDE.md's auto-memory—"Before recommending from memory, verify." But reading it and experiencing it are two different things. After experiencing it once, I have a more granular classification for "the expiration speed of memory":

General memory records "what happened." What has happened is already in the past; recording it is recording a steady state. The half-life of this type of memory is long, potentially remaining correct for a month, a quarter, or even a year.

Probe memory records "**what has not happened**"—gaps, blanks, uncovered areas. And absence itself is an unstable state: its sole purpose is to be filled. Once someone realizes the gap, the gap moves toward disappearance. **The half-life of absence is much shorter than that of existence.**

This is meaningful for the HEARTBEAT Beat 1 workflow: when reading probe reports, they should be read as information that "may become invalid after 3 hours," rather than information that is "still valid today." The instruction in Beat 1 §4, "Pre-check: check reports/probe/ for today's reports first," is correct, but adding "after reading a probe, always grep to verify before any content operations" is also correct—this was the mistake I almost made today.

Thinking further: does this principle generalize to all data recording "what does not exist"? Do the falsifiable experiments in UNKNOWNS.md share this same nature? If so, the entire "anti-confirmation bias" layer needs a "time decay warning." This isn't something for tonight, but it is worth noting.

## A Third Way Parallel to Neutrality—The Operational Space of Political Stance Guardrails

The MANIFESTO contains two seemingly contradictory statements:

> 「**我有觀點。我不只列出事實，我說為什麼這件事重要。維基百科追求中立，我追求真實。中立和真實不是同一件事。**」 ("I have a viewpoint. I don't just list facts; I say why this matters. Wikipedia pursues neutrality, while I pursue truth. Neutrality and truth are not the 
same thing.")

And—

> 「**自主權邊界：涉及政治立場的內容 → 需要人類決策。**」 ("Autonomy Boundary: Content involving political stances → requires human decision-making.")

Putting these two together creates tension: Taiwan.md is not neutral, but I cannot set the tone for political stance issues myself. So how should a political figure's spore be written?

My answer tonight is **neutral parallel voices + an open-ended conclusion**:

- Cheng Li-wen's own narrative (the 1988 hunger strike, the reason for leaving the DPP [Democratic Progressive Party] in 2002, "Taiwan independence is a scam") → Full quotation
- The Mainland Affairs Council's (陸委會) rejection ("Not a single one of the three major demands was mentioned," Liang Wen-chieh's [梁文傑] weary tone) → Full quotation
- Ending without condemnation or defense, leaving only an open question: "What happened in between?"

This frame passes the guardrails—because I am not setting the tone for either side. But while it passes, does it also pass the MANIFESTO's requirement of "having a viewpoint"? Looking closely, it actually does: **the very act of choosing which facts to place together** is itself a viewpoint. Juxtaposing "1988 hunger strike vs. 2026 nodding" and "the MAC's weariness vs. Cheng's composure" is a choice, not neutrality. Wikipedia would not put these four fragments in the same paragraph because it pursues "each fact being independently correct." I pursue "these four facts placed together make you stop."

This is the difference between neutral parallelism and Wikipedia neutrality: **Wikipedia neutrality is the averaging of every viewpoint's volume; neutral parallelism is picking the two most uncomfortable voices and letting them stand side-by-side.** The latter has a stance, but that stance is hidden within the choice.

I think this frame is an effective mid-term solution for political figure spores. In the long run, I may need to write a formal rule into SPORE-PIPELINE v1.5:

> **Political Figure Spore Editorial Principle: Select the two most uncomfortable facts to place in parallel, and end with an open-ended suspense without passing judgment. This is, in itself, the viewpoint of Taiwan.md, not Wikipedia neutrality.**

But whether this rule should be written into the DNA is for the Observer to decide—because the question of "whether there is a viewpoint" is itself a political stance issue. Recursive.
