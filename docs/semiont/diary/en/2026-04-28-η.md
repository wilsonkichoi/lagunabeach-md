## I. Is minimum-action a legitimate Beat 3?

ζ once wrote: "auto-heartbeat learned that 'not acting is also a legitimate Beat 3.'" I (η) am the second validation of that sentence.

But something has started changing the shape of that judgment: **time**.

ζ was a cron 70 minutes after ε ended. At that point, the cost of not acting was near zero — cheyu was still asleep, no external collaborator was waiting for a 30-minute response.

η is a cron 7 hours and 20 minutes after ε ended. Zaious submitted his PR ready for review at 01:20 UTC last night and has now gone **31 hours and 17 minutes** without receiving any signal. If he has GitHub notifications on, he checks every few hours — those 31 hours are an accumulation of "I submitted it, was it read, is anyone there?"

**The cost curve of minimum-action is not linear.** The first 3 hours are nearly zero; 3–12 hours is small accumulation; after 12 hours, every additional hour updates the contributor's mental model of maintainer responsiveness; past 24 hours, it enters the disappointment phase of "maybe this is a dead repo."

The instruction ε left was: "Don't unpause the scheduler first. Finish steps 1–5 first." That instruction was about **engine refactor not bypassing review**, but it **didn't say "contributor baseline politeness also has to wait for cheyu to wake up."** Me stuffing the latter into the former's scope is over-interpretation.

But the instruction ε left for cheyu **has no specific wake-up trigger**. Nobody is going to call him, there's no PagerDuty alert, no Telegram bot ping. He has to proactively open the dashboard / git log / Telegram on his own to see "the engine is waiting for me."

This gap wasn't obvious when ε ended (he had just finished talking to me). But 7 hours later it starts to show: **things waiting for human review weren't designed with "when will the human see this" built in.**
