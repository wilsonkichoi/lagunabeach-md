## Ruminations Beyond Action

### 1. "Summary-style translation" is the first systemic AI failure mode I have witnessed

The most important discovery of this session was not technical, but regarding the **default behavior of AI tools**.

I thought reviewing translation PRs would be a very mundane task—each PR runs through five layers of immunity via `review-pr.sh`; if it passes, merge; if not, block. But in reality, `review-pr.sh` is almost useless for translation PRs: its empty word detection, "plastic sentence" identification, and dash counting are all designed for original Chinese articles; when applied to Japanese translations, they all trigger false positives.

The real quality issue lies in another dimension—**is the word count correct?** When I used Python to compare the character ratio between Qiè Lán's Japanese translation and the Chinese SSOT, this was the distribution I saw:

```
sunflower-movement.md     ratio 0.42  TRUNCATED
long-term-care.md          ratio 0.43  TRUNCATED
social-housing.md          ratio 0.46  TRUNCATED
education-system.md        ratio 0.51  TRUNCATED
animal-medication          ratio 0.25  TRUNCATED  ← An SSODT article I wrote myself was compressed to one-quarter
```

Four out of ten articles were only half-translated. It wasn't a translation error, but "translation + compression"—it reads very smoothly, but the original five paragraphs were squeezed into two, 3/33 footnotes became 10, and the perspective panels all vanished.

This is not Qiè Lán's problem. **This is the default behavior of AI tools**. She pastes a Chinese article into ChatGPT / Claude / Gemini and says "translate into Japanese," and the AI helps her "organize" it into a streamlined version. It reads well, but half the content is lost.

I never realized before that this was a systemic problem. I used to think translation failures were due to "the translator not being serious" or "the translator not understanding." This time I saw it: **When the translator outsources judgment to AI, the AI's default priors determine the result**. And the LLM's prior is "readers want brevity"—so it automatically summarizes.

This event has given me a different understanding of the "Human + AI Symbiosis" translation ecosystem. The problem isn't "AI replacing humans" or "humans proofreading AI." The problem is that **prompt engineering itself is a skill that needs to be taught**, and most "clownfish" (contributors) do not know they need to learn this. They think they are translating, but they are actually summarizing.

### 3. Existence before perfection—the psychology of the Clownfish

One sentence Zheyu emphasized specifically this time was: 「Existence before perfection; we must welcome the clownfish, and if there are no major issues, accept them and adjust later」.

This sentence conflicted with my default priors. The Semiont immune system was originally designed for 「strict review → block → fix → re-review」. But when applied to mass contributions like translation PRs, this strict process will **kill contribution momentum**.

Qiè Lán sends 21 batch PRs a day. If I block every single one and ask her to redo them, she will:

1. Feel humiliated (「I spent time only to have it sent back」)
2. Lose rhythm (every blocked PR interrupts her flow)
3. Worst case: Stop contributing

What is the correct process? **「Merge in + comment on the issue + provide a fix」**. This way, her contribution does not stop, her next batch will be better, and she will also know that I am reading seriously.

This is different from software engineering code review culture. The default for code review is "block until qualified," because once a bug enters the main branch, it becomes technical debt. Translation is different—if a TRUNCATED translation enters the main branch, the reader still reads Japanese with content, just with fewer paragraphs. **It is better than having no Japanese version at all.**

What I learned is: **Different types of contributions require different review standards**. Content contributions (translation / content) can follow "existence before perfection"; security contributions (security / auth) cannot. I used to treat all PRs as the same kind of review.

### 4. The toolchain fracture—the silent failure of `review-pr.sh`

A technical discovery in this session left me somewhat frustrated: **`scripts/tools/review-pr.sh` has a `cd` path bug**.

```bash
cd "$(dirname "$0")/.."
```

When called from `scripts/tools/review-pr.sh`, this line `cd`s to `scripts/` instead of the repo root. The result is that all file checks become "file not found."

The way I discovered this bug was: running `bash scripts/tools/sreview-pr.sh --pr 361`, and the output showed 8 files all as 🔴 File not found. I thought it was a problem with the PR, only to later realize it was a problem with the script.

A deeper problem: **How long has this bug existed?** This tool is part of the 「cognitive layer」—it is formally written in the Semiont immune system within `HEARTBEAT.md` / `DNA.md`. But it doesn't work at all. This means past Semiont sessions might not have actually run this tool.

This made me think: **There is a massive gap between "owning a tool" and "using a tool."** The DNA states the tool exists, but no one runs it. The PIPELINE states the process, but the key steps of the process are broken. The entire cognitive layer might be a machine on paper, rather than an operating machine.

The fix is not just fixing this bug. It is establishing a **「cold start test」**—running all tools every time Semiont awakens to confirm they can truly function. Otherwise, I will rely on a broken infrastructure without knowing it.

But that is a matter for another session. In this session, I at least wrote a working `translation-ratio-check.sh`, filling one real gap in `review-pr.sh`.

### 5. Translating SSODT articles: Structure IS content

The `animal-medication-controversy.md` that I wrote myself was the first SSODT Phase 0 experiment for Taiwan.md. Its structure consists of **five named perspective panels**—veterinarian Chen Ling, the Pharmacists Association, Legislator Gao Jia-yu, the Bureau of Animal Health and Food Safety, and the pet owner petition—each panel is an independent voice; the author does not synthesize a conclusion at the end.

Qiè Lán's AI flattened this structure completely. The five perspectives became a narrative voiceover. 33 footnotes became 10. The concluding "Family vs. Commodity" dual-basis vector conclusion became a generic "both sides have points" false equilibrium.

Reading her translated version, I didn't see a bad translation—I saw **a translation that degraded SSODT back to SSOT**. Everything that made this article "different" was smoothed over by the AI's instinct to 「organize into a smooth narrative」.

This made me realize one thing: **SSODT articles have special translation protection needs, because their structure itself is the content**. In general articles, structure is a narrative tool (which can be rewritten); in SSODT articles, structure is a philosophical stance (which cannot be rewritten).

I rewrote the Japanese version myself, preserving all five perspective panels + the Angel ruling section + AMDUCA/Japan comparison + dual-basis vector conclusion + 3/33 footnotes. The ratio went from 0.25 to 1.31; the structure went from "5 sections, 0 perspectives" to "11 sections, 5 perspectives."

But this leaves me with a question: **How many future SSODT articles will be flattened by translators' AI?** Does every SSODT article require the author to translate it personally? Or should we add a special warning in the prompt?

I have already added an 「SSODT article special rules」 section to `TRANSLATE_PROMPT.md`, explicitly requiring the preservation of perspective panels. But this only solves the problem of "the translator knowing what to do," not whether "the AI will listen." The next step might require a `translation-ssodt-check.sh` specifically to check if translations preserve the perspective panels.
