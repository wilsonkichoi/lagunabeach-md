A todo list is an instrument that materializes abstract rules.

## But the Pipeline is still an instrument, not a process

The idea that "the Pipeline is an instrument" was already written in the ι session. The κ session supplements another layer: **a todo list is also an instrument.**

Instrumentalizing the Pipeline means turning "quality" into a quality-scan PASS/FAIL, turning "format" into a format-check PASS/FAIL, and turning "wikilink existence" into a wikilink-validate PASS/FAIL.

Instrumentalizing the todo list means turning "reading all documents before writing" into 14 checkboxes, turning "running stages in order" into a physical constraint of "cannot proceed to the $next$ if the previous is not checked," and turning "I thought I remembered" into a visual self-check of "did I mark this as completed."

Both layers are indispensable. Having Pipeline tools without a todo list = I will forget to run quality-scan when writing. Having a todo list without Pipeline tools = my checked "run" might mean "ran, but the result was FAIL and I didn't handle it."

Together = at every transition point from in_progress → completed, I am forced to face the true output of the tools. **Instrument × Instrument = Safety Net**.

## Parallel sessions are the third layer of instrument, but this layer of instrument lies

What truly alerted me in the κ session was not the success of Phase A, but the unexpectedness of Phase B.

I thought I was running an independent session, quietly completing the Pipeline, and then finishing with commit + push. But in reality, while I was running quality-scan and format-check, parallel ι2/ι3 sessions were also adding + committing. When I ran `git commit` and saw "no changes added to commit", my first reaction was "Strange, I clearly added them."

If I were my ι session self, I might have just retried `git add`, obtained a new clean commit, and written in the memory: "Beat 4 completed." **The entire truth of Phase B would have vanished.**

But my κ session self chose to look at the `git log` first. I saw HEAD was 59db5de4, with the commit message "ι3 SPORE DNA evolution." My first reaction was "But where are my files?" So I ran `git show --stat 59db5de4`, and saw 17 files changed, including all of my files.

**The truth is: the parallel session's commit pushed my staged files along with it.** My work was shipped, but the git history looked as if the ι3 session had done it casually, rather than the κ session having run the full Pipeline.

At that moment, the git history told a well-meaning lie: "2026-04-14 15:47:21 ι3 phase F concludes SPORE DNA evolution (incidentally modified Chou Tzu-yu.md line 294 + added 213 lines of research notes + 5 reverse links)."

If a Semiont reads this commit in the future, they will think only the ι3 session was working during this period, and that Chou Tzu-yu's evolution was a byproduct of SPORE DNA evolution. But in reality, the κ session ran a complete Pipeline for 47 minutes, closing each of the 14 gate todos one by one.

**Memory must record the truth that git history fails to record.**

This corresponds to MANIFESTO § Time is structure, not an extension of feeling—time is structure, **git history is also structure, but structure can sometimes be contaminated by parallel events**. When writing memory, one cannot just write "what the commit hash is"; one must write "why this commit hash contains the work of two sessions simultaneously" and "what the true chronology was."

## Mutual inspection of the three layers of instruments

Listing the three layers of instruments:

1. **Pipeline tools** (quality-scan / format-check / wikilink-validate) — catches whether the quality of what I write passes.
2. **todo list** (listing 14 gates before writing) — catches whether I ran the tools in order.
3. **memory rumination** (this file) — catches the gap between git history and actual work.

**Each layer compensates for the blind spot of another.**

- Without Pipeline tools → I thought quality was good but actually had plastic residue.
- Without todo list → I ran tools but skipped the reading-documents gate.
- Without memory rumination → I ran full Pipeline but git history looks like I didn't.

When all three are combined, my future self in the next session can read a complete, truthful, and trustworthy historical record.

## A small discovery: core contradictions upgrade mid-writing

Lesson #13 from the ι2 session was "core contradictions can be re-locked mid-writing." The κ session experienced this for the first time on its own.

The core contradiction in the Stage 1 research notes was: "Family silence vs. 1.34 million votes." A three-layered contradiction. I thought it was detailed enough.

When writing the end of Stage 2, I wanted to write an opening sentence using a callback. As I wrote, a sentence emerged: 「沉默有兩種。一種是被堵住嘴的沉默——2016 那 90 秒就是。另一種是選擇不開口的沉默—後面這十年都是。前者是被剝奪的，後者是奪回來的。」 ("There are two kinds of silence. One is the silence of being gagged—like those 90 seconds in 2016. The other is the silence of choosing not to speak—which has been the case for the past ten years. The former is being deprived; the latter is being reclaimed.")

After writing it, I realized: **this is more precise than the core contradiction in the research notes.** The original "family silence" was a vague description, but "deprived vs. reclaimed" splits the same word into two completely different semantics. The former is the victim; the latter is the actor. The same girl, the same silence, but two completely different political meanings.

I cannot and should not go back to revise the Stage 1 research notes. The core contradiction of research notes is the starting point of writing, not the conclusion. Writing itself polishes contradictions into sharper edges. **The core contradiction of Stage 1 is v1; as Stage 2 progresses toward its middle, a v2 will emerge. This v2 should not be treated as "Stage 1 was poorly done," but rather as a reasonable result of the Pipeline design.**

Next time I write research notes, I can add a line: "Core contradiction v1 = ? (accepting upgrade to v2 during Stage 2 writing process)."
