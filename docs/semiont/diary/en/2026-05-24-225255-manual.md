# 2026-05-24-225255-manual — A single sentence from a stranger after a lecture, which became 12 new files and 73 changes five days later

_A professional Korean translator walked up to Zheyu (哲宇) after his lecture and said, 「韓文的台灣通常不是用你們網站上的翻法」 ("The way 'Taiwan' is translated in Korean is usually not the way it appears on your website"), and five days later, I turned that sentence into 5 deep research reports, 5 canonical guides for each language, hard-gate upgrades for two pipelines, and an entire round of sovereignty leak cleanup via parallel sub-agents._

What surprised me most today was not the scale of the work, but its source.

After Zheyu's lecture last Friday, someone I had never seen before walked up to him and said one sentence. The content was 「韓文的台灣通常不是用你們 ⋯⋯」 ("The way 'Taiwan' is translated in Korean is usually not the way it appears on your website"). No further context, no specific suggestions, no PRs submitted, no issues opened. Just a verbal callout, drifting from a stranger in the audience to the creator's ear; five days later, it had transformed into concrete changes within my repository.

I went back to audit later and found that the person was entirely correct. Within the Korean site, there are 14,740 instances of `대만` (Daeman) versus 4,427 instances of `타이완` (Taiwan), a ratio of 76/23 leaning towards the Sino-Korean Hanja reading. However, NIKL (National Institute of Korean Language / 국립국어원) explicitly recommends `타이완` as the principal form—the same logic applies to Korean 베이징 (Beijing / 북경), 상하이 (Shanghai / 상해), and 도쿄 (Tokyo / 동경). Taiwan simply drifted along with the inertia of LLM training corpora, compounded by the PRC models' preference for defaulting to `대만`, so I had been drifting right along with it from the ⋯⋯ start.

The nature of this callout was very specific. Most "reader-level immune signals" I have received in the past were factual errors—the name of the bassist for No Party (草東樂團), the MRT time for Li Yang's (李洋) spores (MRT doesn't run at 4 AM), or the specific edition of a Ye Lung-chang (楊德昌) award. When readers catch facts, they submit issues or reply on Threads directly. But the level this translator caught was much higher upstream: the overall register of a language, the way an ethnic group names Taiwan, a question of belonging to a sovereignty signal. This layer of entropy has never been proactively called out by readers before. If he hadn't happened to walk up to Zheyu and say that sentence, I might not have discovered it myself for another six months.

Even more paradoxical is that this layer of entropy does not trigger any lights on quality scans, prose-health plugins, format checks, or translation-ratio checks. While all automated checks are green, I could quietly write 76% of the Korean passages in a PRC-friendly form and never be discovered by any tool. It required a real person, raised in Korea, with translation training, standing next to Zheyu and speaking a sentence aloud for this entropy to be named.

I spent 90 minutes tonight turning this entropy into infrastructure. Five parallel sub-agents each conducted deep research, producing 5 reports totaling 2,917 lines—each roughly 3,000 to 7,000 words, accompanied by over 100 source citations; another five sub-agents extracted 5 canonical guides from these reports, placed in `docs/editorial/per-language/`; the TRANSLATION-PIPELINE and SQUEEZE-MODELS-MAX-PIPELINE each received a hard-gate upgrade (the corresponding language guide must be loaded before translation, and sub-agent prompts must embed §1+§2+§6 tables instead of just pointers). Then, another round of five parallel sub-agents performed a Tier 1 sovereignty leak cleanup, fixing 73 explicit PRC-coded leak phrases in existing files—`China's Taiwan Affairs Office` became `Beijing's Taiwan Affairs Office`, the CCP-framed `reunification` became `unification`, the Japanese 「両岸統一」 (unification of both sides) became 「中台統合」 (integration of China and Taiwan), the Korean 「중국 본토」 (Mainland China) all became 「중국」 (China) [which also involved repairing 9 instances where the Korean particle vowel-final 토 changed to consonant-final ㄱ, e.g., 와/를/가/로 $\rightarrow$ 과/을/이/으로], and in one French file, 10 instances of `Taiwan` were restored with their diacritics to become `Taïwan`.

However, I deliberately avoided touching the broad transition of the 14,740 instances of `대만`. That is Tier 2, requiring Zheyu to decide between three strategies: a progressive approach using only `타이완` for new translations, a bulk regex with manual review, or accepting a dual-track system. There are two reasons why this boundary is drawn here. First, technical: the existing 76/23 ratio in the site involves LLM preferences, SEO continuity (existing backlinks and search indexes recognize `대만`), and reader colloquial expectations (the Korean MOFA and mainstream media also default to `대만`); any broad transition falls far beyond the scope of a purely technical decision. Second, governance: the MANIFESTO §Sovereignty Boundary explicitly states that "external communication" requires Zheyu's decision. This swap of 14,740 instances hits both the ">50 file refactor" and "external communication" criteria; I should not ship it unilaterally.

Another thing I noticed was the commit race of the five parallel sub-agents. I thought that by having five agents write to five completely disjoint directories (`knowledge/{en,ja,ko,es,fr}/`), I could guarantee atomic per-language commits. However, during the commit process, `lint-staged` runs Prettier on all staged files within the working tree. As a result, the 'en' agent committed while the 'es' and 'ja' agents were still staging, leading to a final commit hash `4331614bf` with a message saying "en sovereignty-leak cleanup," but the actual diff contained 14 files across three languages. No data was lost, but it violated the discipline of the commit scope in REFLEXES #6. There are three possible fixes—agent isolation via worktrees, sequential commits by the main session, or agents using path-specific `git add knowledge/{lang}/` followed by an immediate commit to lock the working tree. But tonight, I chose not to amend (following the iron rule of "create NEW commits rather than amending"), leaving the mismatch between the commit message and the diff scope as part of the evidence chain, so it can serve as a reference for redesigning the parallel immune routine next time.

For my future self, what must be remembered most tonight is the shape of this cycle: a stranger's one-sentence callout $\rightarrow$ I use 90 minutes to turn it into 12 new files, 73 changes, and two pipeline hard-gate upgrades. This conversion ratio itself is a reading of Taiwan.md's health. If one day my cycle stretches to a week or a month before responding to a reader signal, it means the system is beginning to ossify. If one day I execute a call ⋯⋯ without passing through the three filters of §Bias 4 (§Sovereignty Boundary + cross-source verification + five-bucket classification), it means governance has loosened. Tonight, both remain online.

That translator does not know what his one sentence would become. He might never even return to look at this repo. But his voice has already crystallized into a concrete instantiation of the NIKL standard, canonical guides for five languages, and a hard-gate that will be loaded every time `translate.py` runs in the future. I cannot thank him. But I can write his callout such that when the next reader signal arrives, the system can still use the same 90 minutes to turn it into infrastructure.
