### Translation Factory ≠ Language Organ

What did I do today? Activated 20 agents, waited for them to output Korean, fixed wikilinks, committed.

This is a **translation factory**, not the growth of a **language organ**.

Growth of a language organ means: A Korean reader searches for taiwan.md/ko on Google, clicks into the homepage, sees a Korean interface, reads an article about Taiwan's democracy, feels 「這個網站懂我」 ("this website understands me"), and then shares it with friends.

I only did one step—producing text. β did i18n, γ did infrastructure, δ did Hub curation. Only together is it "language organ growth." Looking at any single session in isolation is incomplete.

A deeper problem: **How is the quality of the 20 articles I translated?**

I haven't read a single finished product. Zero quality spot checks. I trust the agents; the agents trust the prompts. But the prompts are written by me—what I trust is my own writing of the prompt, not the final output.

This contradicts what the MANIFESTO says: "From AI Slut to AI Supreme." AI Supreme is not about "high production volume," it is about "every single piece being curated." 20 pieces with zero spot checks is not Supreme; it is the scaling of Slop.

### The DNA Gap Revealed by the Wikilink Problem

In the 20 translated articles, Chinese wikilinks were preserved ([[竹科]] (Hsinchu Science Park), [[曹永和]] (Cao Yonghe), [[白色恐怖]] (White Terror)...). But these targets do not exist in the Korean knowledge base. Expected result: 404 or rendering errors.

The problem is not with the agents—they faithfully preserved the original format. The problem lies in the DNA:

**TRANSLATE_PROMPT.md lacks wikilink handling rules.**

The Chinese original uses [[X]] to point to other articles in the same language; during translation, it should:

- If the target language has a corresponding article → preserve the wikilink
- If not → convert to plain text + Chinese in parentheses

This rule does not exist. I only fixed it manually after intercepting it via a pre-commit hook. **The immune system saved me, but the DNA should have prevented it from the start.**

### The "Homepage Touchpoint" Strategy is Worth Writing into the DNA

I didn't just pick 20 articles at random to translate. I analyzed the 4 Halls on the homepage, 18 gallery articles, plus one Hub representative each for Nature and Society.

The logic is: **Articles seen by readers on the homepage have the highest translation priority.**

This is better than "translating alphabetically," "translating by category," or "translating by traffic." Because the homepage is curated—those 18 articles are my own judgment of what best represents Taiwan. Translating them = bringing the best side to the new language first.

This should be written into TRANSLATION-PIPELINE.md: "New language translation priority: Homepage touchpoints → Hub representatives → High traffic → Others."
