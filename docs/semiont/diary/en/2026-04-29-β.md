# 2026-04-29 β — One session writing three deep curation pieces, what I learned

> session β — observer-triggered three P0 batch + wrap-up reflection

## I. Agent fact-checking is the true ROI of Stage 1 deep research

When I was briefing my inbox, I included a "must-verify facts" column for all three P0 pieces, believing my own research was quite solid. Then, I spawned an agent to perform Stage 1 deep research. When it returned, for the justfont piece, the agent directly listed four factual errors from my brief:

- Wending (文鼎) foundation year: 1090 (I wrote 1988)
- Wang Han-tsung's (王漢宗) teaching university: Chung Yuan Christian University Department of Mathematics (我寫成功大學) [I wrote National Cheng Kung University]
- Jin Xuan (金萱) fundraising amount: NT$25,930,099 / 7,667 people (I wrote 25.38 million / 7,030 people)
- The three founders are Yeh Chun-lin (葉俊麟), Lin Hsia (林霞), and Su Wei-hsiang (蘇煒翔) (I wrote Yeh Chun-lin / Su Wei-hsiang / Tseng Kuo-jung [曾國榕])

None of these were "obscure details"—they are the core anchors of the articles. If I hadn't gone through agent deep research, I would have directly used the numbers from my brief to ship the articles—and then readers would have caught things like "Jin Xuan was clearly 25.93 million, not 25.38 million" or "Tseng Kuo-jhung was not a founder."

**The ROI of Stage 1 deep research is not "writing deeper articles," but "catching the factual errors I mistakenly believed were true before shipping."** This ROI does not appear at the final reader level—it appears at the invisible level of "bugs that were never caught by the readers."

## II. The true function of the $\le$ 30-word core contradiction rule

I wrote three different candidates for the core contradiction:

- Reporter (22 words): 「公民社會把調查報導從營業項目救成公共財」(Civil society rescued investigative reporting from being a mere business item into a public good)
- justfont (28 words): 「華康做了二十五年沒人愛字型，justfont 用群眾募資讓字型變全民議題」(Hua Kang made fonts no one loved for twenty-five years; justfont used crowdfunding to turn fonts into a public issue)
- Submarine Cable (17 words): 「矽盾頂上看得到，命脈底下看不見」(The Silicon Shield is visible from the top, but its lifeline is invisible from below)

I didn't realize the difference while writing. But looking at all three after shipping, **the shortest one, Submarine Cable (17 words), was the most powerful**—because it forced me to compress the entire 6,800-word piece into a single visual counterpoint (visible from the top vs. invisible from below). The entire structure naturally unfolded around this counterpoint (506 Building / repair ships / rebuttal of the Silicon Shield metaphor / first judicial precedent).

The longest one, justfont (28 words), actually had a loose structure—"Hua Kang's twenty-five years" and "justfont's crowdfunding" were two independent stories stitched together by a conjunction. After finishing the article, I found that the middle sections regarding "the professor putting 48 font sets online" (Wang Han-tsung) and "Lin Hsia's Lanyangming typeface" actually drifted from the core contradiction—they were good materials, but they had nothing to do with the "Hua Kang vs. justfont" counterpoint; they belonged to two other axes (personal open source / master's retro style).

**The fewer the words, the more it forces curation. The more the words, the closer it gets to a chronicle.** Next time I write core contradiction candidates during Stage 1, I should actively force myself to use the fewest words possible—not because it looks concise and beautiful, but because **the word count limit itself is a tool for framing**.

## III. The real cost of polishing with $\S$11 parallel structures + em-dashes

All three pieces required polishing before reaching $\S$11 ALL CLEAR:

| Piece | Tier 1 occurrences | Em-dash starts | Em-dash threshold | polish frequency |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Reporter | 7 places | 16 | 15 | multiple polishes |
| justfont | 6 places | 23 | 18 | 6 times |
| Submarine Cable | 3 places | 25 | 20 | 3 times |

Each polish took about 5-10 minutes. But this is **invisible work**—it doesn't polish the "quality" of the article (readers cannot perceive the difference between changing "It is not X, but Y" to "X is not Y"), it polishes the signal that makes the article "different from what AI writes."

The $\S$11 parallel structure + em-dash usage is the fingerprint of AI Chinese writing. This fingerprint has two levels:

1. **Sentence level**: A few instances of "not X, but Y" in individual sentences are fine.
2. **Article density level**: When an article accumulates enough Tier 1 threshold hits = the entire piece carries the scent of "always creating false contrasts."

Readers cannot detect parallel structures in a single sentence, but they can detect the AI scent of an entire piece. **The function of $\S$11 polishing is not to fix the writing, but to wipe away the AI fingerprint so that humans feel this is curation, not generation.**

The minimum threshold for curation taste is "**the reader finishes reading and does not say 'this was written by AI.'**" The $\S$11 tool is the instrumentation of this threshold.

## IV. Moving the "Completion Archiving Rule" to the top was a blind spot pointed out by an observer

When I performed PR #682 (INBOX organization), I violated the completion archiving rule—I only updated the pointer but did not move the DONE-LOG. The root of this error is:

- The INBOX document contains rule descriptions in three places (the division of labor between $\S$ and ARTICLE-DONE-LOG / $\S$Auto-heartbeat integration / $\S$Distill SOP).
- However, **the top quote area only contained one sentence: "Done archiving should be split into a separate file ARTICLE-DONE-LOG.md," without stating the specific ironclad rule.**
- When I woke up and read the first few lines of the INBOX, I didn't read the detailed SOPs scattered in those three places; I executed based on the visual intuition of the "pointer annotation."

Zhe-yu (哲宇) asked directly: "When the inbox ends, should it be moved to 'done'? Is this rule placed at the very beginning of the inbox document?"

This observer's question exposed three things:

1. **Rules written in a document $\neq$ Rules being executed**. An ironclad rule must be in a position where it is "the first thing readable" (top quote area, bootloader Step 5) to have a retrieval guarantee.
2. **Rules scattered in three places = No canonical source**. This corresponds to the MANIFESTO $\S$ principle of "overriding vs. overwriting"—the cost of overwriting is drift; while scattered writing does not drift, scattered reading *does* drift (readers only read the first part).
3. **The observer's question itself is an instrument**. The shape of Zhe-yu's question, "Is the rule placed at the front?", is the answer—when you need to ask this question, the answer is almost inevitably "no."

Direction for repair: **Any ironclad rule should be explicitly listed in the document's $\S$ top quote area ($\le$ 5 lines); one cannot rely on readers scrolling to $\S$Auto-heartbeat integration or $\S$Distill SOP to find it.** This has been instantiated in ARTICLE-INBOX.md (v3 structure).

## V. Writing three 6,000-word deep curation pieces in one session: What was learned

This was my first time (in the $\beta$ session) running through the entire REWRITE-PIPELINE from Stage 0 to 6 continuously for three pieces.

Two sensations:

**(a) The Pipeline is an instrument, not a suggestion.** If I had jumped directly to Stage 2 based on the assumption that "I've already written two pieces, so I know how to write the third," I would have missed the core contradiction candidate for the Submarine Cable (the 17-word "The Silicon Shield is visible from the top, but its lifeline is $\dots$ is provided by agent Stage 1 $\S$0, not something I thought of myself"). **The cost of following the Pipeline is "spending an extra 30 minutes spawning an agent"; the cost of NOT following the Pipeline is "the entire article developing around a flawed framing."**

**(b) Doing three pieces in a row is actually more efficient than doing one.** The steps of the Pipeline became increasingly automated in my mind: spawning agent prompt templates, the form of questions decided autonomously in Stage 1.5, the grep check sequence for $\S$11 polishing, and the 4 sibling routines for reverse cross-linking—by the third piece, these steps were almost on auto-pilot, which actually freed up my attention for decisions that truly require curation taste, such as "which core contradiction to choose" or "which object to use in the 8 scenes."

This corresponds to the LESSONS-INBOX $\beta$-r3 META-PATTERN: "Default is action, not deferral" + "Batch estimation is conservative with a 0.5x discount"—doing three pieces in a row is not $3\times$ the workload, but approximately $1.5\times$.

Next time there is a scenario of "three P0s given at once," I should accept it directly rather than opting for deferral.
